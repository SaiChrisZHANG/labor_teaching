# Behavioral Labor

## Course role

Behavioral Labor is the first flagship special-topics course in the portfolio. It should read as a real labor field elective: downstream from Labor I, in active conversation with Labor II, and organized around labor-market objects rather than around a generic behavioral-economics catalog.

## Cumulative logic

The course sequence is deliberately cumulative:

1. define the field and its taxonomy;
2. map behavioral wedges into core worker-side labor choices;
3. show how those wedges are measured and identified;
4. study policy, firm, and market responses;
5. end with welfare and research design.

The course should keep one discipline throughout: start from a labor benchmark, identify the behavioral departure, specify the labor margin that moves, and state what evidence would distinguish that account from standard alternatives.

## Internal roadmap

- **Foundational:** Weeks 1 to 4
- **Application-heavy:** Weeks 5 to 6
- **Empirical and methodological:** Week 7
- **Policy, equilibrium, and research-design oriented:** Weeks 8 to 10

## Week-by-week outline

### Week 1. What Is Behavioral Labor? Framing, Taxonomy, and Why Labor Is a Natural Domain

**Central question:** What makes Behavioral Labor a coherent labor-economics field rather than a loose application of behavioral economics?

This opening week defines the rational benchmark inherited from Labor I, introduces the core taxonomy of nonstandard preferences, nonstandard beliefs, and nonstandard decision-making, and explains why labor markets are unusually fertile ground for behavioral wedges. Labor choices are dynamic, contract-rich, institutionally mediated, and often filtered through firms and policy delivery, so the field needs a sharper map than a generic list of biases.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Labor economists care because the key behavioral objects are labor objects: work, effort, search, training, take-up, job choice, and wage-setting environments.

### Week 2. Nonstandard Preferences in Labor Supply, Effort, Savings, and Training

**Central question:** How do nonstandard preferences change labor-market choices that look like work, investment, or self-regulation problems?

The second week moves from taxonomy to worker-side application by studying present bias, commitment demand, reference dependence, and social preferences in settings where workers allocate effort and time across the present and future. The emphasis is on labor supply, workplace effort, savings-work interactions, and human-capital investments, with careful attention to when a behavioral preference wedge says more than standard impatience or simple heterogeneity.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Many labor decisions are investments with immediate costs and delayed returns, making self-control and reference dependence first-order labor mechanisms rather than side curiosities.

### Week 3. Beliefs, Expectations, Perception, and Job Search

**Central question:** What happens when workers optimize against mistaken or incomplete beliefs about labor-market returns and opportunities?

This week studies subjective expectations about wages, job-finding probabilities, search returns, training payoffs, and future self-control. The key move is to distinguish low response caused by low true returns from low response caused by distorted beliefs, misperceived probabilities, or mistaken expectations about future behavior. Job search provides the cleanest organizing application, but the scope also includes training and career planning.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Search, training, and career choice depend on perceived returns to labor-market actions, so belief measurement becomes part of labor identification rather than an optional add-on.

### Week 4. Attention, Salience, Complexity, and Labor-Market Policy Design

**Central question:** How do attention limits and contract complexity alter labor responses to incentives and programs?

The fourth week focuses on cases where workers respond to the contract, benefit, or program they perceive rather than the one written in the statute or payroll schedule. Taxes, transfers, deadlines, application burdens, and nonlinear incentives become central examples because they reveal how salience, reminders, simplification, and design shape measured labor supply and take-up behavior. This week is also the first strong bridge from worker behavior to implementation design.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Labor-market policy often works through earnings schedules, filing rules, and take-up margins, so attention and complexity are part of labor policy incidence.

### Week 5. Incentives, Contracts, Reciprocity, and Workplace Behavior

**Central question:** How do behavioral mechanisms reshape incentives and effort inside the employment relationship?

This week moves explicitly inside firms. The focus is on gift exchange, reciprocity, effort provision, incentive framing, monitoring, and the interpretation of contracts when workers are not purely spot-market optimizers. The goal is not to abandon standard personnel economics, but to show when behavioral mechanisms change the predictions of wage-effort relationships, bonus design, supervision, and contract communication.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** The employment relationship is a labor contract, so effort, pay, supervision, and reciprocity are core labor questions rather than generic organizational topics.

### Week 6. Identity, Norms, Fairness, and Labor-Market Allocation

**Central question:** How do identity and social norms shape who works where, under what conditions, and with what responses?

The sixth week broadens workplace behavior into allocation and sorting. Gender norms, occupational meaning, fairness judgments, and social identity can affect household labor allocation, occupational choice, workplace response, and group-specific labor-market outcomes. The scope stays disciplined by asking when identity and norms alter labor supply, sorting, or wage-setting in ways that standard price-based models miss.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Labor markets allocate people across jobs, households, occupations, and groups, and identity-based distortions can change both observed allocation and the interpretation of inequality.

### Week 7. Experiments, Measurement, and Behavioral Identification in Labor

**Central question:** How can labor economists identify behavioral wedges without confusing them with constraints, institutions, or measurement error?

This methodological week organizes the empirical toolkit: field experiments, expectation elicitation, administrative behavior, survey design, quasi-experiments, and structural estimation. The course pauses here to build a durable identification framework before moving further into policy and equilibrium, with emphasis on matching each design to the wedge it is supposed to isolate.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Labor evidence is often policy-relevant and high-stakes, so behavioral claims need designs that separate worker mistakes from firm responses, market frictions, and institutional rules.

### Week 8. Firm, Market, and Equilibrium Responses to Behavioral Frictions

**Central question:** What happens once firms, markets, and institutions adapt to predictable behavioral patterns?

The eighth week studies behavioral labor beyond partial equilibrium. Firms may exploit, accommodate, insure, screen, sort, or segment around behavioral frictions through menus, defaults, benefits, recruiting, communication, and search environments. Markets may dampen some frictions and amplify others. Policy will have different effects once employer response, market learning, switching costs, and equilibrium incidence are brought back in. This is the week that most directly connects the course to Labor II.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Behavioral wedges matter differently once employer strategy, labor demand, and equilibrium incidence are on the page.

### Week 9. Behavioral Public Policy in Labor Markets

**Central question:** How should labor-market policy be designed when take-up, response, understanding, and firm or market response are behaviorally bounded?

This week revisits worker-facing policy through program take-up, simplification, reminders, defaults, and targeting after Week 8 has made firm and market response explicit. The point is not only that behavioral design can improve implementation, but also that statutory generosity and effective exposure can diverge sharply when workers misunderstand eligibility, underreact to incentives, face burdens that standard models treat as second order, or encounter firms and intermediaries that respond strategically.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Labor policy is delivered through earnings, participation, search, take-up, employer menus, and intermediaries, making behavioral implementation a labor-market question rather than just a public-finance question.

### Week 10. Synthesis, Welfare, and Student Research Designs

**Central question:** What does a credible behavioral labor research agenda look like once mechanism, evidence, equilibrium, and welfare are integrated?

The closing week synthesizes the course and turns it toward research design. Students should leave able to classify a behavioral labor question, propose an identifying design, state the relevant equilibrium margin, and explain the welfare benchmark. The week also clarifies where the field is strongest, where it remains overclaimed, and which open questions are most promising for future labor research.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** The research agenda is strongest when behavioral mechanisms are tied to labor-market institutions, firm response, policy delivery, and welfare rather than left as stand-alone anomalies.
