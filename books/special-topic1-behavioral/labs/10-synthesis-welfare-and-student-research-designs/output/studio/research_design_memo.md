# Week 10 behavioral-labor research-design memo

## Anchor selection

- Primary anchor: `muellerSpinnewijnTopa2021` (Mueller, Spinnewijn, and Topa (2021))
- Comparison anchor: `bhargavaManoli2015` (Bhargava and Manoli (2015))
- Studio logic: Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer

## 1. Puzzle or fact

Starter puzzle from the primary anchor:
Job seekers' subjective expectations differ systematically from realized employment prospects.

Your project question:
Replace this sentence with a labor-market fact or puzzle that is narrow enough to study.

## 2. Behavioral mechanism

- Primary wedge: Biased beliefs, heterogeneous updating, and duration-dependent expectations.
- Comparison wedge: Psychological frictions, inattention, complexity, perceived eligibility, and administrative burden.

Your task:
Name the behavioral wedge, one standard labor-economics alternative, and the evidence that would distinguish them.

## 3. Labor-market setting

- Primary setting: Unemployment spells with repeated belief measures and employment outcomes.
- Comparison setting: Benefit claiming and policy take-up interfaces that affect earnings-linked program exposure.

Your task:
Explain why the institution, firm, market, platform, or policy environment makes the wedge first-order.

## 4. Outcome and unit of observation

- Primary labor margin: Search intensity, perceived job-finding probabilities, and unemployment exit.
- Comparison labor margin: Application completion, claiming, timing, persistence, and possible labor-supply interaction.

Your task:
Name the observed outcome, unit of observation, and the broader labor-market object you are tempted to infer.

## 5. Identifying variation

- Primary variation: Within-person belief dynamics linked to realized job-finding outcomes.
- Comparison variation: Randomized notices, simplification, salience, or assistance treatments.

Your task:
State the variation that identifies your bounded claim and one threat to interpretation.

## 6. Econometric method

- Primary method: Panel fixed effects, forecast-error diagnostics, and hazard or duration analysis.
- Comparison method: Randomized encouragement, treatment-effect summaries, heterogeneity analysis, and hazard-style claiming timing.

Your task:
Choose the method because it matches the estimand, not because it is familiar.

## 7. Welfare or policy interpretation

- Primary welfare object: Welfare depends on whether mistaken beliefs distort search effort, reservation choices, or training decisions.
- Comparison welfare object: Welfare depends on whether non-take-up reflects mistakes, hassle costs, stigma, or low private value.

Your task:
Define the benchmark action, long-run learning criterion, or welfare limitation.

## 8. Contribution relative to the frontier

Primary transfer prompt:
Apply repeated beliefs and forecast-error logic to job search after a sectoral, technology, or displacement shock.

Comparison transfer prompt:
Apply take-up and implementation logic to UI, training, disability, EITC, or local job services.

Your task:
Say what the literature already knows, what your project adds, and why the added object matters for behavioral labor.

## What this design does not identify

Primary limitation:
A pure welfare benchmark or general-equilibrium search response without additional assumptions.

Comparison limitation:
Complete welfare without benefit values, costs, stigma, targeting, and equilibrium program response.

Your task:
Add one honest sentence about a parameter, welfare claim, or equilibrium response that remains outside your design.
