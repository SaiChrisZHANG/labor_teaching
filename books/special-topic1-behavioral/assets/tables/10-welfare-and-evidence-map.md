## Week 10 table — welfare and evidence map

| Domain | What is observed? | Why welfare is hard | Useful evidence / methods |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Labor supply / commitment | Effort, hours, savings, training take-up | Observed choice may reflect temptation, present bias, or missing commitment | Field experiments, contract/menu variation, structural estimation |
| Job search and beliefs | Applications, search intensity, reservation beliefs, unemployment duration | Search choices may reflect biased expectations or slow learning | Panel beliefs data, hazard models, information interventions |
| Attention / complexity / claiming | Enrollment, claiming, schedule response, form completion | Non-response may reflect burden or inattention rather than preferences | Reminder experiments, administrative simplification, dynamic learning analysis |
| Workplace incentives / reciprocity | Output, attendance, quality, gaming, subjective effort | Output mixes motivation, monitoring, multitasking, and social preferences | Field experiments, heterogeneity analysis, contract design inference |
| Identity / norms / culture | Sorting, peer effects, behavior under social comparison | Choices may respond to social image or local norms, not stable tastes | Experiments, network/peer designs, workplace culture communication data |
| Policy design | Take-up, claiming, persistence, targeting, learning | Need to distinguish correction, implementation, and paternalism | Sufficient-statistics, calibrated welfare, structural or hybrid approaches |
| Firm / market response | Menus, defaults, sorting, equilibrium pass-through | Worker-level wedge may not survive once firms respond | Menu design evidence, market-level comparisons, equilibrium modeling |
