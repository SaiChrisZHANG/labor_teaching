| Role of behavioral friction in policy design | Core question | Typical labor-policy examples | Main empirical object | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Bias to be corrected** | Is the wedge mainly misunderstanding, inattention, present bias, or low knowledge? | EITC schedules, disability claiming, retirement-benefit rules | response to information / simplification / learning | Standard incentives may underperform if workers misperceive the environment |
| **Lever to be harnessed** | Can defaults, reminders, commitment, or framing improve outcomes? | 401(k) enrollment, savings escalation, deadlines, active choice | response to defaults / reminders / framing | Policy can sometimes improve welfare without changing prices |
| **Implementation friction** | Is low participation driven by hassle, stigma, procedural complexity, or trust? | benefit claiming, UI application, training enrollment | take-up / completion / claiming timing | Implementation is an economic margin, not just an administrative afterthought |
| **Dynamic learning margin** | Do workers learn schedules, rules, or procedures over time? | tax-benefit schedules, Social Security claiming, repeated filing | dynamic response / lagged effects / neighborhood diffusion | Short-run estimates can miss long-run policy effects |
| **Equilibrium / intermediary channel** | How do firms, counselors, tax preparers, or peers mediate policy? | plan design, tax preparers, job-center guidance, employer retirement menus | mediated response / pass-through / local variation | Behavioral policies are filtered through institutions and market actors |
| **Welfare / targeting complication** | Which mistakes are welfare-relevant, and who should be targeted? | defaults, simplification, reminders, labor-tax design | welfare under distorted choice / heterogeneous mistakes | Policy evaluation becomes a normative and distributional problem |
