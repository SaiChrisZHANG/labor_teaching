# Firm, Market, and Equilibrium Responses to Behavioral Frictions

## Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 8, students should be able to:

1. explain why worker-level behavioral wedges do not map one-for-one into observed market outcomes;
2. classify firm responses as exploitation, accommodation, insurance, or screening, sorting, and segmentation;
3. identify the market conditions under which behavioral wedges are attenuated, amplified, or preserved;
4. connect employer-sponsored benefits, plan-choice markets, worker beliefs, search, and firm communication to equilibrium response;
5. distinguish worker-level reduced-form effects, firm response margins, and market or equilibrium outcomes;
6. state the welfare and policy-design limits of partial-equilibrium behavioral evidence.

## Opening Orientation

Weeks 2 through 4 studied worker-side behavioral objects: nonstandard preferences, mistaken beliefs, limited attention, complexity, and learning. Weeks 5 and 6 showed that firms, supervisors, peers, and workplace meanings can shape how those objects appear inside organizations. Week 7 supplied the identification discipline. Week 8 now asks the next-level labor-economics question:

**Once worker-level behavioral wedges exist, how do firms and markets respond, and what survives in equilibrium?**

The point is not to deny worker mistakes, inertia, or misperceptions. The point is that observed wages, benefits, plan choices, job applications, search outcomes, and welfare are not raw reflections of worker psychology. They are equilibrium objects produced by workers, firms, intermediaries, disclosure rules, search frictions, switching costs, and market power.

A compact way to write the problem is:

```{math}
:label: eq:week8-equilibrium-outcome
Y_{im} = g\!\left(\theta_i,\; R_f(\theta_i,\mathcal{M}_m),\; \mathcal{M}_m\right),
```

where {math}`\theta_i` is a worker-level behavioral wedge, {math}`R_f(\cdot)` is firm response, and {math}`\mathcal{M}_m` is the market environment. The same worker wedge can generate very different outcomes depending on whether firms compete strongly, disclose clearly, exploit inertia, simplify choices, screen workers, or exercise labor-market power.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important

- Worker-level behavioral wedges are inputs into markets, not final market outcomes.
- Firms can exploit, accommodate, insure, or screen and sort around predictable behavioral patterns.
- Behavioral wedges survive when competition, transparency, and learning are weak, or when switching costs, search frictions, heterogeneity, and labor-market power are strong.
- Employer-sponsored benefits and retirement or health-plan markets are central because worker choices, employer menus, defaults, disclosure, and firm incentives are observed in the same institutional setting.
- Distorted outside-option beliefs can affect search, bargaining, and segmentation; this turns a Week 3 belief object into a labor-market allocation object.
- Welfare analysis must separate attenuation from amplification, partial from equilibrium effects, and worker mistakes from endogenous firm design.
- Week 9 policy design should be asked only after the equilibrium response problem is visible.

:::

## Bridge

This week deliberately avoids replaying the Week 5 lecture on incentives, monitoring, reciprocity, and supervision or the Week 6 lecture on identity, norms, fairness, and culture. Those mechanisms may matter in the background, but they do not organize the week. The organizing object is endogenous response.

Week 3 asked how worker beliefs affect search. Week 8 asks whether distorted outside-option beliefs survive once firms set wages, workers search across segmented markets, and bargaining positions depend on perceived alternatives. Week 4 asked how salience and complexity affect policy response. Week 8 asks whether firms design menus and disclosures in ways that reduce complexity, profit from it, or sort workers by sophistication. Week 7 asked how to identify behavioral objects. The lecture uses three empirical domains:

- worker beliefs, search, bargaining, and segmentation;

Together they show students how researchers move from a behavioral wedge to an equilibrium labor question.

## Field Core

### Why Worker-Level Wedges Are Not Market Outcomes

Suppose a worker is inattentive to fees, anchored on a current wage, slow to switch plans, or uncertain about outside offers. A partial-equilibrium interpretation might say the outcome is simply "the bias." In a market, that is rarely enough. Firms may change plan menus, defaults, recruiting messages, prices, wages, fees, or job attributes. Other workers may be sophisticated and discipline the market. Search frictions may prevent discipline. Regulation may alter disclosure. Market power may let firms keep rents from inattentive or pessimistic workers.

The first decomposition is conceptual:

```{math}
:label: eq:week8-decomposition
\Delta Y =
\underbrace{\Delta Y^{worker}}_{\text{worker-level reduced form}}
+
\underbrace{\Delta Y^{firm}}_{\text{menu, wage, message, default, price}}
+
\underbrace{\Delta Y^{market}}_{\text{sorting, entry, competition, incidence}}.
```

The terms are not mechanically separable in every dataset. The decomposition is a research habit. A default experiment may identify a worker-level participation response, but welfare depends on the default chosen by the employer. A disclosure reform may shift demand elasticity, but the equilibrium object includes firm prices, advertising, and sales effort. A belief intervention may change search, but market outcomes depend on wage-setting, bargaining, and the distribution of outside options.

This distinction matters most when the partial-equilibrium evidence is strong. Large default effects, large inertia, or large belief errors are not yet equilibrium welfare statements. They are evidence that a market response is worth studying.

### A Taxonomy Of Firm Responses

Firms do not have one response to worker-level behavioral frictions. Four response families are useful for labor applications.

| Firm response | What the firm does | When it is profitable or durable | Main empirical objects |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exploit | Designs menus, fees, defaults, messages, or job attributes to profit from inertia, confusion, or low switching | Low transparency, high switching costs, weak competition, heterogeneous sophistication | Fees, dominated plan choices, default stickiness, sales effort, shrouded attributes |
| Accommodate | Simplifies choices, improves disclosure, reduces hassle, or makes good choices easier | Worker mistakes reduce productivity, retention, match quality, or plan value; competition rewards clarity | Simplification, active choice, clearer menus, better matching, reduced mistakes |
| Insure | Uses defaults, restricted menus, benefits, or compensation structure to protect workers from volatility or mistakes | Employers internalize worker welfare, retention, productivity, or regulatory risk | Defaults, contribution floors, benefit menus, plan design, risk protection |
| Screen, sort, segment | Uses contracts, messages, plan menus, or application design to attract or separate worker types | Workers differ in sophistication, beliefs, patience, risk, or search costs; firms can target types | Applicant pools, take-up composition, wage bids, plan choice by type, match quality |

This taxonomy is intentionally broad. A default can insure some workers and exploit others. A complex benefit menu can screen sophisticated workers, segment inattentive workers, and create rents for intermediaries. A recruiting message can inform applicants or steer them into a job where their beliefs and constraints make them more profitable. The research question is which response is actually operating and whether the market environment disciplines it.

### Market Conditions That Govern Survival

The second table links market conditions to the survival of behavioral wedges.

| Market condition | When wedges are attenuated | When wedges survive or amplify | Diagnostic evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Competition | Firms compete for total compensation and clear menus; sophisticated workers discipline offers | Firms compete on salient attributes while shrouding fees, risks, or future costs | Price and wage pass-through, entry, fee compression, menu redesign |
| Transparency | Workers can compare wages, benefits, fees, and outside options | Attributes are opaque, multidimensional, delayed, or broker mediated | Disclosure effects, fee salience, comprehension, comparison behavior |
| Switching costs and inertia | Switching is cheap, automatic, and salient | Enrollment, job change, plan change, or search is costly and delayed | Default persistence, plan switching, tenure gradients, response to active choice |
| Search frictions | Workers observe offers and learn outside options quickly | Workers search little, believe current wages reveal outside options, or face segmented networks | Applications, reservation wages, beliefs, offer arrivals, wage posting dispersion |
| Worker heterogeneity | Sophisticated workers discipline the market for everyone | Firms can price discriminate, segment, or target less sophisticated workers | Heterogeneous effects by sophistication, income, beliefs, tenure, or channel |
| Labor market power | Workers can credibly move and bargain | Firms can retain workers with pessimistic beliefs, low mobility, or local concentration | Markdowns, quits, outside-option beliefs, bargaining response, mobility |

The broad conceptual anchors are [@gabaixLaibson2006] and [@spiegler2015]. Their value for this course is not that they are labor papers. Their value is that they formalize the equilibrium mistake in a common intuition: competition does not automatically eliminate exploitation when some agents are inattentive, attributes are shrouded, or firms can profit from behavioral heterogeneity. Labor markets add search, matching, benefits, employer mediation, and labor-market power to that logic.

### Employer-Sponsored Benefits, Savings, And Plan-Design Markets

Employer-sponsored benefits are the central empirical domain for this week because they make three objects observable at once: worker choice, firm or intermediary design, and market structure. Benefits are compensation, but they are not a simple wage. They are menus, defaults, enrollment deadlines, plan attributes, fees, risk protection, and administrative interfaces.

In employer-sponsored retirement plans, [@bernheimFradkinPopov2015] studies how default options affect 401(k) choices and welfare. The worker-level reduced form is default stickiness: many workers remain at or near the default contribution and asset allocation. The firm response margin is plan design: employers choose defaults and menus that shape the passive option. The market or welfare object is not simply participation. It is whether the default-induced allocation moves workers toward choices they would make under better attention, lower procrastination, or clearer preferences.

The identifying variation in this block comes from differences in default plan design and worker exposure to those defaults. The observed margins include participation, contribution rates, asset allocation, and welfare-relevant distance from benchmark choices. The empirical lesson is sharp: a large default effect can be welfare-improving, welfare-reducing, or ambiguous depending on who is passive and what the default does for them.

Health-plan choice adds another layer. [@handelKolstad2015] studies information frictions and health insurance plan choice. The worker-level object is imperfect information and costly plan comparison. The observed margin is plan choice relative to expected costs and plan attributes. The firm or institutional response is the menu environment in which choices are made. The welfare object is money left on the table, risk exposure, and the value of better information. This is a labor-market domain because health insurance is part of compensation and is often mediated through the employer.

The Mexico social-security and retirement-plan evidence makes the market-response logic even more direct. [@duarteHastings2012] studies "fettered consumers and sophisticated firms" in privatized social security. The worker-level friction is limited attention to fees, returns, and account attributes. The firm margin includes advertising, sales-force effort, and product positioning. The observed market margin is demand elasticity, switching, and the distribution of account choices. [@hastingsHortacsuSyverson2017] pushes this further by studying sales force and competition. The key variation includes disclosure, advertising, and competitive conditions; the observed margins include demand sensitivity, sales-force allocation, fees, and plan switching.

These papers should be read as an empirical sequence:

| Paper | Identifying variation | Observed margin | Worker-level object | Firm response margin | Market or welfare object |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [@bernheimFradkinPopov2015] | Variation in retirement-plan defaults and worker exposure | Participation, contributions, asset allocation | Inertia, procrastination, passive choice | Employer default and menu design | Welfare of default-induced allocations |
| [@handelKolstad2015] | Variation in information and plan-choice environment | Health-plan choice and spending risk | Information frictions, plan misunderstanding | Employer or institutional menu design | Welfare loss from dominated or costly plan choices |
| [@duarteHastings2012] | Disclosure and market variation in retirement accounts | Switching, plan demand, fee sensitivity | Limited attention, low fee salience | Sophisticated firm marketing and positioning | Whether disclosure disciplines firms |
| [@hastingsHortacsuSyverson2017] | Sales force and competitive variation | Demand, fees, switching, plan shares | Inattention and search or switching costs | Sales-force allocation and competition | Equilibrium fees and consumer surplus |

The frontier question is external validity. A nudge or disclosure that works in one menu may not work after firms redesign menus, change sales effort, or target different workers. A default that raises saving in one employer setting may not imply a general default policy unless the researcher understands who chooses the default and why the employer selected it.

### Search, Bargaining, And Segmentation Under Distorted Worker Beliefs

The second empirical block starts from beliefs but does not stay at the worker level. Beliefs about outside options matter for search, bargaining, quits, and wage-setting. If workers underestimate their outside options, they may search too little, bargain weakly, accept low offers, or remain in low-wage matches. If those beliefs are persistent and firms recognize them, distorted beliefs become a segmentation and market-power problem.

[@jagerRothRoussilleSchoefer2022] is the central anchor. The worker-level object is beliefs about outside options. The observed margins include perceived outside wages, applications, job search, mobility, bargaining, and wage response. The identifying variation comes from elicited beliefs and information that lets researchers compare subjective expectations with objective or revealed outside-option measures. The equilibrium interpretation is larger than misperception. Pessimistic outside-option beliefs can create a wedge between actual and perceived mobility, making labor-market power stronger than a model with fully informed workers would predict.

This block is a bridge from behavioral labor to Labor II. In a standard search or bargaining model, outside options discipline wages because workers know or learn alternatives. With distorted beliefs, perceived outside options may be lower than true alternatives:

```{math}
:label: eq:week8-outside-options
\tilde w_i^{out} = w_i^{out} - \mu_i,
```

where {math}`\mu_i>0` is pessimism or underperception. A firm that faces workers with large {math}`\mu_i` can retain them at lower wages even if objective outside options are better. Whether this becomes equilibrium segmentation depends on search costs, offer arrival, wage transparency, bargaining institutions, and competition for workers.

[@menzio2022] gives the conceptual search-equilibrium discipline. Stubborn beliefs can persist and affect search equilibrium rather than being washed out by market experience. The value of this reading is that it forces students to ask what kind of learning environment is needed for beliefs to converge. If beliefs are sticky and search is costly, market outcomes may reflect a distribution of perceived outside options, not only actual outside options.

The key empirical distinction is:

| Level | Object | Example measure | Interpretation limit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Worker-level reduced form | Belief error or information effect | Stated outside-option belief, search response, bargaining intention | Does not alone identify firm wage-setting or market power |
| Firm response margin | Wage offers, retention strategy, bargaining response | Raises, counteroffers, posted wages, retention after belief shocks | Requires observing employer response, not only worker beliefs |
| Market outcome | Segmentation, wage dispersion, markdowns, mobility | Quits, reallocation, wage growth, local concentration, mobility gaps | Requires equilibrium or market-level design |

The research frontier is behavioral monopsony and search. The strongest designs will connect belief measurement to wage-setting, employer response, and reallocation, not only to individual search intentions.

### Sorting, Information, And Market Communication

The third empirical block studies how firms communicate with workers and how that communication changes sorting. [@hortonKuchlerStanton2021] provides the anchor because it studies cheap talk and evidence from a labor market. The worker-level object is uncertainty about job or employer attributes. The firm response margin is communication: what firms say, when messages are credible, and how messages target workers. The observed margins include applications, wage bids, matching, and sorting.

This is not a repeat of Week 6 firm culture. Week 6 studied identity, norms, and culture as labor objects. Week 8 uses communication as a market mechanism. The question is whether messages improve information, direct search toward better matches, or strategically segment workers. A message can reduce mismatch if it credibly reveals job attributes. It can also exploit uncertainty if workers over-interpret cheap talk or cannot verify attributes before accepting work.

The empirical discipline is again a three-part distinction:

- **Worker-level effect:** Does a message change applications, wage bids, or beliefs?
- **Firm response margin:** Which messages do firms choose, and do firms target messages to worker types?
- **Market outcome:** Does communication improve matching, reduce search costs, segment workers, or change wage dispersion?

The sorting logic is central. Behavioral wedges are not only mistakes conditional on a job. They can change who applies to which jobs, which firms attract which workers, and how the market partitions sophistication, risk tolerance, beliefs, and attention. This is where screening and segmentation enter the equilibrium lecture.

### Identification Across Empirical Blocks

Students should leave this week knowing what researchers actually identify. The following table is the practical methods map.

| Empirical block | Identifying variation | Observed margin | What is identified first | What needs extra evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Employer retirement defaults | Differences in default design, timing, or exposure | Participation, contribution rate, asset allocation | Worker default response under a given menu | Employer objective in setting defaults and welfare benchmark |
| Health-plan information frictions | Plan-choice environment, information, and model-based counterfactuals | Plan selection, expected costs, risk exposure | Mistakes or welfare losses under observed menus | How employers or insurers redesign menus after disclosure |
| Retirement account markets | Disclosure, sales force, advertising, competition | Demand elasticity, switching, fees, plan shares | Demand response to information and marketing | Equilibrium price, fee, and sales-force response |
| Outside-option beliefs | Elicited beliefs, objective outside-option measures, information variation | Search, bargaining, quits, wage response | Belief distortion and worker response | Firm wage-setting response and market segmentation |
| Labor-market communication | Message variation, platform design, firm text, applicant response | Applications, wage bids, matches | Response to information or cheap talk | Whether messages reveal quality or strategically sort workers |

The recurring warning is that worker-level reduced forms are necessary but not sufficient. A good Week 8 research design either observes firm response directly or states exactly what assumptions allow the analyst to move from worker response to market interpretation.

### Welfare Interpretation And Empirical Pitfalls

Behavioral equilibrium evidence creates several welfare pitfalls.

First, attenuation is not the same as correction. Competition may attenuate a worker wedge by forcing firms to offer better defaults or clearer menus. But competition may also redirect exploitation toward less salient attributes, as in shrouded-attribute logic [@gabaixLaibson2006].

Second, amplification is not only manipulation. Firms may amplify a wedge through fees, defaults, or sales effort, but they may also amplify a beneficial commitment or insurance device. A high default contribution rate can help present-biased workers save. It can also over-save for workers with liquidity needs.

Third, partial effects are not equilibrium effects. A disclosure reform can raise price sensitivity in the short run; firms may respond by changing prices, advertising, menus, or product design. A belief correction can raise search; firms may respond through wages, retention, or recruiting. A platform message can change applications; firms may change messages once the platform rule is known.

Fourth, revealed preference is weaker when the choice architecture is endogenous. If firms design menus around inertia, observed plan choice does not automatically reveal welfare-relevant preference. If workers believe outside options are poor because they rarely search, staying in a job does not automatically reveal the job is their best available match.

Fifth, selection is not a nuisance detail. Behavioral frictions often change composition. Defaults may mostly bind passive workers. Disclosure may move sophisticated workers first. Firm messages may sort workers who are already more responsive to posted information. The welfare effect depends on who moves, who remains, and how firms respond to the new composition.

A useful welfare checklist is:

1. What is the worker-level behavioral wedge?
2. What margin did firms or intermediaries change?
3. Which market condition lets the wedge survive or disappear?
4. Who is marginal: inattentive workers, sophisticated workers, low-mobility workers, new hires, incumbents, or high-switching-cost workers?
5. What is the welfare benchmark: full information, low hassle, sophisticated choice, structural utility, or planner-specified target?
6. What policy conclusion would change once firm response is endogenous?

### Frontier Directions

The frontier is not "add equilibrium" as a slogan. It is measurement and design.

**Equilibrium measurement.** Researchers need data linking workers, firms, menus, communication, wages, benefits, and market structure. Worker surveys alone rarely identify firm response. Firm menus alone rarely identify worker welfare. Linked designs are the frontier.

**External validity of nudges.** Defaults, disclosure, simplification, and messages may work differently once firms anticipate them. The external-validity question is whether the effect survives when the market redesigns around the intervention.

**Welfare under endogenous firm response.** Welfare analysis must let firms change defaults, prices, wages, sales effort, and communication. This is especially important in employer-sponsored benefits because the employer is both compensation designer and intermediary.

**Segmentation and behavioral monopsony.** Distorted outside-option beliefs can interact with search costs, local concentration, and worker heterogeneity. The frontier is to measure whether belief distortions produce persistent wage markdowns, low mobility, and segmented job ladders.

**Sorting and market communication.** Firms increasingly communicate job attributes through platforms, postings, ratings, and algorithmic interfaces. The open question is whether this communication informs workers, screens types, or creates new behavioral segmentation.

## Research Lab

The Week 8 lab is organized around **Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer**.

**Reproduce.** The primary anchor is [@bernheimFradkinPopov2015]. Students use deterministic synthetic data to reproduce a compact retirement-default exercise. The objective is not an official replication. The bounded exercise estimates how default assignment changes participation, contribution rates, passive choice, and a simple welfare-distance measure.

**Diagnose.** Students then diagnose whether the observed default effect is a worker-level reduced form, a firm-response margin, or a market outcome. They classify the firm response as exploitation, accommodation, insurance, or sorting. They also ask whether the welfare interpretation depends on menu design, default quality, heterogeneous liquidity needs, or worker sophistication.

**Transfer.** The challenge anchor is [@jagerRothRoussilleSchoefer2022]. Students transfer the same diagnostic logic to outside-option beliefs. They compare belief distortions, search response, bargaining response, and a segmentation index. The exercise forces the distinction between a worker belief effect and a labor-market allocation effect.

**Optional extension.** Students can use [@duarteHastings2012] or [@hastingsHortacsuSyverson2017] as the optional extension. This branch asks how disclosure, sales effort, and switching costs change demand elasticity and the interpretation of firm strategy in retirement-plan markets.

The lab is built around four diagnostic objects:

- menu design and defaults;
- demand elasticity and switching-cost interpretation;
- belief distortions and market segmentation;
- equilibrium interpretation limits.

The deliverable is a short research memo that states the identifying variation, observed margin, first-stage behavioral object, firm-response margin, and market or welfare interpretation for each block.

## Methods Box

:::{admonition} Methods box: identifying equilibrium response to behavioral wedges
:class: note

**Default and menu designs** usually begin with worker-level contrasts in participation, contribution rates, or plan choice. Equilibrium interpretation requires evidence on why the menu or default was chosen and which workers remain passive.

**Disclosure and information designs** estimate how demand, switching, or plan choice respond when attributes become more visible. Market interpretation requires observing prices, fees, sales effort, advertising, entry, or plan redesign.

**Belief and search designs** use elicited beliefs, objective outside-option measures, and information variation. Labor-market interpretation requires linking belief response to search, bargaining, wage-setting, quits, and reallocation.

**Communication and sorting designs** use message variation, platform rules, job text, or firm-posting changes. They must distinguish information provision from strategic screening and segmentation.

**Welfare designs** must specify the benchmark choice. Common benchmarks include full-information choice, low-hassle choice, sophisticated-worker choice, a structural utility model, or a sufficient-statistics welfare formula.

Across all designs, name three margins separately: worker-level reduced-form effects, firm response margins, and market or equilibrium outcomes.

:::

## Bridge To Week 9 Policy

Week 9 should begin from the conclusion of this week: behavioral policy is not designed for isolated workers. It is designed inside markets where firms choose menus, set defaults, communicate attributes, target workers, and adjust to disclosure or nudges. A policy that corrects a worker mistake in partial equilibrium may have different welfare effects once employer response, market segmentation, and incidence are on the page.
