:::{table} Monopsony concepts and measurement objects
:label: tbl:monopsony-concepts-map

| Object | What it means | Typical empirical proxy or design | Key caution |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Classic monopsony | One dominant employer faces upward-sloping labor supply and chooses wage/employment jointly | Textbook benchmark; occasionally useful for intuition in thin local markets | Too stark for many modern labor markets |
| Dynamic/search monopsony | Firms have wage-setting power because workers move across jobs slowly and outside options are imperfect | Search models, quit elasticities, offer arrival rates, job ladders | Power can exist even with many firms |
| Labor supply to the firm | Elasticity of a firm's employment or applicant flow with respect to its own wage | Wage variation, online markets, applications data, dual-jobholder settings | Different margins can produce different elasticities |
| Markdown | Gap between marginal revenue product of labor and wage | Production-side estimation, matched employer--employee data | Requires stronger structure and harder-to-observe objects |
| Concentration | Market-share or HHI proxy for employer dominance | Commuting zone × occupation concentration, merger shocks | Not a structural measure of power on its own |
| Firm wage premium | Persistent firm-specific pay component after worker controls | AKM-style wage decompositions | Not all firm wage premia reflect monopsony |
| Rent-sharing / pass-through | Wage response to firm or outside-option shocks | Firm-shock designs, bargaining/pass-through designs | Can arise under multiple wage-setting models |
:::
