:::{table} Observed labor margins and what they reveal
:label: tbl:observed-margins-week2

| Observed margin | What it can reveal | What it can miss | Why it matters for interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Headcount / employment | Permanent staffing adjustment | Intensive-margin responses and hidden labor hoarding | A weak headcount response does not imply no labor-demand response |
| Hours worked | Short-run flexibility and utilization of existing workers | Longer-run workforce reorganization | Hours often move before employment |
| Hires / vacancies | Expansion frictions and recruiting constraints | Contraction-side rigidity | Useful for separating positive-shock adjustment from weak demand |
| Separations / layoffs | Contraction margins and firing costs | Expansion frictions | Especially informative for asymmetry and employment protection |
| Overtime / temporary labor | Buffer-stock use of flexible labor inputs | Permanent headcount strategy | Helps explain why firms delay changes in core employment |
| Wages / compensation | Possible rent sharing or internal adjustment | True quantity adjustment if wages are sticky | Dynamic incidence can be split between wages and employment |
| Capital or production | Complementary firm response outside labor | Pure labor-market mechanism | Ignoring other margins can bias labor-demand interpretation |

:::
