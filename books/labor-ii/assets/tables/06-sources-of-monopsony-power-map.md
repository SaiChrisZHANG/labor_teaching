:::{table} Sources of monopsony power
:label: tbl:sources-of-monopsony-power

| Source of power | Mechanism | Canonical implication | Example empirical setting |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Search and mobility frictions | Workers do not instantaneously find or move to best alternatives | Finite labor-supply elasticity to each firm | Job ladders, quit hazards, unemployment-to-job flows |
| Job differentiation and amenities | Jobs differ in schedule, safety, prestige, flexibility, location, and internal rules | Workers tolerate wage gaps to obtain valued nonwage attributes | Teachers, hospitals, remote work, public-sector jobs |
| Information frictions / wage opacity | Workers do not fully observe wages or alternatives | Outside options are weaker than observed nominal competition suggests | Online markets, salary-history settings, transparency reforms |
| Geographic and occupational immobility | Workers are locally or skill constrained | Thin markets amplify employer power | Rural labor markets, healthcare, specialized occupations |
| Concentration and employer size asymmetry | Large employers internalize less competitive pressure | Wages fall after consolidation or in highly concentrated markets | Hospital mergers, local occupation markets |
| Contractual or institutional barriers | Noncompetes, visa restrictions, licensing, pension lock-in | Switching costs rise and outside options weaken | Healthcare, guest workers, public pensions |
| Internal wage rules | Firms constrain wage competition through grades, schedules, or administrative pay | Market shocks pass through incompletely to wages | Large organizations, public-sector pay, internal labor markets |
:::
