:::{table} Empirical design map for Week 3
:label: tbl:design-map-week3

| Design family | Source of variation | Main observed margin | What it identifies best | Main limitation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Firm field experiments | Randomized bonuses, targets, teams, monitoring, or scheduling rules | Output, effort, attendance, sales, profits | Incentive and practice effects within a specific firm setting | External validity can be narrow |
| Internal policy changes / phased rollouts | New evaluation rules, promotion criteria, remote-work eligibility, or workflow tools | Productivity, retention, promotion, job quality | Treatment effects of organizational practices over time | Policy adoption may be endogenous |
| Personnel records with promotions or manager switches | Reassignment of workers or supervisors inside the firm | Promotion probabilities, subordinate output, retention | Manager effects, internal labor markets, assignment quality | Promotion and reassignment are often selective |
| Management surveys linked to firm outcomes | Cross-firm variation in measured practices | Productivity, growth, wages, survival | Correlates of management quality and possible causal practice shocks | Measurement error and policy endogeneity |
| Digital trace / time-use data | Meetings, messages, schedules, logins, workflow traces | Time allocation, collaboration, monitoring, task sequencing | How work is organized at high frequency | Data are rich but often setting-specific and proprietary |
| Public policy or threshold shocks affecting workplace practices | Worker voice mandates, pay transparency rules, labor standards | Productivity, wage dispersion, separations, communication | Causal effects of institutional changes on internal organization | Institutions may bundle multiple channels at once |
| Remote-work and technology rollouts | Eligibility changes or staggered deployment of new tools | Productivity, promotions, quits, collaboration | Selection versus treatment in new workplace designs | General equilibrium and long-run adaptation remain hard |

:::
