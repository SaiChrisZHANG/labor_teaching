:::{table} Adjustment-cost taxonomy for dynamic labor demand
:label: tbl:adjustment-taxonomy

| Cost structure / friction | Core intuition | Typical dynamic signature | Main labor margin implications | What researchers try to identify |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Convex adjustment costs | Small changes are cheap; large changes are increasingly costly | Smooth partial adjustment toward target employment | Frequent modest changes in hours or headcount | Speed of adjustment, short-run vs long-run elasticity |
| Fixed / nonconvex adjustment costs | Any change triggers a fixed reorganization or hiring/firing cost | Inaction punctuated by lumpy jumps | Spikes in hiring/firing, long spells of no change | Inaction bands, hazard of adjustment, lumpiness |
| Quasi-fixed labor | Some workers or employment relationships are costly to vary quickly | Persistence even when output fluctuates | Firms use hours, overtime, temps, or inventories first | Whether headcount is the true flexible margin |
| Hiring costs | Recruiting, screening, onboarding, and training are costly | Sluggish expansion after positive shocks | Vacancies rise before employment rises | Hiring frictions versus true demand weakness |
| Firing / separation costs | Layoffs are legally, financially, or organizationally costly | Slow contraction after negative shocks | Downward rigidity in headcount, more hours compression | Asymmetry between expansion and contraction |
| Uncertainty / option value of waiting | Firms delay irreversible changes when the environment is noisy | Short-run hesitation and delayed response | Vacancies or hours adjust before permanent staff | Distinguishing delay from low desired employment |

:::
