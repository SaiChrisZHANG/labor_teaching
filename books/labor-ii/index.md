# Labor II: Firms, Frictions, and Institutions

Labor II is the second semester of the core labor-economics field sequence. It studies labor markets once firms, contracting, search frictions, labor market power, institutions, and economy-wide shocks are brought to the center of the analysis.

Where Labor I asks how workers choose, invest, sort, and respond, Labor II asks how those outcomes are shaped by labor demand, internal firm organization, wage-setting, regulation, macro adjustment, technology, and trade. The course is designed as both a continuation of Labor I and a shift toward firm-side and market-wide reasoning.

## Course position in the sequence

Labor II builds directly on Labor I. The first semester supplies the worker-side foundations; the second studies firms, frictions, institutions, and shocks that determine how worker-side choices translate into equilibrium wages, employment, mobility, and welfare.

## Expected background

- Labor I or an equivalent worker-side labor-economics background
- Graduate microeconomic theory and applied econometrics
- Readiness to move between firm-level intuition, equilibrium reasoning, and empirical identification

## Learning goals

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

1. explain labor demand, internal firm organization, search frictions, wage-setting, and labor market power within one framework;
2. evaluate labor institutions and regulations from both firm and market perspectives;
3. analyze labor-market adjustment to aggregate, technological, and trade shocks across multiple margins;
4. distinguish partial-equilibrium intuition from equilibrium incidence and propagation;
5. frame research questions that connect workers, firms, institutions, and shocks.

## Course format

The course is organized as weekly modules. Each chapter keeps the same layered structure used in Labor I: Bridge for intuition, Field Core for models and empirical designs, and Research Lab for frontier questions and extensions.

The weekly teaching rhythm follows a 3-hour core structure: one block for the organizing model, one for evidence and identification, and one for discussion, policy application, or reproducibility-oriented extension. Optional extensions appear where the topic naturally widens into frontier debates or adjacent literatures.

## Weekly modules

- Week 1: [Labor demand and production](01-labor-demand-and-production.md). The semester opens from the firm's side with derived labor demand, production, and the classic own-wage response logic.
- Week 2: [Dynamic labor demand and adjustment costs](02-dynamic-labor-demand-and-adjustment-costs.md). The static target becomes a dynamic adjustment problem once hiring, firing, and timing frictions matter.
- Week 3: [Personnel economics and internal labor markets](03-personnel-economics-and-internal-labor-markets.md). The course then moves inside the firm to incentives, promotions, monitoring, teams, and internal allocation.
- Week 4: [Search, matching, turnover, and unemployment](04-search-matching-turnover-and-unemployment.md). Firm decisions are embedded in frictional labor markets with flows, vacancies, unemployment, and job ladders.
- Week 5: [Wage posting, bargaining, and wage-setting](05-wage-posting-bargaining-and-wage-setting.md). Search models become wage-setting models once surplus division and outside options enter.
- Week 6: [Monopsony, measurement, and sources of power](06-monopsony-measurement-and-sources-of-power.md). Labor market power is introduced through modern monopsony frameworks, measurement, and worker mobility frictions.
- Week 7: [Minimum wages and wage regulations](07-minimum-wages-and-wage-regulations.md). Wage floors are studied as policy interventions that reveal how competition, monopsony, and compliance interact.
- Week 8: [Unions, collective bargaining, and worker voice](08-unions-collective-bargaining-and-worker-voice.md). Collective institutions bring bargaining regimes, governance, and worker representation into the sequence.
- Week 9: [Labor regulation, enforcement, and insurance](09-labor-regulation-enforcement-and-insurance.md). The policy block widens to labor standards, enforcement capacity, social insurance, and spillovers.
- Week 10: [Aggregate fluctuations and labor market adjustment](10-aggregate-fluctuations-and-labor-market-adjustment.md). The course scales up to cyclical adjustment through stocks, flows, mobility, and wage dynamics.
- Week 11: [Technology, automation, AI, and labor market](11-technology-automation-ai-and-labor-market.md). Structural change is then studied through tasks, worker adaptation, firm redesign, and market-level incidence.
- Week 12: [Trade, offshoring, and labor market adjustment](12-trade-offshoring-and-labor-market-adjustment.md). External shocks from trade and global production are traced through workers, firms, sectors, and places.
- Week 13: [Synthesis and student research designs](13-synthesis-and-student-research-designs.md). The semester closes by integrating firms, frictions, institutions, and shocks into a unified research architecture.

## Sequence bridge

Labor II completes the move from worker-side foundations to equilibrium labor economics. It is meant to be read after Labor I, and it returns repeatedly to Labor I objects while showing how firms, market structure, and shocks reshape them.
