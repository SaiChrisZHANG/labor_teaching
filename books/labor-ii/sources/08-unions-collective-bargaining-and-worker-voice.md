# Week 8 source pack — Unions, Collective Bargaining, and Worker Voice

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Labor II Week 8. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

What do unions and collective bargaining actually change in labor markets, how should labor economists distinguish membership from coverage, and what does modern empirical work say about organizing, wage-setting, spillovers onto non-union workers, and the broader political role of unions?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor II
- Week: 8
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60-90 minute extension block because this is one of the core institution weeks of Labor II
- Position in sequence: follows Week 7 on minimum wages and wage regulations and precedes Week 9 on labor regulation, enforcement, and insurance
- Goal: give students a clean conceptual framework for collective bargaining while introducing the modern empirical literatures on union takeup, coverage, wage compression, spillovers, and the political-economy interface of unions

## Why this week comes eighth

Weeks 4-7 focused on search frictions, wage-setting, monopsony, and wage regulation. Those lectures ask how markets and rules shape pay. This week adds a different source of labor-market power and coordination: organized workers and bargaining institutions.

The key transition is:
- from **wage-setting by firms and markets** to **wage-setting with organized worker voice**;
- from **policy floors such as the minimum wage** to **institutional bargaining arrangements that can reshape entire wage structures**;
- from **individual employment contracts** to **collective determination of wages and nonwage terms**;
- from **direct treatment effects on covered workers** to **spillovers, threat effects, and political feedbacks**.

This week should make five distinctions explicit:

- **membership** is not the same thing as **coverage**;
- **firm-level unionization** is not the same thing as **sectoral or multi-employer bargaining**;
- **direct bargaining effects** are not the same thing as **spillovers onto non-union workers**;
- **organizing / takeup decisions** are not the same thing as **post-unionization outcomes**;
- **labor-market effects of unions** are not the same thing as the broader **political-economy role of unions**, even though the two are linked.

The chapter should also be explicit about what is not fully in scope yet:
- this is not primarily a labor-history lecture;
- this is not a general political-economy lecture, even though a brief political bridge is required;
- this is not the full labor-law and enforcement lecture that comes next, although bargaining institutions and legal coverage rules must appear here.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. explain the economic objects that unions and collective bargaining alter: wages, wage dispersion, amenities, job security, and workplace voice;
2. distinguish union membership, bargaining coverage, union density, and bargaining regimes;
3. explain why collective bargaining can compress wages and potentially affect uncovered workers;
4. describe the main empirical strategies used to study unionization and bargaining coverage;
5. explain how organizing / certification / takeup differs from estimating coverage or wage premia;
6. interpret modern evidence on direct effects, spillovers, and selection;
7. describe at a high level how unions connect labor-market outcomes to political and regulatory outcomes;
8. connect this lecture to earlier Labor II material on monopsony and minimum wages and to the later lectures on labor regulation and institutions.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should sound like an advanced PhD labor field-course lecture, not a nostalgic or normative essay about unions.
- It should remain labor-focused: bargaining, wage-setting, coverage, organizing, spillovers, and worker voice.
- It should be very explicit about the object of interest in each paper:
  - organizing success,
  - wage premia,
  - coverage effects,
  - employment/survival,
  - spillovers to the uncovered sector,
  - or political outcomes.
- It should tell students where the literature has become more persuasive:
  - causal identification around certification or legal changes,
  - matched employer-employee data,
  - harmonized international coverage data,
  - and distributional decompositions with spillover terms.
- It should not turn into a complete survey of every union effect. The lecture should stay structured around a few core labor questions.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening: why unions belong in the core of Labor II

This section must:
- explain why unions and collective bargaining are not "just institutions" added after the main model;
- connect the lecture explicitly to Weeks 5-7:
  - wage-setting,
  - monopsony,
  - minimum wages,
  - and the broader question of worker bargaining power;
- preview the chapter's organizing distinction between:
  - membership,
  - coverage,
  - bargaining regimes,
  - and spillovers;
- explain why the week includes a brief political-economy bridge but remains labor-centered.

The opening should preview:
- the collective-bargaining framework,
- the organizing/takeup section,
- the spillover section,
- the empirical-design map,
- and the political-economy bridge.

### 2. Conceptual framework: what unions and collective bargaining change

This is the first theoretical backbone.

Minimum content:
- the difference between unionization, collective bargaining, and worker voice;
- why coverage can matter even when membership is low;
- firm-level bargaining versus sectoral/extension bargaining regimes;
- direct wage effects, wage compression, rent-sharing, and nonwage terms;
- why unions can affect workplace governance, dispute resolution, and voice even when pure wage premia are modest.

This section should make clear that unions can matter through:
- wage levels,
- the wage structure,
- benefits and amenities,
- job protection and grievance rules,
- and the threat or possibility of organizing.

### 3. Organizing, takeup, certification, and who gets covered

This section should be much clearer than a generic "union decline" discussion.

Minimum content:
- the difference between worker demand for unions and observed unionization outcomes;
- why organizing/takeup is jointly shaped by worker demand, employer opposition, bargaining law, and labor-market conditions;
- certification elections and why they are central to the U.S. empirical literature;
- who unions target and when establishments are more likely to be organized;
- why the mapping from union desire to realized coverage is imperfect.

This section should use the takeup lens to organize the literature, not just list facts about declining density.

### 4. Direct labor-market effects: premia, compression, selection, and survival

This is the second backbone of the lecture.

Minimum content:
- union wage premia versus coverage premia;
- wage compression and within-firm or within-sector inequality;
- selection into union jobs or union firms;
- employment, payroll, and establishment survival effects;
- how causal papers can disagree because they estimate different objects or local treatment effects.

The chapter should be explicit about the difference between:
- average effects for workers who become newly unionized,
- equilibrium changes in the wage structure,
- and coverage effects in countries with broader bargaining institutions.

### 5. Spillovers and threat effects on non-union workers

This is a non-negotiable section.

Minimum content:
- spillovers within firms, within industries, and through outside options;
- threat effects when nonunion employers adjust to avoid organizing;
- the distinction between positive spillovers, negative crowd-out, and composition effects;
- why spillovers matter for interpreting the aggregate role of unions in inequality;
- why non-union workers in partially unionized firms are not necessarily clean controls.

This section should also explain why collective bargaining can reshape the wage structure beyond the workers formally covered by a contract.

### 6. Data and empirical designs: how we identify union and bargaining effects

Organize this section by design.

Minimum design buckets:
- certification-election regression discontinuity;
- matched employer-employee designs;
- long-run survey and historical decompositions;
- cross-country or cross-regime coverage comparisons;
- policy-shock/event-study designs such as right-to-work laws or bargaining-law reforms;
- descriptive and causal uses of Unionstats/CPS coverage data.

For each design, the chapter should say:
- what varies,
- what unit is observed,
- what object is identified,
- and what limitation remains.

### 7. Brief political-economy bridge

This section should be brief but explicit.

Minimum content:
- unions as labor-market institutions and political actors;
- voting, lobbying, party coalitions, and support for labor-market regulation as political channels;
- why this matters for labor economists even if the main estimands remain wages, employment, and bargaining coverage;
- a clear statement that the political-economy literature is an adjacent field rather than the main object of this lecture.

This section should point outward without letting the chapter drift away from labor economics.

### 8. Extension block: frontier directions and research opportunities

This extension block should work as:
- an extra session,
- an appendix-slide set,
- or a research-idea workshop.

The extension should cover at least four of the following:
- bargaining coverage in cross-country data and institutional heterogeneity;
- the interaction between unions and monopsony;
- within-firm spillovers and uncovered workers in union firms;
- new data on union campaigns, NLRB elections, and worker beliefs;
- union responses to automation, algorithmic management, and remote work;
- union takeup under tight labor markets;
- political spillovers and regulation;
- coverage extension regimes versus worker membership models.

The extension should explicitly identify:
- new data opportunities,
- quasi-experimental openings,
- unresolved measurement problems,
- and theory/empirics gaps.

## Required formal objects

The chapter should contain at least four formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: membership versus coverage accounting identity

A compact object such as

```tex
\text{Coverage}_{it} \geq \text{Membership}_{it}
```

or a decomposition of employment into covered/uncovered workers.

### Equation 2: simple bargaining / rent-sharing object

A compact bargaining expression such as

```tex
w = w_0 + \beta R
```

where the lecture interprets {math}`\beta` as bargaining power and {math}`R` as rent or surplus.

### Equation 3: direct and spillover decomposition

A schematic object such as

```tex
w^{N}_{rt} = \alpha + \gamma \, \text{UnionPresence}_{rt} + X_{rt}'\delta + \varepsilon_{rt}
```

used only to clarify what a spillover or threat-effect coefficient means.

### Equation 4: organizing/takeup object

A reduced-form probability or latent-index object such as

```tex
Pr(\text{Union Win}_{jt}=1) = F(Z_{jt}, M_{jt}, L_{jt})
```

with interpretation of establishment characteristics, employer opposition, and labor-market conditions.

## Research Lab architecture

The lab should follow Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer.

### Primary lab anchor

- [@farberHerbstKuziemkoNaidu2021]

Why:
- strong public-data orientation,
- clear union membership / coverage / inequality objects,
- feasible bounded path with public or synthetic CPS-style data,
- natural transfer exercises using sector, state, or demographic heterogeneity.

### Secondary / challenge anchor

- [@dinardoLee2004]

Why:
- canonical certification-election design,
- forces students to think about what close-election RD actually identifies,
- good for diagnosing the difference between organizing effects and broad equilibrium coverage effects.

### Optional extension anchor

- [@fortinLemieuxLloyd2021]

Why:
- sharp introduction to spillovers and threat effects,
- links this week to the broader distributional role of unions,
- gives a clean path to transfer the design to a synthetic or reduced-form setting.

### Bounded pedagogical path

The student path should run without confidential data.

Acceptable bounded paths include:
- public CPS / Unionstats style panels,
- a synthetic certification-election panel,
- a small synthetic state-year coverage/inequality panel,
- a reproduced descriptive figure plus one transfer exercise.

The handout must tell students exactly:
- what the outcome object is,
- what the treatment / organizing / coverage object is,
- what the main identification problem is,
- and how the transfer exercise modifies the original design.

## Bridge language

The chapter must bridge both directions.

### Backward bridge to Week 7

Explain that minimum wages impose a statutory wage floor, whereas unions and bargaining institutions can reshape wage-setting and employment conditions through negotiated worker power.

### Forward bridge to Week 9

Explain that once bargaining institutions are on the table, the next natural question is how labor law, enforcement, and insurance rules interact with bargaining power and coverage.

## Things to avoid

- Do not collapse union membership and coverage into one object.
- Do not present union effects as only wage premia.
- Do not ignore spillovers on non-union workers.
- Do not let the political-economy bridge take over the lecture.
- Do not turn the chapter into a pure U.S.-only lecture; at least some attention should be paid to coverage regimes outside the United States.
- Do not present every empirical paper as answering the same question.

## Definition of success

A strong Week 8 chapter will make students feel that unions are not an add-on topic but a core institution for understanding wage-setting, coverage, spillovers, and worker voice in modern labor markets.
