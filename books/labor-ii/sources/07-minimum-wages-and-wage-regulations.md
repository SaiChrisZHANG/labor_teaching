# Week 7 source pack — Minimum Wages and Wage Regulations

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Labor II Week 7. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

What do wage floors and related wage laws do in imperfect labor markets, what can we credibly say about employment effects, and how should labor economists organize the evidence across the United States, Europe, Latin America, and other institutional settings?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor II
- Week: 7
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 90 minute extension block because this is one of the policy centerpieces of Labor II
- Position in sequence: follows Week 6 on monopsony and precedes Week 8 on unions, collective bargaining, and worker voice
- Goal: give students a clean but expansive map of minimum-wage economics, the employment debate, global evidence, and the broader literature on wage law beyond minimum wages

## Why this week comes seventh

Week 6 established that firms may have wage-setting power. That changes how students should think about minimum wages:
- in a competitive benchmark, a binding wage floor reduces employment;
- in monopsonistic or frictional settings, a wage floor can raise wages with small employment losses and may even increase employment over a range;
- the key empirical question is therefore not simply "does the minimum wage ever reduce employment?" but:
  - for which workers,
  - in which market structures,
  - on which margins,
  - over which horizon,
  - and under which enforcement regime?

This week should make six transitions explicit:

- from **monopsony as a positive theory** to **minimum wages as a policy test of labor-market structure**;
- from **headcount employment** to **hours, hiring, separations, job creation, job destruction, and reallocation**;
- from **U.S. quasi-experimental case studies** to **global evidence and cross-country institutional variation**;
- from **minimum wages** to a broader class of **wage regulations and wage-law interventions**;
- from **partial-equilibrium treatment effects** to **compliance, enforcement, prices, profits, and job quality**;
- from **minimum wage policy** to later lectures on unions, labor regulation, and enforcement.

The chapter should also be explicit about what is *not* fully in scope yet:
- this is not a full public-finance treatment of redistribution;
- this is not the unions lecture;
- this is not the full enforcement/insurance lecture that comes later, although enforcement and compliance must appear here because they are essential to wage laws.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. explain why the minimum wage is a central empirical test of labor-market competition versus monopsony;
2. distinguish classic competitive predictions from monopsony or frictional predictions;
3. explain the core employment debate without caricaturing either side;
4. distinguish employment levels from hours, hiring, turnover, composition, and reallocation margins;
5. interpret the main modern empirical designs used in minimum-wage research;
6. summarize what the global evidence adds beyond the canonical U.S. debate;
7. explain why wage-law literatures include more than minimum wages, including transparency, equal-pay rules, and labor-standards enforcement;
8. connect this week to later Labor II material on unions, institutions, and regulation.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should sound like an advanced PhD labor field-course lecture, not a political debate brief.
- It should remain labor-focused: wage floors, hiring, separations, turnover, job quality, noncompliance, and firm adjustment.
- It should explicitly tell students whether a given result is about:
  - wages,
  - employment,
  - hours,
  - hiring,
  - separations,
  - firm composition,
  - prices,
  - or compliance.
- It should be clear about where the literature agrees, where it disagrees, and why design choices matter.
- It should not become a generic labor-law survey; wage law should be introduced as an empirical literature on labor-market rules that shape pay-setting and compliance.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening: why minimum wages are the policy sequel to monopsony

This section must:
- explain why the minimum wage is the most famous empirical test of competitive versus monopsonistic labor-market models;
- define the week's organizing question around employment, but also explain why employment is not the only relevant outcome;
- preview that the chapter will distinguish:
  - wage effects,
  - employment effects,
  - hours and turnover effects,
  - reallocation effects,
  - and compliance/enforcement effects;
- explain why this week broadens from minimum wages to wage law more generally.

The opening should preview:
- the long-running employment debate;
- the global evidence section;
- the broader wage-law section;
- and the bridge to unions and later labor regulation lectures.

### 2. Benchmarks: competitive, monopsony, and frictional labor markets under a wage floor

This is the first theoretical backbone.

Minimum content:
- competitive benchmark with a binding wage floor;
- monopsony benchmark where a wage floor can increase employment over a range;
- interpretation of minimum wages as a test of labor-market structure;
- role of search frictions and adjustment margins even when the formal model is not pure monopsony;
- distinction between a wage floor and other wage regulations.

This section should tell students clearly that the sign of the employment effect is ultimately empirical, but the mechanisms are not theory-free.

### 3. The employment debate: what exactly is being debated?

This section should be longer than usual and extremely clear.

Minimum content:
- what Card--Krueger changed;
- what the Neumark--Wascher critique emphasized;
- why local controls, case studies, border designs, synthetic controls, and modern event-study methods matter;
- why measured employment effects depend on:
  - the population studied,
  - the horizon,
  - the outcome variable,
  - and the definition of treatment intensity;
- why "no employment effect" and "employment loss on some margins" are not logically inconsistent.

This section should explicitly distinguish:
- headcount employment,
- hours worked,
- hiring,
- separations,
- and job-to-job or worker-firm reallocation.

### 4. The modern evidence map: wages, jobs, hours, composition, and reallocation

This is the second backbone of the lecture.

Organize the evidence by margin, not by chronology.

Minimum buckets:
- wage compression and spillovers;
- low-wage job counts and threshold-based approaches;
- hours and intensive-margin responses;
- hiring standards and recruiting margins;
- turnover, retention, and worker flows;
- firm exit, entry, and reallocation;
- prices, profits, and pass-through.

The chapter should make clear that employers may adjust on margins other than employment headcount.

### 5. Global evidence: what students should know beyond the U.S.

This section is non-negotiable.

Minimum content:
- a U.S. debate block;
- a Europe/Germany block emphasizing reallocation and prices/hours/employment composition;
- a Latin America or emerging-markets block emphasizing informality/compliance and distributional effects;
- a clear statement that minimum-wage institutions differ across countries:
  - national vs regional floors,
  - hourly vs monthly wage floors,
  - compliance and enforcement strength,
  - coverage in formal and informal sectors,
  - collective bargaining interactions.

This section should make students understand that "global evidence" is not just more estimates; it is evidence under different institutions and margins.

### 6. Wage law beyond minimum wages

This section should broaden the week without losing focus.

Minimum content:
- why labor economists should think in terms of wage law, not just one wage floor statute;
- transparency and salary disclosure laws;
- equal-pay / equal-pay-for-similar-work laws;
- labor-standards enforcement and wage-law compliance;
- underpayment, evasion, wage theft, and inspection-based compliance;
- if overtime or hours regulations are mentioned, keep them brief and labor-focused.

This section should not become a legal treatise. It should ask:
- what is the rule,
- what labor-market margin does it target,
- what data/designs identify its effect,
- and how it relates back to wage-setting and monopsony.

### 7. Data and empirical designs: how modern research identifies minimum-wage and wage-law effects

Organize this section by design.

Minimum design buckets:
- contiguous-border or local comparison designs;
- stacked event studies / dynamic difference-in-differences;
- threshold-based or job-distribution approaches;
- synthetic-control or city-specific reforms;
- national reform settings with exposure-based identification;
- inspection/enforcement quasi-experiments;
- within-firm disclosure or transparency reforms.

For each design, the chapter should say:
- what varies,
- what unit is observed,
- what margin is measured,
- and what key limitation remains.

### 8. Frontier extension block: unresolved questions and research opportunities

This extension block should be substantial. It should work as:
- an extra 90 minute session,
- an appendix-slide set,
- or a research-idea workshop.

The extension should cover at least four of the following:
- own-wage elasticities and the interpretation of minimum-wage evidence;
- interaction between minimum wages and monopsony measures;
- spillovers higher up the wage distribution;
- dynamic hiring standards and screening margins;
- automation, task substitution, or quality upgrading;
- informal-sector adjustments and noncompliance;
- local versus national policy comparisons;
- links between wage-law enforcement and worker sorting;
- pay transparency and equal-pay rules as complements or substitutes to wage floors.

The extension should explicitly identify:
- new data opportunities,
- quasi-experimental openings,
- unresolved measurement problems,
- and theory/empirics gaps.

## Required formal objects

The chapter should contain at least five formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: competitive benchmark under a wage floor

Use a compact object such as

```tex
N_t = D(w_t), \qquad w_t = \max\{w_t^*, \bar w_t\}
```

Interpretation:
- in a competitive benchmark, a binding floor {math}`\bar w_t` raises wages and moves the firm along labor demand.

### Equation 2: monopsony benchmark under a wage floor

Use a compact object such as

```tex
N(\bar w) = S_f(\bar w), \qquad \text{for } w^m < \bar w < w^c
```

Interpretation:
- if the floor lies between the monopsony wage and the competitive wage, employment may rise.

### Equation 3: Kaitz / bite measure

Use a compact object such as

```tex
K_{rt} = \frac{\bar w_{rt}}{\operatorname{median}(w_{rt})}
```

Interpretation:
- minimum-wage effects depend on the policy bite, not just the nominal change.

### Equation 4: dynamic treatment / event-study object

Use a reduced-form object such as

```tex
Y_{rt} = \sum_{\ell \neq -1} \beta_\ell \mathbf{1}\{t-T_r=\ell\} + \alpha_r + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{rt}
```

Interpretation:
- much of the modern literature asks whether pre-trends are absent and which post-treatment horizons matter.

### Equation 5: compliance / effective wage-law object

Use a compact object such as

```tex
w_{it}^{obs} = (1-\theta_{it}) w_{it}^{rule} + \theta_{it} w_{it}^{noncomp}
```

Interpretation:
- legal rules need not translate one-for-one into observed wages because compliance may be incomplete.

## Research Lab requirements

The lab should be organized around **Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer**.

### Primary anchor

- [@cengizDubeLindnerZipperer2019]

Why:
- clean modern design around low-wage jobs below and around the new minimum;
- good for teaching threshold-based outcome construction and margin decomposition;
- naturally connects the wage effects/employment effects debate to job counts and composition.

### Secondary / challenge anchor

- [@dubeLesterReich2010]

Why:
- canonical border design;
- directly useful for teaching credible local controls and design controversies;
- good for a diagnose section about what different treatment-control comparisons do and do not identify.

### Optional extension anchor

- [@engbomMoser2022]

Why:
- brings in global evidence, equilibrium reallocation, inequality, and firm-side adjustment;
- useful for showing that employment effects can be muted while distributional and reallocation effects are large.

### Bounded pedagogical path

The bounded local path should:
- run without confidential microdata;
- use a small synthetic border-county panel or grouped low-wage-job panel;
- let students recreate one wage effect and one employment/composition effect;
- let students transfer the estimator/design to a public or synthetic policy-change panel.

The lab handout should be explicit about:
1. what the paper's employment object is;
2. whether the identified effect is on headcount, hours, jobs below a threshold, or some other margin;
3. what treatment intensity / bite measure is used;
4. how the transfer exercise changes the setting without changing the core design.
