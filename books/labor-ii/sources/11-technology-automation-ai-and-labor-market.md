# Week 11 source pack — Technology, Automation, AI, and Labor Market

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Labor II Week 11. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How should labor economists think about technology, automation, and AI as shocks that reshape worker decisions, firm labor demand, and labor-market equilibrium at the same time?

This lecture should not treat technology as a pure labor-demand shock. It should instead organize the literature around three linked objects:

1. **Supply-side adjustment**
   - skill acquisition,
   - learning,
   - retraining,
   - worker sorting,
   - application/search technology,
   - and skill obsolescence.

2. **Demand-side adjustment**
   - task substitution,
   - task complementarity,
   - automation,
   - augmentation,
   - organizational redesign,
   - hiring changes,
   - and within-firm labor demand.

3. **Market-level adjustment**
   - local labor-market reallocation,
   - industry and occupation restructuring,
   - wage and employment incidence,
   - market concentration or winner-take-most dynamics,
   - productivity and labor share changes,
   - and global adjustment patterns.

The goal is to teach students a coherent labor-economics framework for studying technological change rather than a loose collection of robotics, computerization, and AI papers.

## Week identity

- Course: Labor II
- Week: 11
- Title: Technology, Automation, AI, and Labor Market
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 90 minute extension block because this is one of the major shock-and-adjustment lectures in Labor II
- Position in sequence: follows Week 10 on aggregate fluctuations and labor-market adjustment and precedes Week 12 on trade, offshoring, and labor-market adjustment
- Goal: introduce students to the modern task-based technology framework, connect it to worker-side adaptation and market equilibrium, and then organize historical, contemporary, and global evidence on technology and labor-market change

## Why this week comes eleventh

Weeks 1–10 built the core logic of Labor II:
- static and dynamic labor demand,
- within-firm organization and personnel economics,
- search, unemployment, and wage-setting,
- regulation and institutions,
- and aggregate fluctuations.

Week 11 applies that machinery to one of the most important and fast-moving classes of labor-market shocks: technological change.

The lecture should make clear that technology is not an add-on topic. It is a setting in which the mechanisms from all prior weeks interact:
- labor demand moves because tasks and production methods change;
- workers adjust because skills depreciate, new skills are rewarded, and search/application technologies shift;
- markets reallocate because firms adopt unevenly, regions and occupations are exposed differently, and equilibrium spillovers matter.

This is the first major “market-level shock” lecture in the sequence, so it should also foreshadow Week 12 on trade/offshoring:
- Week 11 studies how new technologies and AI reallocate work and wages.
- Week 12 studies how global integration reallocates work and wages.
- Together, they form the course’s broader adjustment block.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. distinguish automation, augmentation, new-task creation, and organizational redesign as different labor-market channels of technological change;
2. explain why technology affects labor supply, labor demand, and market equilibrium simultaneously;
3. use a task-based production framework to organize the literature;
4. explain the difference between direct task displacement and indirect equilibrium/productivity effects;
5. connect technology to worker-side adaptation via training, learning, skill obsolescence, and occupational switching;
6. compare historical, robot-era, and AI-era evidence without collapsing them into one story;
7. understand the main empirical designs used in this literature, including shift-share exposure, matched worker-firm data, field experiments, vacancies/online-job-posting designs, and cross-country industry panels;
8. explain what the literature says about employment, wages, job composition, labor share, inequality, and reallocation;
9. identify frontier research opportunities using new data, quasi-experiments, and policy variation.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should sound like an advanced PhD labor field lecture, not a technology-studies survey and not a general AI essay.
- The lecture should remain disciplined and structured:
  - framework first,
  - then historical context,
  - then evidence by margin,
  - then methods,
  - then frontier directions.
- It should avoid becoming a bag of examples.
- It should repeatedly distinguish:
  - **tasks** from **occupations**,
  - **exposure** from **realized effects**,
  - **firm adoption** from **worker impact**,
  - **partial-equilibrium within-firm evidence** from **market-level equilibrium evidence**,
  - and **productivity gains** from **distributional incidence**.

## Lab design spine

The lab should follow the standard Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer structure.

### Primary anchor
- [@aghionEtAl2025HowDifferentUsesAI]

Why:
- recent and directly about AI adoption and labor demand;
- has an official AEA page with a replication package;
- works well for teaching students how different technologies or uses of the same technology can have heterogeneous labor-demand effects;
- connects directly to firm-level adoption data and modern AI measurement.

### Secondary / challenge anchor
- [@acemogluRestrepo2020RobotsJobs]

Why:
- canonical causal local-labor-market design;
- sharpest benchmark for displacement, wages, and equilibrium spillovers;
- useful contrast with within-firm AI evidence.

### Optional extension anchor
- [@brynjolfssonLiRaymond2025GenerativeAIWork]

Why:
- worker-side adaptation and learning complement the demand-side focus;
- useful for teaching students the difference between productivity effects inside a firm and broader labor-market equilibrium effects;
- data availability is explicit on the journal page.

### Transfer ideas

Bounded transfer ideas could include:
- computing a simple occupation-level technology exposure measure and relating it to wage or employment trends in public aggregate data;
- building a small synthetic panel of local markets with robot or AI exposure and estimating descriptive differences in employment and wages;
- comparing exposure-based designs with realized adoption designs;
- using public job-posting or occupation-description data to classify changes in skill demand after a technology shock;
- asking students to reframe the same technology through supply-side, demand-side, and market-level lenses.

The lab handout should force students to name:
1. the technology or exposure object,
2. whether the design identifies supply-side, demand-side, or market-level effects,
3. the unit of observation,
4. the observed margin,
5. the key equilibrium margin that remains partially offstage.

## Chapter architecture

This should be one of the longer and more research-oriented Labor II weeks.

### 1. Bridge and reorientation

The Bridge section should connect Week 11 to Week 10.

Minimum content:
- Week 10 asked how cyclical shocks change unemployment, flows, match quality, and wages.
- Week 11 asks how technological shocks change tasks, skills, firm behavior, and labor-market equilibrium.
- Week 11 should make clear that the same labor-market objects still matter:
  - employment and unemployment,
  - hiring and separations,
  - wage setting,
  - job ladders,
  - and reallocation.
- The difference is that the shock now comes from technology adoption, automation, or AI rather than business-cycle conditions.

The opening question should be explicit:
**How do technology, automation, and AI reshape work through worker adaptation, firm labor demand, and market-level reallocation?**

### 2. General framework: technology as a labor-market shock

This is the lecture’s conceptual core.

The week should classify technologies into:
- information and communication technologies,
- robots and physical automation,
- software and data systems,
- AI prediction/classification tools,
- generative AI and decision-support systems.

Then classify effects into:
- **displacement / substitution**
- **augmentation / complementarity**
- **productivity scaling**
- **new-task creation**
- **organizational redesign**
- **search/application or matching technology**
- **skill obsolescence and retraining**

The chapter should emphasize that the same technology can generate more than one channel at once.

### 3. Formal core

The chapter should contain real equations, not just verbal claims.

At minimum it should include:
1. one task-based aggregate production object;
2. one task-allocation or automation-boundary object;
3. one decomposition that distinguishes displacement from productivity/reinstatement/new-task effects;
4. one worker-skill or human-capital adjustment object;
5. one reduced-form local-equilibrium or exposure equation used in the empirical literature.

A good benchmark set would be something like:

```text
Y = \left[\int_0^1 y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}} di\right]^{\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}}
```

with tasks assigned to labor or capital/AI depending on relative productivity, plus a decomposition such as:

```text
\Delta \log L = \text{displacement} + \text{productivity/output expansion} + \text{new tasks/reinstatement}
```

and a worker-adjustment object such as:

```text
H_{t+1} = (1-\delta_t)H_t + I_t
```

where technological change increases the obsolescence component {math}`\delta_t`.

There should also be one empirical reduced-form object such as:

```text
\Delta Y_{ct} = \beta \, TechExposure_{ct} + X_{ct}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{ct}
```

or an occupation/firm exposure equation.

The chapter should explain what each object captures and what it leaves out.

### 4. Historical and long-run perspective

This section should keep the lecture from sounding like a “ChatGPT-only” week.

Minimum content:
- long-run automation anxieties and why labor demand has not collapsed;
- task creation and labor complementarities;
- skill-biased and routine-biased technological change;
- ICT, polarization, and occupational restructuring.

This is where [@autor2015WhyStillManyJobs], [@acemogluRestrepo2019AutomationNewTasks], [@acemogluAutor2011SkillsTasksTechnologies], and [@michaelsNatrajVanReenen2014ICTPolarizedSkillDemand] should appear.

### 5. Demand-side evidence: technology inside firms and across local labor markets

This section should study how technology changes hiring, employment, wage bills, occupational mix, and firm growth.

Minimum content:
- robots and direct displacement;
- firm-level AI adoption and hiring changes;
- local or regional exposure designs;
- distinction between within-firm effects and equilibrium effects.

This is where [@acemogluRestrepo2020RobotsJobs], [@graetzMichaels2018RobotsWork], [@dauthFindeisenSuedekumWoessner2021AdjustmentRobots], [@acemogluAutorHazellRestrepo2020AIJobsVacancies], and [@aghionEtAl2025HowDifferentUsesAI] should be central.

### 6. Supply-side evidence: worker learning, training, and skill obsolescence

This should be its own major block, not a side note.

Minimum content:
- how workers adjust when the content of jobs changes;
- training, learning, and within-job adaptation;
- obsolescence of prior skills;
- sorting out of faster-changing occupations;
- how AI tools alter learning curves, supervision, and worker progression.

This is where [@bartelSicherman1998TechnologicalChangeSkillAcquisition], [@demingNoray2020EarningsDynamicsChangingJobSkills], and [@brynjolfssonLiRaymond2025GenerativeAIWork] should be central.

The lecture should explicitly note that supply-side adjustment is not just schooling. It includes:
- retraining,
- on-the-job learning,
- occupational switching,
- and search/application adaptation.

If the current literature on technology and application processes is thinner than the literature on demand and training, say so clearly and treat it as a frontier area rather than overselling it.

### 7. Market-level equilibrium and distribution

This section should tie supply and demand together.

Minimum content:
- local labor-market incidence;
- service-sector offsets versus manufacturing losses;
- occupational polarization or upgrading;
- employment and wage effects across skill groups;
- labor share, rents, and inequality implications;
- how technology affects not just average outcomes, but their distribution across workers, firms, and places.

This is where the lecture should most clearly connect to labor-market equilibrium.

### 8. AI-specific measurement and new data

This should be one of the most distinctive parts of the chapter.

Minimum content:
- occupation-level exposure measures;
- firm-level adoption data;
- online vacancies and job postings;
- text/LLM-based measures of task exposure;
- matched worker-firm data and digital trace data;
- the difference between “AI exposure” and actual adoption or realized effects.

This is where [@feltenRajSeamans2018MethodAIAbilities], [@labaschinEtAl2025ExtendingGPTsFirms], and [@acemogluAutorHazellRestrepo2020AIJobsVacancies] should be highlighted.

### 9. Global and comparative evidence

The lecture should include explicit non-U.S. evidence.

Minimum content:
- cross-country robot evidence;
- German worker and local labor-market adjustment;
- France firm-level AI adoption;
- at least one explicit discussion of why equilibrium responses and institutions differ across settings.

This should keep the lecture from reading as purely U.S.-centric.

### 10. Research frontier and extension block

This should be longer than usual.

It should explicitly introduce students to frontier directions such as:
- AI exposure vs adoption measurement;
- worker-side use of AI in job search and application processes;
- technology and wage-setting or rent sharing;
- organizational complements and the productivity J-curve;
- historical patent/text data and long-run labor-demand shifts;
- technology, gender, inequality, and market concentration;
- policy design around training, diffusion, and worker transition support.

The extension block should help students see where open questions live.

## Important empirical distinctions to force throughout the chapter

The chapter must repeatedly distinguish:

- **automation** vs **augmentation**
- **exposure** vs **adoption**
- **firm-level** vs **market-level** effects
- **worker productivity** vs **worker welfare**
- **occupation/task content** vs **employment counts**
- **short-run disruption** vs **long-run adjustment**
- **historical ICT/robot evidence** vs **very recent AI evidence**
- **measured technology use** vs **predicted technology exposure**

## What this week should not become

- Not a generic AI lecture.
- Not a machine-learning-for-economists lecture.
- Not a pure inequality lecture.
- Not a pure labor-demand lecture.
- Not a speculative future-of-work essay with no labor-economics structure.

The lecture should always come back to the same labor-market objects:
- tasks,
- skills,
- labor demand,
- worker adaptation,
- reallocation,
- wages,
- employment,
- and equilibrium incidence.

## Bridge to Week 12

The final section should say clearly:

- Week 11 studied technology as a shock generated inside production and organizational change.
- Week 12 studies trade and offshoring as a shock generated through global goods and production integration.
- Both lectures are about labor-market adjustment, but the mapping from exposure to incidence differs.

The bridge should make Week 12 feel like the natural next step rather than a separate topic.
