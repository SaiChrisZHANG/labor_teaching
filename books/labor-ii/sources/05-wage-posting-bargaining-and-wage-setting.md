# Week 5 source pack — Wage Posting, Bargaining, and Wage-Setting

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Labor II Week 5. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

Once workers and firms meet in a frictional labor market, how are wages actually set—and how can labor economists distinguish posted wages, bargaining, standardized wage rules, discretionary pay, and rent-sharing in the data?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor II
- Week: 5
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 75--90 minute extension block because this is one of the conceptual centerpieces of Labor II
- Position in sequence: follows Week 4 on search, matching, turnover, and unemployment; precedes Week 6 on monopsony and labor market power
- Goal: give students a clean but expansive map of wage-setting protocols in labor economics, connect theory to the main empirical objects and identification strategies, and show how wage-setting sits between search frictions and later market-power analysis

## Why this week comes fifth

Week 4 introduced the frictional labor market:
- workers search imperfectly,
- firms post vacancies imperfectly,
- matches arrive stochastically,
- separations occur,
- unemployment and job-to-job mobility are flow outcomes.

But search theory is incomplete without a wage-setting protocol. Once a worker and a firm meet, the model must say:
- is the wage posted in advance?
- is the wage bargained bilaterally?
- is it pinned down by a standardized schedule?
- does it depend on outside offers or only on nonemployment?
- is it constrained by internal equity, contracts, or collective rules?
- how much of a firm shock passes through to workers?

This week should make five transitions explicit:

- from **meeting frictions** to **surplus division**;
- from **hazards and flows** to **wages, rents, and pass-through**;
- from **search as a mobility theory** to **search as a wage-dispersion theory**;
- from **frictional models of unemployment** to **frictional models of wage determination**;
- from **wage-setting protocols** to the later Labor II themes of monopsony, institutions, and policy.

The chapter should also be explicit about what is *not* fully in scope yet:
- a full monopsony lecture belongs to Week 6;
- the policy/institutions block comes later;
- dynamic adjustment and macro wage rigidity are only foreshadowed here.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. explain why frictional labor markets require an explicit wage-setting protocol;
2. distinguish posted wages, individualized bargaining, standardized pay schedules, and collective or administrative wage-setting;
3. explain how wage posting can generate wage dispersion and job ladders even among similar workers;
4. explain how bargaining objects differ from outside-option objects and why Hall--Milgrom changed the benchmark interpretation;
5. connect the value of nonemployment to wage setting without conflating it with realized outside offers;
6. explain what empirical evidence on pass-through, rent-sharing, and firm wage premia does—and does not—identify;
7. understand how this week bridges search theory to later discussions of monopsony and labor market power.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should sound like an advanced PhD labor field-course lecture, not a generic search macro note or an industrial-relations survey.
- It should remain labor-focused: wages as the outcome of worker mobility, firm wage rules, outside options, and internal pay-setting.
- It should be theoretically clean but tied closely to empirical observables.
- It should explicitly tell students whether a given object is:
  - a search-theoretic object,
  - a bargaining object,
  - a wage-rule object,
  - or a reduced-form empirical object.
- It should make clear that wage-setting is not one thing. Different sectors, firms, and institutional settings may use different protocols.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening: from search to wage-setting

This section must:
- explain why a search model is incomplete until it specifies how wages are set;
- define the main wage-setting protocols covered this week:
  - posted wages,
  - individualized bargaining,
  - standardized or administrative pay schedules,
  - collective or sectoral pay-setting as a boundary case;
- preview why wage-setting matters for wage dispersion, outside options, rent-sharing, and labor-market power.

The opening should preview:
- Week 6 monopsony and market power;
- later policy/institution lectures;
- the idea that wage-setting is the bridge between worker flows and wage/employment incidence.

### 2. Benchmarks and wage-setting regimes

This section should set up the conceptual map.

Minimum content:
- contrast a competitive benchmark with frictional wage setting;
- explain what is exogenous and endogenous under each wage-setting regime;
- distinguish a worker's reservation value, outside offer, value of nonemployment, and bargaining threat point;
- explain why wage-setting can be decentralized, standardized, or institutionally constrained.

This is the right place to tell students that "wage-setting" means a protocol for converting match surplus into pay.

### 3. Wage posting, directed/undirected search, and wage dispersion

This is the first theoretical backbone.

Minimum content:
- explain why wage posting is natural in frictional models;
- discuss the Burdett--Mortensen logic linking search frictions to wage dispersion;
- explain the difference between a posted wage and a bargained wage;
- connect posted wages to job ladders, worker mobility, and wage distributions;
- preview how empirical tests can distinguish posting from bargaining.

This section should make clear that the wage distribution is not automatically a productivity distribution.

### 4. Bargaining, outside options, and the Hall--Milgrom critique

This is the second theoretical backbone.

Minimum content:
- introduce a bilateral bargaining problem;
- explain the difference between threat points based on exit and threat points based on delay;
- discuss why Hall--Milgrom implies a weaker link between unemployment and bargained wages than the standard outside-option view;
- show that bargaining and posting can have different comparative statics and different empirical signatures.

This section should not become a full macro wage-rigidity lecture, but it should make students understand why bargaining protocol assumptions matter.

### 5. Wages and the value of nonemployment

This section should connect theory to a key modern empirical literature.

Minimum content:
- define the value of nonemployment clearly;
- explain why it matters for both bargaining and posting models;
- distinguish direct wage responses to outside-option shocks from the more primitive value-of-nonemployment object;
- discuss the measurement and interpretation issues in empirical work on wage pass-through from nonemployment value.

This section should emphasize that the value of nonemployment is conceptually central but empirically hard to observe directly.

### 6. Standardized versus discretionary pay-setting inside firms

This section should connect wage-setting theory to the interior of the firm.

Minimum content:
- explain why some firms use schedules, grades, or administrative rules while others rely on discretion or bargaining;
- discuss why standardized pay can compress wages and limit within-job dispersion;
- discuss why discretionary pay can amplify heterogeneity, bargaining, and inequality;
- connect this to personnel economics and to later discussions of labor-market power;
- mention collective bargaining or sectoral coordination only as needed to clarify the spectrum of wage-setting rules.

This section should make clear that the firm's wage rule is a substantive empirical object, not just a nuisance detail.

### 7. Data and empirical designs: how modern research identifies wage-setting

Organize this section by empirical design, not by chronology.

Minimum design buckets:
- revealed-preference tests using dual jobholders or outside-offer variation;
- policy or institutional reforms that shift wage-setting rules;
- quasi-experimental changes in the value of nonemployment or outside options;
- matched employer-employee data for firm wage premia and pass-through;
- establishment or occupation microdata on standardized versus discretionary pay rules;
- survey evidence on bargaining protocols, outside-option beliefs, or attachment.

For each design, the chapter should say:
- what varies,
- what unit is observed,
- what wage-setting object is identified,
- and what major unobserved object remains.

### 8. Frontier extension block: open directions after the core wage-setting week

This extension block should be substantial. It should work as:
- an extra 75--90 minute session,
- an appendix-slide set,
- or a research-idea workshop.

The extension should cover at least four of the following:
- worker beliefs about outside options and firm-specific pay;
- bargaining and within-firm inequality;
- discretionary pay-setting and organizational change;
- wage-setting under remote or platform-mediated hiring;
- pay transparency, salary-history bans, and offer disclosure;
- standardized national or multi-establishment wage-setting;
- algorithmic pay-setting and offer management;
- how wage-setting protocols interact with later monopsony measures.

The extension should explicitly identify:
- new data opportunities,
- quasi-experimental openings,
- unresolved measurement problems,
- and open theory/empirics gaps.

## Required formal objects

The chapter should contain at least five formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: reservation / acceptance object

Use a compact object such as

```tex
w \geq w^R \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad V^E(w) \geq V^U
```

Interpretation:
- a worker accepts if the value of employment at wage {math}`w` exceeds the value of nonemployment.

### Equation 2: posted-wage firm problem

Use a stylized object such as

```tex
\max_w \; \pi(w) = (p - w)\,\ell(w)
```

Interpretation:
- under wage posting, the firm chooses a wage recognizing how labor supply or recruiting responds.

### Equation 3: Nash bargaining object

Use a compact object such as

```tex
w^* = \arg\max_w \left[V^E(w)-V^U\right]^\beta \left[J(w)-V\right]^{1-\beta}
```

Interpretation:
- wages solve a surplus-splitting problem rather than a one-sided posting problem.

### Equation 4: value-of-nonemployment pass-through object

Use a reduced-form object such as

```tex
\Delta \log w_{it} = \rho \, \Delta \log V^U_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}
```

Interpretation:
- empirical work often asks how much an exogenous shift in the value of nonemployment passes through to wages.

### Equation 5: rent-sharing / pass-through object

Use a compact reduced-form object such as

```tex
\Delta \log w_{ijt} = \theta \, \Delta \log \text{VA}_{jt} + \alpha_i + \psi_j + \varepsilon_{ijt}
```

Interpretation:
- wage-setting can be studied through the pass-through of firm shocks to worker pay.

### Lab design requirements
The lab handout must:
1. clearly state what wage-setting protocol is under test;
2. identify the observable proxy for outside options, bargaining, or pay rules;
3. make the identification challenge explicit;
4. explain what students can reproduce exactly and what must be approximated in the bounded pedagogical path;
5. include one transfer exercise using either:
   - a public salary-schedule setting,
   - a small synthetic worker-offer-separation panel,
   - or a public wage-cell panel illustrating standardized versus discretionary pay compression.

## Bridge to Week 6

This chapter must end by telling students:

- Week 5 asks **how wages are set once workers and firms meet**.
- Week 6 asks **how much wage-setting power firms have over workers once labor supply to the firm is elastic but not infinite**.

So the bridge should emphasize:
- wage posting and bargaining are protocols;
- monopsony is about the elasticity and conduct environment in which those protocols operate;
- the two weeks are complements, not substitutes.

## Things the chapter must not do

- Do not turn into a general labor-market institutions lecture.
- Do not absorb the whole monopsony literature.
- Do not reduce all wage setting to one formula.
- Do not present "outside options" as a single observed variable without discussing what is latent.
- Do not present firm wage premia or rent-sharing estimates as if they directly reveal the full structural protocol.
- Do not bury the distinction between search frictions and wage-setting rules.
