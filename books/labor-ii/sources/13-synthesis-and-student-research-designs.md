# Week 13 source pack — Synthesis and Student Research Designs

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Labor II Week 13. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How do firm behavior, market frictions, institutions, and major shocks fit into one labor-market framework, and how can students turn that framework into a serious research idea?

This week should not feel like a loose recap. It should function as a capstone that helps students:
1. see the architecture of Labor II as a coherent field,
2. understand how Labor I and Labor II fit together,
3. learn how to choose among descriptive, reduced-form, and structural approaches,
4. identify where open questions still live.

## Week identity

- Course: Labor II
- Week: 13
- Title: Synthesis and Student Research Designs
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60–90 minute workshop extension
- Position in sequence: final week of the Labor II field course
- Goal: synthesize the semester, convert the field sequence into a research agenda, and bridge to future labor-field and dissertation work

## Why this week is last

The point of Week 13 is not just to summarize Weeks 1–12. It is to reorganize the semester into a research framework.

Labor II has moved through four layers:
1. **firm-side partial equilibrium**
   - labor demand,
   - adjustment costs,
   - personnel economics;
2. **market interaction under frictions**
   - search,
   - unemployment,
   - wage-setting,
   - monopsony;
3. **institutions and regulation**
   - minimum wages,
   - wage law,
   - unions and collective bargaining,
   - broader labor regulation;
4. **major market-level shocks**
   - macro fluctuations,
   - technology / automation / AI,
   - trade / offshoring.

This final week should make clear that these are not separate silos. They are interacting components of modern labor-market analysis.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. place any labor-market question inside a coherent map of workers, firms, frictions, institutions, and shocks;
2. explain how Labor I worker-side objects and Labor II market-side objects complement one another;
3. choose the right unit of analysis for a research question;
4. distinguish when a descriptive, reduced-form, or structural approach is appropriate;
5. articulate the difference between outcome effects and welfare conclusions;
6. identify major open questions at the frontier of labor economics;
7. produce a research-design memo that could plausibly seed a field-paper or dissertation idea.

## Tone and authorial voice

- The chapter should read like a capstone labor-field lecture, not an inspirational essay.
- It should stay concrete and method-aware.
- It should keep asking:
  - what is the mechanism?
  - what is the unit of treatment?
  - what margin is observed?
  - what equilibrium channels are offstage?
  - what counterfactual claim is justified?
- It should repeatedly emphasize that labor economics is about linking worker decisions, firm behavior, institutions, and market adjustment rather than studying any one object in isolation.

### What students should learn
- how to diagnose the central mechanism in a paper,
- how to identify the unit of treatment and observation,
- how to distinguish the observed margin from the latent equilibrium object,
- how to see when a result is local / partial-equilibrium versus broader / equilibrium,
- how to generate a bounded but nontrivial extension idea.

### Capstone deliverable
The default deliverable should be a short research-design memo with the following sections:
1. question,
2. mechanism,
3. treatment / exposure object,
4. unit of observation,
5. observed outcome margin,
6. identifying variation,
7. equilibrium concern,
8. proposed extension,
9. why the extension matters.

The bounded path can be memo-first rather than estimation-heavy, but it must still create a concrete artifact that can be smoke-tested locally.

## Chapter architecture

This should be a capstone week with a strong organizing structure.

### 1. Bridge and reorientation

The Bridge section should connect back to Labor I and explain what Labor II added.

Minimum content:
- Labor I centered the worker: labor supply, human capital, households, inequality, discrimination, mobility, policy targeting workers, and behavioral frictions.
- Labor II embedded those worker-side objects into firms, markets, institutions, and major shocks.
- Taken together, the two-course sequence gives students a general labor-economics map.

The opening question should be explicit:
- When a labor-market outcome changes, is the key object worker choice, firm behavior, market friction, institution, or shock?
- In practice, the answer is usually “some combination of all five.”

### 2. Non-negotiable formal objects

The final chapter must contain all of the following formal objects.

#### A. Integrative labor-market architecture object

Use an equation like:

```{math}
:label: eq:labor2_architecture
Y_{u,t+h} = \mathcal{G}\left(X^{\text{worker}}_{u,t},\; X^{\text{firm}}_{u,t},\; \mathcal{F}_{m,t},\; \mathcal{I}_{m,t},\; \mathcal{S}_{m,t:h}\right)
```

where:
- {math}`u` can be a worker, firm, establishment, region, or market depending on the question,
- {math}`X^{\text{worker}}` captures worker heterogeneity and skills,
- {math}`X^{\text{firm}}` captures technology, productivity, and organization,
- {math}`\mathcal{F}` are frictions,
- {math}`\mathcal{I}` are institutions and regulations,
- {math}`\mathcal{S}` are shocks.

The point is not that this equation is a structural model. The point is to give students one compact architecture for organizing Labor II.

#### B. Generic empirical treatment object

Include a general empirical object such as:

```{math}
:label: eq:labor2_empirical
\Delta y_{u,t+h} = \beta D_{u,t} + X_{u,t}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{u,t+h}
```

and explain that the choice of {math}`u`, {math}`D`, and {math}`y` determines what the design can actually say.

This section must explicitly compare cases where:
- {math}`u` is a worker,
- {math}`u` is a firm,
- {math}`u` is a local labor market,
- {math}`u` is a market / policy regime.

#### C. Welfare / equilibrium caution object

Include a short cautionary object such as:

```{math}
:label: eq:labor2_welfare_caution
\Delta W \neq \omega \cdot \beta
```

without additional assumptions on spillovers, incidence, adjustment, and distributional weights.

Use this as a teaching device to reinforce that reduced-form outcome effects and welfare conclusions are not the same object.

### 3. Core synthesis block

Organize the chapter around three comparative syntheses.

#### A. Wage-setting and labor market power

This section should connect:
- labor demand,
- search,
- bargaining,
- wage posting,
- monopsony,
- unions and collective bargaining,
- minimum wages and wage law.

The key idea should be that wage determination is not a single model. It is a family of mechanisms that differ by:
- outside options,
- information,
- labor supply to the firm,
- institutional coverage,
- and the margin on which worker voice enters.

#### B. Institutions and regulation

This section should connect:
- minimum wages,
- collective bargaining,
- labor standards,
- employment protection,
- enforcement,
- insurance,
- and information / transparency policies.

The core pedagogical question should be:
- What margin is targeted?
- Who is directly covered?
- Where do spillovers go?
- What equilibrium and welfare objects matter?

#### C. Shocks and adjustment

This section should connect:
- cyclical fluctuations,
- technology / automation / AI,
- trade / offshoring.

The key question should be:
- how do shocks propagate through employment, unemployment, wages, mobility, firm behavior, and long-run reallocation?

Make explicit that adjustment can differ by:
- worker versus place,
- short run versus long run,
- direct exposure versus indirect spillovers,
- outcome effects versus welfare effects.

### 4. How to choose a research design

This section should be very concrete.

It should teach students to move from:
- question -> mechanism -> unit -> data -> design -> counterfactual -> contribution.

It must explicitly distinguish:
- descriptive decomposition projects,
- causal reduced-form designs,
- structural equilibrium / counterfactual projects,
- measurement or classification projects,
- linked worker-firm / administrative-data projects.

This should not be presented as a ranking. It should be presented as a choice problem.

### 5. Common failure modes

The chapter should include a section or figure on common mistakes in labor-market research design, such as:
- confusing gaps with mechanisms,
- treating a place-level effect as a worker-level welfare statement,
- presenting short-run adjustment as a long-run conclusion,
- ignoring uncovered margins or spillovers,
- treating descriptive exposure as treatment without defending the design,
- using “equilibrium” language without an equilibrium object.

### 6. Frontier directions and bridge beyond Labor II

This section should be forward-looking.

Suggested buckets:
- worker power, labor market power, and antitrust,
- AI / algorithmic management and labor-market institutions,
- worker voice, bargaining, and democratic governance inside firms,
- services trade, supply chains, and cross-border labor-market exposure,
- labor standards, enforcement, and informality,
- long-run worker adjustment and administrative panel data,
- linking labor economics to culture / politics / history without losing labor-market discipline,
- connecting field-course ideas to dissertation-scale questions.

The chapter should end by making clear that Labor II is not the end of the field. It is the market-side backbone for later special topics.

## Empirical and methods requirements

The final chapter must distinguish:
- worker-side versus firm-side versus place-side outcomes,
- policies versus shocks,
- direct effects versus spillovers,
- static versus dynamic adjustment,
- descriptive versus causal versus structural work,
- outcome effects versus welfare claims,
- short-run identification versus long-run interpretation.

Do not let any section make a broad statement without clarifying:
- what object is being measured,
- what mechanism is hypothesized,
- what design is used,
- and what remains offstage.
