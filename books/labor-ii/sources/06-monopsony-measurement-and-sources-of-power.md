# Week 6 source pack — Monopsony, Measurement, and Sources of Power

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Labor II Week 6. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

What does it mean to say that firms possess monopsonistic power in the labor market, how can that power be measured, and what are the main frictions and market structures that generate it?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor II
- Week: 6
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 75--90 minute extension block because this is one of the conceptual centerpieces of Labor II
- Position in sequence: follows Week 5 on wage posting, bargaining, and wage-setting; precedes Week 7, where the course turns more explicitly to policy and institutions
- Goal: give students a clean and modern map of monopsony, connect the theory of wage-setting power to the main measurement objects in the empirical literature, and organize the evidence on where monopsony power comes from

## Why this week comes sixth

Week 5 established that wages are not mechanically pinned down by a competitive spot market. They depend on a wage-setting protocol:
- wages may be posted,
- bargained,
- partially administratively determined,
- or shaped by outside options and internal pay rules.

Week 6 asks the next question:
- when do firms have enough wage-setting power that the monopsony lens is the right way to think about labor markets?
- what does monopsony mean in modern labor economics, beyond the old textbook one-buyer town?
- how do labor economists measure monopsony power when the relevant objects are only partially observed?
- what frictions or structures make employer power stronger or weaker?

This week should make five transitions explicit:
- from **wage-setting protocols** to **wage-setting power**;
- from **posted or bargained wages** to **downward-sloping labor supply to the firm**;
- from **search frictions** to **dynamic monopsony**;
- from **reduced-form wage-setting evidence** to **measurement of markdowns, elasticities, and concentration**;
- from **positive analysis** to the later policy and institutions lectures.

The chapter should also be explicit about what is *not* fully in scope yet:
- the minimum-wage and policy discussion that follows next week;
- the unions and collective-bargaining lecture later in the course;
- product-market power matters, but this is still primarily a labor-market lecture.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. define monopsony as employer wage-setting power in a way that is useful for modern labor economics;
2. distinguish textbook monopsony, oligopsony, and dynamic/search monopsony;
3. explain why downward-sloping labor supply to the firm is central but not the only empirical object;
4. distinguish labor-supply elasticities, markdowns, concentration proxies, firm wage premia, and rent-sharing measures;
5. explain why concentration is not the same thing as monopsony, and why concentration measures can still be useful;
6. summarize the literature's main approaches to measuring monopsony power and the tradeoffs across them;
7. explain the leading candidate sources of monopsony power: search frictions, job differentiation, preferences and amenities, information frictions, concentration, mobility constraints, and institutional barriers;
8. connect monopsony to later questions about minimum wages, noncompetes, mergers, wage transparency, and worker voice.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should sound like an advanced PhD labor field-course lecture, not an antitrust policy memo or a generic IO note.
- It should stay labor-focused: monopsony as a way of organizing wage determination, turnover, pass-through, and labor-market policy.
- It should be conceptually clean but tied closely to empirical observables.
- It should explicitly tell students whether a given object is:
  - a theoretical primitive,
  - a reduced-form statistic,
  - a structural parameter,
  - or a proxy.
- It should tell students clearly where the literature currently agrees and where measurement remains unsettled.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening: why monopsony is the natural sequel to Week 5

This section must:
- explain why wage-setting protocols alone do not tell us how much power firms have;
- define the week's organizing question around employer wage-setting power;
- preview the main empirical objects:
  - labor supply to the firm,
  - markdowns,
  - concentration,
  - merger evidence,
  - and wage pass-through;
- explain why monopsony is no longer just the old "company town" model.

The opening should preview:
- the minimum-wage and policy discussion that follows in Week 7;
- the later institutions block;
- and the idea that monopsony is one of the main modern bridges between labor economics and IO.

### 2. What monopsony is: classic vs modern frameworks

This section should set up the conceptual map.

Minimum content:
- define a classic one-firm monopsony benchmark;
- contrast it with modern dynamic/search monopsony;
- explain why jobs are differentiated and why workers face imperfect mobility across firms;
- explain why wage-setting power can exist even with many firms;
- distinguish employer size, concentration, and power.

This is the right place to tell students that monopsony is best thought of as a spectrum of wage-setting power rather than a binary market structure.

### 3. Labor supply to the firm, marginal cost of labor, and markdowns

This is the first theoretical backbone.

Minimum content:
- define labor supply to the firm;
- explain why the relevant elasticity is not market labor supply;
- derive the relation between a finite elasticity and a wedge between wages and marginal revenue product;
- define a markdown or equivalent wedge object;
- explain why markdowns, wage premia, and rent-sharing are related but non-identical concepts.

This section should make clear that "monopsony" is often empirically operationalized in more than one way.

### 4. How the literature measures monopsony power

This is the second backbone of the lecture and should be longer than usual.

Organize it by measurement object, not chronology.

Minimum buckets:
- labor-supply elasticities to the firm,
- recruiting and applications elasticities,
- separation and quit elasticities,
- markdowns using production-side information,
- concentration and market-share proxies,
- quasi-experimental merger or market-structure shocks,
- pass-through and firm-shock approaches.

For each bucket, the chapter should say:
- what varies,
- what unit is observed,
- what monopsony object is identified,
- what key unobserved object remains,
- and why estimates from different methods need not line up numerically.

### 5. Sources of monopsony power

This section should be explicit and comprehensive.

Minimum content:
- search and mobility frictions;
- job differentiation and amenity preferences;
- information frictions and wage opacity;
- geographic or occupational mobility constraints;
- concentration, large employers, and thin local labor markets;
- contracts and institutional barriers such as noncompetes or immigration restrictions, only insofar as they illuminate the positive theory;
- internal wage rules and administrative wage-setting as a potential amplifier of firm power.

This section should tell students that the literature increasingly treats monopsony power as arising from *multiple overlapping sources*, not just concentration.

### 6. Data and empirical designs: what the best papers actually identify

Organize this section by empirical design.

Minimum design buckets:
- direct wage-variation or applications-variation designs;
- online or platform settings with cleaner wage variation;
- matched employer-employee production designs for markdowns;
- mergers or market-structure shocks;
- broader equilibrium models that map concentration or size into wage effects.

For each design, the chapter should say:
- what the identifying variation is,
- what unit is observed,
- which margin is measured,
- and what is still missing for a complete monopsony interpretation.

### 7. Frontier extension block: unresolved measurement issues and new opportunities

This extension block should be substantial. It should work as:
- an extra 75--90 minute session,
- an appendix-slide set,
- or a research-idea workshop.

The extension should cover at least four of the following:
- why concentration measures can understate or misclassify true labor-market power;
- interactions between monopsony and amenities / compensating differentials;
- mergers, hospital or platform settings, and antitrust-relevant evidence;
- markdown measurement outside manufacturing;
- links between monopsony, inequality, and worker sorting;
- wage pass-through and the boundaries between bargaining and monopsony models;
- the role of new administrative, posting, or platform data;
- open questions around dynamic mobility networks, occupation spillovers, and worker beliefs.

The extension should explicitly identify:
- new data opportunities,
- quasi-experimental openings,
- unresolved measurement problems,
- and theory/empirics gaps.

## Required formal objects

The chapter should contain at least five formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: labor supply to the firm

Use a compact object such as

```tex
\varepsilon_{N,w} = \frac{\partial \log N(w)}{\partial \log w}
```

Interpretation:
- the relevant elasticity is the responsiveness of employment at a firm to that firm's own wage.

### Equation 2: marginal cost of labor under monopsony

Use a stylized object such as

```tex
MCL = w\left(1 + \frac{1}{\varepsilon_{N,w}}\right)
```

Interpretation:
- with finite labor-supply elasticity to the firm, the marginal cost of labor exceeds the wage.

### Equation 3: markdown object

Use a compact object such as

```tex
\mu = \frac{MRPL}{w}
```

Interpretation:
- the markdown summarizes the wedge between the worker's marginal revenue product and the wage.

### Equation 4: concentration proxy object

Use a simple empirical proxy such as

```tex
HHI_m = \sum_j s_{jm}^2
```

Interpretation:
- concentration may proxy for labor-market power, but it is not itself a structural measure of monopsony.

### Equation 5: reduced-form wage response to market-structure change

Use a reduced-form object such as

```tex
\Delta \log w_{it} = \beta \, \Delta Conc_{mt} + X_{it}'\gamma + \varepsilon_{it}
```

Interpretation:
- merger and concentration designs reveal wage responses to changing market structure, not necessarily the full underlying wedge.

## Research lab spine

The lab should be built around **Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer**.

### Primary anchor
- [@dubeJacobsNaiduSuri2020]

Why this paper works:
- it measures labor-supply-to-the-firm elasticity in a setting with unusually clean wage variation;
- it is a good pedagogical anchor because the bounded path can be public and computationally manageable;
- it makes the measurement object concrete.

### Secondary / challenge anchor
- [@yehMacalusoHershbein2022]

Why this paper works:
- it forces students to confront markdown measurement;
- it highlights the difference between labor-supply elasticity and production-side wedges;
- it is ideal for the Diagnose part of the lab because the bounded student path will have to explain what cannot be directly reproduced without confidential inputs.

### Optional extension anchor
- [@pragerSchmitt2021]

Why this paper works:
- it connects monopsony to a clear source of power: employer consolidation;
- it helps students see how mergers and market-structure shocks identify wage effects;
- it provides a natural bridge to the policy block.

### Transfer directions

The lab handout should offer 2--3 bounded transfer options such as:
- a stylized online-task elasticity exercise;
- a synthetic plant panel illustrating markdown measurement;
- a public employer concentration panel using a simplified wage/concentration regression.

The lab should be explicit about:
- what monopsony object each paper measures;
- what identifying variation is used;
- which object remains unobserved;
- and why transferring the design may change the interpretation.

## Bridge notes for the chapter ending

The ending should do three things clearly.

### 1. Summarize the worker-side logic of monopsony
Students should leave knowing that monopsony is about how weak outside options and imperfect mobility give firms wage-setting power.

### 2. Clarify what the literature currently knows
The chapter should end with a short, balanced assessment:
- the literature broadly agrees that monopsony power is empirically relevant;
- the main debate is increasingly about *how much*, *through which mechanism*, and *measured by what object*;
- different measurement strategies reveal related but distinct wedges.

### 3. Bridge to Week 7
The final bridge should say explicitly:
- once firms have monopsony power, policy questions become sharper;
- minimum wages, wage mandates, and worker-protective institutions may have very different implications than in a competitive model;
- this is why the next week turns to policy and institutional incidence.
