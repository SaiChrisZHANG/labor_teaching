# Week 4 source pack — Search, Matching, Turnover, and Unemployment

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Labor II Week 4. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How do search frictions shape hiring, separations, turnover, job-to-job mobility, on-the-job search, and unemployment outcomes once the analysis moves from the firm interior to market interaction?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor II
- Week: 4
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 75--90 minute extension block because this is the first major search-theory week and one of the heaviest theoretical weeks of the course
- Position in sequence: first friction-and-market-interaction week of Labor II; follows static labor demand, dynamic adjustment, and personnel economics; precedes Week 5 on wage posting, bargaining, and wage-setting
- Goal: give students a clean but expansive first map of search theory in labor economics, with explicit attention to separations, churning, job-to-job transitions, on-the-job search, unemployment duration, and the labor-market consequences of unemployment spells

## Why this week comes fourth

Weeks 1--3 stayed largely inside the firm:
- Week 1 asked how much labor the firm wants in a static production setting;
- Week 2 asked why adjustment is gradual and lumpy over time;
- Week 3 asked how firms organize workers internally through incentives, promotions, managers, and teams.

Week 4 changes the unit of analysis. The object is no longer only the firm's internal labor problem. The object is the **frictional labor market**:
- workers search imperfectly,
- firms post vacancies imperfectly,
- meetings take time,
- separations occur,
- jobs are destroyed and re-created,
- some workers search while employed,
- unemployment is a flow equilibrium outcome rather than a static stock.

This week should make five transitions explicit:

- from **firm-side adjustment** to **market-level worker flows**;
- from **internal personnel policies** to **external hiring and mobility frictions**;
- from **employment levels** to **transition hazards and flow accounting**;
- from **one-shot search intuition** to **job ladders, on-the-job search, and churning**;
- from **unemployment as a status** to **unemployment as a dynamic process with duration, selection, and long-run consequences**.

It should also make clear what is deliberately *not* fully in scope yet:
- detailed bargaining and wage determination belong mostly to Week 5;
- labor market power / monopsony belongs later;
- aggregate macro-labor fluctuations are not the main focus yet, though cyclicality should be foreshadowed where useful.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. explain why search frictions generate unemployment, vacancies, turnover, and dispersion in outcomes;
2. distinguish worker flows from job flows, and distinguish separations, layoffs, quits, job destruction, and churning;
3. derive and interpret a basic matching-function / flow-balance framework;
4. explain why separation rates, job-finding rates, and vacancy-filling rates are distinct objects and why each matters empirically;
5. explain how job-to-job transitions and on-the-job search change the interpretation of labor-market adjustment;
6. understand why unemployment duration reflects both causal effects of nonemployment and dynamic selection across workers or jobs;
7. connect search-theory objects to modern empirical designs using CPS flows, JOLTS, matched employer-employee data, online search/application data, audit-style field experiments, and policy shocks.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should sound like a serious labor field-course lecture, not a generic macro search note.
- It should remain labor-focused: worker flows, job ladders, separations, job quality, and unemployment consequences.
- It should be theoretically clean but not stripped down to the point that important empirical objects disappear.
- It should explicitly tell students when a given object is:
  - a worker-flow object,
  - a firm/vacancy object,
  - a job-flow object,
  - or a wage/job-quality object.
- It should be clear that search theory is not just about matching unemployed workers to vacant jobs. It is also about mobility among jobs, worker sorting, churning, selective hiring, and the consequences of interrupted employment.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening: why search enters Labor II here

This section must:
- explain why the course now leaves the firm's interior and moves to labor-market interaction under frictions;
- define the main empirical objects: unemployment, vacancies, separations, job-finding, vacancy-filling, quits, layoffs, job-to-job transitions;
- warn students that not all turnover is the same object.

The opening should preview:
- Week 5 wage posting, bargaining, and wage-setting;
- later weeks on monopsony and policy;
- the idea that search theory is the bridge between firm-side labor demand and market-level wage/employment outcomes.

### 2. The core search-and-matching backbone

This is the first theoretical backbone of the week.

Minimum content:
- introduce the meeting/search friction problem;
- define the matching function and the distinction between worker-side and vacancy-side hazards;
- define job-finding and vacancy-filling rates;
- derive a steady-state or near-steady-state flow relation for unemployment;
- connect matching to unemployment, vacancies, and congestion.

This section should be clear that the matching function is a *reduced-form technology* for meetings, not itself a full theory of who searches, which jobs they accept, or why wages differ.

### 3. Separations, job destruction, turnover, and churning

This is the second conceptual backbone and should be much more developed than the baseline outline.

Minimum content:
- distinguish separations from layoffs, quits, and job destruction;
- explain why worker turnover can be high even when net employment is stable;
- define churning / excess worker reallocation and explain what it captures;
- discuss why separations are not just an exogenous nuisance term but often the main margin governing unemployment dynamics;
- connect plant/firm shocks, personnel policies, and cyclical conditions to separation behavior.

This section should make students understand why a theory of unemployment that only talks about job finding is incomplete.

### 4. Job-to-job transitions, on-the-job search, and the job ladder

This section is essential and should not be treated as a small appendix.

Minimum content:
- explain why many labor-market transitions happen without passing through unemployment;
- explain on-the-job search as a mechanism for wage growth, sorting, and job ladders;
- distinguish unemployment-to-employment flows from job-to-job flows;
- discuss why job ladders reshape how we think about business cycles, firm quality, and worker progression;
- connect employed search to the later wage-setting and monopsony blocks.

This is the right place to explain why search theory is not only a theory of unemployment but also a theory of mobility and sorting across firms.

### 5. Unemployment duration, search intensity, and the effects of unemployment

This section should pull together several literatures cleanly.

Minimum content:
- define observed duration dependence and distinguish causal duration effects from dynamic selection;
- discuss search intensity over the spell and what modern data show;
- explain reservation wages, job quality, and the tension between quick reemployment and better reemployment;
- discuss the effects of unemployment on later wages, occupation, match quality, or scarring;
- connect this to selectivity during recessions and the distribution of reemployment outcomes.

This section should not become a policy lecture, but it should make clear why unemployment is not merely a neutral waiting state.

### 6. Data and empirical designs: how modern search empirics identify frictions

Organize this section by data/design, not as a chronology.

Minimum design buckets:
- CPS-style labor-force-status flows and transition matrices;
- JOLTS or vacancy-based matching evidence;
- matched employer-employee or administrative data for job-to-job flows and job ladders;
- online applications / search-intensity data;
- field experiments on callbacks, offers, or duration dependence;
- policy shocks that shift search behavior, reservation wages, or job quality.

For each design, the chapter should say:
- what varies,
- what unit is observed,
- what margin is identified,
- and what key object remains unobserved.

### 7. Frontier extension block: open directions after the first search week

This extension block should be substantial. It should work as:
- an extra 75--90 minute session,
- an appendix-slide set,
- or a research-idea workshop.

The extension should cover at least four of the following:
- selective hiring and occupational downgrading during recessions;
- beliefs, expectations, and perceived job-finding hazards;
- reservation wages and job-quality tradeoffs;
- search intensity measured from digital traces or applications;
- geographic or occupational search frictions;
- mismatch between vacancies and job-seeker composition;
- search by employed workers and connections to wage-setting or monopsony;
- distinguishing unemployment effects from layoff effects and firm-quality loss.

The extension should explicitly identify:
- new data opportunities,
- quasi-experimental openings,
- unresolved measurement problems,
- and open theory/empirics gaps.

## Required formal objects

The chapter should contain at least five formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: matching function

Use a compact object such as

```tex
M_t = \mathcal{M}(U_t, V_t)
```

Interpretation:
- matches are produced by unemployed job seekers and vacancies through a meeting technology.

### Equation 2: worker-side and vacancy-side hazards

Use objects such as

```tex
f_t = \frac{M_t}{U_t}, \qquad q_t = \frac{M_t}{V_t}
```

Interpretation:
- the same matching technology implies different hazards for workers and vacancies.

### Equation 3: steady-state unemployment or flow-balance relation

Use a compact object such as

```tex
u_{t+1} - u_t = s_t (1-u_t) - f_t u_t
```

or the steady-state counterpart

```tex
u^* = \frac{s}{s+f}.
```

Interpretation:
- unemployment is governed jointly by separations and job finding.

### Equation 4: on-the-job search / job-ladder hazard object

Use a stylized object such as

```tex
\lambda^{EE}_{it} = \lambda\big(w_{it}, x_i, \phi_t, \text{firm quality}_j\big)
```

Interpretation:
- employed-to-employed mobility depends on job quality, worker type, and market conditions.

### Equation 5: unemployment duration and reemployment quality object

Use a compact object such as

```tex
y^{reemp}_{i} = \alpha + \beta D_i + \gamma X_i + \varepsilon_i
```

with a discussion that {math}`D_i` reflects both causal effects of duration and dynamic selection.

Interpretation:
- observed duration gradients are not automatically causal unemployment effects.

## Reading ladder design

The reading ladder should be explicit, not just a long bibliography.

Minimum buckets:
1. **Core theory**
   - search/matching backbone

2. **Worker flows and job ladders**

## Research-lab design priorities

The research lab should be particularly well designed this week, because Week 4 is where many students first see how search theory maps to empirical labor research.

The lab should:
- clearly name one primary paper and one challenge/extension paper;
- say what is replicated, what is diagnosed, and what is transferred;
- explain why the bounded student path does not require confidential microdata;
- propose at least one transfer direction using public CPS/JOLTS-style flows or a small synthetic job-ladder dataset.

The lab handout should explicitly guide students through:
1. which object they are reproducing,
2. which hazard or margin they are measuring,
3. what the identifying variation is,
4. what a sensible transfer exercise looks like.

## Bridges to the rest of the course

The chapter must end with a short bridge section.

### Bridge backward to Week 3

Explain how internal labor markets and personnel practices affect separations, quits, hiring, and worker retention, even though Week 3 focused on inside-firm choices.

### Bridge forward to Week 5

Explain that once search frictions, vacancies, and outside options exist, wages can no longer be treated as purely competitive objects. That is the entry point for wage posting, bargaining, and rent-sharing logic next week.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- Do not collapse all turnover into one object.
- Do not discuss unemployment without separations.
- Do not discuss job-finding without explaining what happens to employed searchers.
- Do not treat observed duration dependence as automatically causal.
- Do not drift into a full bargaining lecture; save wage-setting detail for Week 5.
- Do not turn the whole week into macro fluctuation calibration; keep it labor-market-theory-and-evidence focused.
- Do not ignore the job-quality and scarring consequences of unemployment spells.
