# Week 3 source pack — Personnel Economics and Internal Labor Markets

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Labor II Week 3. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

Once the firm has chosen how much labor to employ and how quickly to adjust, how should it organize, motivate, evaluate, assign, promote, and retain workers inside the firm?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor II
- Week: 3
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60--90 minute extension block if you want a deeper frontier discussion on managers, workplace policies, remote/hybrid work, digital trace data, AI, training, or external-validity questions
- Position in sequence: third firm-side week of Labor II; follows static and dynamic labor demand and precedes Week 4 on search, turnover, and unemployment flows
- Goal: define personnel economics as labor economics inside firms, show how internal organizational choices shape productivity and worker outcomes, and give students a map of current research directions and open questions

## Why this week comes third

Week 1 treated labor as a variable input in a static production problem. Week 2 explained why employment adjustment is gradual and lumpy over time. Week 3 asks a different question: given a workforce, how does the firm *run it*?

Personnel economics sits between the firm's demand for labor and the market's allocation of labor. It studies what happens *inside* the firm: contracts, evaluation, promotion, teams, managers, workplace rules, and organizational practices. This week should make three transitions explicit:

- from **how much labor the firm wants** to **how the firm organizes labor internally**;
- from **employment paths** to **performance, incentives, and worker-firm organization**;
- from **comparative statics and adjustment** to **personnel data, firm experiments, and internal policy evaluation**.

It should also make clear what is deliberately *not* in scope yet:
- no full search-and-matching equilibrium yet (Week 4),
- no bargaining or wage-setting institutions yet (later weeks),
- no monopolistic or monopsonistic market-structure logic yet, except brief foreshadowing.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. define personnel economics and explain where it sits inside labor economics;
2. distinguish incentives that affect **effort** from practices that affect **selection, assignment, promotion, and retention**;
3. explain the core agency, multitasking, and career-incentives logic that motivates modern personnel economics;
4. explain why promotions and internal labor markets create a tradeoff between incentives in current jobs and assignment quality in future jobs;
5. distinguish manager effects, peer effects, team incentives, and broader management practices;
6. understand the empirical designs that identify internal organizational effects, including firm experiments, manager-switch designs, linked personnel records, management surveys, digital traces, and policy shocks;
7. articulate current frontiers and open questions in personnel economics, especially around managers, remote work, voice, training, digital monitoring, algorithmic management, and external validity.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should sound like a modern advanced PhD labor field-course week, not a generic management lecture.
- It should emphasize that personnel economics is part of labor economics: the firm's internal labor problem.
- It should begin from the logic of the firm and then move to incentives, internal labor markets, managers, peers, and frontier research.
- It should remain explicit about where the evidence comes from and what kinds of settings generate modern personnel data.
- The extension block should be ambitious and idea-generating, because this is a fast-moving area of research.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening: what personnel economics is and why it belongs here

This section must:
- define personnel economics as labor economics inside firms;
- explain why it naturally follows static and dynamic labor demand;
- state that the key objects are practices: incentives, evaluation, promotion, team structure, management, and workplace rules.

The opening should preview:
- Week 4: search, turnover, and unemployment flows outside the firm;
- later Weeks 5--6: wage-setting, bargaining, and labor market power;
- how internal organization interacts with market frictions but is analytically distinct from them.

### 2. Incentives, compensation, and the agency benchmark

This is the first theoretical backbone of the week.

Minimum content:
- define the classic agency problem inside firms;
- explain the risk-incentive tradeoff;
- distinguish fixed pay, performance pay, and team-based incentives;
- link incentives to both worker effort and worker sorting.

This section should also make clear why personnel economics moved beyond a narrow contract-design view: firms use hiring, assignment, promotion, monitoring, culture, and teams to shape outcomes too.

### 3. Multitasking, subjective evaluation, and career incentives

This section should explain why simple output-based pay is often incomplete.

Minimum content:
- explain multitasking and why narrow performance measures can distort effort;
- discuss subjective evaluation and relational incentives;
- discuss career concerns, tournaments, and promotions as incentives;
- connect the theory to settings where managerial jobs require different skills than frontline jobs.

This is the right place to introduce the idea that personnel economics studies both *current-job incentives* and *future-job assignment*.

### 4. Promotions, managers, and internal labor markets

This is the second conceptual backbone of the week.

Minimum content:
- define internal labor markets and promotion ladders;
- explain the tension between promoting the best current performer and the best future manager;
- discuss the Peter Principle and related assignment-versus-incentives tradeoff;
- explain what manager value-added means and what it does *not* mean.

This section should be explicit that managers are themselves a labor input and an organizational technology.

### 5. Peers, teams, and organizational spillovers

This section should make clear that work often happens in social production environments.

Minimum content:
- distinguish peer pressure from knowledge spillovers;
- explain why team incentives differ from individual incentives;
- discuss complementarities, free-riding, and monitoring;
- discuss why firms may reorganize teams or jobs rather than only changing pay.

### 6. Data and empirical designs: what modern personnel economists observe

Organize this section by data and design, not by a chronological literature review.

Minimum design buckets:
- firm field experiments;
- internal policy changes or phased rollouts;
- promotions and manager switches in personnel records;
- management surveys linked to firm outcomes;
- digital traces, time-use data, and communication data;
- workplace practices affected by public policies such as worker voice or pay-transparency rules.

For each design, the chapter should say:
- what varies,
- what unit is observed,
- what margin is identified,
- and why external validity is difficult.

### 7. Recent directions, gaps, and research opportunities (long extension block)

This extension block should be substantial. It should work as:
- an extra 60--90 minute session,
- an appendix-slide set,
- or a research-idea workshop.

The extension should cover at least four of the following:
- managers below the C-suite and how to identify good managers;
- remote and hybrid work as a personnel-economics problem of selection, monitoring, promotion, and retention;
- worker voice, communication, and internal governance;
- training inside firms and the undermeasurement of learning and development;
- digital trace data, meetings data, scheduling data, and algorithmic management;
- pay transparency, compression, and within-firm inequality;
- team composition, collaboration networks, and knowledge spillovers;
- external validity, scaling interventions, and how personnel practices interact with labor-market conditions.

The extension should not just summarize papers. It should explicitly identify:
- promising data sources,
- quasi-experimental openings,
- policy-relevant organizational changes,
- and important unanswered questions.

## Required formal objects

The chapter should contain at least four formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: firm output with internal organization

Use a compact object such as

```tex
Y = F\big(K, L, e(\omega), m, T\big)
```

where {math}`e(\omega)` denotes effort under workplace practice {math}`\omega`, {math}`m` denotes managerial or supervisory input, and {math}`T` denotes team or organizational structure.

Interpretation:
- personnel economics changes productivity through organization, not only through employment levels.

### Equation 2: incentive / effort object

Use a simple incentive relation such as

```tex
e_i = e\big(w_i, b_i(y_i), p_i, \text{monitoring}_i\big)
```

Interpretation:
- effort depends on compensation design, career incentives, and monitoring.

### Equation 3: multitasking / mismeasurement object

Use a compact object such as

```tex
q_i = \theta_1 a_{i1} + \theta_2 a_{i2} + \varepsilon_i
```

where measured performance {math}`q_i` does not fully span the tasks relevant for firm value.

Interpretation:
- high-powered incentives tied to incomplete measures can distort effort allocation.

### Equation 4: promotion / assignment tradeoff object

Use a stylized promotion rule such as

```tex
\Pr(\text{promote}_i=1) = g\big(y_i, z_i, \widehat{m}_i\big)
```

where current performance {math}`y_i`, collaboration or soft-skill proxies {math}`z_i`, and expected managerial productivity {math}`\widehat{m}_i` all matter.

Interpretation:
- promotions are both assignments and incentives.

### Equation 5: peer or team spillover object

Use a compact object such as

```tex
y_i = a_i e_i + \rho \bar e_{-i} + u_i
```

Interpretation:
- peer spillovers can arise through observation, pressure, learning, or coordination.

## Suggested lab direction

The lab should follow the now-standard two-part structure:

### Part A: Reproduce

Primary anchor: `@friebelHeinzKruegerZubanov2017`

Students should reproduce one or two key moments about team incentives, effort complementarities, or profit consequences using the bounded pedagogical path.

### Part B: Diagnose

Students should identify:
- what varies in the experiment or design,
- which outcomes are individual and which are team-level,
- what the main interpretation relies on,
- and where external validity may be limited.

### Part C: Transfer

Students should transfer the estimator or design logic to a small new dataset related to:
- team output,
- promotions / assignment,
- remote work / flexible work,
- or management-practice variation.

The transfer exercise should stay bounded:
- one design,
- one new table or figure,
- one short memo.

## Bridge statements the chapter should make explicit

### Backward bridge to Week 2

Week 2 asked how fast firms change labor input. Week 3 asks how firms govern and organize labor once they have it. Adjustment costs explain why headcount moves slowly; personnel economics explains why two firms with similar workforces may generate very different productivity, retention, or promotion outcomes.

### Forward bridge to Week 4

Personnel practices interact with turnover, search, and vacancies. Promotions, internal hiring, and retention policies affect who leaves and who searches. Week 4 will move from internal labor markets to worker flows between firms and unemployment states.

## What this week should leave students with

After this week, students should feel they can answer three big questions:
1. What does personnel economics add beyond static and dynamic labor demand?
2. How do firms use incentives, promotion, teams, and managers to shape productivity and retention?
3. Why has this become a particularly active research area in recent years, and where are the most promising opportunities for new work?
