# Lecture 5. Regression Discontinuity, RKD, Bunching, and Local Designs

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:

1. explain when a local-threshold design is preferable to experiments, DID/event studies, or IV;
2. derive and interpret sharp RD, fuzzy RD, RKD, and bunching estimands;
3. explain why local-polynomial estimation, bandwidth choice, and weighting are design decisions rather than tuning details;
4. understand the logic behind modern RD practice, including robust bias correction, honest inference, covariates, and polynomial discipline;
5. distinguish RD in time from event studies and interrupted time-series logic, and understand when spatial RD is credible;
6. evaluate the strengths, limitations, and practical failure modes of RD-style methods;
7. compare RD to other core causal methods from Block 1 in terms of assumptions, SUTVA/interference exposure, credibility, and best-use scenarios;
8. translate an RD-style question into a publishable applied project with clear implementation and robustness logic.

## Opening Orientation

Regression discontinuity is often sold as the closest thing economists have to a randomized experiment. That is a useful instinct, but it becomes misleading if students leave thinking RD is “just compare above and below a cutoff.” The real design problem is whether local continuity is believable, whether agents sort or manipulate around the threshold, whether the researcher is transparent about bandwidth and weighting choices, and whether the estimand is the one the paper needs.

This lecture therefore treats RD, RKD, and bunching as a family of **local designs**. They all exploit sharp changes—levels, slopes, or densities—around a threshold, but each relies on a different identifying object and a different set of behavioral assumptions. Modern practice also matters much more here than in older textbook presentations. Bandwidth choice, robust bias correction, honest inference, and specification discipline are now part of the method itself, not optional polishing.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: note

- RD-style methods are strongest when institutional thresholds create sharp local comparisons and continuity is more believable than global functional-form assumptions.
- The estimand is local: RD identifies a jump at the cutoff, fuzzy RD a local Wald ratio, RKD a slope discontinuity ratio, and bunching a response around a kink or notch under a behavioral model.
- Local polynomial estimation, bandwidth choice, weighting, and inference discipline are part of identification practice, not technical afterthoughts.
- `rdrobust`-style robust bias correction and `rdhonest`-style honest inference address different concerns; students need to know what each correction is doing.
- Time RD and spatial RD require extra discipline because time trends, serial correlation, anticipation, geographic sorting, and multidimensional boundaries create new threats.
- RD is often high-credibility, but only when the threshold is substantively meaningful, sorting is limited or diagnosed, and the local comparison answers the economic question of interest.
:::

## Field Core

### Why Local Threshold Designs?

A threshold design is most persuasive when the institutional rule itself is central to the question. Assignment, eligibility, subsidies, probation rules, score-based admission, and enforcement cutoffs all create locally meaningful contrasts. The reason to use RD is not that “a cutoff exists,” but that the local contrast near the cutoff is more credible than global selection-on-observables, panel-comparison, or instrument-based alternatives.

### Sharp and Fuzzy RD

Let {math}`X_i` be a running variable with threshold {math}`c`. In a sharp RD, treatment is fully determined by the threshold:

```{math}
:label: eq:sharp-rd-assignment
D_i = 
\mathbf{1}\{X_i \ge c\}.
```

The sharp RD estimand is the discontinuity in the conditional expectation of the outcome at the cutoff:

```{math}
:label: eq:sharp-rd-estimand
\tau_{SRD} = \lim_{x \downarrow c} \mathbb{E}[Y_i \mid X_i=x] - \lim_{x \uparrow c} \mathbb{E}[Y_i \mid X_i=x].
```

Its identifying logic is continuity of untreated and treated potential outcomes in the running variable at the cutoff. Intuitively, units just above and just below the threshold should be comparable apart from treatment status.

In a fuzzy RD, the threshold changes the probability of treatment but does not fully determine it. The estimand becomes a local Wald ratio:

```{math}
:label: eq:fuzzy-rd-estimand
\tau_{FRD} = 
\frac{\lim_{x \downarrow c} \mathbb{E}[Y_i \mid X_i=x] - \lim_{x \uparrow c} \mathbb{E}[Y_i \mid X_i=x]}{\lim_{x \downarrow c} \mathbb{E}[D_i \mid X_i=x] - \lim_{x \uparrow c} \mathbb{E}[D_i \mid X_i=x]}.
```

This is a local average treatment effect for compliers at the cutoff, so interpretation again depends on the economic content of the first-stage jump.

### Local Polynomial Estimation, Bandwidth, and Weighting

Modern RD is built around local polynomial regression. A generic weighted least-squares representation is:

```{math}
:label: eq:local-polynomial
\min_{\alpha_-,\alpha_+,\beta_-,\beta_+}
\sum_i K\!\left(\frac{X_i-c}{h}\right)
\Big[Y_i - \alpha_- - \beta_-(X_i-c)\Big]^2 \mathbf{1}\{X_i<c\}
+ \sum_i K\!\left(\frac{X_i-c}{h}\right)
\Big[Y_i - \alpha_+ - \beta_+(X_i-c)\Big]^2 \mathbf{1}\{X_i\ge c\},
```

where {math}`h` is the bandwidth and {math}`K(\cdot)` is the kernel/weighting function. In practice, local linear or local quadratic estimators with **triangular weights** are often preferred because they concentrate identifying variation near the cutoff and behave well at boundaries.

Students should treat bandwidth as part of the estimand, not just a tuning parameter. A larger bandwidth reduces variance but risks bias from curvature or heterogeneity farther from the cutoff. A smaller bandwidth strengthens local comparability but can make estimates noisy and fragile. The practical question is not “which bandwidth is correct?” but “how local should the comparison be, given the available data and the substantive question?”

### Modern RD: `rdrobust`, `rdhonest`, Covariates, and Polynomial Discipline

The modern RD toolkit has two especially important branches.

**Robust bias correction (`rdrobust`).** Local polynomial point estimates can be biased because the bandwidth that is optimal for mean squared error is not necessarily optimal for inference. The robust bias-correction approach estimates and removes the leading bias term, then adjusts standard errors accordingly. This is the logic behind `rdrobust` [@calonicoCattaneoTitiunik2014].

**Honest inference (`rdhonest`).** Armstrong and Kolesár’s honest inference framework instead asks: given an explicit bound on curvature / smoothness, what confidence interval remains valid under worst-case bias? This is the logic behind `rdhonest` [@armstrongKolesar2020].

These tools answer different questions:
- `rdrobust` is about correcting and re-centering local-polynomial inference under standard smoothness assumptions;
- `rdhonest` is about reporting inference that is valid under an explicit smoothness class, even when bias could be adversarial within that class.

**Covariates.** Adding predetermined covariates can improve precision, but they do not create identification in a valid RD. They should be included only when clearly pre-treatment, measured comparably on both sides of the cutoff, and not themselves affected by threshold manipulation. The key point for students is that covariates are a precision choice, not a rescue strategy.

**Polynomial discipline.** High-order global polynomials are now widely discouraged because they can generate unstable boundary behavior and misleading extrapolation [@gelmanImbens2019]. In modern practice, local polynomial estimation dominates global flexible polynomial fitting.

### RKD and Bunching

A regression kink design exploits a change in the slope of treatment intensity or incentives at a threshold, rather than a discontinuous level shift. A generic RKD estimand can be written as:

```{math}
:label: eq:rkd-estimand
\tau_{RKD} = 
\frac{\lim_{x \downarrow c} \frac{d}{dx}\mathbb{E}[Y_i\mid X_i=x] - \lim_{x \uparrow c} \frac{d}{dx}\mathbb{E}[Y_i\mid X_i=x]}{\lim_{x \downarrow c} \frac{d}{dx}\mathbb{E}[D_i\mid X_i=x] - \lim_{x \uparrow c} \frac{d}{dx}\mathbb{E}[D_i\mid X_i=x]}.
```

RKD is attractive when policy changes the *marginal incentive* rather than the level of treatment. But it is often more fragile than level RD because derivative estimation is noisier and because policy rules may not induce clean local slope changes in behavior.

Bunching designs use excess mass around a kink or notch to infer behavioral responses to incentives. In a simple kink setting, the key idea is that the observed density around the threshold can be decomposed into a counterfactual smooth density plus excess mass generated by behavioral response. Under a maintained optimization model, the amount of bunching can be mapped into an elasticity or other structural object.

Bunching is powerful when:
- the institutional schedule is sharp and salient,
- optimization frictions are limited or modeled explicitly,
- and the counterfactual density is credibly approximated.

It is fragile when salience is weak, adjustment frictions are strong but unmodeled, or the counterfactual density is highly uncertain. Students should therefore treat bunching as a **design plus model** approach, not a model-free discontinuity design.

### RD in Time and How It Differs from Event Studies

When time itself is the running variable, the threshold is a date. This looks superficially like a sharp RD, but the identification threats are different. Time RD often faces serial correlation, seasonality, anticipation, simultaneous shocks, and serially evolving unobservables. These are not secondary details; they are central to whether the local comparison around the date is credible.

A useful distinction is:
- **time RD** is strongest when the treatment date is sharp, agents cannot manipulate timing meaningfully, and a very local comparison around the threshold is credible;
- **event studies / DID** are stronger when untreated units or untreated periods provide a more credible comparison path over time.

Time RD should therefore not be treated as “event study with no controls.” It is a local continuity design around a date, with all the complications that date-based thresholds imply.

### Spatial RD: One-Dimensional and Multi-Dimensional Boundaries

Spatial RD treats geography as the running variable. In the simplest one-dimensional case, units are ordered by signed distance to a border. In richer cases, the relevant object is a multidimensional boundary where distance, geography, and local context must all be handled carefully.

Spatial RD is attractive because nearby places on opposite sides of a boundary can be plausibly similar, but it is also tricky because:
- borders may coincide with historical, administrative, ethnic, or geographic discontinuities unrelated to treatment;
- sorting may occur if people or firms choose locations near the boundary strategically;
- continuity may hold in some dimensions but not others.

A good spatial RD needs a strong institutional reason for why the border matters *and* a credible local comparison in geography, not just a map with a line on it.

### Best Scenarios, Strengths, and Limitations

**Best scenarios for RD-style methods.**
- There is a substantively meaningful threshold built into policy or institutional assignment.
- Manipulation around the cutoff is limited or diagnosable.
- A local comparison near the threshold answers the economic question.
- The outcome surface is smooth enough locally that continuity is plausible.

**Advantages.**
- Often very high local credibility.
- Transparent design logic.
- Strong connection between institutional rule and estimand.
- Frequently persuasive in publication settings when the threshold is important and the implementation is disciplined.

**Limitations.**
- Local estimand may have weak external validity.
- Sorting/manipulation can be fatal.
- Bandwidth, weighting, and inference choices matter a great deal.
- Time RD and spatial RD bring additional threats.
- Bunching needs stronger behavioral assumptions than many users admit.

### Theory-to-Applied Research Logic

A strong applied paper in this family usually has this architecture:

1. **Institutional threshold.** What exact rule or schedule creates the local variation?
2. **Local comparability.** Why are units just on either side of the cutoff plausibly comparable?
3. **Estimand.** Is the target a level discontinuity, a local Wald ratio, a slope discontinuity, or a bunching-based structural parameter?
4. **Implementation choices.** How are bandwidth, weighting, polynomial order, covariates, and inference handled?
5. **Threats.** What about manipulation, anticipation, spatial sorting, seasonality, or functional-form dependence?
6. **Interpretation.** Is the resulting estimate local but substantively important? Does the local effect answer the paper’s main question?

This is why Lecture 5 is not “the cutoff lecture.” It is the lecture about when local identification is convincing, and how modern implementation turns a threshold into a credible design.

## Research Lab

The Lecture 5 Research Lab follows **Reproduce → Diagnose → Transfer**.

**Primary anchor.** Lee’s close-election design is the main reproduction anchor [@lee2008]. It is the cleanest way to teach sharp RD logic, local polynomial implementation, bandwidth sensitivity, and continuity-based interpretation.

**Diagnosis anchor.** Calonico, Cattaneo, and Titiunik [@calonicoCattaneoTitiunik2014], Armstrong and Kolesár [@armstrongKolesar2020], and Gelman and Imbens [@gelmanImbens2019] provide the diagnosis logic for why bandwidth, bias correction, honest inference, and polynomial discipline change applied practice.

**Transfer anchor.** Dell’s spatial RD [@dell2010] and Saez’s bunching design [@saez2010] provide natural transfer extensions: one pushes students toward geographic boundaries, the other toward threshold-induced density responses.

**Reproduce.** Students reproduce a bounded local-polynomial RD on a reduced or synthetic teaching dataset modeled on a canonical threshold application.

**Diagnose.** Students then compare naive specifications to robust-bias-corrected and honest-inference versions, explicitly explaining how bandwidth and weighting alter the precision–bias tradeoff.

**Transfer.** Students then transfer the logic either to a spatial boundary design or to a bunching setting, forcing them to name the threshold, explain the identifying object, and diagnose the new threats.

## Causal Inference Block Summary

This lecture closes Block 1. The main methods in the block can be compared using a simple rule: each method creates a different answer to the question “what stands in for the missing counterfactual?”

- **Selection-on-observables** replaces the missing counterfactual by conditioning on observables and assuming no unobserved selection after conditioning.
- **Experiments** replace the missing counterfactual by design through random assignment.
- **DID / event studies / synthetic control** replace the missing counterfactual with untreated time paths.
- **IV** replaces endogenous treatment variation with instrument-induced variation.
- **RD / RKD / bunching** replace the missing counterfactual with a local comparison around a threshold.

A rough applied-economics credibility hierarchy often treats well-executed experiments and sharp threshold designs as especially persuasive, with DID / event-study designs next when untreated trends are compelling, IV designs varying widely depending on instrument credibility, and selection-on-observables usually requiring the heaviest sensitivity analysis. But this hierarchy is only heuristic. Publication quality depends on the question, design fit, transparency, data quality, and mechanism clarity—not on the method label alone.

A useful way to remember the SUTVA/interference angle is:
- experiments and DID can fail badly when spillovers or equilibrium responses are ignored;
- IV can fail when the instrument changes untreated units or operates through equilibrium channels;
- RD can fail when treatment spillovers or sorting contaminate the local comparison;
- selection-on-observables can fail when treatment changes peers, markets, or institutions in unmeasured ways.

So the right question is never “which method is best in general?” It is “which design creates the most credible counterfactual for this economic mechanism?”

**Use these papers to answer four design questions:**
- What is the threshold and why does it matter economically?
- What is the local identifying object?
- How do bandwidth, weights, and inference affect the estimate?
- What threatens continuity or local comparability?
