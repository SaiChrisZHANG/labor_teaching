| Identification problem | Typical labor-network setting | Why ordinary methods fail | Network-aware response |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reflection / simultaneity | coworker productivity, peer job search, team effort | peer outcomes and own outcomes move together contemporaneously | exploit topology, timing, or random assignment; avoid pure group means |
| Endogenous group formation | workers sort into firms, teams, neighborhoods, or referral pools | peers share latent traits and common shocks | use random assignment, natural experiments, or structural/control-function approaches |
| Interference / spillovers | referrals, manager training, platform visibility rules, information diffusion | untreated units are indirectly affected by others’ treatment | define exposure mappings and estimate direct vs spillover effects |
| Correlated local shocks | teams, firms, schools, villages | shared shocks mimic network effects | cluster or dyadic-aware inference; design around common shocks |
| Dyadic dependence | worker–firm ties, referral dyads, collaboration pairs | dyads sharing one unit are not independent | use dyadic-robust variance logic or pair-aware resampling |
| Misspecified exposure | treatment of neighbors summarized too crudely | estimated effect does not match the true channel of propagation | justify the exposure mapping substantively and test alternatives |
| Partial-interference failure | open labor markets, platforms, citywide policies | spillovers cross the assumed cluster boundary | use broader design, graph-based exposure, or be explicit about estimand limits |
| Network measurement error | missing edges, mismeasured referrals, censored communication | identification uses a distorted network | assess sensitivity to network construction and missing links |
