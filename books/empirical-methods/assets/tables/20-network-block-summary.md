# Network Methods Block Summary

## What Lectures 18–20 together should teach

| Lecture | Core question | Main takeaway |
|---|---|---|
| 18. Curating network data | What is the network? | Node and edge definitions are economic assumptions, not formatting choices. |
| 19. Causal inference with network data | How do we identify effects under dependence? | Exposure mappings and interference assumptions determine what is identified. |
| 20. Structural modeling with network data | When do we need a network model for counterfactuals? | If policy changes links, information, or matching opportunities, reduced-form effects may be insufficient. |

## Where the literature is now
- Strongest on measurement of labor and peer networks from administrative and platform data.
- Strongest on reduced-form identification of peer effects, referrals, and spillovers.
- Increasingly strong on econometric models of network formation and degree heterogeneity.
- More uneven when reduced-form evidence and structural counterfactuals are combined in one design.

## Where the opportunities are
- Worker-firm bipartite networks with richer dynamic formation.
- Referral and visibility policies on online labor platforms.
- Structural network models of workplace teams, managerial assignment, and internal labor markets.
- Network redesign and fairness in matching/assignment systems.
- Combining network data with text, platform telemetry, and experimental interventions.
