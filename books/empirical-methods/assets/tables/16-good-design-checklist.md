# Good Design Checklist

1. **Name the spatial mechanism first.** Is the relevant channel commuting access, neighborhood exposure, local labor demand, institutional borders, or spillovers?
2. **Choose geography based on the mechanism, not convenience.** Explain why tracts, counties, commuting zones, buffers, or travel-time surfaces are the right empirical object.
3. **State whether geography is the source of identification, a threat to inference, or the mechanism itself.**
4. **Write down the interference assumption.** Are spillovers absent, bounded, exposure-mapped, or part of the estimand?
5. **Separate bias problems from variance problems.** Spatially robust standard errors do not make a biased design credible.
6. **Check boundary or exposure plausibility.** Borders, radii, and commuting sheds should be justified institutionally and economically.
7. **Stress-test the geography.** Vary the unit, radius, or travel-time threshold and interpret changes mechanistically.
8. **Treat sorting as central whenever place is the treatment.** Ask who chose the place, when, and under what alternatives.
9. **Document timing carefully.** Outcomes, exposures, boundaries, and covariates must line up in time.
10. **Be explicit about the counterfactual.** Nearby places, untreated time, other boundaries, or alternative shock exposure all imply different designs.
