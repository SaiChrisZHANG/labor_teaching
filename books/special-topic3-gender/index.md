# Gender Study

Gender Study is a flagship 10-week special-topics course on gender, work, households, organizations, and inequality. It is designed as a serious labor-economics field offering rather than a detached add-on, with sustained attention to measurement, mechanisms, and policy interpretation.

The course sits at the intersection of Labor I and Labor II. Labor I contributes households, labor supply, human capital, discrimination, and mobility; Labor II contributes organizations, wage-setting, institutions, and policy. Gender Study uses those foundations to build a coherent field around gendered labor-market outcomes across the life cycle.

## Course type

10-week flagship special-topics course

## Prerequisites

- Labor I or an equivalent worker-side labor background
- Graduate microeconomic theory and applied econometrics
- Readiness to move between family economics, personnel economics, and inequality research

## Course goals

- measure and interpret gender gaps without collapsing distinct mechanisms into one residual;
- connect households, care, human capital, organizations, discrimination, and policy in one framework;
- study gender inequality as a lifecycle and institutional process rather than a single wage-gap fact;
- equip students to frame frontier gender-and-work questions with credible identification strategies.

## Course format

Each week follows the Bridge, Field Core, and Research Lab structure used elsewhere in the portfolio. The course works well as a quarter-length field elective: one conceptual block, one empirical block, and one discussion block focused on interpretation, policy, or frontier research design.

## Week-by-week path

- Week 1. Measuring gender gaps and framing the field. The first week establishes the main outcome objects and why wage gaps alone are too narrow.
- Week 2. Households, care, and time allocation. Labor-market inequality is grounded in family structure, unpaid work, and time-use constraints.
- Week 3. Human capital, schooling, and career choice. Students study how early choices and expectations translate into later labor-market divergence.
- Week 4. Hiring, promotions, and organizations. The course moves inside firms to ask how evaluation, promotion, and organizational design generate unequal careers.
- Week 5. Child penalties and lifecycle inequality. This week centers the dynamic consequences of fertility, caregiving, and career interruption.
- Week 6. Bargaining, norms, and intra-household power. Household models are revisited with explicit attention to bargaining power and social expectations.
- Week 7. Discrimination, bias, and evaluation. Students separate discrimination mechanisms from differences in sorting, information, and institutional context.
- Week 8. Occupations, sectors, entrepreneurship, and sorting. Gender inequality is studied through occupational structure, self-employment, and labor-market segmentation.
- Week 9. Intersectionality, identity, and heterogeneity. The course broadens beyond average gaps to examine how gender interacts with race, class, migration, and place.
- Week 10. Policy design and frontier research. The final week turns the literature into a forward-looking agenda on equality, care infrastructure, and workplace design.

## Research orientation

Gender Study is built to be public-facing and job-market-facing at the same time. It aims to show that the strongest gender-and-work research is not just descriptive but structurally clear about where inequality enters, how it persists, and which policies plausibly move the relevant margins.
