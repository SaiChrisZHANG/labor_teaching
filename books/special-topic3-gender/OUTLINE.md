# Gender Study: Work, Care, Firms, and Labor-Market Inequality

## Course role

Gender Study is the third flagship 10-week special-topic course in the portfolio. It is a labor-economics course on how gender shapes work, care, labor supply, skills, sorting, hiring, promotion, pay-setting, authority, policy incidence, institutions, and worker welfare.

The course should not become a generic gender-studies survey. Every week begins from a labor-market object and uses gender to discipline the question, the mechanism, and the evidence.

## Cumulative logic

The course sequence is deliberately cumulative:

1. define gender as a labor-economics object and clarify measurement;
2. build household, care, fertility, human-capital, and worker-side mechanisms;
3. move into firms, pay-setting, promotion, authority, norms, and institutions;
4. study policy incidence, safety, mobility, and comparative evidence;
5. pause on frontier data and methods before the final research-design synthesis.

The course is broader than the male-female wage gap, but it remains anchored in labor economics. Gender enters through paid and unpaid work, employment, hours, earnings, job quality, safety, authority, mobility, and welfare. Future chapters should make those labor objects explicit before moving to mechanism or interpretation.

## Internal roadmap

- **Foundational:** Week 1
- **Household and worker-side:** Weeks 2 and 3
- **Firm and institution-side:** Weeks 4 and 5
- **Policy, comparative, and frontier focused:** Weeks 6 to 9
- **Optional LGBTQ+ long lecture:** after Week 9 and immediately before Week 10
- **Final synthesis and research-design oriented:** Week 10

## Week-by-week outline

### Week 1. Why Gender Matters for Labor Economics: Concepts, Measurement, and Framing

**Central question:** What makes gender a core labor-economics object rather than a single wage-gap statistic?

The opening week defines the main objects of the course: gender differences in employment, hours, earnings, wages, occupations, firms, authority, safety, care work, and welfare. It separates description from mechanism, explains why average male-female gaps are useful but incomplete, and introduces gender identity, institutional setting, and lifecycle timing as measurement choices rather than background details.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Gender matters here because it changes how workers allocate time, enter jobs, move across firms, receive pay, gain authority, face risk, and experience welfare at work.

### Week 2. Labor Supply, Care Work, Fertility, and Household Allocation

**Central question:** How do care responsibilities, fertility, and household allocation shape labor supply and earnings trajectories?

This week places market work inside the household production and lifecycle setting. Students study labor supply, unpaid care, fertility timing, parental specialization, child penalties, intrahousehold allocation, and control over resources, with attention to how family decisions create dynamic effects on participation, hours, occupations, earnings, and job quality.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Household allocation is labor economics because care work, fertility, and bargaining determine who works, how much they work, which jobs are feasible, and how earnings evolve.

### Week 3. Education, Skills, Aspirations, and Occupational Sorting

**Central question:** How do education, skills, aspirations, and early occupational choices generate later gendered labor-market outcomes?

The third week studies pre-market and early-career mechanisms: schooling choices, field of study, expectations, competitiveness, risk, information, aspirations, skills, commuting constraints, and entry into occupations or sectors. The goal is to connect early choices to later wages, authority, job quality, and occupational segregation without treating observed sorting as either pure preference or pure constraint.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Education and occupational sorting are labor mechanisms because they shape skill prices, job access, search behavior, career ladders, and long-run wage and employment inequality.

### Week 4. Hiring, Promotion, Pay-Setting, and Firm-Side Gender Gaps

**Central question:** How do firms and internal labor markets produce gender differences in pay, promotion, authority, and retention?

This week moves inside organizations. Students study hiring, screening, referrals, evaluation, promotion systems, supervision, bargaining, firm wage premiums, pay-setting rules, old-boy networks, and leadership pipelines. The emphasis is on separating worker sorting from firm behavior and on using personnel, administrative, audit, and matched employer-employee evidence to identify mechanisms.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Firms are labor-market institutions that allocate jobs, authority, rents, schedules, evaluation, promotions, and pay, so firm-side gender gaps are central labor outcomes.

### Week 5. Social Norms, Bargaining, and Institutions Shaping Gendered Work

**Central question:** How do norms, bargaining power, and institutions shape gendered work even when prices and formal rules change?

This week studies social norms about breadwinning, caregiving, appropriate jobs, authority, mobility, and workplace behavior. It connects household bargaining to labor-market institutions, employer expectations, occupational status, worker voice, and local culture, while emphasizing how norms can operate through constraints, beliefs, coordination, identity, and strategic anticipation.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Norms and institutions become labor mechanisms when they change participation, search, occupational choice, bargaining, promotion, retention, and job quality.

### Week 6. Gendered Policy Incidence: Childcare, Leave, Taxation, Quotas, Transparency, and Protection

**Central question:** Which gendered labor-market margins do policies actually move, and who bears the incidence?

The sixth week gives policy its own architecture. Students study childcare, parental leave, tax and transfer design, quotas, pay transparency, equal-pay rules, anti-discrimination enforcement, scheduling and flexibility regulation, and workplace protections. The focus is not only whether a policy reduces a gap, but which margin moves, which firms respond, which workers benefit, and whether welfare improves.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** These policies operate through labor supply, hiring, wages, hours, retention, promotion, compliance, firm organization, and worker welfare.

### Week 7. Violence, Safety, Mobility, and Labor-Market Access

**Central question:** How do safety, harassment, violence, and mobility constraints shape labor-market access and worker welfare?

This week treats safety as a labor-market constraint rather than a separate social issue. Students study domestic violence, workplace harassment, commuting risk, public-space safety, occupational exposure, reporting incentives, and mobility barriers. The week asks how safety affects labor supply, job search, occupation choice, workplace exits, segregation, earnings, and welfare, and how to measure harms that are partly invisible in standard wage data.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Safety changes the feasible set of jobs, the disutility of work, the value of mobility, the pattern of sorting, and the welfare interpretation of wages and employment.

### Week 8. Comparative and Global Gender in Labor-Market Development

**Central question:** Which gender-and-labor mechanisms travel across countries, and which depend on development, institutions, norms, and labor-market structure?

The eighth week places the course in comparative perspective. Students examine female labor force participation, care regimes, labor demand, service-sector growth, informality, migration, development, social norms, legal regimes, family policy, and public employment across countries. The aim is to distinguish portable mechanisms from setting-specific institutional detail.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Comparative gender gaps reflect labor demand, labor supply, sectoral structure, formality, care infrastructure, wage-setting institutions, mobility, and policy design.

### Week 9. Frontier Methods, New Data, and Evolving Measurement of Gender in Labor Research

**Central question:** How are new data, designs, and measurement choices changing gender-and-labor research?

This methodological capstone studies matched employer-employee data, administrative panels, audits and field experiments, surveys and expectations data, time-use data, job postings, text-as-data, platform data, and measurement of gender identity and workplace climate. It also asks how to handle small-cell measurement, intersectional heterogeneity, privacy, changing identity categories, and the gap between legal categories and lived workplace treatment.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** New measurement changes what labor economists can see about jobs, firms, hours, authority, safety, identity, sorting, and welfare.

### Optional long lecture. LGBTQ+ Identities, Work, Discrimination, and Labor-Market Institutions

**Central question:** How do sexual orientation, gender identity, workplace climate, and legal institutions shape employment, earnings, hiring, benefits, and worker welfare?

This optional long lecture sits after the frontier methods week and immediately before final synthesis. It studies measurement of LGBTQ+ populations, employment and earnings differences, hiring discrimination, workplace disclosure, harassment and climate, benefit design, family recognition, legal protections, and the labor-market effects of institutions such as anti-discrimination laws and marriage recognition. The lecture is substantial by design: it broadens the course beyond male-female gaps while staying centered on labor outcomes and labor-market institutions.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** LGBTQ+ labor research studies employment, hiring, wages, benefits, workplace treatment, legal protection, identity disclosure, and welfare inside labor markets.

### Week 10. Synthesis, Frontier Questions, and Student Research Designs

**Central question:** What does a credible frontier research design on gender and labor markets look like?

The final week integrates the course into a research architecture. Students choose a labor-market object, specify a mechanism, define a comparison or counterfactual, identify the relevant worker, firm, household, or policy margin, assess identification threats, and state the welfare object. The week should surface open questions around care infrastructure, firm organization, authority, safety, policy incidence, measurement, and identity.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** The course succeeds when gender becomes a disciplined research lens for work, wages, firms, households, institutions, mobility, policy, and worker welfare.
