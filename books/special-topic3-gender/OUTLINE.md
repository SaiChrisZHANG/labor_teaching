# Gender Study

## Course role

Gender Study is a flagship 10-week labor-economics course on gendered inequality in work, households, organizations, and public policy.

## Weekly outline

### Week 1. Measuring gender gaps and framing the field

**Central question:** Which outcome gaps matter most for a serious labor-economics study of gender?

The opening week distinguishes wages, earnings, hours, occupations, promotions, wealth, and time use as separate but connected objects. It establishes why the course must move beyond one-summary-statistic accounts of gender inequality.

### Week 2. Households, care, and time allocation

**Central question:** How do unpaid care, household production, and family structure shape labor-market outcomes?

This week places labor supply inside time allocation and care responsibilities. The focus is on how household organization and care infrastructure influence participation, hours, occupation choice, and dynamic career paths.

### Week 3. Human capital, schooling, and career choice

**Central question:** How do educational choices, expectations, and skill investments generate later gender divergence?

Students study early pipeline mechanisms, field-of-study decisions, job ladders, and returns to flexible versus rigid careers. The aim is to connect pre-market and early-market choices to later observed labor inequality.

### Week 4. Hiring, promotions, and organizations

**Central question:** How do firms and organizations contribute to gender inequality over the career ladder?

The week moves inside firms to study evaluation, promotion, performance pay, workplace culture, and internal opportunity structure. This is the main bridge from household-centered explanations to organizational and personnel explanations.

### Week 5. Child penalties and lifecycle inequality

**Central question:** How do fertility and caregiving reshape earnings trajectories over the life cycle?

This week centers dynamic career effects after childbirth and caregiving transitions. Students analyze persistence, recovery, and cross-country heterogeneity in child penalties and related lifecycle inequalities.

### Week 6. Bargaining, norms, and intra-household power

**Central question:** How do bargaining power and social norms shape decisions inside households?

The focus is on collective household models, control over resources, and norm-mediated bargaining. The goal is to explain why similar households can generate different labor outcomes depending on power, beliefs, and social expectations.

### Week 7. Discrimination, bias, and evaluation

**Central question:** What counts as discrimination in gendered labor-market outcomes, and how can it be identified?

Students separate discrimination from sorting, anticipation, information, and institutional design. The week emphasizes audit evidence, evaluation bias, and the interpretive limits of reduced-form gap decompositions.

### Week 8. Occupations, sectors, entrepreneurship, and sorting

**Central question:** How do occupational structure and labor-market sorting generate gendered inequality?

The course studies segregation across occupations, industries, firms, and entrepreneurial pathways. The main issue is whether gender inequality is produced mostly by preferences, barriers, career design, or cumulative sorting.

### Week 9. Intersectionality, identity, and heterogeneity

**Central question:** How should labor economists study gender when it interacts with race, class, migration, and place?

This week broadens the analysis beyond average effects and emphasizes heterogeneity in both mechanisms and policy incidence. It is designed to prevent the course from collapsing into a one-dimensional account of gender inequality.

### Week 10. Policy design and frontier research

**Central question:** Which policies target the most important gendered labor-market margins, and where are the main research frontiers?

The final week integrates the course into a research and policy map spanning leave, childcare, workplace flexibility, pay transparency, anti-discrimination design, and organizational change. Students leave with a structured agenda for future work.
