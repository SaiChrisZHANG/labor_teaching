| Question | What students should do |
|---|---|
| What is the labor object? | State clearly whether the paper is about participation, hours, occupation, wages, formality, mobility, authority, care burden, or welfare. |
| What is the mechanism? | Lead with the mechanism, not the country: care constraint, legal barrier, public employment, migration regime, norm, service-sector demand, etc. |
| Why is this setting informative? | Explain the source of leverage: a reform, rare data, legal heterogeneity, historical shock, discontinuity, rollout, or unusually stark institutional contrast. |
| What comparison class matters? | Compare the setting to other relevant labor-market environments instead of presenting it as unique or exotic. |
| What should travel? | Specify which mechanism should generalize under similar institutional conditions. |
| What should not travel? | Be explicit about context variables that limit transportability. |
| What are common critiques? | Overclaiming external validity, weak mechanism articulation, country-as-case-study framing, and failure to connect to broad labor-market objects. |
| How to answer “why should I care if I do not know this country?” | Show how the paper teaches a labor mechanism that informs theory, measurement, or policy beyond the immediate setting. |
