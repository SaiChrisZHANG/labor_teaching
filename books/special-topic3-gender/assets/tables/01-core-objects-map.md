**Core objects of gender inequality in labor economics**

| Object | What is being measured? | Why it matters for labor economics | Why average wages alone miss it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Employment / participation | Whether people work at all | Captures attachment to market work and extensive-margin exclusion | Wage data miss nonparticipants |
| Hours / schedules | Time supplied in paid work and timing of work | Central for earnings, flexibility, and work-family tradeoffs | Equal wages can coexist with unequal hours |
| Wages | Pay per hour or comparable pay rate | Reveals pay-setting and marginal compensation | Misses hours, spells out of work, and career timing |
| Earnings | Annual or period income from work | Captures the combined effects of wages, hours, and participation | Can diverge sharply from hourly pay gaps |
| Occupations / tasks | Where workers are allocated | Reveals sorting, barriers, and job design differences | Same occupation shares do not imply equal jobs or authority |
| Firms / workplaces | Where people work and what firms pay | Shows sorting, bargaining, and workplace pay-setting | Aggregate wage gaps hide firm heterogeneity |
| Authority / promotion | Supervisory roles, rank, promotion speed | Connects gender to careers, power, and rent-sharing | Wage gaps can be small while authority gaps are large |
| Safety / harassment / risk | Exposure to violence, harassment, unsafe commutes, risky tasks | Changes labor supply, job acceptance, and welfare | Often omitted from standard earnings-based analyses |
| Care work / unpaid labor | Home production and caregiving | Shapes labor supply, career dynamics, and policy incidence | Outside-market work is invisible in earnings data |
| Welfare | Job quality, flexibility, security, autonomy | Links gender to utility, not just market returns | Equal earnings can mask unequal welfare |
