# Synthesis, Frontier Questions, And Student Research Designs

The final week is a research-launch capstone. The central question is: **what does a credible frontier research design on gender and labor markets look like?** Students should leave with a practical template for moving from a gendered labor-market fact to a research question, a comparison group, an empirical design, and a welfare or distributional interpretation.

The week is not a recap. Weeks 1 through 9 supplied objects, mechanisms, and methods. Week 10 asks students to use that material like researchers: identify what the literature has answered, state what remains open, explain why a setting is informative, and write a design that a labor-economics seminar would recognize as a contribution.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important

- Gender-and-labor research is now a cumulative field spanning households, firms, occupations, norms, policy incidence, safety, comparative institutions, measurement, identity, and welfare.
- The strongest projects begin with a labor-market object, not a broad social topic or a generic "gender gap."
- A credible research design names the mechanism, comparison group, labor margin, data source, identification strategy, welfare or distributional object, and contribution.
- The largest frontier openings are where important labor margins remain undermeasured: authority, job design, safety, climate, hidden harms, policy incidence, identity, intersectional heterogeneity, and portability.
- Interdisciplinary work is most valuable when it brings urban, political economy, health, technology, organizational, or safety questions back to work, wages, firms, households, mobility, institutions, and worker welfare.

:::

## Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 10, students should be able to:

1. summarize where the gender-and-labor literature currently stands without reducing it to a single wage-gap narrative;
2. distinguish major questions that are substantially answered from questions that remain open;
3. move from a gendered labor fact to a precise labor object, mechanism, counterfactual, data source, identification strategy, and welfare object;
4. diagnose the biggest threats to identification, interpretation, and external validity in candidate project ideas;
5. explain why a setting-specific gender-and-labor study is broadly relevant to labor economics;
6. identify frontier contribution margins in firms, care, policy, safety, identity, measurement, and interdisciplinary domains;
7. draft a short research memo that turns a topic into a feasible student project.

## Bridge

The course began by defining gender as a labor-economics object: not only wages, but also employment, hours, care, search, occupations, firms, authority, safety, mobility, policy incidence, and welfare. It then built a cumulative sequence. Household allocation and child penalties made lifecycle timing central [@klevenLandaisSogaard2019; @cortesPan2023]. Education and occupational sorting showed how early expectations and constraints can shape later careers. Firm-side weeks studied hiring, promotion, pay-setting, networks, evaluation, and authority [@cardCardosoKline2016; @cullenPerezTruglia2023]. Norms and institutions made beliefs, sanctions, bargaining, and law part of the labor mechanism [@bertrandKamenicaPan2015; @jayachandran2021social]. Policy weeks treated childcare, leave, tax rules, quotas, transparency, equal-pay law, and protection as interventions with incidence across workers, firms, and households [@olivettiPetrongolo2017; @bakerHalberstamKroftMasMessacar2023; @blundellDuchiniSimionTurrell2025]. Safety, violence, mobility, and reproductive autonomy expanded the feasible set and the welfare object [@folkeRickne2022]. Comparative and frontier-methods weeks asked which mechanisms travel and which data can actually see the relevant margin.

Week 10 turns that sequence into research design. The discipline is simple but demanding: start from the labor object, specify the mechanism, define the counterfactual, choose the margin and data, state the identifying variation, and say what readers should learn.

## Field Core

### Where The Literature Is Now

The field has made real progress. It has moved from documenting average gaps to studying mechanisms, dynamics, institutions, and welfare.

```{include} assets/tables/10-where-the-literature-is-and-where-it-is-going.md
```

Five achievements are especially important.

First, lifecycle work has made parenthood, care, and household allocation central to labor-market inequality. Child penalties are now a core empirical object rather than a family-economics side note [@klevenLandaisSogaard2019; @cortesPan2023].

Second, firm-side research has shown that gender gaps are produced inside organizations as well as before entry. Firm wage premia, pay-setting, evaluation, promotion ladders, schedules, networks, and policy compliance all matter [@cardCardosoKline2016; @cullenPerezTruglia2023; @blundellDuchiniSimionTurrell2025].

Third, norms and institutions are increasingly measured rather than invoked as residual explanations. The best work links norms, beliefs, legal rules, and bargaining environments to concrete labor margins such as search, hours, occupations, applications, mobility, and promotion [@bursztynFujiwaraPallais2017; @jayachandran2021social; @hylandDjankovGoldberg2024].

Fourth, policy evidence has shifted from "does the gap fall?" to incidence: which workers, households, firms, coworkers, applicants, and future career margins move when policy changes [@olivettiPetrongolo2017; @bakerHalberstamKroftMasMessacar2023].

Fifth, new data have made more of the labor-market process visible. Administrative panels, matched employer-employee data, audits, job-posting data, expectations surveys, time-use data, text, platform data, and identity or climate measures now let researchers observe margins that earlier work had to infer [@goldinRouse2000; @bertrandMullainathan2004; @fluchtmannGlennyHarmonMaibom2024; @kuhnShen2023; @eames2025].

### What Has Been Answered

Some questions are no longer open in the broad sense. Students should not write papers as if these facts are still unknown.

Children and care responsibilities are major contributors to persistent gender differences in earnings, hours, career continuity, and advancement. The exact mechanism varies by setting, but the lifecycle fact is central [@klevenLandaisSogaard2019; @cortesPan2023].

Firms matter. Sorting across firms, jobs, managers, teams, schedules, and promotion tracks cannot be treated as background controls. They are part of the allocation process [@cardCardosoKline2016].

Policy labels are insufficient. Childcare, leave, transparency, quotas, taxes, and protections operate through multiple margins. The same policy can change wages, hours, hiring, retention, sorting, reporting, compliance, and firm organization.

Gendered labor inequality is not only a high-income-country question. Development, informality, public employment, legal capacity, sectoral change, mobility, and norms shape which mechanisms appear in which outcomes [@olivettiPetrongolo2017; @jayachandran2021social; @hylandDjankovGoldberg2024].

Measurement is part of the estimand. Legal sex, self-identified gender, perceived gender, parental status, sexual orientation, disclosure, workplace climate, and administrative category are different empirical objects. A paper that uses one should not claim to measure all.

### What Remains Open

The field is vibrant because the answered questions have made sharper questions possible.

Large contribution margins remain in **firm organization**: who gets authority, which tasks are visible, how managers assign flexible work, how evaluation language changes promotion, how informal networks operate, and how firms respond when rules change.

Large margins remain in **hidden harms and welfare**: safety, harassment, mobility risk, reproductive autonomy, stigma, mental health, dignity, concealment, retaliation, and option value are often invisible in wage or employment data.

Large margins remain in **identity and measurement**: LGBTQ+ labor evidence, nonbinary and transgender measurement, intersectional heterogeneity, privacy-safe small-cell inference, and the gap between legal categories and workplace treatment are frontier problems rather than footnotes [@badgettCarpenterSansone2021lgbtqeconomics; @badgettCarpenterLeeSansone2024review; @eames2025].

Large margins remain in **policy incidence and equilibrium response**: a policy may close one measured gap while changing hiring, hours, promotion, outsourcing, compliance, reporting, or sorting. A good paper asks who bears the policy and which margin moved.

Large margins remain in **portability**: many estimates are internally credible but unclear about what travels. Students must state whether the portable object is the mechanism, the sign, the magnitude, the policy package, or only the measurement strategy.

### What Good Gender-And-Labor Questions Look Like

A good question is narrow enough to identify and broad enough to matter. It usually has four properties.

First, it names a labor object: employment, hours, wages, applications, promotions, retention, safety, mobility, firm assignment, benefits, authority, time use, or welfare.

Second, it names a mechanism that is gendered but not tautological. "Gender affects wages" is a fact. "Care constraints reduce access to high-premium firms" is a mechanism. "Evaluation systems assign lower potential ratings after schedule changes" is a mechanism. "Legal protection changes applications by reducing expected workplace risk" is a mechanism.

Third, it states the counterfactual. Compared with whom? Compared with what period, firm, household, policy regime, applicant signal, commuting environment, or legal setting?

Fourth, it explains why the result matters beyond the setting. A good paper does not ask readers to care because the country, policy, or group is intrinsically interesting. It shows what the setting teaches about labor markets.

The full design sequence is:

1. **Labor-market object.** What outcome or allocation problem is central?
2. **Mechanism.** Why should gender shape that object?
3. **Comparison group or counterfactual.** What would have happened under a different rule, timing, signal, firm, household constraint, or policy?
4. **Key labor margin.** Is the moving margin search, applications, hiring, hours, retention, promotion, firm sorting, job design, care time, safety, benefits, or welfare?
5. **Data source.** Which data observe that margin rather than a proxy several steps away?
6. **Identification strategy.** What variation distinguishes the mechanism from selection, omitted institutional detail, and reverse causality?
7. **Welfare or distributional object.** Whose welfare, risk, option value, time, income, authority, or dignity changes?
8. **Contribution.** What should readers update about labor economics?

### Identification Threats To Diagnose Early

Most weak proposals fail before estimation because the comparison group is vague. Common threats include:

- **Selection into the observed setting.** Wage data see workers with jobs. Firm data see workers hired by that firm. Application data see applicants, not all potential applicants.
- **Controls that are mechanisms.** Occupation, hours, tenure, firm, manager, and promotion can be outcomes of the gendered process.
- **Timing and anticipation.** Workers, firms, and households may adjust before childbirth, policy rollout, disclosure, reporting, or legal change.
- **Reporting versus prevalence.** Complaints, harassment reports, safety incidents, and survey disclosure measure reporting behavior as well as underlying exposure.
- **Firm response.** Policies can change pay, hiring, job design, outsourcing, sorting, or compliance intensity.
- **Measurement mismatch.** The data may observe legal category when the mechanism is perceived treatment, self-identity, disclosure, or workplace climate.
- **Underpowered heterogeneity.** Intersectional analysis is essential, but small cells require pre-specified aggregation, honest uncertainty, and privacy protection.

### Portability, External Validity, And Broad Relevance

Gender-and-labor research often uses specific policies, countries, firms, institutions, or data sources. Specificity is a strength when it identifies a mechanism. It becomes a weakness only when the paper cannot explain why the result should update readers outside the setting.

```{include} assets/tables/10-portability-and-relevance-map.md
```

A strong portability claim separates four objects:

**Internal validity.** Does the design credibly identify the effect or mechanism in the study setting?

**Transportability.** What conditions must hold for the same mechanism to operate elsewhere?

**External validity.** How should evidence from this setting update beliefs about other populations, firms, or policies?

**Policy transferability.** Can the specific policy or institutional design be adopted elsewhere with similar effects?

The safest broad-interest framing is mechanism-first: "This setting identifies how care constraints affect access to high-premium firms"; "this policy reveals firm pay-setting response"; "this audit isolates screening-stage treatment of an identity signal"; "this mobility shock shows how safety changes job search." Those claims travel better than "this country is interesting" or "this group has a gap."

### Current Literature Gaps And Contribution Margins

The largest current gaps are not places where no one has written papers. They are places where existing evidence does not yet identify the key labor margin.

**Authority and job design.** We know less about who receives authority, client contact, evaluation visibility, leadership sponsorship, schedule control, and low-promotability tasks than about wages.

**Care infrastructure and firm adjustment.** We know that care matters, but less about how firms redesign jobs, promotion clocks, flexibility, and evaluations when care constraints change.

**Safety and hidden costs.** We know safety and harassment shape work, but standard data often miss fear, avoidance, reporting costs, mental health, mobility, and long-run career effects.

**Identity, climate, and disclosure.** LGBTQ+ and gender-identity evidence is growing, but many datasets still cannot distinguish legal category, self-identity, perceived identity, disclosure, climate, and treatment.

**Policy incidence.** Many policy papers estimate a reduced-form effect on one outcome. More work can decompose workers, firms, households, applicants, coworkers, and nonparticipants.

**Global and comparative mechanisms.** More evidence is needed on informality, migration, public employment, enforcement capacity, care regimes, and institutional complementarities.

**Technology and task change.** AI, monitoring, algorithmic scheduling, remote work, platform rules, and task automation may change gendered work through job design, flexibility, evaluation, surveillance, and skill demand [@cazzanigaCelasunHidalgoMontoyaNolis2025].

### Interdisciplinary Directions

Interdisciplinary work is strongest when it keeps the labor object visible.

```{include} assets/tables/10-interdisciplinary-directions-map.md
```

**Urban and spatial economics.** Commuting, housing, public transit, neighborhood safety, childcare geography, and local amenities shape job access, search radius, hours, sorting, and welfare. A labor-focused urban project asks how space changes the feasible set of work.

**Political economy, representation, and institutions.** Representation, voice, lawmaking, enforcement, public employment, courts, unions, and bureaucratic capacity shape gendered labor outcomes. A labor-focused political-economy project asks which institution changes applications, hiring, protection, reporting, bargaining, or policy incidence.

**Health, reproduction, and well-being.** Contraception, abortion access, IVF, pregnancy health, menopause, disability, mental health, violence, and well-being affect timing, labor supply, job continuity, productivity, mobility, and welfare [@goldinKatzPowerPill2002; @lundborgPlugRasmussen2017; @arakiEtAl2025].

**Technology and AI.** Automation, algorithmic management, remote work, scheduling tools, monitoring, and AI exposure may change task allocation, flexibility, evaluation, and worker bargaining power. The labor question is which margin moves and for whom.

**Organizations and management.** Leadership, mentorship, evaluation systems, teams, task assignment, and workplace culture are central to authority gaps and promotion pipelines.

**Crime, safety, and mobility.** Violence, harassment, commuting risk, and public-space safety affect search, work location, occupation, retention, and welfare.

## Research Lab

### Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Design

The Week 10 lab is intentionally bounded. It is not a heavy replication package. Its purpose is to make students practice the final-week research move: compare project ideas, diagnose identification threats, match outcomes to methods, and produce a short research memo.

**Reproduce.** Students begin with a small deterministic idea bank and reproduce a structured design table. Each candidate idea is coded by labor object, mechanism, comparison group, data source, method, identification threat, portability claim, and welfare object.

**Diagnose.** Students score each idea on labor focus, mechanism clarity, counterfactual clarity, data fit, identification credibility, welfare relevance, and portability. The diagnostic step forces students to explain why a descriptive fact is not yet a research design.

**Design.** Students choose one idea and produce a one-page research memo. The memo must state the question, labor object, mechanism, comparison group, margin, data, strategy, threats, portability claim, welfare or distributional object, and contribution.

### Project-Generation Guidance

Students should generate projects by changing one component at a time.

- Start with an object: applications, hours, firm sorting, safety, promotion, benefits, time use, or welfare.
- Choose a mechanism: care, evaluation, norms, legal protection, information, mobility, climate, identity, or firm response.
- Find variation: policy timing, thresholds, randomized information, audit signals, event timing, worker moves, manager assignment, platform rule changes, survey experiments, or institutional contrasts.
- Ask what is missing: nonapplications, hidden harms, unpaid work, firm adjustment, identity measurement, reporting, or long-run careers.
- State the contribution in one sentence.

### Research Memo Output

The lab deliverable is a short memo with these headers:

1. Research question
2. Labor-market object
3. Mechanism
4. Comparison group or counterfactual
5. Key labor margin
6. Data source
7. Identification strategy
8. Main threats
9. Portability and broad relevance
10. Welfare or distributional object
11. Contribution

## Methods Box

:::{admonition} Methods Box: From Gendered Fact To Credible Design
:class: note

**Begin with the labor object.** A project about gender is not automatically a labor paper. Make the outcome or allocation margin explicit: work, hours, wages, firms, search, authority, safety, care, benefits, mobility, or welfare.

**Name the mechanism before the method.** The same outcome gap can come from care constraints, discrimination, sorting, job design, information, norms, legal protection, reporting, or firm response. The method should identify the mechanism, not decorate the topic.

**Define the counterfactual.** A claim needs a comparison: before versus after, treated versus untreated, exposed versus unexposed, randomized signal versus control, mover versus stayer, eligible versus ineligible, or comparable firms under different rules.

**Match data to the moving margin.** Do not use accepted jobs to infer all applications, complaints to infer prevalence, legal category to infer identity, or wages to infer welfare unless the limits are explicit.

**Diagnose selection and response.** Ask who enters the data, who is missing, how workers anticipate the event, how firms respond, and whether controls are themselves mechanisms.

**State the welfare object.** A labor paper can care about income, time, risk, dignity, safety, autonomy, authority, option value, or distribution. Say which object is observed and which remains interpretive.

**Make portability disciplined.** Identify what travels: the mechanism, the margin, the sign, the measurement strategy, or the policy design. Do not ask readers to infer relevance.

:::

## Reading Ladder And References

**Field framing.** Start with Goldin on gender convergence and job structure, Blau and Kahn on the gender wage gap, and Olivetti, Pan, and Petrongolo on the evolving labor-market field [@goldin2014; @blauKahn2017; @olivettiPanPetrongolo2024].

**Households, care, and lifecycle.** Use Kleven, Landais, and Sogaard for child penalties and Cortes and Pan for the broader synthesis of children and remaining gaps [@klevenLandaisSogaard2019; @cortesPan2023].

**Firms and workplace organization.** Use Card, Cardoso, and Kline for firm sorting and wage premia; Cullen and Perez-Truglia for informal networks; and pay-transparency papers for firm policy response [@cardCardosoKline2016; @cullenPerezTruglia2023; @bakerHalberstamKroftMasMessacar2023; @blundellDuchiniSimionTurrell2025].

**Norms, institutions, and policy incidence.** Use Bertrand, Kamenica, and Pan; Bursztyn, Fujiwara, and Pallais; Jayachandran; Hyland, Djankov, and Goldberg; and Olivetti and Petrongolo to connect norms, law, family policy, and labor outcomes [@bertrandKamenicaPan2015; @bursztynFujiwaraPallais2017; @jayachandran2021social; @hylandDjankovGoldberg2024; @olivettiPetrongolo2017].

**Methods and new data.** Use Goldin and Rouse, Bertrand and Mullainathan, Fluchtmann and coauthors, Kuhn and Shen, and Cook and coauthors for audits, application data, postings, and platform decompositions [@goldinRouse2000; @bertrandMullainathan2004; @fluchtmannGlennyHarmonMaibom2024; @kuhnShen2023; @cookDiamondHallListOyer2021].

**Identity, hidden harms, and frontier measurement.** Use Folke and Rickne for harassment and hidden harms, Badgett, Carpenter, and Sansone for LGBTQ+ economics, and Eames for nonbinary audit evidence [@folkeRickne2022; @badgettCarpenterSansone2021lgbtqeconomics; @badgettCarpenterLeeSansone2024review; @eames2025].

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. Choose one candidate project idea. Fill out the research-design template. Which cell is weakest?
2. Take one descriptive gendered labor fact and rewrite it as three possible mechanisms. What data would distinguish them?
3. Pick a policy paper from the course. Identify who bears the incidence: workers, firms, households, applicants, coworkers, or nonparticipants.
4. Write a portability paragraph for a country-specific or firm-specific study. What travels, and what does not?
5. Design a project on an interdisciplinary margin such as urban mobility, political representation, reproductive health, AI exposure, or workplace climate. State the labor object first.
6. Rewrite a proposed title so that it names the labor object, not only the population or policy.
