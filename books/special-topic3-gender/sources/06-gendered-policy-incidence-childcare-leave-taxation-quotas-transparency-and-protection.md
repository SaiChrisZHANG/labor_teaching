## Opening orientation

This week gives policy its own architecture. The goal is not to ask whether a policy “closes the gender gap,” but rather which labor-market margin it moves, who bears incidence, how firms respond, and whether welfare improves. The same policy can raise labor-force attachment, change wages through pay-setting, shift promotion or authority, reallocate care within households, or alter worker sorting across firms. Those distinctions are central for labor economists because the policies studied here are also empirical devices: they reveal which constraints and mechanisms generate gendered labor-market outcomes in the first place.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Gender-related policies move different margins: participation, hours, attachment, pay, promotion, sorting, care allocation, and welfare.
- Incidence can fall on workers, firms, coworkers, households, and future careers rather than only on the nominal beneficiary.
- Firm response, compliance, and implementation are part of the treatment, not background noise.
- Gap reduction is not the same as welfare improvement.
- Policy variation can be used to identify deeper labor-market mechanisms such as time constraints, second-earner elasticities, bargaining, promotion pipelines, and firm adjustment.
:::

## Bridge from earlier weeks

Weeks 2–5 showed that gender gaps emerge through household allocation, early choices, firm-side personnel processes, and norms/bargaining. This week uses policy variation to stress-test those channels. Childcare and leave policies speak to time constraints and household bargaining; tax design speaks to second-earner responses; quotas and transparency reveal promotion and pay-setting mechanisms; and equal-pay or protection rules illuminate how firms reorganize around regulation.

## Bridge

A useful way to organize policy research is to separate **policy domain** from **margin moved**. The same policy may look effective in one outcome and ineffective in another because it works through different margins.

- **Participation / hours / attachment**
- **Wages / bonuses / pay-setting**
- **Promotion / authority / leadership**
- **Occupational and firm sorting**
- **Fertility / care allocation / household bargaining**
- **Worker welfare / child welfare / firm profits / distribution**

A second useful distinction separates **nominal target** from **incidence**. Childcare subsidies may nominally target mothers, but incidence may partly fall on fathers, firms, or children. Pay transparency may nominally target information, but its effect may come through bonus compression or public scrutiny. Quotas may target representation at the top, but the relevant question is whether they change hiring pipelines, bargaining power, or downstream authority.

## Field Core

### A policy-incidence framework

Let {math}`y_{if}` denote a labor outcome for worker {math}`i` at firm {math}`f`, such as participation, hours, pay, promotion, or retention. Let {math}`P` denote a policy. A useful decomposition is

```{math}
:label: eq:policy-incidence
\Delta y_{if} = \underbrace{\Delta y_{if}^{\,\text{worker}}}_{\text{preferences, constraints, bargaining}}
+ \underbrace{\Delta y_{if}^{\,\text{firm}}}_{\text{pricing, hiring, promotion, schedules}}
+ \underbrace{\Delta y_{if}^{\,\text{household}}}_{\text{care allocation, fertility, time use}}
+ \underbrace{\Delta y_{if}^{\,\text{equilibrium}}}_{\text{sorting, wages, market adjustment}}.
```

Policy evaluation should therefore ask:
1. which component moved,
2. which population bore incidence,
3. whether the observed effect reflects a partial-equilibrium response or a broader reallocation.

A compact welfare object is

```{math}
:label: eq:policy-welfare
W = W^{\text{workers}} + W^{\text{households}} + W^{\text{firms}} + W^{\text{children}} ,
```

where the point is not to fully estimate welfare in every paper, but to force clarity about which welfare margins a policy plausibly affects and which are left out.

### Childcare and care-support policies

Childcare policies are often interpreted as labor-supply interventions, but the more interesting question is **whose time constraint they relax and with what downstream incidence**. Recent experimental evidence from Uganda shows that childcare subsidies increased labor supply and earnings for single mothers, while among couples the main labor-market response ran partly through fathers' salaried employment, highlighting that household incidence can differ sharply by family structure [@bjorvatnFerrisGulesciNasgowitzSomvilleVandewalle2025]. Global legal evidence on childcare laws similarly suggests that availability, affordability, and quality provisions can raise women's labor-force participation, but with substantial heterogeneity across settings [@anukriti2025].

Classic work on universal childcare in Quebec already taught the field that childcare can move maternal employment, child outcomes, and family welfare simultaneously, which is why a labor economist should not reduce the question to a participation coefficient [@bakerGruberMilligan2008]. In gender research, childcare policy is also a measurement device: if childcare strongly shifts labor supply, that reveals the quantitative role of time constraints and care complementarities in generating gender gaps.

### Parental leave and return-to-work policies

Parental leave policies are especially useful because they can move **attachment**, **job continuity**, **experience accumulation**, and **career penalties** in opposite directions. Short-duration paid leave in the United States raised labor-force attachment around birth [@byker2016], but recent long-run evidence from California's paid family leave finds little overall improvement in employment or earnings and even negative long-run effects for first-time mothers [@baileyBykerPatelRamnath2025]. The Austrian long-run policy history studied by Kleven and coauthors shows that large expansions in parental leave and childcare did remarkably little for gender convergence overall [@klevenLandaisPoschSteinhauerZweimueller2024].

For researchers, leave reforms help answer deeper questions:
- is the dominant margin job continuity or human-capital accumulation?
- are firms changing promotion standards or simply delaying career progression?
- do leave policies insure workers without altering the deeper gendered organization of care?

### Taxation, transfers, and second-earner incentives

Tax design is not just public finance; it is a gendered labor-market institution because many systems implicitly tax the secondary earner more heavily. Comparative work on joint taxation shows large predicted increases in married women’s hours under separate taxation [@bickFuchsSchuendeln2017]. Older optimal-tax and behavioral arguments also remind us that taxation changes not only labor supply but domestic allocation and bargaining [@alesinaIchinoKarabarbounis2011].

This literature is valuable for labor economists because taxes help identify:
- participation vs hours elasticities,
- entry vs intensive-margin responses,
- whether gendered labor supply is primarily a price effect or a household-bargaining effect,
- whether policy incidence differs between singles, couples, and parents.

### Quotas, representation, and leadership pipelines

Quota policies are especially informative because they help distinguish **top-level representation** from **broader labor-market spillovers**. Norway’s board quota dramatically changed board composition but did not generate large broad-based gains for the wider pool of highly qualified women [@bertrandBlackJensenLlerasMuney2019]. That finding is useful well beyond quotas: it shows that changing one node in an organizational hierarchy may not relax the deeper pipeline constraints beneath it.

For this course, quotas should therefore be read less as a narrow policy topic and more as a test of where gendered bottlenecks actually sit:
- top-board allocation,
- promotion ladders,
- management pipelines,
- or earlier educational and occupational sorting.

### Transparency, equal-pay rules, and legal protections

Pay transparency and equal-pay rules provide unusually clean windows into firm-side margins. Canadian salary-disclosure laws reduced the gender gap by roughly 20–40 percent [@bakerHalberstamKroftMasMessacar2023], while UK disclosure rules reduced the gap mainly by slowing men's pay growth and bonus progression [@blundellDuchiniSimionTurrell2025]. Those studies show that transparency works through **pay-setting**, **bonus growth**, and **public scrutiny**, not only through bargaining by women.

Equal-pay-for-similar-work rules are even sharper because they force firms to respond to within-firm pay regulation. New evidence from Chile shows that equal-pay rules can induce workforce segregation and may even widen gender gaps depending on local market composition [@gentilePassaroKojimaPakzadHurson2026]. This is a perfect example of why incidence and firm response must be central: a policy can target equality while shifting margins through segregation, job assignment, and composition.

Global legal evidence from the Women, Business and the Law framework is useful here because it makes formal legal discrimination itself a measurable labor-market institution [@hylandDjankovGoldberg2020]. Such data are not just descriptive; they create comparative variation that can motivate research on how formal legal protections do or do not map into labor outcomes.

### Workplace protections, reporting, and safety

Protections against harassment, dismissal, unsafe work, or inflexible schedules often matter because they alter the feasibility of work rather than simply wages. Reporting costs and retaliation risk are themselves labor-market frictions, which means the existence of formal protection is not enough if it is not credible or usable [@bayerBhalotraMiller2024]. In many settings, these protections also affect sorting: workers may leave, avoid, or never enter jobs or firms perceived as unsafe.

For labor researchers, workplace protections are valuable because they identify how safety, dignity, and reporting risk enter labor-market choice, retention, and wage compensation.

### A framework for policy incidence

Across these policy domains, the empirical question can be summarized in one template:

1. **Which margin moves?**
2. **Who bears incidence?**
3. **Which firms respond, and how?**
4. **What welfare object is relevant?**
5. **What labor-market mechanism does the policy reveal?**

## Research Lab

### Policy as an identification device

A strong gender-policy paper does not stop at “the policy worked.” It uses policy variation to identify a deeper labor mechanism.

- Childcare helps identify time constraints and the distribution of care incidence.
- Leave helps identify attachment, continuity, and dynamic career penalties.
- Tax reforms help identify second-earner elasticities and household bargaining.
- Quotas help identify where leadership bottlenecks sit.
- Transparency and equal-pay laws help identify firm pay-setting and promotion margins.
- Legal protections help identify how safety, compliance, and credibility affect labor-market participation and retention.

### Practical methods bridge

Some empirical settings and common methods:

- **childcare expansions / subsidies**
  - randomized experiments
  - staggered policy rollout
  - event studies
  - heterogeneity by family structure

- **leave reforms**
  - event studies around birth
  - regression discontinuity in eligibility or leave duration
  - long-run panel earnings trajectories

- **tax and transfer reforms**
  - bunching and nonlinear-budget-set methods
  - simulation / structural elasticities
  - cross-system policy comparisons

- **transparency / equal-pay laws**
  - threshold-based DID/event studies
  - matched employer-employee decompositions
  - bonus / promotion / occupation-margin decomposition

- **quotas**
  - reform timing
  - exposure by firm type or organizational layer
  - pipeline and spillover analysis

### Student project directions

Good projects in this area usually start from a policy but then ask a sharper labor question:
- Which margin is hidden beneath a reduced-form gap?
- Why does the same reform work differently across firms?
- Does the reform change worker choice, firm behavior, or both?
- Which populations gain on welfare, not just observed outcomes?
- What does the policy reveal about the deeper structure of gendered labor allocation?
