# Week 3 source pack: Education, Skills, Aspirations, and Occupational Sorting

## Central question
How do education, skills, aspirations, and early occupational choices generate later gendered labor-market outcomes?

## Week identity
This lecture studies **pre-market and early-career channels** behind later gendered labor-market inequality. The goal is to move from “women and men end up in different jobs” to a sharper decomposition of *why* that happens. The week should make students distinguish between two broad channels:

1. **Early-choice channel:** gender shapes schooling, field-of-study, training, career aspirations, competitiveness, beliefs, information, commuting tolerance, and perceived fit before or at labor-market entry;  
2. **Later-adjustment / inertia channel:** even conditional on similar early choices, gender can alter persistence, switching, promotion, family timing responses, and the ability to adjust away from early placements.

This is a labor-economics week because these channels shape labor supply, job access, career ladders, occupational segregation, skill prices, wage growth, and long-run welfare.

## Required narrative
The chapter should not present occupational sorting as either pure preference or pure constraint. It should teach students that:
- early educational and occupational choices are endogenous to incentives, information, aspirations, stereotypes, anticipated discrimination, and expected job design;
- later career paths are not frictionless: early matches, timing of family formation, mobility constraints, and workplace structures can create path dependence;
- gender matters both **before** and **after** labor-market entry.

The lecture should also include a short bridge to Week 5:
- norms and traditions help shape aspirations, feasible identities, and acceptable jobs;
- but Week 3 should not become a norms lecture;
- Week 5 will return to norms directly and systematically.

## Recommended chapter arc

### 1. Opening orientation
Why is occupational sorting one of the deepest labor-market mechanisms behind long-run gender inequality? Why is it not enough to say “women choose different jobs”?

### 2. Core points box
Must include concise bullet points on:
- early choices vs later adjustment;
- aspirations / information / competition / stereotypes / anticipated discrimination;
- early-career path dependence;
- bridge to norms without letting norms dominate the week.

### 3. Bridge
Connect to Week 2:
- household expectations, care constraints, and anticipated family roles can already influence pre-market choices and early career planning.

### 4. Field Core

#### A. Conceptual decomposition
Introduce a clean decomposition:
- pre-market choices and human-capital accumulation,
- entry sorting into fields/sectors/occupations,
- dynamic persistence and later adjustment.

A simple object is enough. For example, represent occupational outcome as a function of:
- acquired skills,
- expectations/aspirations,
- entry assignment,
- later switching or promotion frictions.

#### B. Early-choice channel
This should cover:
- field of study / track choice,
- aspirations and self-beliefs,
- competitiveness and willingness to enter high-variance or high-status paths,
- role models and mentors,
- teacher bias / stereotype transmission / information,
- anticipated discrimination affecting major or career choice.

Good anchors:
- Porter and Serra on female role models and choice of major;
- Buser, Niederle, and Oosterbeek on competitiveness and later track choice;
- Lavy and Sand on teacher bias and field choice;
- Lepage, Li, and Zafar on anticipated discrimination and major choice.

#### C. Later-adjustment / inertia channel
This section should be explicitly separate from the early-choice section.
It should cover:
- early-career placement and path dependence,
- career ladders and switching costs,
- flexibility constraints,
- timing of family formation and direct barriers to later adjustment,
- persistence of early mismatches.

Good anchors:
- Fadlon, Lyngse, and Nielsen on early-career setbacks and long-run labor/family trajectories;
- Bertrand, Goldin, and Katz as a bridge from early career structure to later gendered wage/career divergence.

#### D. Interaction between channels
Show students that these are not independent:
- anticipated future constraints may shape early choices;
- early choices may make later adjustment harder;
- perceived discrimination can affect both field choice and later persistence.

#### E. Brief bridge to Week 5
Explain briefly that some early-choice mechanisms are culturally mediated:
- aspirations,
- acceptable jobs,
- self-promotion,
- willingness to move or compete,
- expectations about care and family.
This should be short and clearly labeled as a bridge, not the main topic.

### 5. Research Lab
The lab should train students to see the difference between:
- interventions that shift early choices,
- evidence on direct later-career inertia,
- settings where both channels are bundled.

Suggested lab logic:
- **Primary anchor:** Porter and Serra (2020) for role models and major choice;
- **Challenge anchor:** Buser, Niederle, and Oosterbeek (2014) for competitiveness and track choice;
- **Optional extension:** Fadlon, Lyngse, and Nielsen (2022/2024 revision) or Lepage, Li, and Zafar (2025) for later persistence / anticipated discrimination.

### 6. Forward bridge
Connect forward to:
- Week 4 on firm-side hiring, promotion, pay-setting, and workplace authority;
- Week 5 on social norms, bargaining, and institutions shaping gendered work.

## Methods emphasis
This week should be especially clear about design:
- field experiments / information interventions,
- role-model interventions,
- teacher-bias designs,
- survey-expectation / anticipated-discrimination evidence,
- lifecycle administrative evidence,
- quasi-random early-career assignment,
- descriptive decomposition vs causal identification.

Students should come away able to say not only “what mechanisms matter,” but also “how researchers separately identify those mechanisms.”

## What to avoid
- Do not let norms dominate the lecture; save the fuller norms treatment for Week 5.
- Do not collapse later-adjustment frictions into the early-choice story.
- Do not reduce the lecture to STEM alone.
- Do not imply that occupational sorting is only a worker-preference phenomenon.
