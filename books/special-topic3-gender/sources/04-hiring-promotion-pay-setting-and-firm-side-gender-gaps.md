# Week 4 source pack: Hiring, Promotion, Pay-Setting, and Firm-Side Gender Gaps

## Central question
How do firms and internal labor markets produce gender differences in pay, promotion, authority, and retention?

## Week identity
This week moves inside organizations. The key claim is that firms are not passive containers of gender inequality. They actively shape who gets hired, which jobs they enter, who receives authority, who is mentored, who is evaluated as “high potential,” who gets low-promotability work, how wages are set, and who remains in the organization.

This is a labor-economics week because firms allocate jobs, schedules, rents, authority, performance metrics, and wage premiums. Gendered outcomes therefore emerge not only from worker characteristics and household constraints, but from the internal rules and practices of hiring, promotion, evaluation, bargaining, and retention.

## Required narrative
The chapter should separate three broad channels:

1. **Sorting into firms and jobs**  
   Workers sort into firms, occupations, and teams with different wage premiums, career ladders, and workplace conditions.

2. **Within-firm personnel processes**  
   Screening, referrals, networks, supervision, evaluation, promotion, and task allocation can create or amplify gender gaps even conditional on observed productivity.

3. **Firm-level pay-setting and bargaining**  
   Firms differ in wage policies, bargaining strategies, transparency, and compliance with equal-pay rules, which can generate gender gaps through both sorting and within-firm pay-setting.

Students should leave the lecture able to distinguish:
- hiring discrimination from downstream promotion inequality,
- sorting into high-premium firms from lower pay within the same firm,
- explicit pay rules from informal networks and subjective ratings,
- worker-side differences from firm-side mechanisms.

## Recommended chapter arc

### 1. Opening orientation
Why do firm-side processes matter so much for gender inequality, even after conditioning on education, occupation, and experience? Why is the “same worker, different firm” comparison often more informative than a broad wage-gap comparison?

### 2. Core points box
Must include concise bullet points on:
- firms as labor-market institutions;
- sorting vs within-firm treatment vs firm pay-setting;
- hiring, promotion, task allocation, authority, and retention as distinct margins;
- recent personnel-data work as a major frontier of gender-and-labor research.

### 3. Bridge
Connect back to Week 3:
- early educational and occupational sorting affects which firms and career tracks workers enter;
- but once workers enter firms, new processes generate additional divergence.
Connect forward:
- Week 5 will study norms and bargaining more directly;
- Week 6 will focus on gendered policy incidence and institutional interventions.

### 4. Field Core

#### A. Conceptual framework: where firm-side gaps come from
Introduce a simple decomposition:
- sorting into firms / jobs,
- treatment within firms,
- pay-setting / bargaining / transparency.
A simple object is enough. For example:
- gender gap in pay = sorting component + within-firm pay-setting component + dynamic promotion/retention component.

#### B. Hiring, screening, and entry
Cover:
- gender-blind vs non-blind screening,
- referrals and informal access channels,
- screening on fit, commitment, and future availability,
- how hiring margins differ from later promotion margins.
Good anchor:
- Goldin and Rouse on blind auditions as a classic hiring-screen design.

#### C. Promotion, authority, and leadership pipelines
Cover:
- subjective evaluation vs objective performance,
- “potential” vs realized performance,
- leadership pipelines,
- old-boy networks / social access to managers,
- authority and managerial tracks.
Good anchors:
- Cullen and Perez-Truglia on schmoozing and promotions;
- Benson, Li, and Shue on subjective potential ratings and promotion gaps.

#### D. Task allocation, supervision, and low-promotability work
Cover:
- committee work / service / low-promotability tasks,
- informal expectations about helping and office housework,
- supervision and subjective visibility,
- how task allocation affects later authority and promotion.
Good anchor:
- Babcock et al. on low-promotability tasks.

#### E. Firm pay-setting, bargaining, and wage premiums
Cover:
- firm wage premiums,
- sorting into high-premium firms,
- within-firm bargaining differences,
- ask salaries / posting / transparency as wage-setting institutions.
Good anchors:
- Card, Cardoso, and Kline on sorting and bargaining;
- pay transparency as a modern pay-setting reform.

#### F. Firm-facing policy and design frontier
Cover:
- pay transparency,
- equal-pay rules,
- how firms adapt to salary-history bans, pay disclosure, or equal-pay requirements,
- whether firms compress, segregate, or redesign jobs in response.
Good anchors:
- Blundell et al. on pay transparency;
- Gentile Passaro, Kojima, and Pakzad-Hurson on equal pay for similar work.

#### G. Retention, exit, and workplace conditions
Cover:
- retention and exit as firm-side margins,
- workplace conditions that affect who stays or leaves,
- harassment and authority as part of the cost of remaining in high-wage or male-dominated workplaces.
Good anchor:
- Folke and Rickne on sexual harassment, segregation, and pay inequality.

### 5. Research Lab
The lab should teach students to distinguish:
- clean hiring-screen designs,
- personnel-data promotion designs,
- matched employer-employee wage decompositions,
- policy-shock designs around transparency or equal-pay rules.

Suggested lab logic:
- **Primary anchor:** Cullen and Perez-Truglia (2023), because it is recent, well identified, and clearly within-firm;
- **Challenge anchor:** Goldin and Rouse (2000), because it is a classic screening design;
- **Optional extension:** Blundell et al. (2025) or Benson, Li, and Shue (2026), depending on feasible bounded replication.

### 6. Forward bridge
Connect forward to:
- Week 5 on social norms, bargaining, and institutions around gendered work;
- Week 6 on gendered policy incidence, transparency, and protection.

## Methods emphasis
This week should be especially clear about design:
- audit / hiring-screen designs,
- quasi-random manager or supervisor assignment,
- personnel records on ratings, promotions, and task allocation,
- matched employer-employee decompositions,
- firm-policy reforms (transparency, equal pay),
- survey-linked administrative evidence,
- retention/exit studies.

Students should come away able to say not only “what mechanisms matter,” but also “what data and design isolate each margin.”

## What to avoid
- Do not collapse firm-side mechanisms into a generic discrimination lecture.
- Do not treat firms as passive containers of worker-side inequality.
- Do not present sorting and within-firm treatment as the same object.
- Do not let transparency/equal-pay reforms crowd out the core personnel-economics structure.
