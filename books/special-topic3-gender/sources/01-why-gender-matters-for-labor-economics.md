# Week 1 source pack — Why Gender Matters for Labor Economics: Concepts, Measurement, and Framing

## Central question

What makes gender a core labor-economics object rather than a single wage-gap statistic?

## Week identity and intent

This opening week should do more than motivate the course in a generic way. Students usually already believe that gender matters. The real pedagogical task is to show *why gender is a first-order labor-economics field*:

1. it changes major labor outcomes, not just wages;
2. it forces measurement choices that alter empirical conclusions;
3. it links worker behavior, household behavior, firms, institutions, and policy;
4. it remains one of the most methodologically active and policy-relevant areas in labor economics.

The week should feel like a **field-defining lecture**, not a descriptive preface.

## Chapter-level goals

By the end of the lecture, students should be able to answer:

- What are the main labor-market objects of gender inequality?
- Why are average male-female gaps useful but incomplete?
- What are the major mechanism classes behind those gaps?
- Why is gender a productive area for labor research rather than a narrow applied niche?
- How do measurement choices discipline the research agenda?

## Boundaries

Keep the week labor-focused.

This is **not**:
- a generic introduction to gender studies,
- a one-dimensional wage-gap lecture,
- a week only about women,
- a week only about discrimination.

This **is**:
- an introduction to gender as an organizing axis of labor allocation, firm behavior, household bargaining, institutional incidence, and worker welfare.

## Core framing to preserve

The lecture should revolve around the idea that gender changes outcomes through multiple labor-market objects:

- employment and labor-force attachment,
- hours and schedules,
- wages and earnings,
- occupations and task allocation,
- firms and careers,
- authority and promotion,
- safety and exposure to risk,
- care work and unpaid time,
- welfare at and outside work.

A key theme is that the answer depends on *what object you measure* and *when you measure it*.

## Core points box content to support

The chapter should include a visible **Core points** box near the top. It should summarize points like:

- Gender is a labor-economics field because it changes work allocation, pay, authority, risk, and welfare.
- Average gaps are useful starting points but are not themselves mechanisms.
- The same gender gap can look different depending on whether we study wages, earnings, hours, promotions, occupations, firms, or lifecycle timing.
- Modern gender research is active because it sits at the intersection of labor supply, family, firms, institutions, and policy.

## Bridge section

Use the bridge to explain where this course sits relative to Labor I and Labor II:

- Labor I introduced worker-side allocation, family, discrimination, and policy incidence.
- Labor II introduced firms, wage-setting, labor-market power, regulation, and shocks.
- This course uses gender to connect those objects into one research field.

## Field Core structure

### 1. What are the objects of gender inequality?

Students should see that “the gender gap” is not one thing.

Possible categories:
- participation and attachment,
- hours and flexibility,
- hourly wages,
- annual earnings,
- occupational allocation,
- firm sorting,
- promotions / leadership / authority,
- safety / harassment / risk,
- unpaid care and welfare.

This section should make clear why the choice of outcome matters substantively.

### 2. Why description is necessary but incomplete

Introduce the idea that a simple gap:
```{math}
:label: eq:gender-gap-source
G_Y = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid F=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y \mid F=0]
```
is useful but cannot by itself distinguish among:
- labor supply differences,
- care constraints,
- firm-side wage-setting,
- occupational sorting,
- bargaining,
- discrimination,
- legal/institutional incidence.

This should set up the rest of the course.

### 3. Main mechanism classes

The lecture should preview the mechanism families the rest of the course will unpack:

- household and care mechanisms,
- education / skill / aspiration channels,
- firm-side hiring, promotion, and pay-setting,
- social norms and bargaining,
- legal and policy incidence,
- violence, safety, and mobility,
- institutions and global development.

This section should be a preview map, not a deep dive.

### 4. Why timing and lifecycle matter

Introduce a dynamic object:
```{math}
:label: eq:child-penalty-source
Y_{it} = \sum_{k \neq -1}\beta_k \mathbf{1}\{t-T_i=k\}+\alpha_i+\gamma_t+\varepsilon_{it}
```
to show that many gender gaps emerge through lifecycle events, especially family formation.

The point is not to teach event-study econometrics in detail, but to make clear that a cross-section can conceal dynamic processes.

### 5. Why firms and authority matter

Introduce a framing object like:
```{math}
:label: eq:firm-gap-source
Y_{if}=\alpha_i+\psi_f+\delta F_i+\varepsilon_{if}
```
to show that gender inequality is not only about worker traits or household choices; firms, career ladders, and authority structures matter too.

This should preview later weeks on hiring, promotion, pay-setting, and workplace authority.

### 6. Why labor researchers should invest in this field

This is the philosophical layer the user explicitly wants.

Make the case systematically:

- the remaining gender gaps are quantitatively large and policy-relevant;
- gender is a route into core labor questions, not a side application;
- it connects labor supply, firms, contracts, occupations, and institutions;
- it is methodologically innovative;
- it has rich linked-data environments and growing frontier work;
- it is central to welfare, inequality, and development.

This should be explicit enough that students can defend *why this field matters* to skeptical audiences.

## Research Lab framing

The research lab should not just say “here are some papers.”

It should teach students to ask:
- What outcome is the paper measuring?
- Is the paper descriptive, causal, or decomposition-based?
- What mechanism is being claimed?
- What level is doing the work: worker, household, firm, institution, or policy?
- Why should a labor economist care?

## Reading ladder guidance

The reading ladder should be real and cited, but not too long.

Suggested tiers:
- framing / overview,
- one lifecycle paper,
- one firm-side paper,
- one institutional/global paper.

## Lab guidance

Preferred lab logic:
- **Primary anchor:** [@hylandDjankovGoldberg2020genderedlaws]
- **Challenge anchor:** [@klevenLandaisSogaard2019children]
- **Optional extension:** [@bertrandGoldinKatz2010dynamics]

This gives students:
- a global institutional dataset / cross-country descriptive-causal hybrid setting,
- a lifecycle event-study benchmark,
- a firm/career-style professional trajectory example.

## Slide guidance

The eventual slide deck should do the following:
1. ask the central question,
2. explain why gender is a labor-economics field,
3. define the main outcome objects,
4. distinguish description from mechanism,
5. highlight lifecycle timing,
6. preview firms, authority, and institutions,
7. explain why this is a high-return research field,
8. bridge to Week 2.

## Things to avoid

- treating the week as only a wage-gap lecture,
- treating gender as equivalent to women only,
- implying that all gender research is discrimination research,
- letting the week become too generic or too normative without labor objects,
- making the philosophy layer vague.

## Desired end state

By the end of the chapter, students should feel:
- they understand what the field studies,
- they know why simple gap statistics are only a starting point,
- they see why the field is central to labor economics,
- they are curious to unpack the mechanisms in later weeks.
