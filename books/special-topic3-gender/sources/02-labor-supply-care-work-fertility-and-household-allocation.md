# Week 2. Labor Supply, Care Work, Fertility, and Household Allocation

## Week identity

**Central question:** How do care responsibilities, fertility, and household allocation shape labor supply and earnings trajectories?

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Household allocation is labor economics because care work, fertility, bargaining, and control over resources determine who works, how much they work, which jobs are feasible, and how earnings evolve over the lifecycle.

## Core design goal

This week should treat market work as embedded in the household and lifecycle. The lecture is **not** only about mothers reducing labor supply after children. It should connect:
- labor supply,
- unpaid care and time allocation,
- fertility timing,
- intrahousehold bargaining and control over resources,
- dynamic earnings penalties,
- job feasibility and job quality,
- childcare and policy design,
- and a foreshadowing bridge to **Week 5 on social norms**, since household specialization and domestic responsibility allocation are partly sustained by gender norms, traditions, and beliefs.

The chapter should make clear that:
1. description of child penalties or care gaps is not a mechanism by itself;
2. household responses reflect prices, constraints, bargaining, policy, and norms simultaneously;
3. some of the most important modern gender inequalities are generated through dynamic household allocation.

## What the chapter should do

### Opening orientation
The opening should explain why a labor economist should care about domestic work and fertility:
- they generate participation effects,
- hours effects,
- occupation and firm-choice effects,
- wage and promotion effects,
- dynamic career penalties,
- and heterogeneous responses across household structure, class, and institutional setting.

### Core points box
The required **Core points** box should summarize at least the following:
- care work is an allocation problem, not a residual category;
- fertility and childcare generate dynamic labor-supply and earnings effects;
- household models differ in whether they treat resources and preferences as pooled or bargained;
- modern gender gaps are often mediated by children and care responsibilities;
- household specialization is partly shaped by norms, foreshadowing Week 5.

## Required section structure

### 1. Bridge
Use this section to move from Week 1 into a household/lifecycle setting.

Must distinguish:
- individual labor supply vs household allocation,
- static vs dynamic effects,
- descriptive gaps vs mechanisms.

### 2. Field Core

The Field Core should be organized into **five blocks**.

#### Block A. Household production and time allocation
Students should see the household as a production unit.

Must include:
- market work vs unpaid care,
- time constraints,
- household production,
- specialization and comparative advantage,
- why gender-neutral comparative advantage is often insufficient.

A simple Becker-style allocation-of-time object is useful, but keep the chapter labor-focused.

#### Block B. Fertility, timing, and dynamic labor supply
This section should cover:
- fertility as a labor-market shock,
- timing and spacing,
- short-run vs long-run labor-supply effects,
- dynamic earnings trajectories,
- how fertility changes not only participation/hours but also occupations, firms, promotion paths, and job quality.

Use child penalties as the main modern reduced-form object.

#### Block C. Intrahousehold bargaining and control over resources
This section should explain:
- why unitary models can be insufficient,
- what collective/bargaining approaches buy us,
- why control over resources matters for labor supply and care allocation,
- how nonlabor income, transfers, and bargaining position affect behavior.

This section does **not** need to become a full theory lecture, but students should leave knowing the conceptual distinction between pooling and bargaining.

#### Block D. Childcare, policy, and constraints
This section should cover:
- availability and affordability of childcare,
- time constraints,
- single-parent vs coupled households,
- household-level reallocation,
- when childcare changes mothers’ labor supply directly and when it shifts household specialization.

This is a good place for recent frontier evidence.

#### Block E. Norms bridge to Week 5
This should be a short but explicit bridge:
- household specialization is not only prices and technology,
- norms about caregiving, acceptable work, motherhood, and relative earnings matter,
- these social norms will be the object of Week 5.

The point is **not** to teach the norms week early.
The point is to flag that domestic responsibility allocation partly stems from gender norms and traditions.

### 3. Research Lab
The Research Lab should train students to think like researchers, not just readers.

It should:
- separate descriptive child-penalty event studies from causal fertility designs;
- distinguish fertility shocks, childcare interventions, bargaining shifts, and norm shocks;
- emphasize what each empirical design identifies;
- ask how one would tell apart prices, bargaining, and norms.

## Methods and measurement requirements

The chapter should explicitly distinguish:
- child penalties vs fertility effects,
- reduced-form event studies vs causal fertility IV designs,
- household bargaining shifts vs pure price/income effects,
- care-time reallocation vs labor-market reallocation,
- extensive margin vs hours vs earnings vs occupation/firm margins.

It should also mention key empirical objects students should know:
- event studies around childbirth,
- IVF or infertility-based fertility designs,
- childcare-policy experiments or quasi-experiments,
- expenditure/control-over-resources evidence,
- time-use data vs earnings/admin data.
