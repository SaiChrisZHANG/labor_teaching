# Week 5 source pack: Social Norms, Bargaining, and Institutions Shaping Gendered Work

## Central question
How do norms, bargaining power, and institutions shape gendered work even when prices and formal rules change?

## Week identity
This week studies gendered work through the joint lens of **informal institutions** (norms, expectations, sanctions, conventions) and **formal institutions** (laws, workplace rules, family policy, access to finance, contract design). The goal is not to treat norms as a residual explanation, but to organize them as identifiable labor-market mechanisms.

This is a labor-economics week because norms and institutions change participation, hours, specialization, education and labor-market investments, search, commuting, bargaining, promotion, retention, and job quality. The lecture should make clear that gendered work persists not simply because of prices or legal text, but because norms operate through beliefs, sanctions, coordination, household threat points, and the interaction between informal expectations and formal labor-market rules.

This week should be **longer and more literature-rich** than a standard module. It should use at least **15 high-quality papers** and should be organized by **mechanism and empirical strategy**, not by a loose list of specific norms.

## Required narrative
The chapter should present a mechanism-first taxonomy of how norms and institutions matter:

1. **Norms as preference or identity shifters**  
   People may experience utility or disutility from violating gendered expectations about work, breadwinning, caregiving, authority, or competition.

2. **Norms as beliefs about sanctions or acceptability**  
   Workers, spouses, families, employers, and communities may believe certain jobs, work hours, commuting patterns, or leadership roles are socially acceptable or unacceptable.

3. **Norms as coordination devices**  
   Marriage markets, household specialization, workplace practices, and local labor markets may settle on gendered equilibria even when many individuals privately support more equal arrangements.

4. **Norms as bargaining modifiers**  
   Gender norms can alter outside options, threat points, perceived obligations, control of resources, and willingness to negotiate inside households and workplaces.

5. **Norms embedded in institutions**  
   Formal rules about work, mobility, pay, parenthood, and legal rights may either reinforce or weaken informal norms. The key task is to explain interaction, not to treat law and culture as separate worlds.

Students should leave the lecture able to distinguish:
- norms from prices,
- norms from pure legal barriers,
- norms from selection,
- bargaining channels from pure preferences,
- and formal institutions from informal ones.

## Recommended chapter arc

### 1. Opening orientation
Why do gendered work patterns survive even when women’s education rises, laws improve, and labor demand expands? Why do similar price changes produce different labor-supply and career responses across households, regions, and countries?

### 2. Core points box
Must include concise bullet points on:
- formal vs informal institutions;
- norms as labor mechanisms rather than residual explanations;
- bargaining and threat points as channels through which norms matter;
- why the same norm can affect multiple labor margins;
- why empirical work must separate norms from prices, laws, and selection.

### 3. Bridge
Connect back to earlier weeks:
- Week 2: domestic responsibility allocation and child penalties are partly shaped by norms and bargaining, not just childcare technology or wages;
- Week 3: aspirations, major choice, and early career paths are affected by anticipated marriage-market and work-family norms;
- Week 4: firm-side outcomes depend on the worker norms and bargaining environment that shape who enters, negotiates, stays, and competes.

Connect forward:
- Week 6 will move to **gendered policy incidence** and ask how childcare, leave, taxation, transparency, safety, and protection interact with the norms and bargaining channels developed here.

### 4. Field Core

#### A. What counts as a norm? What counts as an institution?
Introduce a clean distinction:
- **formal institutions**: laws, workplace rules, contract structures, parental leave, mobility restrictions, financial control, legal rights;
- **informal institutions**: breadwinner norms, caregiving norms, norms about acceptable jobs, status/authority norms, mobility norms, competition norms, self-promotion norms.

Stress that the empirical problem is often to show whether a labor-market outcome is driven by:
- prices,
- legal constraints,
- social expectations,
- bargaining,
- or interaction among them.

#### B. A brief bargaining framework with a gender margin
Include one compact bargaining subsection.
A simple object is enough. For example:
- household utility or surplus depends on market income, home production, and a norm term;
- outside options or threat points differ because social sanctions, legal institutions, or asset control differ;
- female labor supply, specialization, or career investment can then differ even when wages move favorably.

The goal is not a long theory lecture. The goal is to show students how a norm can enter:
- utility,
- the feasible set,
- the outside option,
- or beliefs about future payoffs.

#### C. Methodological taxonomy of norms in labor research
Organize the evidence by mechanism rather than by anecdote:

1. **Marriage/breadwinner/care norms**  
   Examples: relative income, maternal employment, marriage-market incentives, fertility/work beliefs.

2. **Competition/authority/self-promotion norms**  
   Examples: competitive workplaces, overwork norms, leadership expectations.

3. **Mobility/commuting/acceptable-job norms**  
   Examples: willingness to commute, which jobs are socially acceptable, search constraints.

4. **Financial control and bargaining institutions**  
   Examples: bank accounts, direct deposit, legal/contractual changes that shift control within households.

5. **Formal legal institutions interacting with norms**  
   Examples: gendered laws, workplace rights, legal permissions, and how law can lag or reshape social expectations.

#### D. Evidence across labor-market margins
Use a wide but coherent set of papers to show how norms enter:
- participation,
- hours and specialization,
- education and labor-market investments,
- search and commuting,
- bargaining and control over resources,
- workplace competition and career progression,
- policy incidence.

The emphasis should be methodological: for each paper, what is the labor margin and what is the identifying variation?

#### E. Formal institutions and the persistence / relaxation of norms
Explain how laws and formal institutions matter in two directions:
- they can codify norms and make them harder to escape;
- they can also relax norms by changing feasibility, information, or bargaining power.

A strong chapter will not treat “culture” and “law” as competing explanations. It will show how they interact.

### 5. Research Lab
The lab should teach students how to investigate norms without treating them as unobserved magic.

The lab logic should highlight:
- migrant / ancestry designs,
- norm-misperception interventions,
- household bargaining interventions,
- commuting/search revealed-preference designs,
- legal-institution interactions,
- and the difference between describing a norm and identifying its labor-market consequences.

Suggested lab logic:
- **Primary anchor:** Bursztyn, González, and Yanagizawa-Drott (2020), because it is a clean norms-and-job-search intervention;
- **Challenge anchor:** Bertrand, Kamenica, and Pan (2015), because it connects norms to earnings, marriage, labor supply, and home production;
- **Optional extension:** Field et al. (2021) or Le Barbanchon, Rathelot, and Roulet (2021), depending on feasible bounded replication.

### 6. Forward bridge
Connect forward to Week 6:
- Week 5 treats norms as rules of conduct, bargaining environment, and informal institutions;
- Week 6 will study how childcare, leave, taxation, transparency, safety, and protection interact with gendered work through policy incidence.

## Methods emphasis
This week should be especially clear about design:
- migrant / second-generation / ancestry designs,
- historical-origin designs,
- norm-misperception interventions,
- household-control or bargaining interventions,
- labor-market field experiments on job entry and competition,
- commuting/job-search revealed-preference evidence,
- cross-country legal-institutions evidence.

Students should come away able to say not only **which norms matter**, but **how labor economists identify them** and **why one empirical strategy supports one mechanism rather than another**.

## What to avoid
- Do not let the lecture become a bucket of specific norms.
- Do not reduce norms to a single male-breadwinner story.
- Do not collapse bargaining into household labor supply from Week 2.
- Do not let formal institutions crowd out the norms channel, or vice versa.
- Do not foreshadow a nonexistent identity/hierarchy lecture.
- Do not present norms as untestable residuals.
