# Optional long lecture. LGBTQ+ Identities, Work, Discrimination, and Labor-Market Institutions

## Opening orientation

This optional long lecture broadens the course beyond male–female comparisons while staying squarely inside labor economics. The key question is not only whether LGBTQ+ workers experience different labor-market outcomes, but how measurement, disclosure, workplace climate, legal recognition, benefit design, and labor-market institutions jointly shape employment, hiring, earnings, benefits, and worker welfare. The point of the lecture is to show students that this is no longer a tiny side literature: it now has a real review article, multiple high-quality audit studies, administrative designs, policy/event-study work, and a set of still-open questions that are especially promising for new researchers.

## Core points

:::{admonition} Core points
- LGBTQ+ labor research is a labor-economics field because it studies hiring, wages, benefits, workplace treatment, disclosure, legal protection, job sorting, and worker welfare.
- Measurement is central: sexual orientation, gender identity, disclosure, and legal status are related but distinct empirical objects.
- High-quality research now covers hiring discrimination, within-job treatment, administrative earnings measurement, legal recognition, and employer benefit design.
- The biggest research opportunities come from linking identity and law to firm behavior, hidden harms, dynamic careers, and new data environments.
:::

## Bridge

Weeks 1–9 of the gender course established a broad set of labor-market margins: labor supply, care, sorting, firm-side pay and promotion, norms, policy incidence, safety, global comparison, and frontier methods. This optional lecture asks how those margins look when the relevant identities are sexual orientation and gender identity rather than only male–female difference. It also provides an important conceptual bridge to the final synthesis week: what does a credible, labor-focused research design look like in an area where measurement is difficult, legal categories move, and lived workplace treatment is often partly hidden?

## Field Core

### 1. Why LGBTQ+ labor research belongs in labor economics

The most useful framing paper is the new economics review by Badgett, Carpenter, Lee, and Sansone, which shows that LGBTQ+ economics now spans family formation, human-capital investments, hiring, earnings, benefits, and discrimination [@badgettCarpenterLeeSansone2024review]. The earlier JEP overview makes the same point more compactly and is especially good for teaching because it shows how economists moved from documenting gaps to studying law, marriage, benefits, and labor-market behavior [@badgettCarpenterSansone2021lgbtqeconomics].

The main labor objects in this lecture are:
- employment and participation
- wages and earnings
- occupation and firm sorting
- hiring and callback rates
- benefits and employer-sponsored insurance
- workplace climate, disclosure, and harassment
- legal protection and family recognition
- worker welfare beyond observed pay

See [](#tbl-lgbtq-measurement) and [](#tbl-lgbtq-institutions).

### 2. Measurement: identity, disclosure, and the problem of population definition

The first challenge is measurement. Sexual orientation, gender identity, and disclosure are not the same thing. Some datasets identify same-sex couples but not individual orientation. Some surveys include direct questions on sexual orientation but not gender identity. Some direct questions on gender identity are recent and vulnerable to small-cell issues, under-reporting, or strategic misreporting. Administrative designs can be high-quality for some questions, but they may measure legal or administrative transitions rather than the whole LGBTQ+ population.

This is not a side issue. It changes what parameter a paper identifies. For example:
- couple-based census identification is good for analyzing partnered same-sex households, but not all LGBTQ+ individuals;
- direct identity questions may better capture self-identification but often yield smaller samples;
- administrative gender-marker or name-change designs may identify a high-fidelity subgroup of transgender workers, but not all trans or nonbinary workers.

The transgender earnings paper by Carpenter and Lee is a useful example because it exploits administrative gender-marker and name-change data to identify a very large, high-fidelity sample of transgender workers and then compares workers before and after gender affirmation and relative to siblings and coworkers [@carpenterLee2024transgenderearnings]. That design makes the measurement problem visible instead of hiding it.

### 3. Employment, earnings, occupation, and benefits

The broad empirical facts are now well reviewed in [@badgettCarpenterLeeSansone2024review] and [@badgettCarpenterSansone2021lgbtqeconomics]. But broad averages can hide large heterogeneity across men, women, trans workers, disclosure regimes, occupations, and institutional settings.

Three labor margins are especially useful for students:

**Earnings and employment**
The labor-market effects of sexual orientation and gender identity are not symmetric across groups. Gay men, lesbians, bisexual workers, transgender workers, and nonbinary workers may face different combinations of discrimination, occupational sorting, selection, and compensation. The administrative transgender earnings paper is especially valuable here because it shows how one can move beyond small surveys to estimate within-person, sibling, and coworker comparisons [@carpenterLee2024transgenderearnings].

**Employer-sponsored benefits**
Benefits are labor-market objects. Same-sex domestic partner benefits, spousal insurance, and recognition of partners affect both worker welfare and labor supply/incentives. Carpenter, Postolek, and Warman show that employers changed same-sex domestic partner benefits after nationwide marriage equality, making employer benefit design part of the institutional story rather than just background law [@carpenterPostolekWarman2024sameSexBenefits]. Carpenter et al. also show that public policy can change employer-sponsored insurance access for individuals in same-sex couples [@carpenterGonzalesMckaySansone2021aca].

**Family recognition and labor supply**
Same-sex marriage recognition changes labor supply, employment, and specialization margins, which makes it a labor-economics topic rather than only a family-law topic. Sansone’s “Pink Work” is the cleanest labor-market anchor here because it studies how marriage equality affected employment and discrimination-relevant outcomes among same-sex couples [@sansone2019pinkwork].

### 4. Hiring discrimination and access to jobs

Hiring discrimination is one of the strongest parts of the field because it uses designs labor economists already know how to evaluate.

Tilcsik’s U.S. field experiment remains the canonical anchor for openly gay male hiring discrimination [@tilcsik2011prideprejudice]. Weichselbaumer’s lesbian hiring experiment is a useful complement because it highlights how sexual orientation and gender presentation can jointly matter [@weichselbaumer2003sexualorientation]. Granberg, Andersson, and Ahmed provide one of the first field experiments on hiring discrimination against transgender applicants [@granbergAnderssonAhmed2020transhiring].

A second frontier is the move from small correspondence designs to large-employer audits. Kline, Rose, and Walters show how audit data can be used to study whether discrimination is concentrated at particular firms rather than spread evenly across the market, and their design includes sexual orientation and gender identity dimensions starting in the post-Bostock waves [@klineRoseWalters2021systemic].

The big labor-economics lesson is that we must separate:
- whether access to jobs differs at the hiring margin,
- whether differences are concentrated in a subset of firms,
- whether discrimination changes sorting into firms and occupations,
- and whether legal protection changes those margins.

### 5. Workplace climate, disclosure, and hidden harms

Not all labor-market harms show up in wages. Disclosure, climate, and harassment matter because they affect whether workers can remain in jobs, seek promotion, use benefits, reveal identity, or accept particular work environments.

The field is still weaker here than on hiring discrimination, but the research opportunities are large. One strand measures views toward transgender workers and support for employment non-discrimination, which is relevant because perceived workplace climate can differ sharply from actual support [@aksoyCarpenterSansone2025understanding]. Another strand uses organizational and survey data to study how climate, inclusion, and openness relate to outcomes like job satisfaction, retention, or willingness to disclose [@badgettCarpenterSansone2021lgbtqeconomics].

This is one area where the lecture should explicitly tell students that the literature is thinner than it should be. Climate, harassment, disclosure, and identity concealment are labor-market objects, but they are hard to observe in standard employer payroll data. That makes them attractive topics for new data and mixed-method designs.

### 6. Legal institutions: anti-discrimination protection, marriage, and benefits

The legal-institutions block should not become a policy-shop. The labor question is: how do legal protections and recognition alter labor-market behavior, employer choices, and worker welfare?

Three legal channels matter most:

**Anti-discrimination protection**
Legal protection can change employer behavior directly, but it can also change worker beliefs, willingness to apply, or expectations of treatment. The field still needs more clean labor-market evidence on the mechanisms through which anti-discrimination law operates.

**Marriage and family recognition**
Marriage recognition affects labor supply, specialization, access to family-linked benefits, and social legitimacy. That is why [@sansone2019pinkwork] is so useful for the course.

**Employer benefit design**
Employer-sponsored insurance and domestic-partner benefits are labor-market institutions. Papers like [@carpenterPostolekWarman2024sameSexBenefits] and [@carpenterGonzalesMckaySansone2021aca] show that the benefit margin is one of the clearest places where family recognition and labor institutions interact.

### 7. Transgender and nonbinary workers: measurement frontier and emerging evidence

This is probably the fastest-growing empirical frontier in the field. The big questions concern:
- hiring discrimination,
- earnings trajectories,
- administrative and legal affirmation,
- benefit access,
- workplace climate,
- interaction between legal protection and employer practices.

The strongest current anchors are [@granbergAnderssonAhmed2020transhiring], [@aksoyCarpenterSansone2025understanding], and [@carpenterLee2024transgenderearnings]. Together they show three different but complementary strategies:
- correspondence experiments for hiring,
- survey/list methods for stigma and support,
- administrative linkage for earnings and careers.

The nonbinary frontier is even newer. There is now emerging experimental work on pronoun disclosure and hiring, but this is exactly the kind of frontier where replication, measurement, and design questions remain open [@eames2025nonbinary].

## Research Lab

### Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer

**Primary anchor:** [@tilcsik2011prideprejudice]  
Use a hiring-discrimination design to study how identity signaling enters callback rates, and what is actually identified by the field experiment.

**Challenge anchors:** [@granbergAnderssonAhmed2020transhiring]; [@sansone2019pinkwork]  
Contrast hiring discrimination with policy/event-study evidence on marriage recognition and labor supply. The goal is to see how different designs answer different margins.

**Optional extension anchors:** [@carpenterLee2024transgenderearnings]; [@klineRoseWalters2021systemic]; [@carpenterPostolekWarman2024sameSexBenefits]  
These give three especially useful frontier designs: administrative identity linkage, large-employer audit data, and employer benefit response.

### Research opportunities

This is one of the clearest growth areas in labor economics because several contribution margins remain underdeveloped:
- linking hiring discrimination to later firm sorting and career progression,
- measuring hidden harms such as disclosure costs and climate effects,
- understanding how legal protection affects worker beliefs and application behavior,
- improving measurement for transgender and nonbinary workers without collapsing identity categories,
- studying benefits, leave, family recognition, and insurance as labor-market institutions,
- building credible evidence outside the U.S. and a handful of Northern European settings,
- integrating intersectionality without losing identification clarity.

### Reading ladder

Start with the field reviews [@badgettCarpenterLeeSansone2024review; @badgettCarpenterSansone2021lgbtqeconomics]. Then read the hiring papers [@tilcsik2011prideprejudice; @granbergAnderssonAhmed2020transhiring]. Then move to legal and employer-response papers [@sansone2019pinkwork; @carpenterPostolekWarman2024sameSexBenefits; @carpenterGonzalesMckaySansone2021aca]. Finish with the administrative and frontier evidence [@carpenterLee2024transgenderearnings; @klineRoseWalters2021systemic; @eames2025nonbinary].
