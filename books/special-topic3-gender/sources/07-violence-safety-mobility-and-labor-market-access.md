# Week 7 source pack: Violence, Safety, Mobility, and Labor-Market Access

## Week identity

- Course: Gender Study
- Week: 7
- Canonical chapter title: **Violence, Safety, Mobility, and Labor-Market Access**
- Central question: **How do safety, harassment, violence, and mobility constraints shape labor-market access and worker welfare?**

## Why this week exists

This week treats safety as a labor-market constraint rather than a separate social issue. The lecture should show that violence, harassment, mobility barriers, and reproductive autonomy alter the **feasible set of jobs**, the **cost of participating**, the **ability to search and commute**, the **timing of fertility and career investment**, and the **welfare value** of wages and employment. The main labor-economics contribution is to convert these harms into observable margins and credible research designs.

This week should remain distinct from Week 6. Week 6 studies **policy incidence**: which labor-market margins policy moves, who bears incidence, and how welfare changes. Week 7 studies **constraints and hidden harms**: why some jobs, commutes, schedules, or career paths are not truly available even when they exist on paper, and how reproductive control changes the timing and option set underlying labor decisions.

## Why this is specifically labor economics

Safety and reproductive control matter in labor economics because they change labor supply, search intensity, reservation jobs, occupation choice, commuting patterns, turnover, workplace exits, fertility timing, promotion opportunities, and worker welfare. They also create measurement problems because the most important harms are often not fully visible in wages or employment status.

## Core points

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important

- Safety changes the feasible set of jobs, commutes, schedules, and workplaces, not just the utility of a given job.
- Violence and harassment affect labor supply, job search, occupational segregation, quits, retention, and welfare.
- Domestic violence and reproductive autonomy alter labor outcomes through timing, bargaining power, outside options, and control over future work/care paths.
- Mobility constraints can reduce application intensity, acceptance sets, and access to higher-wage jobs even without changing formal wages directly.
- Researchers need designs that can detect harms that are partly invisible in standard wage and employment data.
:::

## Bridge

This week sits after Week 6 because some of these margins are policy-sensitive, but the main object is not policy incidence. The lecture asks a more basic question: when safety and control over fertility are themselves constraints, what labor choices are actually available to workers? The forward bridge to Week 8 is that once these worker constraints exist, firms and markets may respond endogenously.

## Field Core: required architecture

### 1. Feasible-set framing
Start with a clear labor-economics framework. Show that safety and reproductive autonomy can enter labor decisions through:
- disutility of work and commuting,
- search and mobility constraints,
- reservation job sets,
- timing/control over future fertility and care,
- hidden welfare costs not seen in wages alone.

A concise conceptual object like the following should appear somewhere:
- job utility includes wage, schedule, commute, safety risk, harassment risk, fertility-control/timing constraints, and job-specific amenities;
- accepted jobs are those inside a constrained feasible set rather than those with the highest wage alone.

### 2. Domestic violence / intimate-partner violence
Treat domestic violence as a labor-market constraint.
Key mechanisms:
- control over mobility and job search,
- psychological and physical costs,
- reduced bargaining power / outside options,
- employer absenteeism / retention consequences,
- endogenous labor-market withdrawal or reduced search.

Required anchor papers:
- [@heathMobilityResourcesIntimate2014]
- [@frankenthalFemaleLaborProductivity2024] as an optional newer extension if useful

### 3. Workplace harassment and workplace violence
Separate harassment/violence at work from generic discrimination.
Core points:
- harassment can alter sorting, quits, workplace exits, and segregation;
- workplace violence affects victims, perpetrators, firm hiring, and coworker composition;
- reporting incentives and organizational response matter.

Required anchor papers:
- [@folkeRickne2022]
- [@adamsPrasslHuttunenNixZhang2024]

Optional recent angle:
- office relationships / power asymmetry / harassment-risk spillovers:
  - [@macdonaldMontonenNix2025]

### 4. Mobility, commuting, and public-space safety
This block should make clear that safety matters before the worker even reaches the job.
Core points:
- commuting risk changes the set of jobs that are effectively reachable;
- safer transport can operate through applications, acceptance, hours, and household time allocation;
- mobility is a labor-market access margin, not just an urban or transport topic.

Required anchor paper:
- [@garlickFieldVyborny2025]

Optional extension:
- [@chenSekhri2025] if useful as a complementary mobility/time-use framing

### 5. Reproductive autonomy: birth control, abortion access, and IVF
This should be a major block of the lecture, not a side note.
Organize it around **control over timing, fertility, and career paths**.

Sub-blocks:
- birth control / contraception access as an education-career-timing margin,
- abortion access / denial as labor-market and financial trajectory margin,
- fertility treatment / IVF as a career/fertility timing and identification margin.

Required anchor papers:
- [@goldinKatzPowerPill2002]
- [@millerWherryFoster2023] or the corresponding abortion-denial labor/economic paper in the bibliography
- [@londonoVelezSaravia2025]
- [@lundborgPlugRasmussen2017]

Important framing:
- these are not only “policy debates”; they are labor-economics questions about career timing, control over labor supply, and feasible future paths.

### 6. Hidden harms, reporting, and welfare
Explain why standard wage/employment outcomes can miss key harms.
Possible margins:
- delayed job search,
- moving to lower-wage safer jobs,
- quitting high-wage male-dominated or high-risk jobs,
- lower search radius,
- hidden welfare losses within employment,
- underreporting and measurement error.

This section should make the welfare interpretation explicit:
- a higher wage in a riskier/hostile workplace is not necessarily a welfare improvement;
- job quality and safety must enter the labor-economics welfare object.

## Methods box: what students should learn

This week should explicitly name the actual designs and margins used.

Required design families:
- administrative police-report or legal-report linkage to labor-market admin data,
- survey + admin linkages for workplace harassment,
- randomized mobility / transport interventions,
- legal or judicial quasi-experiments for abortion access/denial,
- IVF-based IV for fertility timing/career effects,
- historical or policy access discontinuities when relevant.

The chapter should clearly distinguish:
- safety as a participation margin,
- safety as a search/mobility margin,
- safety as a retention/exit margin,
- safety as a hidden welfare margin,
- reproductive autonomy as a timing/control margin.

## Research Lab

The research lab should feel like a frontier field module, not a loose “future work” section.

### Lab spine
- Primary replication / reduced pedagogical path: [@adamsPrasslHuttunenNixZhang2024]
- Challenge anchor: [@folkeRickne2022]
- Optional extension: [@macdonaldMontonenNix2025]

### Research-design questions
Students should leave the week able to ask questions like:
- When does harassment generate quits versus re-sorting inside the same firm?
- How do mobility constraints alter application sets versus accepted-job sets?
- When do reproductive-control shocks affect education/career timing versus later family structure?
- How should researchers value hidden harms when wages move in the opposite direction?
- Which settings allow researchers to observe the constraint directly, and which only reveal it through downstream labor outcomes?
