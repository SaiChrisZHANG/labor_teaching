# Labor Markets and Political/Cultural Institutions

Labor Markets and Political/Cultural Institutions is a flagship 10-week special-topics course on how state capacity, law, norms, identity, and political conflict shape labor-market outcomes. The course treats labor markets not as institution-free allocation mechanisms, but as embedded systems whose rules and meanings vary across places and over time.

Relative to the core sequence, this course sits across both Labor I and Labor II. Labor I contributes worker choice, households, mobility, and inequality; Labor II contributes institutions, regulation, and enforcement. This special topic asks how those mechanisms change once political structure, social norms, and cultural persistence are treated as central explanatory forces rather than background conditions.

## Course type

10-week flagship special-topics course

## Prerequisites

- Labor I and ideally Labor II, or a strong equivalent background in labor economics
- Graduate microeconomics and applied econometrics
- Comfort moving between political economy, economic history, and empirical labor research

## Course goals

- build a framework for linking labor outcomes to political and cultural institutions;
- distinguish laws on the books from enforcement, state capacity, and lived labor-market rules;
- analyze how norms, identity, and family systems shape labor allocation and labor-market inequality;
- prepare students to use historical, cross-regional, and policy-shock designs in institution-focused labor research.

## Course format

The course uses the same Bridge, Field Core, and Research Lab logic as the labor sequence, but with heavier emphasis on mechanism mapping and comparative institutional interpretation. Each week combines one conceptual anchor, one empirical identification block, and one discussion of portability across places, periods, and institutional settings.

## Week-by-week path

- Week 1. Institutions, culture, and labor-market mechanisms. The course opens by defining the institutional objects that labor economists can and cannot plausibly measure.
- Week 2. State capacity, labor law, and enforcement. Students study how labor rules become real only through inspection, administration, and enforcement capacity.
- Week 3. Family systems, norms, and labor supply. The household is placed in cultural context through marriage systems, kinship structures, and intergenerational expectations.
- Week 4. Gender norms, identity, and work. This week focuses on how social expectations shape labor-force participation, occupation choice, and bargaining power.
- Week 5. Religion, ethnicity, and social stratification. Labor-market allocation is examined through identity boundaries, group hierarchy, and persistent social categories.
- Week 6. Political economy of labor regulation and redistribution. Students ask how coalitions, parties, elites, and voter incentives shape labor-market policy.
- Week 7. Conflict, migration, and institutional shocks. The course turns to upheaval, displacement, and institutional breaks as sources of labor-market reorganization.
- Week 8. Informality, clientelism, and labor markets. Formal and informal labor institutions are compared in environments where legal rules only partially govern exchange.
- Week 9. Local governance, place, and labor adjustment. Subnational political institutions and place-based governance are studied as determinants of labor-market resilience and segmentation.
- Week 10. Synthesis and research design. The final week turns the course into a usable labor-political-economy research map.

## Research orientation

This course is especially useful for students who want to connect labor economics to political economy, development, history, or culture without losing identification discipline. It is meant to produce research questions that are comparably strong on mechanism, institutional detail, and empirical design.
