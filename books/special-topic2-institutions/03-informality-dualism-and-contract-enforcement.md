# Informality, Dualism, and Contract Enforcement

## Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 3, students should be able to:

1. distinguish informal workers, informal firms, informal jobs inside registered firms, and missing payroll or benefit compliance;
2. explain dualist, competitive, and modern equilibrium views of formal-informal labor markets;
3. connect informality to wages, benefits, risk, mobility, job ladders, worker sorting, firm productivity, compliance, and scale;
4. explain why formal legal status and effective contract enforceability are different labor-market objects;
5. evaluate empirical papers by naming the observed margin, the identifying variation, and whether the design identifies worker sorting, firm response, wage-setting, contract enforceability, formalization, or welfare.

Week 3 asks how labor markets are organized when formal contracts, payroll systems, and legal protections cover only part of work. This is not a generic development-economics survey. The labor-market question is sharper: when a worker-firm match can be formal, informal, partially reported, or legally registered but weakly enforceable, which margins determine wages, benefits, mobility, productivity, risk, and worker welfare?

Week 2 studied labor law, enforcement, and state capacity. Week 3 follows the same logic into informality. If enforcement is costly and selective, workers and firms do not simply comply or violate. They choose contract form, payroll reporting, benefit compliance, firm registration, self-employment, subcontracting, and scale. Informality is therefore a two-sided institutional equilibrium involving workers, employers, and the state.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important

- Informality is a labor-market institution, not only a measurement residual.
- The worker side and the firm side must be analyzed jointly: informal workers, informal firms, and informal jobs inside formal firms are different objects.
- The extensive margin of informality concerns whether a firm, job, or worker is formal at all; the intensive margin concerns partial reporting, missing benefits, underreported wages, or unenforced contract terms.
- Dualist and competitive views make different claims about segmentation, choice, productivity, and mobility; modern equilibrium work often combines both.
- Registration, payroll reporting, formal contracts, and effective enforceability are separate institutional margins.
- Empirical work is most useful when it names the observed margin and the identifying variation before interpreting wages, employment, formalization, or welfare.

:::

## Bridge

Week 2 separated law on the books from effective law in practice. A labor standard can be written into statute, but workers and firms respond to the expected constraint: inspections, records, courts, worker knowledge, sanctions, and retaliation risk. Week 3 asks what happens when that effective constraint covers only part of the labor market.

The formal-informal boundary is not a single line. A business may be unregistered. A registered business may hire workers off the books. A wage worker may receive cash pay without payroll contributions. A self-employed worker may operate outside tax and benefit systems. A worker may have a written contract but no credible ability to claim unpaid benefits or severance. A firm may register but underreport wages or hours.

This week therefore treats informality as a set of legal, administrative, and contractual margins. The economic question is how those margins shape the employment relationship. Ulyssea's overview frames informality as a central development and labor-market fact, but the course reads it through the worker-firm match: who is covered, who pays, who benefits, who evades, and which productive matches become feasible or infeasible [@ulyssea2020].

## Field Core

### Why Informality Is An Institutional Labor-Market Object

Informality matters for labor economics because it changes the employment contract. It affects the cash wage, benefit bundle, risk exposure, enforceability of claims, mobility, and access to public protection for workers. It also affects registration, payroll costs, sanctions, access to credit, eligibility for procurement, firm scale, productivity, and competitive strategy for employers. A formal-informal indicator is therefore never just a demographic characteristic. It is a description of the institutional environment in which labor is exchanged.

A useful taxonomy separates five objects.

First, **unregistered firms** operate outside business registries, tax systems, or licensing systems. Their workers may also be informal, but the firm-side object is registration and visibility. Second, **off-the-books workers in registered firms** are employed by firms that are visible to the state but not fully reporting all employment relationships. Third, **informal wage employment** refers to wage work without payroll registration, mandated benefits, or enforceable employment protection. Fourth, **self-employment and own-account work** may reflect entrepreneurship, exclusion from wage jobs, tax avoidance, or household risk management. Fifth, **missing payroll or benefit compliance** describes partial formality: the job is visible in some records but contributions, benefits, hours, wages, or contract terms are underreported or unenforced.

The distinction between registration and payroll reporting is especially important. Registration asks whether the firm exists in an official system. Payroll reporting asks whether workers, wages, hours, and contributions are recorded. A registered firm can still hide workers. An unregistered firm can still maintain long-term relational contracts. A payroll record can exist while benefits or severance remain practically unenforceable. The lecture's first discipline is to state which margin is being studied.

### Dualism Versus Competitive And Equilibrium Views

The older dualist view treats the formal and informal sectors as sharply segmented. Formal jobs are more productive, better paid, more protected, and rationed. Informal workers are often interpreted as trapped in inferior jobs because entry barriers, weak skills, search frictions, or firm heterogeneity block movement into formality. The firm-side counterpart is that informal firms are persistently small, low productivity, and weakly connected to formal markets.

The competitive or choice-based view treats informality as a sorting outcome. Workers and firms compare costs and benefits. A worker may accept an informal job because cash wages, flexibility, location, or self-employment autonomy compensate for missing benefits. A firm may remain informal because registration, payroll taxes, compliance costs, and expected sanctions exceed the benefits of formal status. In this view, observed wage gaps do not automatically imply rationing because compensation includes benefits, flexibility, risk, and future mobility.

The modern equilibrium view is more useful for this course because it allows segmentation and sorting to coexist. Heterogeneous workers face different outside options, benefit valuations, search frictions, and mobility costs. Heterogeneous firms differ in productivity, visibility, compliance costs, technology, and gains from legal status. Some informal jobs may be rationed and low quality; others may be chosen given tax-benefit wedges or household constraints. Some informal firms may be subsistence units; others may be productive firms avoiding payroll obligations at the intensive margin.

La Porta and Shleifer emphasize large productivity differences and the limited growth prospects of many informal firms, which is closest to a strong dualist reading [@laportaShleifer2014]. Ulyssea's work shows why that view is incomplete without firm choices over registration, payroll reporting, and scale [@ulyssea2018; @ulyssea2020]. Meghir, Narita, and Robin push the worker-firm equilibrium angle by modeling wages and informality with search, worker heterogeneity, and compensating differentials [@meghirNaritaRobin2015].

### Worker-Side Margins

The worker side begins with wages, but it cannot end there. Informal workers may earn lower cash wages because they are employed by lower-productivity firms, have weaker bargaining power, lack enforceable claims, or are rationed out of formal jobs. They may also earn similar or higher cash wages if firms compensate them for missing benefits or if payroll costs are partly shifted into the wage. The observed wage gap is therefore a fact to be decomposed, not a conclusion.

Benefits are the second margin. Formal jobs may provide social-security contributions, health insurance, pensions, leave, severance, injury protection, unemployment insurance, or eligibility for other public programs. The value of those benefits depends on worker age, household structure, liquidity constraints, trust in the state, portability, and expected job duration. A young worker with low expected take-up may value payroll benefits differently from an older worker or a worker with dependents.

Risk is the third margin. Informal jobs often expose workers to earnings volatility, dismissal risk, nonpayment, injury risk, and weak claimability of rights. Informal self-employment can provide autonomy but also shifts demand, illness, and business risk onto the worker. A cash wage is therefore not enough to measure welfare when insurance differs across contract forms.

Mobility and job ladders are the fourth margin. Informal jobs can be a waiting state, an entry point, a trap, or a permanent occupational choice. Worker panels and transition matrices are useful because they show whether workers move from informal to formal jobs, whether formal jobs are stable, whether separations push workers into informality, and whether job ladders differ by skill, gender, age, migration status, or region. Low transition rates support a segmentation interpretation; high mobility with systematic wage-benefit tradeoffs supports a sorting or search interpretation.

Sorting is the fifth margin. Informal workers may differ in education, experience, networks, documentation, risk tolerance, family obligations, or access to formal-sector referrals. Any empirical claim about wage penalties must ask whether the comparison holds worker ability, firm productivity, job amenities, and transition prospects fixed. Meghir, Narita, and Robin are important because their equilibrium model forces students to ask which observed patterns are wages, which are worker sorting, and which are market equilibrium [@meghirNaritaRobin2015].

### Firm-Side Margins

The firm side is not an appendix. In many settings, informality is organized through employer choices. A firm chooses whether to register, whether to report workers on payroll, whether to write formal contracts, whether to contribute to social insurance, whether to underreport wages, and how visible to become to tax and labor authorities.

Registration is the extensive firm margin. Formal registration can provide access to courts, credit, procurement, banking, larger buyers, legal protections, and reputational benefits. It can also expose the firm to taxes, inspections, licensing costs, recordkeeping, and sanction risk. A firm may remain unregistered because it is low productivity, because expected compliance costs are high, or because small scale makes formal benefits less valuable.

Payroll reporting is a distinct intensive margin. A registered firm can report some workers and hide others, formalize permanent workers but not temporary workers, underreport wages, or pay contributions irregularly. These choices matter for labor incidence. A policy that raises expected penalties for hidden employment may formalize some incumbents, reduce new hiring, increase outsourcing, or induce firms to shrink below enforcement thresholds.

Compliance also changes productivity and scale. Formal status may improve productivity by giving firms access to credit, legal contracting, larger buyers, and more stable employment relationships. But compliance can reduce measured employment if low-productivity firms cannot absorb payroll costs. Ulyssea's Brazil paper is the central anchor because it models heterogeneous firms choosing among informality margins and uses the model to study how enforcement and formalization policies reallocate employment, output, and welfare [@ulyssea2018].

Firms also adapt to enforcement. They may register but keep workers off payroll, substitute toward family labor or independent contractors, rotate workers before benefits vest, change firm size, relocate to lower-enforcement areas, automate tasks, or split production across legal entities. Samaniego de la Parra and Fernández Bujanda provide a modern enforcement setting in which increasing the cost of informal employment changes firm and worker margins in Mexico [@samaniegoParraFernandezBujanda2024]. The lesson is not simply that enforcement "works" or "fails." The lesson is to state whether the observed margin is payroll reporting, formal employment, wages, firm size, or reallocation.

### Contract Enforcement And Legal Status

Formal status and contract enforceability are related but distinct. A formal contract may be recorded, but the worker's claim may still be costly, slow, risky, or inaccessible. An informal relationship may lack legal form but be supported by reputation, repeated interaction, family ties, or local sanction. The labor-market institution is not only the contract's written form. It is the credible enforcement environment around promises of wages, benefits, hours, tenure, training, severance, and discipline.

The central contract-enforcement distinction is between **formal contracts** and **effective enforceability**. Formal contracts define terms and legal status. Effective enforceability determines whether the worker can claim unpaid wages, benefits, severance, injury compensation, or protection from arbitrary dismissal, and whether the employer can rely on attendance, effort, notice, confidentiality, or tenure promises. If courts are inaccessible, inspections weak, complaints risky, or payroll records missing, the formal contract may have limited value.

A compact teaching decomposition is:

```{math}
:label: eq:informal-contract-value-week3
V^{formal}_{ij} - V^{informal}_{ij}
= B_{ij} - C_{ij}(R,E,\tau) + X_{ij}(K,Q),
```

where {math}`V^{formal}_{ij} - V^{informal}_{ij}` is the net value difference between formal and informal status for worker-firm match {math}`ij`; {math}`B_{ij}` denotes the benefits of formalization, including social insurance, benefit receipt, legal recognition, public-program access, and more credible job ladders; {math}`C_{ij}(R,E,\tau)` collects registration, tax, payroll, compliance, and expected enforcement costs as functions of rules {math}`R`, enforcement {math}`E`, and taxes or contributions {math}`\tau`; and {math}`X_{ij}(K,Q)` captures gains from contract quality, courts, credit, procurement, reputation, or match-specific investment under institutional quality {math}`K` and contract quality {math}`Q`.

Equation {eq}`eq:informal-contract-value-week3` is not meant to be estimated directly. It is a map. It shows why the same policy can have different incidence across workers and firms. A worker may value benefits highly while the employer faces high payroll costs. A firm may value legal registration for credit access while a worker does not value distant pension rights. An enforcement reform can raise {math}`C_{ij}` for evasion while also raising {math}`B_{ij}` and {math}`X_{ij}` if formal promises become more credible.

Naidu and Yuchtman provide a historical benchmark for why contract enforcement is a labor-market institution. Their study of nineteenth-century Britain shows how coercive labor-contract enforcement shaped wages and labor-market adjustment when the law strengthened employers' ability to enforce labor agreements [@naiduYuchtman2013]. For this course, the point is broader than the historical setting: enforcement can protect workers, discipline firms, discipline workers, restrict mobility, or support productive long-term matches depending on whose claims are enforceable and at what cost.

### Equilibrium Interactions

The formal-informal margin is an equilibrium object because workers and firms respond to each other. A worker's value of formality depends on firm productivity, benefit credibility, wage offsets, household insurance, and outside options. A firm's value of formality depends on worker skill, payroll costs, expected enforcement, credit access, buyer requirements, and competitors' compliance.

Stricter enforcement can formalize some jobs and destroy or reclassify others. If enforcement raises expected penalties for hidden workers, compliant firms may expand relative to evading firms. But firms at the margin may shrink, outsource, substitute capital for labor, move workers into self-employment contracts, or exit. Workers may gain benefits but pay for them through lower cash wages. If benefits are valued less than their cost, formalization can reduce joint surplus for some matches. If benefits and enforceability are valuable, formalization can improve retention, mobility, and match quality.

Equilibrium interactions also shape competition. Noncompliant firms may undercut compliant firms by avoiding payroll costs. Enforcing payroll rules can therefore reallocate output toward formal firms, but the welfare effect depends on whether reallocation moves workers toward more productive matches or simply raises costs in low-productivity labor markets. Ulyssea's structural framework is useful because it makes firm registration, payroll informality, enforcement, productivity, and worker allocation part of the same system [@ulyssea2018].

Social programs can interact with the formal-informal boundary. Gerard studies how health-insurance discontinuities in Mexico affect informal labor and the efficiency cost of social programs [@gerard2021]. The lesson for Week 3 is that benefits tied to formal status can change worker and firm incentives even when the policy is not labeled a labor-market regulation. The observed margin must be named: labor supply, formalization, insurance take-up, wages, or welfare.

### Empirical Strategies And What They Identify

Good informality research starts by naming the observed margin. Is the paper measuring worker transitions, firm registration, payroll reporting, benefit receipt, wage-setting, contract disputes, firm scale, productivity, or welfare? The identifying variation matters because each design answers a different question.

Worker transition and panel designs follow individuals across formal jobs, informal wage jobs, self-employment, unemployment, and nonemployment. They identify mobility patterns, wage changes around transitions, job ladders, and sorting. Their limitation is that firm response and unobserved worker heterogeneity can remain hard to separate.

Inspection or enforcement-intensity designs identify the effect of a higher expected probability of detection or sanction. They are strongest for firm response, payroll reporting, compliance, wages, formalization, and employment adjustment when enforcement exposure is plausibly exogenous or carefully controlled. They do not automatically identify the value workers place on benefits.

Matched worker-firm equilibrium models identify wage-setting, sorting, compensating differentials, and market-clearing interactions by jointly modeling worker and firm decisions. They are useful when reduced-form transitions cannot separate selection from prices, but they require stronger assumptions about preferences, search, productivity, and policy counterfactuals.

Structural models of registration and payroll choices identify how firms choose among extensive registration, intensive payroll reporting, output, employment, and evasion margins. They are central for welfare and counterfactual policy because formalization policies change multiple margins at once. Ulyssea's model is the primary Week 3 anchor for this logic [@ulyssea2018].

Legal or policy discontinuities affecting formality identify how discrete eligibility, benefit, tax, or enforcement rules change formal status, labor supply, wages, or welfare. Gerard's social-program setting is useful because the discontinuity changes the value of formal or informal status through policy design, not only through inspections [@gerard2021].

Historical labor-contract enforcement settings identify how legal enforceability itself changes labor-market outcomes. Naidu and Yuchtman identify how coercive enforcement affected wages and labor-market adjustment under a specific legal regime [@naiduYuchtman2013]. The portability question is not whether nineteenth-century Britain is today's informal labor market. The portable lesson is to ask whose contract claims are enforceable and how that changes mobility, wages, and bargaining.

### Bridge To Week 4

Weeks 2 and 3 together give students a vocabulary for formal law, enforcement, informality, and effective contracts. Week 4 asks what happens when individual workers cannot solve these problems alone. Worker voice, unions, collective bargaining, works councils, and other collective institutions can make claims more credible, shift bargaining power, monitor compliance, and change how formal and informal rules are enforced at work.

## Research Lab

The Week 3 lab is built around **Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer**. It runs locally with synthetic data and does not require confidential microdata. The goal is not to replicate the confidential administrative data behind the anchor papers. The goal is to train students to see whether an informality paper is identifying worker sorting, firm compliance, wage-setting, contract enforceability, or equilibrium reallocation.

**Reproduce.** The primary anchor is Ulyssea [@ulyssea2018]. Students generate a synthetic firm-worker panel with three firm margins: registration, payroll reporting, and compliance costs. They reproduce a small factbook showing how firm productivity, enforcement exposure, and payroll costs jointly predict formal registration, reported employment, hidden employment, and wages. The reproduction target is the conceptual object: firms can choose multiple informality margins.

**Diagnose.** The secondary challenge anchor is Meghir, Narita, and Robin [@meghirNaritaRobin2015]. Students classify rows from worker transitions and wage observations. For each pattern, they decide whether the evidence mainly speaks to worker sorting, wage-setting, benefit valuation, mobility, or equilibrium matching. The point is to prevent students from reading a formal-informal wage gap as segmentation without diagnosing selection and compensating differentials.

**Transfer.** The optional modern enforcement extension is Samaniego de la Parra and Fernández Bujanda [@samaniegoParraFernandezBujanda2024]. Students transfer the same classification to a compliance setting: if enforcement raises the cost of informal employment, which observed margin moves first: worker status, payroll reporting, firm scale, wages, or reallocation? The bounded transfer choices are to compare worker-side and firm-side incidence, compare dualist and equilibrium interpretations in one setting, or adapt a worker-transition exercise to a firm-formalization design.

The lab lives at `labs/03-informality-dualism-and-contract-enforcement/`. The smoke test builds synthetic data, writes a reproduction summary, and runs a design classifier. Its outputs are deliberately small CSV and text files for seminar discussion.

## Methods Box

The methods lesson of Week 3 is that each design identifies a different institutional margin.

Worker panels and transition designs are best for job ladders, worker sorting, wage changes around formal-informal moves, and mobility constraints. They identify movement across states, not necessarily firm compliance or policy welfare.

Inspection and enforcement-intensity designs are best for compliance, payroll reporting, formalization, firm response, and wage or employment incidence when enforcement variation is credible. They identify the effect of expected detection or sanctions, not informality as a general condition.

Matched worker-firm equilibrium models are best for separating worker sorting, firm productivity, wage-setting, compensating differentials, and market equilibrium. They identify model-based mechanisms and counterfactuals, so students must inspect assumptions carefully.

Structural models of registration and payroll choices are best for policy counterfactuals involving firm formalization, evasion, productivity, scale, and welfare. They are valuable because the extensive registration margin and intensive payroll margin often move differently.

Legal or policy discontinuities affecting formality are best for identifying how benefit eligibility, taxes, mandates, or enforcement thresholds change formal status and worker welfare. The observed margin must be explicit: take-up, formal employment, wages, or insurance.

Historical contract-enforcement designs are best for studying enforceability itself. They identify how legal changes in the credibility of worker or employer claims affect wages, mobility, and bargaining. They are not generic lessons about development; they are institutional labor designs.

The diagnostic rule is simple: do not present a result without naming the observed labor margin and the identifying variation. "Informality fell" is incomplete. Did firm registration rise, payroll reporting rise, benefit receipt rise, self-employment fall, wage employment shift, or contract claims become enforceable?

## Reading Ladder And References

**Map the field.** Start with Ulyssea for the broad overview of informality's causes and consequences, while keeping the course focus on labor-market margins [@ulyssea2020].

**Compare conceptual views.** Read La Porta and Shleifer as the clean dualist and productivity-gap benchmark, then compare it with Ulyssea's emphasis on heterogeneous firm choices and policy margins [@laportaShleifer2014; @ulyssea2018].

**Worker-firm equilibrium.** Read Meghir, Narita, and Robin for wages, informality, search, compensating differentials, and worker sorting [@meghirNaritaRobin2015].

**Contract enforcement benchmark.** Read Naidu and Yuchtman to see why legal enforceability of labor contracts can change wages, mobility, and bargaining power [@naiduYuchtman2013].

**Modern enforcement design.** Read Samaniego de la Parra and Fernández Bujanda for a contemporary enforcement setting where raising the cost of informal employment changes observed firm and worker margins [@samaniegoParraFernandezBujanda2024].

**Policy incidence extension.** Use Gerard as an optional extension on how social programs and benefit discontinuities interact with informal labor and welfare [@gerard2021].

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. Pick one empirical paper from the reading ladder. State whether the observed margin is worker formality, firm registration, payroll reporting, benefit receipt, wages, contract enforceability, firm scale, or welfare.
2. Construct a dualist interpretation and an equilibrium interpretation of the same formal-informal wage gap. What additional evidence would distinguish them?
3. Suppose a government raises penalties for off-the-books workers in registered firms. Predict effects on wages, benefit receipt, reported employment, hidden employment, firm scale, and worker sorting.
4. Use equation {eq}`eq:informal-contract-value-week3` to explain why a worker and firm might disagree about the value of formalization.

## Bridge Forward

Week 4 moves from individual contract status and firm compliance to collective organization. Informality and weak enforceability often leave individual workers with limited claim power. Unions, collective bargaining, worker associations, works councils, and other voice institutions can change that power by aggregating claims, monitoring compliance, and changing the wage-setting environment. The next question is how collective institutions alter wages, rents, governance, and workplace authority when individual contracts are incomplete.
