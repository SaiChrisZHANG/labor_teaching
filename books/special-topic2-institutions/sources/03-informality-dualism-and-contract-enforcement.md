# Week 3 source pack: Informality, dualism, and contract enforcement

## Central question

How should labor economists think about informality as a **worker–employer two-sided institutional problem**, and what do contract enforcement, legal status, firm productivity, worker sorting, and state capacity imply for wages, employment, mobility, welfare, and development?

## Why this week matters

Week 2 emphasized that labor law only matters insofar as it becomes effective through enforcement, administrative reach, and worker knowledge. Week 3 now moves to one of the central margins through which weak or selective enforcement reshapes labor markets: **informality**.

This week should make clear that informality is not just a residual category for workers outside regulation. It is an institutional equilibrium involving both workers and firms. On the worker side, informal jobs differ in wages, benefits, risk, mobility, and access to social insurance. On the employer side, informality changes registration choices, payroll reporting, contract form, exposure to sanctions, productivity, scale, access to credit, and the ability to compete with formal firms. The key analytical task is to connect these two sides rather than teaching informality as only a worker problem or only a development problem.

The lecture should also explain why the literature historically split between **dualist** views and **competitive/choice-based** views, and how more recent equilibrium and structural work has moved beyond that simple dichotomy. Students should leave the week understanding that the important question is not whether informality is always “bad” or always “voluntary,” but rather which margins are distorted, who sorts where, what frictions block transitions, and how firms and workers jointly respond to legal and contractual institutions.

## Core points to communicate

- Informality is a labor-market institution, not merely a measurement category.
- Informality must be analyzed jointly on the **worker side** and the **firm side**.
- Dualism, segmentation, and competitive sorting are competing but not mutually exclusive ways to understand informal labor markets.
- Contract enforcement is central because it shapes the value of formal status, the credibility of employment contracts, and the margins on which firms hide or comply.
- Empirically, the most informative papers identify specific margins: worker sorting, wage-setting, formalization, firm registration, payroll reporting, benefits, separations, and productivity.
- The firm side is essential: regulation, enforcement, and taxes alter firm behavior, not just worker outcomes.

## Conceptual structure for the lecture

### Bridge
Start from Week 2’s distinction among law on the books, enforcement, and effective compliance. Then ask: if compliance is partial, costly, and selective, what labor-market equilibrium emerges? The answer is often a segmented or dual labor market in which some worker–firm matches are formalized and others are not.

### Field Core

#### A. What is informality?
The chapter should distinguish clearly among:
- unregistered firms,
- registered firms employing workers off the books,
- self-employment / own-account work,
- informal wage employment,
- noncompliance with payroll taxes or mandated benefits,
- missing contracts or unenforceable contracts,
- informal jobs inside otherwise formal firms.

The point is that “informality” bundles together multiple legal and contractual margins. The lecture should not collapse all of them into one binary measure too quickly.

#### B. Dualism, segmentation, and competitive views
Students should see the main competing frameworks:
- **dualist / segmented view**: formal and informal sectors differ sharply in productivity, job quality, and barriers to entry;
- **competitive / choice-based view**: workers and firms sort across formal and informal arrangements depending on costs, benefits, and comparative advantage;
- **modern equilibrium view**: both segmentation and sorting can coexist, with heterogeneous firms exploiting multiple informal margins and heterogeneous workers facing different outside options.

Use La Porta–Shleifer and Ulyssea as the cleanest contrast in how the literature thinks about these questions [@laportaShleifer2014; @ulyssea2020].

#### C. Worker side: wages, risk, benefits, mobility, and sorting
This section should cover:
- wage premia / penalties and compensating differentials,
- missing benefits and insurance,
- risk and earnings volatility,
- job ladders and mobility,
- barriers to formal-sector entry,
- sorting by skill, productivity, or outside options.

The lecture should emphasize that observed wage gaps alone do not settle the dualism question. What matters is whether workers are rationed out of formality, voluntarily selecting, or moving along a job ladder with heterogeneous search frictions.

#### D. Firm side: registration, payroll, productivity, and scale
This is the added angle that should be especially explicit in this week. The chapter should treat informality as a firm problem as well as a worker problem.

Key firm-side margins:
- business registration,
- whether to report workers on payroll,
- contract form and wage reporting,
- social-security contributions,
- response to inspections or sanctions,
- scale/productivity effects,
- competition with formal firms,
- access to credit, procurement, or legal protections.

Use Ulyssea and Samaniego de la Parra–Fernández Bujanda to show that firms can adjust on both the extensive margin (whether to register / formalize) and intensive margins (how many workers to hide, whom to formalize, whether to shrink) [@ulyssea2018; @samaniegoParraFernandezBujanda2024].

#### E. Contract enforcement
This section should make explicit why contract enforcement is central rather than peripheral.

The chapter should distinguish:
- enforceability of employment contracts,
- enforceability of wage payment and mandated benefits,
- employer ability to discipline or retain workers,
- worker ability to claim rights,
- relational vs legal enforcement,
- coercive or mobility-restricting enforcement as a historical benchmark.

A useful pedagogical decomposition is:
```{math}
:label: eq:informal-contract-value-week3
V^{formal}_{ij} - V^{informal}_{ij}
= B_{ij} - C_{ij}(R,E,	au) + X_{ij}(K,Q),
```
where {math}`B_{ij}` denotes benefits of formalization for worker–firm match {math}`ij`, {math}`C_{ij}` collects registration, tax, compliance, and enforcement-related costs as functions of rules {math}`R`, enforcement {math}`E`, and taxes/contributions {math}`\tau`, and {math}`X_{ij}` captures additional gains from contract quality, legal protection, or access to outside institutions such as courts, credit, or social insurance. The object is pedagogical, not structural: it helps students see why the same reform can shift workers and firms differently.

Use Naidu–Yuchtman as the historical contract-enforcement benchmark to show that labor markets can differ radically depending on how employment relations are enforced [@naiduYuchtman2013].

#### F. Equilibrium adjustments and dualism in practice
This section should connect worker and firm margins:
- stricter enforcement can formalize some jobs and destroy others;
- workers may value formal benefits but pay for them through wages;
- firms may shrink, substitute away from labor, or reallocate employment composition;
- informality can create unfair competition between compliant and noncompliant firms;
- social programs can interact with the formal–informal margin.

This is where Gerard and Samaniego de la Parra–Fernández Bujanda are especially useful as modern policy/equilibrium illustrations [@gerard2021; @samaniegoParraFernandezBujanda2024].

## Methods requirements

The methods section should be very explicit that different empirical strategies identify different objects. Distinguish among:
- matched worker–firm wage-setting/search models,
- inspection or enforcement-intensity designs,
- registration / payroll administrative discontinuities,
- legal eligibility thresholds,
- worker panels following transitions between formality and informality,
- structural equilibrium models that jointly discipline worker and firm decisions.

Students should be told clearly what each design identifies:
- worker sorting,
- firm response,
- equilibrium wage effects,
- formalization,
- contract compliance,
- welfare under policy change.

## Research Lab direction

The Research Lab should emphasize the two-sided empirical problem:
- when a paper studies informality, which side is moving more clearly—workers or firms?
- is the treatment changing registration, reporting, hiring, wages, or sorting?
- is the paper identifying contract enforcement, labor costs, informality itself, or the productivity/compliance margin?

Recommended lab structure:
- **Primary anchor:** `[@ulyssea2018]`
- **Secondary / challenge anchor:** `[@meghirNaritaRobin2015]`
- **Optional modern enforcement extension:** `[@samaniegoParraFernandezBujanda2024]`

Bounded transfer ideas:
- compare worker-side vs firm-side incidence in an enforcement setting,
- adapt a worker-transition design to a firm-formalization setting,
- compare dualist vs equilibrium interpretations in a given empirical paper,
- test whether a policy margin changes employment status, reporting, or true contract quality.

## Forward bridge

End the chapter by making clear that:
- Week 2 and Week 3 together give students a language for law, enforcement, informality, and effective contracts;
- Week 4 will move to worker voice, collective organization, and collective bargaining institutions;
- later weeks will broaden from formal legal institutions to norms, identity, persistence, and comparative labor-market institutions.
