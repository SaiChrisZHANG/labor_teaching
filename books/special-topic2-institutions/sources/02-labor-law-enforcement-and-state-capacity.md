# Week 2 source pack: Labor law, enforcement, and state capacity

## Central question

How do labor laws affect labor-market outcomes once we distinguish **rules on the books** from **rules in practice**, and how do enforcement institutions and state capacity determine the actual incidence, effectiveness, and distributional consequences of labor regulation?

## Why this week matters

Week 1 established that labor-market institutions include both formal and informal institutions. Week 2 now zooms in on one of the most important formal domains: labor law and the institutions that make labor law credible, selective, uneven, or ineffective.

This week should make clear that labor law is not just a list of statutory rules. In labor economics, law matters because it changes contracts, dismissal, compliance costs, bargaining positions, firm behavior, worker outside options, and the feasibility of regulation itself. But these effects depend crucially on enforcement and on state capacity. A labor code with weak enforcement may have very different equilibrium effects from the same code under high compliance. Likewise, strong enforcement may raise compliance and worker protection, but it may also shift firms, workers, and tasks across formal and informal margins.

The lecture should therefore distinguish clearly among:
- legal protection on paper,
- expected enforcement,
- actual compliance,
- worker knowledge of rights,
- firm adaptation,
- and labor-market equilibrium effects.

## Core points to communicate

- Labor law affects labor markets through contracts, dismissal, compliance, bargaining, and worker protection.
- The relevant object is not only **law on the books**, but also **enforcement in practice**.
- State capacity shapes whether legal rules become real labor-market institutions.
- Enforcement is selective, costly, and uneven; these features are central, not incidental.
- The same labor law can have different effects depending on administrative capacity, legal knowledge, and the formal–informal margin.
- Empirically, labor economists study labor law using legal variation, inspection intensity, administrative access, worker knowledge interventions, and comparative measurement.

## Conceptual structure for the lecture

### Bridge
Start from Week 1’s formal/informal taxonomy and narrow to the question: when do formal labor rules become operative labor-market institutions? Explain that a labor-law effect requires some combination of legal scope, expected enforceability, worker awareness, adjudication, and firm compliance.

### Field Core

#### A. What is labor law as a labor-market object?
The chapter should distinguish among several legal domains:
- hiring and contract form,
- dismissal and employment protection,
- wage and hours rules,
- mandated benefits,
- anti-retaliation / dispute resolution,
- informational rights and posting requirements,
- inspection and sanction regimes.

The lecture should keep the focus on labor-market consequences rather than doctrinal detail.

#### B. Law on the books vs law in practice
This should be one of the most important conceptual sections. The chapter should separate:
- statutory protection,
- adjudicable rights,
- expected probability of enforcement,
- worker knowledge of the rule,
- employer expectations about inspection or litigation,
- realized compliance.

A useful reduced-form object is:
```{math}
:label: eq:effective-law-week2
L^{eff}_{jt} = L^{book}_{jt} \times E_{jt} \times K_{jt},
```
where {math}`L^{book}_{jt}` is the formal legal rule, {math}`E_{jt}` is an enforcement or implementation object, and {math}`K_{jt}` is worker/firm knowledge or salience of the rule.

The point is not that this identity is structural; it is to force students to separate formal law from effective law.

#### C. Enforcement as a labor-market institution
This section should emphasize that enforcement is not a mechanical afterthought. It involves:
- inspector capacity,
- geographic reach,
- administrative discretion,
- complaints and worker reporting,
- courts and legal delays,
- sanctions and credibility,
- complementarities with unions or civil society.

Use Almeida–Carneiro and Amengual to show that inspection intensity and enforcement organization can change wages, formalization, and compliance, and that labor inspectors may rely on social or organizational linkages when state capacity is weak [@almeidaCarneiro2012; @amengual2014].

#### D. State capacity and heterogeneity of legal effects
The lecture should explain why the same legal reform may have different effects depending on:
- bureaucratic quality,
- territorial reach,
- case backlog,
- union presence / worker organization,
- informality,
- firm size and observability.

This is where state capacity enters as a labor-economics object rather than a generic political variable. The operative question is: can the state identify violations, pursue compliance, and sustain credible enforcement at the margins that matter for workers and firms?

#### E. Formal–informal margin and selection
A key labor-market mechanism is that stronger enforcement may:
- increase worker protection in the formal sector,
- raise firm labor costs,
- compress wages if benefits are shifted to workers,
- push some production or employment into informality,
- or induce re-sorting across firms, sectors, or contracts.

The chapter should emphasize that informality is not just a side topic here; it is one of the central equilibrium margins through which labor law operates in many settings.

#### F. Worker knowledge, implementation, and take-up of rights
This section should make clear that legal protection can fail even without weak inspectors if workers do not know their rights or cannot act on them. Use Bertrand–Crépon to show that knowledge itself is a meaningful implementation margin [@bertrandCrepon2021].

#### G. Legal effects are not one-dimensional
By the end of Field Core, students should see at least five outcome margins:
- employment,
- wages,
- formalization / contract status,
- compliance and benefits receipt,
- distributional/incidence effects across workers and firms.

### Empirical strategies

The methods section should be concrete and labor-focused. It should distinguish:
- legal adoption / repeal designs,
- court-made legal variation,
- inspection-access or distance-to-inspector designs,
- inspector or bureaucracy capacity measures,
- worker-information RCTs,
- cross-country institutional indices,
- matched employer–employee data to study compliance incidence.

Students should be told explicitly that each design identifies a different object:
- law on the books,
- enforcement intensity,
- knowledge of rights,
- or effective compliance.

## Research Lab direction

The research lab should be centered on the difference between legal rules and effective implementation.

Possible lab directions:
- replicate a reduced version of an enforcement / inspection design,
- diagnose whether an empirical setting identifies law, enforcement, or knowledge,
- transfer an enforcement design to another labor margin (e.g. benefits, wage theft, dismissal, posting requirements),
- compare worker-side vs firm-side incidence when compliance rises.

The lab should emphasize:
- what is the institutional treatment?
- what is the labor-market outcome?
- what margin is moving (employment, formalization, wages, compliance)?
- what is the implementation channel?
- what equilibrium response might contaminate interpretation?

End the chapter by saying:
