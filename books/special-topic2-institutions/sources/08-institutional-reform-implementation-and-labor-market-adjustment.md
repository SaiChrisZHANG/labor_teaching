# Week 8 source pack: Institutional Reform, Implementation, and Labor-Market Adjustment

## Central question

Why do similar institutional reforms produce different labor-market effects across places, firms, and workers?

## Why this week exists

Week 7 studied why old labor institutions persist even after the original rules disappear. Week 8 turns to **change rather than persistence**. The key object is not legal text alone, but the chain from reform to implementation to compliance to labor-market adjustment.

This is where many labor-institutions papers become truly labor economics: reforms change hiring, firing, wage-setting, contract form, promotion, worker mobility, formality, and job quality only insofar as they bind on actual margins of worker and firm behavior.

## Distinct boundary from Week 7

This week should not re-teach historical persistence.
- Week 7: persistence of old institutions and historical labor inequality.
- Week 8: contemporary reforms, administrative change, implementation, firm adaptation, worker beliefs, and heterogeneous labor-market incidence.

## Core narrative

The lecture should be organized around a **reform-transmission framework**:

```{math}
:label: eq:reform-transmission
\Delta Y = \text{Legal change} \times \text{Implementation} \times \text{Exposure} \times \text{Behavioral response} \times \text{Equilibrium adjustment}.
```

The point is not the exact algebra, but the conceptual message:
1. reforms differ in whether they alter rights, procedures, disclosure, enforcement, or administration;
2. implementation is heterogeneous across places and agencies;
3. workers may not know the reform or may not be able to invoke it;
4. firms may comply, evade, adapt, screen, or reallocate;
5. equilibrium responses affect the ultimate labor-market incidence and welfare consequences.

## Core points

- Institutional reform is not a binary “law passed / law failed” object.
- The same reform can have different effects because exposure, implementation, worker knowledge, and firm adaptation differ across contexts.
- Labor-market effects may appear on wages, hiring, firing, contract form, turnover, promotion, and informality, not just employment levels.
- Worker beliefs and information can be as important as statutory protection.
- Firm adaptation and equilibrium responses are central for interpretation and welfare.

## Required reform families

### 1. Employment protection / dismissal reform
Use wrongful-discharge or dismissal-protection reforms to show that legal protection changes labor outcomes only through the margins where it binds, and that effects vary by firm structure, worker type, and contractual environment.

**Must-use anchor:**
- [@autorDonohueSchwab2004]

### 2. Enforcement expansions and state capacity in practice
Show that labor law enforcement is mediated by local implementation capacity and that stronger enforcement can affect formality, wages, employment, and compliance margins differently across local labor markets.

**Must-use anchor:**
- [@almeidaCarneiro2012]

### 3. Worker knowledge / beliefs about legal rights
Make clear that institutional change can be muted if workers and firms do not know the law or misperceive its reach. This is one of the cleanest ways to connect institutional economics and labor behavior.

**Must-use anchor:**
- [@bertrandCrepon2021]

### 4. Formalization and compliance reforms
This should connect to Week 3, but from the **reform** side rather than the dualism side. The focus here is: what happens when governments change the costs, information, or enforcement margins that govern formalization?

**Key references:**
- [@ulyssea2018]
- [@deAndradeBruhnMckenzie2016]

### 5. Equal-opportunity / transparency / procedural reforms
The point is not simply “discrimination exists.” It is to show how reforms to procedures, transparency, and information can change hiring, wage dispersion, bargaining, and inequality—and why their effects vary by workplace and sector.

**Key references:**
- [@goldinRouse2000]
- [@bakerHalberstamKroftMasMessacar2023]
- [@blundellDuchiniSimionTurrell2025]

### 6. Administrative / organizational reform
At least one example should show that reform may operate through the organization of matching, benefit administration, or public labor-market services rather than only through private contracting.

**Key reference:**
- [@dustmannFitzenbergerSchonbergSpitzOener2014]

## Empirical and methodological spine

This week should explicitly connect reform settings to the empirical methods students see in the literature:

- staggered policy adoption / event study
- difference-in-differences with heterogeneous exposure
- randomized information or legal-knowledge interventions
- enforcement-intensity variation
- threshold designs and administrative coverage rules
- firm-worker linked data to trace adjustment margins
- distributional and spillover analysis, not just mean employment effects

The chapter should repeatedly ask:
- what is the identifying variation?
- what margin is observed?
- whose adjustment is visible: workers, firms, or both?
- what part of the transmission chain is being estimated?

## Research Lab direction

The Research Lab should not be generic. It should teach students how to think like reform researchers.

### Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer

**Primary anchor:** [@bertrandCrepon2021]  
Why: a clear design where institutional change may be limited by legal ignorance and information frictions.

**Challenge anchor:** [@almeidaCarneiro2012]  
Why: shows how enforcement intensity, compliance, and informal-sector margins interact.

**Optional extension:** [@bakerHalberstamKroftMasMessacar2023]  
Why: a modern transparency reform with wage-distribution and bargaining implications.

### Diagnostic questions for students
- Does the reform change rights, incentives, information, procedures, or enforcement?
- Which workers and firms are actually exposed?
- What prevents mechanical take-up?
- What are the firm adaptation margins?
- What outcomes are short-run vs equilibrium?
- What welfare object is plausible: wages, employment, formality, bargaining power, or information quality?

### Transfer ideas
Possible bounded extension exercises:
- apply the same design logic to another legal-information or transparency policy;
- test heterogeneity by firm size, union status, informality exposure, or labor-market tightness;
- compare intensive-margin vs extensive-margin labor responses to the same reform.
