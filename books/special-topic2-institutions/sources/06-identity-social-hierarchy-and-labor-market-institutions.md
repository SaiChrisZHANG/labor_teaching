## Central question

How do institutionalized social categories structure access to jobs, networks, authority, protection, and mobility in labor markets?

## Why this is specifically labor economics

Social hierarchy becomes a labor-market institution when it changes hiring, promotion, occupational access, bargaining, mobility, legal protection, and exposure to exploitation. This week studies caste, race, ethnicity, gender, religion, migrant status, and related categories not mainly as beliefs about conduct, but as durable systems that allocate labor-market opportunities and constraints.

## Boundary relative to Week 5

Week 5 focused on **norms about conduct and acceptable labor-market behavior**.

Week 6 should focus on **durable social categories and institutionalized hierarchy**.

That means the emphasis now shifts to:
- access and exclusion,
- occupation boundaries,
- employer beliefs and discrimination,
- networks and referrals,
- credential recognition,
- public rules and state action,
- local governance and protection,
- residential and spatial sorting.

Norms may still matter, but they should now appear as part of a broader system of hierarchy rather than as standalone behavioral expectations.

## Narrative backbone

### 1. From social category to labor-market institution

Open by making clear that not every identity marker becomes a labor-market institution. It becomes one when it systematically structures:
- who gets hired,
- who is trusted,
- who is promoted,
- whose credentials are recognized,
- who can migrate safely,
- who gets access to networks,
- who is covered by protection,
- and who bears greater risk of exploitation.

### 2. Main institutional channels of hierarchy

Organize the lecture around six channels.

#### A. Employer beliefs and differential treatment
Examples:
- resume screening,
- customer-facing discrimination,
- name, accent, or credential signals,
- stereotypes about productivity or fit.

Use:
- `[@bertrandMullainathan2004]`
- `[@oreopoulos2011]`

#### B. Networks, referrals, and community organization
Examples:
- caste or ethnic networks,
- social insurance and mobility tradeoffs,
- referrals and segmentation,
- community-based occupation persistence.

Use:
- `[@munshiRosenzweig2006]`

#### C. Occupational boundaries and talent misallocation
Examples:
- barriers to entering high-return occupations,
- exclusion from managerial/professional tracks,
- macro losses from restricted access.

Use:
- `[@hsiehHurstJonesKlenow2019]`

#### D. Public rules, state action, and local governance
Examples:
- segregated public employment,
- government-imposed hierarchy,
- legal status categories,
- unequal enforcement or protection.

Use:
- `[@anejaXu2020]`

#### E. Contact, integration, and hierarchy reproduction
Examples:
- whether cross-group contact reduces exclusion,
- collaborative versus adversarial environments,
- how institutions mediate social distance.

Use:
- `[@lowe2021]`

#### F. Stratification, persistence, and intergroup inequality
Examples:
- hierarchy reproduced through labor-market institutions over time,
- why gaps persist even when some formal rules change,
- how hierarchy operates at both micro and aggregate levels.

Use:
- `[@darity2022]`

### 3. Empirical domains that should be visible

The chapter should explicitly show students multiple empirical domains:
- race and employer callbacks,
- caste networks and occupational mobility,
- migrant status and credential recognition,
- public-sector segregation and state action,
- talent misallocation under race/gender barriers,
- institutional designs that reduce or reinforce social distance.

### 4. Measurement and identification

A major goal of the week is to show that hierarchy is identified through institutions, not just inferred from wage gaps.

The chapter should include explicit discussion of:
- audit studies,
- credential / resume field experiments,
- historical or institutional policy shocks,
- matched worker–firm or occupational-allocation evidence,
- network and migration designs,
- field experiments on contact and integration,
- macro or structural quantification of misallocation.

The chapter must repeatedly ask:
- Is this a price or skill story, or an access story?
- Is the relevant mechanism employer beliefs, networks, law, or local governance?
- Which margins are worker-side and which are institutional?

### 5. Frontier and open questions

Potential frontier questions:
- How should labor economists connect discrimination, hierarchy, and misallocation in one framework?
- How do migrant legal status and credential recognition interact with labor demand?
- Which institutions weaken hierarchy and which merely shift it?
- How do local governments and public employers reproduce or mitigate labor-market hierarchy?
- How should researchers distinguish conduct norms (Week 5) from category-based hierarchy (Week 6) empirically?

where:
- {math}`B_j` = employer beliefs or screening rules,
- {math}`N_i` = network access,

And/or a decomposition like:
```{math}
:label: eq:talent-misallocation-week6
Y = Y^*(1 - \mu_H),
```
where {math}`\mu_H` captures output lost because hierarchy restricts occupational allocation.

## Core points that should be visible near the top

- Week 6 is about category-based hierarchy and institutionalized access, not just norms of conduct;
- social categories become labor-market institutions when they structure hiring, promotion, protection, mobility, and occupational boundaries;
- labor economists study hierarchy through employer beliefs, networks, public rules, local governance, and misallocation;
- hierarchy can persist even when some formal rules improve;
- frontier work links micro discrimination, network exclusion, and macro talent misallocation.

Bounded transfer directions:
- compare belief-based and access-based barriers,
- reinterpret occupational sorting through network or credential channels,
- link micro discrimination evidence to broader allocation or segregation patterns,
- distinguish conduct norms (Week 5) from structural hierarchy (Week 6).

The bounded teaching path must run locally without confidential data.
