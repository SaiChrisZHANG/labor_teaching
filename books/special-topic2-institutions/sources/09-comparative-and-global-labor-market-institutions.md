# Week 9. Comparative and Global Labor-Market Institutions

## Central question

How do labor-market institutions differ across development settings, welfare states, migration regimes, and global production systems, and which lessons travel across contexts?

## Position in the course

Weeks 1–8 established that labor-market institutions work through law, enforcement, informality, worker voice, norms, hierarchy, persistence, and reform. Week 9 steps back and asks how those institutional margins compare across settings. The goal is not to produce a tourist guide to national labor systems. It is to teach students how to think comparatively, how to use cross-setting evidence without overclaiming, and how to frame country-specific evidence so it speaks to a broad labor-economics audience.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Comparative work is valuable when it identifies a labor mechanism that travels, not when it treats one country as interesting by default.
- Formal rules, effective enforcement, coverage, and equilibrium incidence can diverge sharply across settings.
- Welfare states, bargaining systems, informality regimes, migration regimes, and global production systems are all labor-market institutions, but they operate at different levels and through different margins.
- Country-specific evidence is broadly interesting when it is mapped to a comparative institutional taxonomy, makes the mechanism explicit, and states clearly what should and should not transport.
:::

## Bridge

Week 8 studied why similar reforms produce different labor-market effects across places, firms, and workers. Week 9 broadens that insight into a comparative framework: even before reform occurs, institutional environments differ systematically in coverage, enforcement, complementarity, and exposure to global forces. Week 10 will then ask what these comparative differences imply for synthesis, frontier questions, and student research design.

## Field Core

### 1. Why comparative labor-market institutions matter

Comparative work matters because labor-market outcomes are rarely produced by a single institutional margin. Employment protection, bargaining coverage, unemployment insurance, migration rules, informal enforcement, and supply-chain governance interact. The same legal text can bind in one setting and barely matter in another. A proper comparative lecture therefore starts from **institutional bundles** rather than isolated policies [@freeman2007LaborMarketInstitutions; @blanchardWolfers2000].

A useful comparative decomposition has four parts:

1. **rules on paper**: what law or policy formally states;
2. **coverage and effective enforcement**: who is actually reached;
3. **complementary institutions**: how the rule interacts with bargaining, welfare, migration, or production systems;
4. **equilibrium incidence**: how firms, workers, and markets adjust.

That decomposition helps students compare advanced welfare states, emerging-market informality regimes, and transnational supply-chain governance without pretending they are the same object.

### 2. Comparative domains

#### Welfare states, unemployment, and employment protection

A first comparative domain is the interaction between social insurance, employment protection, and macro shocks. The classic lesson from [@blanchardWolfers2000] is not that one institution mechanically causes unemployment. It is that shocks and institutions interact, and that similar macro shocks can generate very different labor-market paths depending on the surrounding institutional environment. Comparative labor-market work here is about interaction, complementarity, and persistence rather than about one-dimensional institutional rankings.

#### Labor regulation and cross-country institutional indices

A second domain is broad comparative measurement. [@boteroDjankovLaPortaLopezdeSilanesShleifer2004] remains a canonical example of how researchers construct cross-country labor-regulation measures and relate them to politics, legal origin, and development. The paper is useful not because index work is the final answer, but because it forces students to confront comparability problems directly: what counts as a comparable unit of labor regulation, what is lost when legal text is coded into indices, and how much implementation heterogeneity hides behind the same numerical score.

#### Informality and development settings

A third domain is the comparison between high-enforcement and low-enforcement labor markets. Informality is not simply “missing institutions.” It is a distinct institutional equilibrium involving registration, taxation, labor inspection, contract form, worker protection, and state capacity. Comparative work on informality asks how labor institutions shape the allocation of workers and firms across formal and informal margins and how those margins differ across development settings [@ulyssea2020InformalityReview].

#### Migration regimes and labor-market integration

A fourth domain is migration policy as labor-market institution. Immigration restrictions, migrant rights, and integration rules affect not only migration flows but wage-setting, occupational allocation, and worker–firm matching. [@beerliPeriRuffnerSiegenthaler2021] is especially useful here because it shows how opening a labor market to cross-border workers changes firm and worker outcomes in ways that are legible far beyond the Swiss setting. The lesson is not “Switzerland is special,” but that a well-defined migration regime can be used to study labor-market integration, firm adaptation, and skill-specific incidence.

#### Global production systems and private labor regulation

A fifth domain moves beyond the nation-state. Global supply chains create labor governance systems that combine private codes, third-party audits, buyer pressure, local state enforcement, and worker voice. This is why comparative labor institutions cannot stop at welfare states and labor law. Research on global production shows that labor conditions can be shaped by transnational institutions that are neither fully public nor fully private [@locke2013PromiseLimitsPrivatePower; @distelhorstHainmuellerLocke2017].

### 3. Method / framing box: how to make country-specific evidence broadly interesting

:::{admonition} Method / framing box: making country-specific research travel
:class: note
A country setting becomes broadly interesting when the paper does at least four things clearly:

1. **Names the labor mechanism.**  
   Do not lead with “my country has policy X.” Lead with the labor question: wage-setting, integration, informality, worker protection, sorting, bargaining, or supply-chain governance.

2. **Maps the setting into a comparative taxonomy.**  
   Explain whether the country is informative because it is a welfare-state regime, an informality regime, a migration regime, a supply-chain node, a bargaining system, or a hybrid case.

3. **Explains the source of leverage.**  
   Why does this setting identify the mechanism unusually cleanly? A discontinuity, reform timing, administrative data system, cross-regional exposure, firm linkage, or unusually sharp institutional contrast should be stated explicitly.

4. **States transportability honestly.**  
   Be explicit about what should travel, what may not, and why. The audience does not need to know the country already; it needs to understand the mechanism and the limits of extrapolation.

Common critiques of country-specific evidence:
- the setting is too idiosyncratic;
- the policy is bundled with many other institutions;
- measurement is not comparable to other countries;
- implementation differs from legal text;
- equilibrium feedback would differ elsewhere;
- the paper claims broad portability without a comparative argument.

A good response is not to claim universal validity. It is to show where the evidence sits in a comparative map and which mechanism the setting isolates [@allcott2015SiteSelectionBias; @dehejiaPopElechesSamii2015ExternalValidity].
:::

### 4. What travels, and what does not

Comparative labor work is strongest when it distinguishes **mechanism portability** from **estimate portability**. A mechanism can be widely relevant even when the effect size is setting-specific. For example:

- the mechanism “migration restrictions alter worker–firm matching and labor supply composition” may travel broadly, even if the size of the effect depends on local bargaining institutions and firm technology [@beerliPeriRuffnerSiegenthaler2021];
- the mechanism “global buyers can reshape local labor standards through private governance” may travel broadly, even if the effectiveness of private regulation depends heavily on state capacity, worker voice, and buyer commitment [@locke2013PromiseLimitsPrivatePower; @distelhorstHainmuellerLocke2017].

This distinction is useful for PhD students because it lowers the pressure to claim that one country stands for the world. The more credible goal is usually to show **which mechanism is being learned about**, **which institutional dimensions are essential**, and **how the finding fits into a broader comparative literature**.

## Research Lab

The Week 9 lab should be comparative in spirit, even if it uses a bounded public-data path. A good lab sequence is:

1. **Reproduce** a small comparative institutional fact or reform contrast.
2. **Diagnose** what the original design identifies and what is setting-specific.
3. **Transfer** the design to a new country, region, or institutional comparison using public data.

### Suggested lab anchors

- **Primary anchor:** [@boteroDjankovLaPortaLopezdeSilanesShleifer2004]  
  Useful for teaching index construction, comparative institutional measurement, and the dangers of relying on rules-on-paper alone.

- **Challenge anchor:** [@beerliPeriRuffnerSiegenthaler2021]  
  Useful for showing how a country-specific institutional reform can answer a broad labor question when the mechanism is explicit.

- **Optional extension:** [@distelhorstHainmuellerLocke2017]  
  Useful for expanding the comparative unit beyond countries to global production networks and private labor regulation.

### What students should learn from the lab

- how to turn a country or institutional setting into a mechanism-based contribution;
- how to specify the relevant comparison class;
- how to think about external validity without hand-waving;
- how to separate the labor mechanism from the local narrative.
