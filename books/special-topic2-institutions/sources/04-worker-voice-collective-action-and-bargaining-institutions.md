# Week 4 source pack: Worker voice, collective action, and bargaining institutions

## Central question

How do worker organizations and bargaining institutions emerge, how do they change wages, rents, governance, and inequality, and what political spillovers do they generate beyond the workplace?

## Why this week matters

Weeks 2 and 3 emphasized law, enforcement, informality, and contract enforceability. Week 4 turns to a different institutional response to fragmented individual bargaining: **collective worker organization**. This includes unions, sectoral bargaining, works councils, board-level representation, strike threats, and other forms of coordinated worker voice.

To many students, this topic will look closest to political economy. The lecture should acknowledge that proximity, but keep the labor-economics core front and center. The central labor questions are: how collective institutions form, who they cover, how they change wage-setting and rent-sharing, how they alter governance inside firms, how they shape spillovers for nonunion workers, and how they change the distribution of labor-market power.

This week should also be explicit that collective bargaining is not just about “union wage premia.” It is about **organization**, **coverage**, **voice**, **governance**, and **political spillovers**. The comparative angle matters because unions, worker representation, and bargaining institutions vary enormously across countries in whether they operate at the firm, industry, or board level, and in how they interact with law, product markets, and macro inequality.

## Core points to communicate

- Collective institutions are labor-market institutions for overcoming fragmented individual bargaining.
- The key objects are not only union membership, but also **organization**, **coverage**, **representation**, and **voice**.
- Collective bargaining changes more than wages: it can alter turnover, retention, governance, information sharing, rents, and inequality.
- Worker organization itself is an equilibrium object shaped by law, labor-market tightness, employer opposition, organizing costs, and institutional design.
- Collective institutions can spill over to nonunion workers through threat effects, coverage rules, norms, and wage structure compression.
- The labor-economics core is positive and organizational: how worker power forms, how it operates, and how it changes labor-market allocation.
- Political spillovers matter, but they should be taught as extensions of labor-market organization rather than as a separate political-science topic.

## Conceptual structure for the lecture

### Bridge
Begin by connecting Week 4 to Weeks 2 and 3. If weak enforcement and incomplete contracting leave workers atomized, one institutional response is collective organization. The lecture should frame collective bargaining and worker voice as organizational technologies for altering surplus division, information flows, and workplace governance.

### Field Core

#### A. What counts as worker voice and collective institutions?
Distinguish clearly among:
- union membership,
- collective bargaining coverage,
- enterprise bargaining,
- sectoral / centralized bargaining,
- works councils,
- codetermination / board representation,
- strike threats and collective action capacity,
- formal union structures vs informal worker organization.

The chapter should make clear that “unionization” is only one piece of a larger institutional menu.

#### B. How are unions and collective institutions formed?
This section should be more explicit than the standard union-effects lecture. Students should learn the margins of organization formation:
- worker demand for representation,
- organizing costs,
- employer opposition,
- labor-market tightness and outside options,
- certification-election rules,
- legal thresholds and recognition procedures,
- broader institutional design (extension rules, sectoral agreements, works councils).

The point is that organization is not exogenous. It depends on labor-market conditions and legal design. Use union certification elections as a central institutional setting and discuss why close-election designs are so important for causal work [@dinardoLee2004; @frandsen2021]. Use Pezold–Jäger–Nüss to show how labor-market conditions affect willingness to organize [@pezoldJagerNuss2023].

#### C. Wages, rents, and bargaining institutions
This section should cover:
- wage premia,
- rent-sharing,
- compression of the wage structure,
- within-firm and between-firm incidence,
- how bargaining structure affects wage coordination,
- why coverage and membership can have distinct effects.

Use Jäger–Naidu–Schoefer and Fortin–Lemieux–Lloyd to keep the coverage/spillover distinction explicit [@jagerNaiduSchoefer2024; @fortinLemieuxLloyd2021].

#### D. Governance, information sharing, and workplace voice
This section should go beyond wage-setting and teach worker voice as governance. Compare:
- traditional collective bargaining,
- works councils,
- codetermination,
- information-sharing institutions,
- shop-floor representation vs board representation.

Use Harju–Jäger–Schoefer and Jäger–Schoefer–Heining to show that representation can affect productivity, information flows, and governance even when wage effects are limited or indirect [@harjuJagerSchoefer2021; @jagerSchoeferHeining2022].

#### E. Spillovers to nonunion workers
This section should make explicit that collective institutions affect more than the organized workers themselves:
- threat effects on nonunion wages,
- spillovers to wage norms and compression,
- product-market responses,
- firm composition and coverage spillovers,
- inequality and labor-share implications.

Students should leave the lecture understanding why the welfare and incidence of union institutions cannot be evaluated only at the organized workplace.

#### F. Political spillovers and feedback
This section should be shorter than the labor-market core, but it should be clearly there. Organize it around two directions:
1. **unions to politics**:
   - turnout,
   - political participation,
   - mobilization,
   - policy advocacy,
   - party coalitions;
2. **politics to unions**:
   - right-to-work laws,
   - legal support or hostility to organizing,
   - public-sector bargaining rules,
   - how political institutions shape worker organization.

Keep this framed as an extension of labor-market organization, not a switch to generic politics [@freeman2003; @feigenbaumHertelFernandezWilliamson2018; @kaplanNaidu2024].

#### G. Comparative and institutional variation
Close the Field Core by emphasizing that worker voice institutions vary across countries in:
- coverage without membership,
- extension mechanisms,
- union certification rules,
- board representation,
- works-council regimes,
- enforcement and strike law.

This is where the lecture should feel most explicitly comparative.

## Methods and identification priorities

The chapter should explicitly distinguish:
- close certification-election regression discontinuity,
- matched employer-employee event-study designs,
- policy reforms to bargaining rights or right-to-work laws,
- cross-country institutional comparison,
- worker-representation threshold designs,
- distributional decompositions for spillovers and coverage effects,
- survey/field-experiment evidence on organizing demand.

The lecture must always name the observed margin:
- organizing success,
- wages,
- employment,
- payroll,
- inequality,
- productivity,
- turnover,
- worker voice,
- voting / political participation.

## Research Lab

### Primary lab anchor
- DiNardo and Lee [@dinardoLee2004]

### Secondary / challenge anchor
- Frandsen [@frandsen2021]

### Spillover / inequality extension
- Fortin, Lemieux, and Lloyd [@fortinLemieuxLloyd2021]

### Voice / governance extension
- Harju, Jäger, and Schoefer [@harjuJagerSchoefer2021]

### Political spillover extension
- Feigenbaum, Hertel-Fernandez, and Williamson [@feigenbaumHertelFernandezWilliamson2018]

The lab should help students diagnose:
- how worker organizations form,
- what close-election designs identify,
- the difference between direct effects and spillovers,
- why governance/voice effects are distinct from wage effects,
- how political spillovers can be framed as downstream consequences of labor institutions.

Bounded transfer ideas:
- compare direct unionization effects to spillover effects on uncovered workers,
- reinterpret a close-election paper in terms of organization formation rather than just treatment effects,
- map a legal reform to both labor-market and political-outcome margins.
