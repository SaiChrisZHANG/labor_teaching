## Central question

How do shared norms about work, family, reputation, competition, leadership, and acceptable labor-market behavior shape labor supply, search, occupational entry, entrepreneurship, and wage acceptance?

## Why this is specifically labor economics

This week treats culture and norms as labor-market allocation mechanisms. The point is not to explain all social hierarchy; the point is to show how shared expectations change who works, which jobs are acceptable, how workers search, whether they compete for promotions, how households allocate time, and how communities discipline labor supply. The labor-market connection should remain explicit at all times.

## Boundary relative to Week 6

To keep Weeks 5 and 6 cohesive but non-overlapping, Week 5 should focus on **norms about conduct, role expectations, and acceptable labor-market behavior**.

Examples:
- family-role norms,
- norms about women’s and men’s work,
- religion and reputation norms governing public work,
- competitiveness norms,
- networking / self-promotion norms,
- work ethic, unemployment stigma, and wage-acceptance norms.

Week 6 should then take up **durable social categories and institutionalized hierarchy**:
- caste,
- race,
- ethnicity,
- migrant status,
- institutionalized gender hierarchy,
- occupation boundaries,
- employer beliefs,
- public rules,
- local governance.

So in Week 5, categories may appear, but they should enter mainly as carriers of **behavioral expectations**, not as full systems of structural hierarchy. That is Week 6.

## Narrative backbone

### 1. Why norms matter for labor-market allocation

Open by distinguishing norms from:
- prices,
- legal rules,
- pure technology,
- formal institutions,
- and individual taste shocks.

Norms should be presented as shared expectations enforced socially or psychologically through:
- approval and stigma,
- identity payoffs,
- reputational costs,
- family obligations,
- status rewards,
- and beliefs about acceptable work.

### 2. A broad but labor-focused taxonomy of norms

#### A. Family and gender-role norms
Examples:
- norms about mothers’ work,
- breadwinner expectations,
- care obligations,
- acceptable occupations,
- work–family specialization.

Use:
- `[@alesinaGiulianoNunn2013]`
- `[@fernandezFogli2009]`
- `[@jayachandran2021]`

#### B. Religion, morality, and reputation norms
Examples:
- norms governing public visibility,
- women’s mobility,
- acceptable sectors of work,
- reputation-preserving ways to work outside the home.

Use:
- `[@carvalho2013]`

#### C. Competition, self-promotion, and leadership-entry norms
Examples:
- willingness to enter competitive jobs,
- reluctance to apply for jobs with aggressive or tournament framing,
- norms around self-promotion,
- networking and informal advancement practices.

Use:
- `[@floryLeibbrandtList2015]`
- `[@delfino2024]`
- `[@cullenPerezTruglia2023]`

#### D. Work ethic, unemployment, and wage-acceptance norms
Examples:
- stigma of unemployment,
- acceptable wages,
- norms around refusing or accepting certain work,
- collective suppression or discipline of labor supply.

Use:
- `[@clark2003]`
- `[@brezaKaurKrishnaswamy2019]`
- `[@eugsterLaliveSteinhauerZweimuller2017]`

### 3. Mechanisms

The chapter should explicitly connect norms to labor-market mechanisms:
- labor-force participation,
- time allocation,
- search intensity,
- reservation jobs,
- entrepreneurship,
- occupational entry,
- promotion entry,
- leadership aspiration,
- migration/mobility,
- reservation wages or wage acceptance.

### 4. Measurement and identification

A major goal of the week is to show that labor economists measure norms rather than invoke them residually.

The chapter should include explicit discussion of:
- inherited-culture and ancestry designs,
- historical norm proxies,
- second-generation / migrant designs,
- job-entry field experiments,
- workplace or promotion data,
- local social-environment variation,
- market-level labor-supply experiments.

The chapter must keep asking:
- Is this norm or price?
- Is this norm or legal constraint?
- Is this norm or selection?
- What is inherited versus currently socially enforced?

### 5. Frontier and open questions

Potential frontier questions:
- How do firm practices interact with work-role norms?
- How fast do norms change when policy, technology, or markets change?
- How do norms shape leadership pipelines and entrepreneurship?
- How do community norms affect local labor-supply equilibrium?
- When do norms survive formal equalization of legal rules?

## Core points that should be visible near the top

- norms are labor-market allocation mechanisms, not residual explanations;
- Week 5 focuses on norms about work, family, competition, reputation, and acceptable labor-market conduct;
- Week 6 will focus on structural social hierarchy and durable category-based exclusion;
- economists identify norms using inheritance, history, experiments, workplace data, and local variation;
- frontier work asks how norms interact with firms, communities, and changing labor markets.

## Research Lab design

Use the standard Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer structure.

Suggested anchors:
- Primary anchor: `[@fernandezFogli2009]`
- Secondary / challenge anchor: `[@floryLeibbrandtList2015]`
- Religion-norm extension: `[@carvalho2013]`
- Leadership/networking extension: `[@cullenPerezTruglia2023]`
- Work-norm extension: `[@brezaKaurKrishnaswamy2019]`

Bounded transfer directions:
- compare norm proxies with price or constraint proxies,
- reinterpret job-entry experiments as acceptable-workplace norms,
- contrast inherited-culture and current-social-pressure designs,
- compare participation/entry margins with promotion/leadership margins.

The bounded teaching path must run locally without confidential data.
