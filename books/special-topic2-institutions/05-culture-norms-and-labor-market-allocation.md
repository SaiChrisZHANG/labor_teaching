# Culture, Norms, and Labor-Market Allocation

## Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 5, students should be able to:

1. explain why norms are labor-market allocation mechanisms rather than residual explanations;
2. distinguish family and gender-role norms, religion and reputation norms, competition and self-promotion norms, networking and leadership-entry norms, and work ethic or wage-acceptance norms;
3. map norms into labor supply, search, occupational entry, entrepreneurship, migration, promotion entry, and wage acceptance;
4. separate Week 5 conduct and role norms from Week 6 structural hierarchy and durable category-based exclusion;
5. evaluate inherited-culture, historical persistence, field-experimental, workplace, local social-environment, and market-level norm designs by naming what each design identifies and what it leaves unresolved.

Week 5 asks how shared expectations about work, family, reputation, competition, leadership, and acceptable labor-market conduct shape allocation. The economic question is not whether "culture matters" in a vague sense. The question is how norms enter worker and household decisions, how they change the feasible or acceptable job set, and how economists can separate norm channels from prices, law, technology, and selection.

Weeks 2 to 4 studied formal rules, enforcement, informality, contract enforcement, worker organization, and collective bargaining. Week 5 turns to informal institutions. Norms can allocate workers before a formal contract is signed, before a union campaign begins, and before an employer observes productivity. They can make some jobs socially legitimate, some search strategies reputationally costly, some promotions uncomfortable to pursue, and some wage offers unacceptable even when they are privately profitable.

This week is deliberately bounded. It is about norms around conduct, roles, reputation, competition, leadership entry, work effort, unemployment, and wage acceptance. Week 6 will study caste, race, ethnicity, migrant status, and other durable social categories as structural systems of hierarchy. Categories can appear in Week 5 when they carry expectations about acceptable behavior, but the full analysis of category-based exclusion, institutionalized hierarchy, occupation boundaries, and employer beliefs belongs to Week 6.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important

- Norms are labor-market allocation mechanisms, not residual explanations after prices and laws have been exhausted.
- Week 5 focuses on conduct and role norms: work, family, competition, reputation, networking, leadership entry, unemployment, and acceptable wages.
- Week 6 focuses on structural social hierarchy and durable category-based exclusion.
- Economists identify norm channels using inherited-culture variation, historical persistence, field experiments, workplace and promotion data, local social-environment variation, and market-level labor-supply experiments.
- Frontier work asks how norms interact with firms, communities, technology, policy, and changing labor demand.

:::

## Bridge

Week 4 studied worker voice and bargaining institutions. Collective institutions change labor markets by aggregating worker claims, changing threat points, and governing wage-setting. Norms operate earlier and more diffusely. They shape whether a worker searches at all, whether a household views market work as appropriate, whether a job advertisement feels acceptable, whether a worker enters a promotion contest, whether community members punish low wage acceptance, and whether unemployment carries social stigma.

The conceptual move is to treat a norm as a shared expectation with consequences. A norm can be enforced by family approval, community reputation, religious standing, peer comparison, identity payoffs, shame, stigma, or beliefs about what a respectable worker should do. Those consequences enter the same labor decisions as wages, commuting costs, childcare constraints, legal rules, and employer screening. The point is not that norms replace standard labor economics. The point is that norms change the choice set, the payoff function, and the meaning of observed choices.

Guiso, Sapienza, and Zingales provide the broad frame: cultural beliefs can affect economic outcomes when they persist, are shared, and change behavior [@guisoSapienzaZingales2006]. In a labor course, that frame becomes disciplined only when it is attached to labor-market margins. Fernandez and Fogli use inherited culture to study women's work and fertility in a host-country setting where current prices and institutions are partly held fixed [@fernandezFogli2009]. Alesina, Giuliano, and Nunn connect historical agricultural technology to persistent gender-role norms and modern female labor-market outcomes [@alesinaGiulianoNunn2013]. Jayachandran synthesizes how norms can restrict women's employment in developing-country labor markets without reducing the mechanism to preferences alone [@jayachandran2021].

A compact allocation object is:

```{math}
:label: eq:norm-allocation-week5
U_{ij} = w_{ij} - c_{ij} - s_{ij}(a_{ij}, n_i) + \varepsilon_{ij},
```

where worker or household {math}`i` chooses action or job {math}`j`; {math}`w_{ij}` is the wage or material return; {math}`c_{ij}` is the direct cost of taking the job or action; {math}`s_{ij}(a_{ij}, n_i)` is the social cost or reward attached to action {math}`a_{ij}` under norm environment {math}`n_i`; and {math}`\varepsilon_{ij}` is an idiosyncratic component. Equation {eq}`eq:norm-allocation-week5` is not a full structural model. It is a teaching map. It makes clear that a norm can change allocation even if wages and formal constraints are unchanged.

## Field Core

### Norms As Labor-Market Mechanisms

Norms are not a label for everything we cannot explain. A norm is useful in labor economics only when it specifies a behavior, an enforcement channel, and a labor margin. A statement such as "culture lowers employment" is too loose. A better statement is: a local norm penalizes mothers' market work through reputational costs, which raises the social cost of accepting a public-facing job and lowers female labor-force participation even when wages are available. That sentence names the conduct rule, the enforcement mechanism, and the outcome.

The mechanism can operate through several margins. First, norms can shift labor supply by changing the perceived cost of work or nonwork. Second, they can alter time allocation within households by making care obligations gendered or status-coded. Third, they can change search intensity by making some search activities visible, shameful, or encouraged. Fourth, they can shrink the acceptable job set by ruling out particular sectors, schedules, locations, or public interactions. Fifth, they can affect occupational entry, entrepreneurship, and migration by attaching status or reputational costs to mobility, risk, and public work. Sixth, they can change promotion and leadership pipelines when self-promotion, networking, or competitive entry are normatively coded. Seventh, they can influence reservation wages and wage acceptance through work ethic, unemployment stigma, and community discipline.

The same observed outcome can have many explanations. Low female employment could reflect wages, childcare prices, safety, legal restrictions, discrimination, household bargaining, or social expectations. Low entry into a competitive job could reflect risk preferences, expected ability, job amenities, discrimination, or norms around competition. Low migration could reflect credit constraints, networks, insurance, housing, credential rules, or obligations to remain near family. A norm interpretation becomes credible only when the evidence narrows these alternatives.

The Week 5 taxonomy therefore starts from norm domains and immediately attaches them to labor outcomes.

### Family And Gender-Role Norms

Family and gender-role norms define which household members are expected to earn, care, search, migrate, or specialize. They can make employment socially costly for mothers, make breadwinner identity central for men, assign unpaid care obligations to women, or define some occupations as inappropriate. In equation {eq}`eq:norm-allocation-week5`, these norms change {math}`s_{ij}(a_{ij}, n_i)` for work, hours, occupation, schedule, commuting, and care choices.

Fernandez and Fogli are the Week 5 lab anchor because they use ancestry-based variation among U.S.-born women to ask whether inherited cultural proxies predict work and fertility behavior in a common host-country environment [@fernandezFogli2009]. The key teaching point is not that ancestry is a perfect measure of culture. It is that second-generation and inherited-culture designs try to hold current labor-market prices and institutions more constant while using origin-country variation as a proxy for transmitted norms.

Alesina, Giuliano, and Nunn move the same question into historical persistence. Their plough-use design links historical agricultural technology to persistent gender-role beliefs and modern female labor-market outcomes [@alesinaGiulianoNunn2013]. The design is powerful because it gives a reason why a particular norm might have emerged and persisted. It is limited because historical proxies can bundle technology, institutions, ecology, and selection. The labor interpretation must still pass through work, specialization, and acceptable roles.

Jayachandran's synthesis helps keep the developing-country labor problem explicit [@jayachandran2021]. Norms can constrain women's employment even when there are returns to work. They may interact with safety, transport, childcare, family bargaining, public visibility, and employer behavior. The lecture should not collapse all of this into "preferences." A woman may prefer to work in a private sense and still face social costs that make visible work unattractive or infeasible.

### Religion, Reputation, And Acceptable Work

Religion and reputation norms often operate by regulating public visibility, mobility, mixed-gender interaction, sector choice, and the reputational meaning of work. The labor-market object is not religion as identity alone. It is how a community's expectations define which jobs preserve status and which jobs threaten it.

Carvalho's work on veiling is a useful anchor because it shows how religious and reputation-preserving conduct can reshape labor-market and public choices [@carvalho2013]. In a Week 5 frame, veiling is not treated as a generic cultural marker. It is a conduct technology and signal embedded in a social environment. It may allow some forms of public participation while preserving reputation, or it may index a broader set of community expectations about mobility and work. The empirical question is what labor margin moves: participation, sector choice, entrepreneurship, commuting, public-facing work, or household bargaining.

This category illustrates the Week 5/Week 6 boundary. Religion can be a conduct norm in Week 5 when the relevant mechanism is reputation, acceptable work, or public behavior. Religion can also be a structural category in Week 6 when it organizes exclusion, employer beliefs, residential sorting, legal treatment, or group hierarchy. The same social label can therefore enter different weeks through different mechanisms. Week 5 asks what conduct is rewarded or punished. Week 6 asks how durable categories structure access, authority, and protection.

### Competition, Self-Promotion, And Job Entry

Some norms operate at the moment of job entry. Competitive framing, tournament pay, self-promotion, and aggressive application language can change who applies, even if the job's material returns are attractive. The labor outcome is entry into a job, occupation, or career track; the mechanism is whether the environment feels acceptable or identity-consistent.

Flory, Leibbrandt, and List use a large-scale natural field experiment to study whether competitive workplaces deter female applicants [@floryLeibbrandtList2015]. The design is useful because it randomizes features of the job-entry environment and observes application behavior. It does not need to infer norms only from aggregate employment gaps. Still, it identifies application responses to a particular recruitment and compensation framing, not a complete theory of gendered labor supply.

Delfino extends the entry-margin logic by studying gender barriers and job-entry behavior in a setting where men may be reluctant to enter jobs coded as female or "pink-collar" [@delfino2024]. That point matters for Week 5 because norms are not only about women's work. Masculinity norms, status expectations, and occupational labels can affect men's entry decisions, occupational sorting, and willingness to accept socially coded jobs. A broad taxonomy prevents the week from becoming only a lecture on female labor-force participation.

The acceptable-job-set version of the same idea is:

```{math}
:label: eq:acceptable-job-set-week5
P(\text{enter } j) = f(w_j, x_i, \text{constraints}_i, \text{norm fit}_{ij}).
```

Here {math}`\text{norm fit}_{ij}` summarizes whether job {math}`j` aligns with the worker's role expectations, reputation constraints, competition norms, and acceptable conduct set. A field experiment that changes job framing can identify one piece of this function: how application behavior responds when the same job is described or rewarded differently.

### Networking And Leadership-Entry Norms

Norms also shape advancement after entry. Promotion systems often reward informal networking, self-advocacy, visibility, and repeated interaction with managers. If those behaviors are normatively easier for some workers than others, advancement gaps can appear before formal promotion rules bind.

Cullen and Perez-Truglia study schmoozing and the gender gap, making informal advancement practices a labor-market object rather than a personality story [@cullenPerezTruglia2023]. The key Week 5 insight is that leadership pipelines can depend on norms around networking, self-promotion, socializing with managers, and claiming credit. These are conduct norms. They differ from Week 6 structural hierarchy, where the question is whether category membership itself changes access to networks, authority, or evaluation. In practice the two can interact, but the analytic distinction helps students avoid explaining every promotion gap with one mechanism.

Workplace and promotion evidence is especially valuable because it observes actual advancement settings. It can show that informal interactions predict promotions, that information or visibility changes outcomes, or that workers differ in willingness to enter promotion tournaments. But it often cannot by itself separate norms from managerial bias, network exclusion, performance differences, scheduling constraints, or household obligations. The design must be explicit about the identified margin.

### Work Ethic, Unemployment, And Wage-Acceptance Norms

Norms can also discipline labor supply after job loss or in low-wage markets. Work ethic norms may make unemployment psychologically costly. Local beliefs about benefit receipt may affect search. Community norms may punish accepting a wage that undermines other workers. These channels affect reservation behavior, search intensity, wage acceptance, and local labor-supply equilibrium.

Clark studies unemployment as a social norm using panel evidence on psychological well-being [@clark2003]. The labor-economics point is that nonemployment has social utility costs that vary with local conditions. If unemployment is common in the reference group, the stigma or psychological penalty may be lower. This does not mean unemployment is chosen freely. It means the social environment can alter the welfare cost of labor-market states.

Eugster, Lalive, Steinhauer, and Zweimuller use the Swiss language border to study culture, work attitudes, and job search [@eugsterLaliveSteinhauerZweimuller2017]. The design is useful because language regions create sharp differences in work attitudes within a shared national institutional environment. It is not a pure randomization of norms, but it pushes students to ask whether local social environments alter benefit use, job search, and labor supply net of common policy rules.

Breza, Kaur, and Krishnaswamy study social suppression of labor supply in labor markets where accepting work at certain terms can be socially punished [@brezaKaurKrishnaswamy2019]. This is the market-level labor-supply norm experiment in the Week 5 toolkit. The key object is not only an individual's reservation wage. It is the social discipline around wage acceptance, undercutting, and collective expectations. This connects back to Week 4 without duplicating it: Week 4 studied formal collective organization; Week 5 studies informal labor-supply discipline that can operate even without recognized bargaining institutions.

### Measurement: How Economists Identify Norms

The measurement problem is central. Norms are often latent. Economists therefore use proxies, designs, and triangulation. Good empirical work asks four diagnostic questions: Is the proxy measuring norms rather than prices? Is the design separating norms from legal constraints? Is the pattern driven by selection into places, jobs, or ancestry groups? Does the estimate identify inherited cultural transmission, current social pressure, or both?

**Inherited-culture identification** uses ancestry, origin-country female labor supply, origin-country beliefs, or second-generation behavior to proxy transmitted norms. Fernandez and Fogli identify whether inherited origin-country proxies predict work and fertility in a host environment [@fernandezFogli2009]. The design is strongest for cultural transmission net of some current host-market conditions. It does not directly identify current peer pressure, and it can still be confounded by migrant selection, origin-country economic history, and unobserved family resources.

**Historical persistence designs** use a historical source of norm variation, such as traditional plough agriculture, to explain modern beliefs and labor outcomes [@alesinaGiulianoNunn2013]. These designs are strongest when the historical channel is specific, measured before modern outcomes, and linked to a plausible mechanism. They do not by themselves prove that today's constraint is social pressure rather than correlated institutions, ecology, or economic structure.

**Job-entry field experiments** randomize job framing, compensation, competition, flexibility, or information and observe application choices [@floryLeibbrandtList2015; @delfino2024]. They are strong for the entry margin because behavior is observed in a real or realistic recruitment environment. They identify responses to the manipulated feature, not the full social system that produced gendered or status-coded preferences.

**Workplace networking and promotion evidence** observes how informal interaction, visibility, self-promotion, and manager contact shape advancement [@cullenPerezTruglia2023]. It is strongest for within-firm career margins. It can identify whether networking behaviors matter, but it may not fully separate conduct norms from exclusion, managerial beliefs, or structural access barriers.

**Local social-environment evidence** uses reference-group unemployment, language borders, regional attitudes, or local norm proxies to study search, unemployment stigma, and benefit behavior [@clark2003; @eugsterLaliveSteinhauerZweimuller2017]. These designs are strongest for current social environment. They require care because local norms may be correlated with labor demand, policy administration, commuting zones, and worker composition.

**Market-level labor-supply norm experiments** manipulate wage offers, job opportunities, or information in settings where communities can monitor labor supply [@brezaKaurKrishnaswamy2019]. They are strongest for social enforcement around wage acceptance and undercutting. They do not automatically generalize to all labor markets because visibility, repeated interaction, and collective expectations are setting-specific.

**Religion and reputation designs** model or measure how religious conduct and reputation-preserving behavior change public work and mobility [@carvalho2013]. They are useful when they specify the reputational technology and the labor margin. They can struggle to separate belief, signaling, family constraint, safety, and community enforcement.

### Methods Box: What Each Design Identifies

The practical rule is to state the identified object before using norm language.

Inherited-culture designs identify whether origin-linked proxies predict labor outcomes among descendants or migrants in a host environment. They are informative about transmission and persistence. They do not directly measure current sanctions or peer pressure.

Historical persistence designs identify whether past technologies, institutions, or social arrangements predict modern norm proxies and labor outcomes. They help explain origins and durability. They do not fully rule out correlated historical channels unless the labor mechanism is carefully pinned down.

Job-entry field experiments identify how application behavior responds to randomized job features such as competition, pay framing, flexibility, or gender-coded information. They are especially useful for acceptable-workplace norms. They do not identify the full lifecycle cost of work or promotion.

Workplace networking and promotion evidence identifies the career value of informal interaction, visibility, and self-promotion inside firms. It is central for leadership-entry norms. It may bundle norms with structural access barriers unless the design separates who can network from who chooses to network.

Local social-environment evidence identifies how reference-group norms, local attitudes, or language-region environments correlate with search, unemployment stigma, benefit behavior, or work attitudes. It is strongest for current social pressure but must address local labor-demand and selection concerns.

Market-level labor-supply norm experiments identify whether social visibility and community enforcement change wage acceptance or labor supply. They are powerful for social discipline around undercutting, but external validity depends on whether the labor market has repeated interaction and observable conduct.

### Frontier Questions

Frontier research studies norms as changing institutional constraints rather than fixed background variables. Three interactions are especially important.

First, firms can amplify or soften norms. Recruitment language, schedules, promotion criteria, networking expectations, remote-work policies, harassment safeguards, and task assignment can change the social cost of entry. A firm that advertises a job as a tournament may unintentionally select workers by competition norms. A firm that rewards informal manager access may widen leadership gaps if networking norms differ across workers.

Second, communities can discipline local labor supply. Work ethic, unemployment stigma, wage-acceptance norms, and anti-undercutting expectations can shift equilibrium labor supply, especially when workers know each other and actions are visible. This creates a bridge from individual choice to market-level outcomes.

Third, norms may change when technology, policy, migration, and labor demand change. New transport, remote work, public childcare, industrial upgrading, or legal reform can alter the social cost of work. But norms may also persist after formal barriers fall. Guiso, Sapienza, and Zingales provide the broad culture-and-outcomes frame, while Jayachandran highlights the practical policy problem: changing the material environment may not fully remove normative constraints [@guisoSapienzaZingales2006; @jayachandran2021].

## Research Lab

The Week 5 lab is built around **Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer**. It runs locally with deterministic synthetic data and does not require confidential data from ancestry-linked microdata, firm recruiting experiments, workplace promotion files, religious-community data, or village labor-market experiments.

**Reproduce.** The primary anchor is Fernandez and Fogli [@fernandezFogli2009]. Students use a synthetic second-generation file with origin-country female labor-force participation, fertility, family-role attitudes, host-market demand, education, and individual work outcomes. They reproduce the inherited-culture logic by comparing whether origin-linked norm proxies predict labor supply after holding current host conditions visible in the table.

**Diagnose.** Students then diagnose whether a proxy is measuring norms rather than prices. They compare origin norms with host demand, wages, childcare cost, education, and household composition. The diagnostic question is whether a regression-style association should be interpreted as inherited cultural transmission, current social pressure, selection, or price/constraint variation. The challenge anchor is Flory, Leibbrandt, and List [@floryLeibbrandtList2015], which shifts the evidence from inherited proxies to randomized job-entry framing.

**Transfer.** The transfer exercise classifies designs and margins. The religion extension uses Carvalho to ask how reputation-preserving conduct changes acceptable work [@carvalho2013]. The leadership/networking extension uses Cullen and Perez-Truglia to separate self-promotion norms from structural access to informal networks [@cullenPerezTruglia2023]. The work-norm extension uses Breza, Kaur, and Krishnaswamy to ask when social discipline affects wage acceptance and labor supply [@brezaKaurKrishnaswamy2019].

The bounded pedagogical path asks students to produce three objects:

1. an inherited-culture summary separating origin norm proxies from host-market conditions;
2. a norm-versus-price diagnostic table that flags when a proxy could be confounded;
3. a transfer classification table that names whether each design identifies cultural transmission, current pressure, job-entry response, workplace advancement, or market-level labor-supply discipline.

The lab lives at `labs/05-culture-norms-and-labor-market-allocation/`. The smoke test builds synthetic data, writes reproduction outputs, classifies transfer designs, and produces text notes for seminar discussion.

## Reading Ladder And References

**Broad frame.** Start with Guiso, Sapienza, and Zingales for the general question of culture and economic outcomes [@guisoSapienzaZingales2006]. Read Alesina and Giuliano for a broader culture-and-institutions map if students need more background [@alesinaGiuliano2015].

**Inherited culture and historical persistence.** Read Fernandez and Fogli as the primary inherited-culture design [@fernandezFogli2009]. Then read Alesina, Giuliano, and Nunn for historical persistence in gender-role norms [@alesinaGiulianoNunn2013].

**Norms and women's employment.** Read Jayachandran to connect social norms to female employment in developing-country labor markets while keeping prices, safety, care, and bargaining in view [@jayachandran2021].

**Religion and reputation.** Read Carvalho as the reputation-preserving conduct extension [@carvalho2013].

**Job entry and competition.** Read Flory, Leibbrandt, and List for competitive workplace entry and Delfino for gender-coded occupational entry [@floryLeibbrandtList2015; @delfino2024].

**Leadership and networking.** Read Cullen and Perez-Truglia to study informal advancement, self-promotion, networking, and promotion pipelines [@cullenPerezTruglia2023].

**Work norms and labor supply.** Read Clark on unemployment as a social norm, Eugster et al. on work attitudes and search near the Swiss language border, and Breza, Kaur, and Krishnaswamy on social suppression of labor supply [@clark2003; @eugsterLaliveSteinhauerZweimuller2017; @brezaKaurKrishnaswamy2019].

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. Choose one low-employment group or occupation. State three explanations: price or constraint, discrimination or employer belief, and conduct norm. What evidence would separate them?
2. In an inherited-culture design, what does ancestry variation identify? What would it miss if current peer pressure is the main mechanism?
3. Use equation {eq}`eq:norm-allocation-week5` to compare a wage increase with a reduction in reputational cost. When would each policy change labor supply more?
4. Design a job-entry experiment that distinguishes competition aversion from norms about acceptable work.
5. Give one example that belongs in Week 5 and one closely related example that belongs in Week 6. Explain the boundary using mechanism language.

## Bridge To Week 6

Week 5 has treated norms as shared expectations about conduct: who should work, where work is acceptable, how one should search, whether competition is appropriate, how leadership entry happens, and when wages can be accepted without social punishment. Week 6 changes the object. It studies institutionalized social categories and hierarchy: caste, race, ethnicity, migrant status, religion as group boundary, gender as structural hierarchy, occupation boundaries, employer beliefs, public rules, and local governance.

The distinction is not that Week 5 is informal and Week 6 is formal. Both weeks can involve informal and formal institutions. The distinction is the mechanism. Week 5 asks how expectations about behavior shape labor allocation. Week 6 asks how durable categories structure access to jobs, networks, authority, protection, and dignity. The bridge question is therefore: when does a conduct norm become a system of hierarchy?
