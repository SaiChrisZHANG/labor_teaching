# Comparative and Global Labor-Market Institutions

## Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 9, students should be able to:

1. compare formal rules, effective implementation, coverage, membership, and enforcement across labor-market regimes;
2. explain how welfare states, bargaining systems, informality, migration regimes, and global production systems shape wages, employment, formality, integration, and working conditions;
3. connect comparative institutional questions to empirical designs and observed labor margins;
4. frame a country-specific labor project around a mechanism, comparison class, and honest transportability claim;
5. evaluate which institutional lessons travel across settings, which estimates do not, and why.

## Opening Orientation

Week 8 studied reform, implementation, and heterogeneous adjustment: why a new rule can affect workers, firms, and places differently once it enters an uneven institutional environment. Week 9 zooms out. The central question is now comparative and global: how do labor-market institutions differ across development settings, welfare states, migration regimes, and global production systems, and which lessons travel across contexts?

This week is not a country-by-country survey. It is a framework for turning institutional difference into labor economics. The object is the relationship between institutional regimes and labor margins: wage-setting, unemployment, formality, worker protection, bargaining coverage, migrant integration, firm adaptation, and supply-chain working conditions. A country setting matters when it clarifies a mechanism or comparison, not because institutional detail is interesting by itself.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important

- Comparative labor economics separates formal rules from effective implementation.
- Coverage, membership, and enforcement are different objects: a worker can be covered by a rule, represented by an organization, both, or neither.
- Institutional complementarities matter. Employment protection, unemployment insurance, bargaining, informality, migration rules, and product-market structure interact.
- Labor governance can be public, private, national, local, or transnational.
- Country-specific evidence is strongest when it names the labor mechanism, maps the setting into a comparative taxonomy, and states honestly what should and should not travel.
- Mechanisms travel more easily than point estimates.

:::

## Field Core

### Comparative Framework

A useful comparative framework has five distinctions.

First, separate **formal rules** from **effective implementation**. Legal text can be comparable across countries while the workplace constraint is not. Labor-regulation indices are valuable because they force systematic coding, but they cannot by themselves measure whether workers and firms face a credible rule [@boteroDjankovLaPortaLopezdeSilanesShleifer2004].

Second, separate **coverage**, **membership**, and **enforcement**. Coverage asks who is legally reached. Membership asks who belongs to a union, association, insurance system, or migration category. Enforcement asks whether covered rules are credible. Bargaining coverage can exceed union membership in extension systems. Legal coverage can exceed enforcement in low-capacity settings. Social-insurance membership can be tied to formal payroll even when informal work remains large.

Third, compare **institutional complementarities**, not isolated policies. Blanchard and Wolfers use cross-country variation in shocks and institutions to show that unemployment paths depend on interactions between macro shocks and institutional environments, not on a one-rule ranking of labor markets [@blanchardWolfers2000]. Freeman's comparative overview makes the same broad point: institutions operate as bundles around wage-setting, security, voice, and adjustment [@freeman2007LaborMarketInstitutions].

Fourth, distinguish **public regulation** from **private regulation**. States write labor law, administer social insurance, regulate migration, and enforce standards. But firms, buyers, auditors, unions, employer associations, and international organizations also create rules that shape working conditions and labor allocation.

Fifth, distinguish **national institutions** from **transnational governance**. Migration regimes and global production systems make the comparison larger than a closed national labor market. Workers cross borders, firms reorganize across borders, and private supply-chain standards can alter local labor conditions even when national enforcement is incomplete.

### Welfare States, Employment Protection, And Unemployment

Welfare states combine unemployment insurance, employment protection, active labor-market policy, payroll finance, bargaining rules, and public administration. The labor margins are unemployment, job-finding, wage-setting, separations, worker security, and adjustment to shocks. The empirical lesson from Blanchard and Wolfers is interaction: similar shocks can generate different unemployment paths when institutions differ [@blanchardWolfers2000]. The identifying variation is cross-country time variation in shocks interacted with institutional measures; the observed margin is aggregate unemployment.

That design is powerful for comparative framing but limited for mechanism. It does not tell us by itself whether the binding channel is firing costs, benefit duration, wage bargaining, taxes, or macro policy. A good reading asks: which institutional component changes the labor margin, which components are complements, and which equilibrium feedbacks are not separately identified?

### Bargaining Coverage And Industrial Relations Systems

Bargaining systems differ not only in union membership but in coverage, extension, coordination, workplace representation, employer organization, and the level of bargaining. A country with low union density can still have broad bargaining coverage if agreements are extended. A country with high membership can still have fragmented wage-setting if bargaining is decentralized.

This distinction changes interpretation of wage and employment evidence. A union membership coefficient captures selection into an organization. A bargaining-coverage measure captures exposure to a wage-setting institution. A coordinated bargaining regime captures how wage floors, firm rents, and macro adjustment are organized across sectors. Comparative industrial-relations evidence therefore has to name the relevant exposure: membership, coverage, agreement level, extension, works councils, or employer coordination.

### Cross-Country Labor-Regulation Indices

Botero, Djankov, La Porta, Lopez-de-Silanes, and Shleifer are the primary Week 9 lab anchor because they turn legal institutions into a comparative measurement problem [@boteroDjankovLaPortaLopezdeSilanesShleifer2004]. The identifying variation is cross-country variation in coded labor-law indices. The observed margins in the paper connect regulation to institutional, political, and development correlates rather than a single micro labor outcome.

The teaching value is not that index construction solves comparative labor economics. It is that index construction makes the measurement problem explicit. What is comparable across legal systems? How should employment law, collective relations law, and social-security law be weighted? When does legal origin predict rules on paper but not implementation? How much effective protection is missed when informality and weak enforcement are large?

### Informality And Development Settings

Informality is not the absence of institutions. It is a labor-market regime in which registration, tax compliance, social insurance, labor inspection, contract enforcement, and worker protection cover only part of economic activity. Ulyssea's review is a useful anchor because it treats informality as a set of firm and worker margins rather than a residual sector [@ulyssea2020InformalityReview].

The comparative question is how the meaning of protection changes when the formal sector is partial. A dismissal rule may bind only for registered firms. Social insurance may be tied to payroll employment. Enforcement may focus on visible firms. Workers may sort into informal jobs because of wages, mobility, household constraints, or lack of access to formal opportunities. The observed margins are formality, earnings, productivity, firm size, compliance, and worker protection.

### Migration Regimes And Labor-Market Integration

Migration policy is a labor-market institution. Entry restrictions, work authorization, skill categories, cross-border commuting rights, recognition of credentials, portability of benefits, and access to collective rights all shape labor supply, matching, bargaining, and integration.

Beerli, Peri, Ruffner, and Siegenthaler use the abolition of immigration restrictions in Switzerland to study how opening access to cross-border workers affects firms and workers [@beerliPeriRuffnerSiegenthaler2021]. The identifying variation is differential exposure to a migration-regime change across regions and firms. The observed margins include firm performance, employment, and worker outcomes. The broader lesson is not that every country will have the same effect. It is that migration regimes can be studied as labor-market institutions when the exposure margin is precise.

Guzi, Kahanec, and Mydlikova extend the comparative perspective to immigrant-native labor-market gaps in Europe and the role of institutions [@guziKahanecMydlikova2021]. The observed margins are immigrant-native differences in labor-market access and outcomes; the comparative object is how country-level institutions condition integration.

### Global Production Systems And Private Regulation

Global production systems move the course beyond national labor law. Multinational buyers, sourcing contracts, private codes of conduct, social audits, certification systems, lean production, and local public enforcement can all govern work. Locke's book is the core framing anchor on the promise and limits of private power in labor standards [@locke2013PromiseLimitsPrivatePower]. Distelhorst, Hainmueller, and Locke study the Nike supply chain and ask whether management practices can improve labor standards [@distelhorstHainmuellerLocke2017]. The observed margins are compliance and social performance in supplier factories; the relevant data are supply-chain audit, management, and factory records.

The key comparative move is to change the unit. Sometimes the right unit is not a country but a buyer-supplier network, factory, product chain, certification regime, or audit system. Private labor standards may complement the state when public enforcement is weak, but they may also substitute imperfectly for worker voice and public accountability.

### Comparative Welfare, Incidence, And Portability

Comparative welfare analysis asks which margins are protected, who pays, and whether the lesson travels. A formal employment-protection rule may improve security for covered workers while reducing hiring for outsiders. A bargaining extension may compress wages but change entry, training, or firm survival. A migration opening may raise firm productivity and alter native-worker task allocation while changing incidence across skill groups. A private audit may improve measured compliance but miss wages, voice, or retaliation.

The main portability distinction is between **mechanism portability** and **estimate portability**. Mechanisms often travel: enforcement changes the credibility of rules; informality changes coverage; migration regimes affect matching and labor supply; private governance can alter supplier incentives. Estimates travel less easily because capacity, market structure, bargaining, informality, and political support differ. A country-specific paper should therefore say what part of the mechanism is general and what part of the magnitude is setting-specific.

## Research Lab

The Week 9 lab uses **Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer**.

**Reproduce.** Students build a small deterministic comparative labor-regulation dataset inspired by Botero, Djankov, La Porta, Lopez-de-Silanes, and Shleifer [@boteroDjankovLaPortaLopezdeSilanesShleifer2004]. They construct a rules-on-paper index from employment law, collective relations law, and social-security law, then compare it with an effective-protection index that incorporates enforcement capacity. The point is to reproduce the logic of cross-country index construction without treating legal text as implementation.

**Diagnose.** Students classify comparative cases by institutional domain, identifying variation, observed labor margin, implementation gap, and portability risk. The challenge anchor is Beerli, Peri, Ruffner, and Siegenthaler because the Swiss migration opening is country-specific but speaks to a broader labor-market integration mechanism [@beerliPeriRuffnerSiegenthaler2021].

**Transfer.** Students map designs to new settings: cross-country legal indices, within-country reforms with comparative interpretation, border or region exposure designs, comparative administrative microdata, supply-chain audit data, and historical-comparative classification. The optional extension uses Distelhorst, Hainmueller, and Locke to move from countries to supply-chain governance [@distelhorstHainmuellerLocke2017].

The lab lives at `labs/09-comparative-and-global-labor-market-institutions/`. It is a bounded teaching analog, not an official replication of the anchor papers.

## Methods Box

The central methodological problem is how to make country-specific or comparative evidence matter without overclaiming. Comparative labor papers are strongest when they state the comparison class, the mechanism, the identifying variation, the observed labor margin, and the transportability claim.

:::{admonition} Method / framing box: making a country setting travel
:class: note

1. **Frame around a mechanism, not novelty.** Start with the labor question: wage-setting, unemployment, formality, bargaining, migration integration, or working conditions.
2. **Map the country into a taxonomy.** Say whether the setting is a welfare-state regime, bargaining regime, informality regime, migration regime, supply-chain node, or hybrid case.
3. **Explain why the country is a useful laboratory.** Identify the discontinuity, reform timing, administrative data, border exposure, firm linkage, or sharp institutional contrast.
4. **Name the identifying variation and observed labor margin.** Do not present a result without saying what varies and what outcome is measured.
5. **Discuss external validity honestly.** Say which mechanism should travel, which institutional complements are essential, and why the estimate may not transport.

:::

The empirical design menu is also comparative:

1. **Cross-country index construction.** Identifying variation is cross-country legal or institutional coding; observed margins may be unemployment, formality, legal protection, or institutional correlates. The main limit is rules-on-paper measurement.
2. **Within-country reforms with comparative interpretation.** Identifying variation is reform timing, threshold exposure, or local rollout; the comparative value comes from mapping the setting into a broader regime.
3. **Border, region, or firm exposure designs in open labor markets.** Identifying variation is differential exposure to migration rules, trade, or cross-border labor access; observed margins are employment, wages, matching, or firm performance.
4. **Comparative administrative microdata.** Identifying variation may come from harmonized policy differences across regimes; observed margins include earnings, unemployment duration, mobility, benefits, and formal employment.
5. **Supply-chain audit, certification, or governance data.** Identifying variation can come from buyer pressure, audit timing, management practices, or certification; observed margins are compliance, working conditions, turnover, and factory performance.
6. **Historical-comparative institutional classification.** Identifying variation comes from inherited regimes, legal origins, coercive labor institutions, or administrative boundaries; observed margins must still be modern labor outcomes rather than institutional labels alone.

The main critiques are real: the setting may be too specific; institutional bundles may confound the mechanism; measurement may be incomparable across countries; equilibrium feedback may differ elsewhere; legal text may not measure implementation. The response is not universalism. The response is disciplined comparison.

## Reading Ladder And References

**Core comparative framing.** Start with Freeman for a broad comparative map, Botero and coauthors for cross-country labor-regulation measurement, and Blanchard and Wolfers for shocks interacting with institutions [@freeman2007LaborMarketInstitutions; @boteroDjankovLaPortaLopezdeSilanesShleifer2004; @blanchardWolfers2000].

**Development, informality, and enforcement.** Read Ulyssea to understand why development settings change the meaning of labor regulation and worker protection [@ulyssea2020InformalityReview].

**Migration regimes and integration.** Use Beerli, Peri, Ruffner, and Siegenthaler for a design where a country-specific migration reform identifies labor-market integration mechanisms, then Guzi, Kahanec, and Mydlikova for a broader European comparative lens [@beerliPeriRuffnerSiegenthaler2021; @guziKahanecMydlikova2021].

**Global production and private governance.** Read Locke for the institutional argument about private power, then Distelhorst, Hainmueller, and Locke for supply-chain evidence on management practices and labor standards [@locke2013PromiseLimitsPrivatePower; @distelhorstHainmuellerLocke2017].

**Transportability and external validity.** Use Allcott and Dehejia, Pop-Eleches, and Samii to discipline claims about what travels across settings [@allcott2015SiteSelectionBias; @dehejiaPopElechesSamii2015ExternalValidity].

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. Pick one labor institution and separate rules on paper, coverage, membership, enforcement, and observed labor margin.
2. Use equation {eq}`eq:comparative-regime-week9` to compare two countries. Which term is easiest to measure, and which term is most likely to be mistaken for another?
3. In Botero and coauthors, what does the cross-country index identify well, and what does it miss about effective implementation [@boteroDjankovLaPortaLopezdeSilanesShleifer2004]?
4. Explain why an estimate from the Swiss migration opening could be valuable for a non-Swiss labor economist without claiming that the Swiss effect size transports everywhere [@beerliPeriRuffnerSiegenthaler2021].
5. Compare a national labor-inspection regime and a supply-chain audit regime. Which actors enforce the rule, and which labor margins are observed?
6. Design a country-specific project from your own setting. State the mechanism, taxonomy, identifying variation, observed labor margin, and transportability claim in five sentences.
