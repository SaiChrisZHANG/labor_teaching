## Why this field matters

| Skeptical question | Strong answer |
|---|---|
| “Why should labor economists care about history or culture?” | Because institutions and norms shape work, wages, bargaining power, mobility, and protection today, not only in the past |
| “Is this just antiquarian detail?” | No. The point is not historical description; it is using institutional variation to identify labor mechanisms |
| “Why should I care about one country or one historical episode?” | A setting matters when it gives unusual leverage on a general labor question and clarifies what should and should not travel |
| “Isn't this just political economy?” | It overlaps, but the labor contribution is specific: who works, where they work, how they bargain, how wages are set, and who receives protection |
| “How is this policy relevant?” | Institutions determine implementation, compliance, access, incidence, and long-run inequality, so policy cannot be interpreted without them |
| “What is the frontier?” | Better linked data, sharper mechanisms, cleaner institutional designs, and stronger portability/welfare arguments |
