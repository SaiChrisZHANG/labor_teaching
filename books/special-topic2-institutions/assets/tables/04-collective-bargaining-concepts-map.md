# Collective bargaining concepts map

| Concept | What it means | Why it matters for labor economics | Canonical examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Union membership | Workers formally join an organization | Measures organized worker power, but is not the whole institution | US firm-level unionization |
| Bargaining coverage | Workers covered by collective agreements whether or not they are members | Coverage can exceed membership and is often the key institutional object | Sectoral bargaining in Europe |
| Enterprise bargaining | Bargaining occurs at the firm or establishment | Closest to firm-level wage-setting and close-election designs | US NLRB elections |
| Sectoral / centralized bargaining | Bargaining is coordinated at industry or national level | Shapes wage coordination, compression, and spillovers | Nordic / continental regimes |
| Works councils | Worker representation on the shop floor or in consultation bodies | Changes information sharing and governance even when wage bargaining is limited | Germany, Netherlands, Finland |
| Codetermination / board representation | Workers represented at the board level | Alters governance, information, and strategic decision-making | Germany |
| Strike capacity / collective action | Workers can credibly coordinate work stoppages or threats | Determines bargaining power and rent division | Union bargaining and strike law |
