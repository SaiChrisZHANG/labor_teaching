# Week 10 institutions-and-labor research-design memo

## Anchor selection

- Primary anchor: `dell2010Mita` (Dell (2010))
- Challenge anchor: `naiduYuchtman2013CoerciveContractEnforcement` (Naidu and Yuchtman (2013))
- Optional extension anchor: `beerliPeriRuffnerSiegenthaler2021` (Beerli, Peri, Ruffner, and Siegenthaler (2021))
- Studio logic: Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer

## 1. Labor outcome

Primary anchor outcome:
Modern household consumption, schooling, market access, public goods, and related labor-market conditions.

Your project:
Replace this sentence with the labor-market outcome you want to explain.

## 2. Institutional mechanism

Primary mechanism:
A colonial forced-labor institution that altered local power, public goods, roads, land tenure, and outside options.

Challenge mechanism:
Master and servant law changed contract enforceability and made quitting more costly for workers.

Your project:
Name the institutional channel and one alternative explanation.

## 3. Setting and leverage

Primary comparison:
Districts on different sides of a historical mita boundary.

Optional extension comparison:
Regions and firms with different exposure to a migration-regime opening.

Your project:
Explain why the setting gives leverage on a broader labor question.

## 4. Counterfactual

Your project:
Define the comparison group, untreated group, less exposed group, boundary neighbor, pre-reform period, or alternative regime.

## 5. Data source

Primary data logic:
Historical boundary records linked to modern district and household outcomes.

Your project:
Name the data source and say whether it observes both the labor outcome and the institutional mechanism.

## 6. Identification strategy

Primary design:
Boundary-style comparison with institutional exposure defined by historical assignment.

Challenge design:
Historical institutional design connecting legal enforcement to labor-market behavior.

Your project:
State the identifying variation, estimand, and one threat.

## 7. Portability claim

Primary portability claim:
Coercive labor institutions can persist through local public goods and market access, but magnitudes depend on local state capacity and geography.

Optional extension portability claim:
Migration rules shape matching and firm labor supply, but effects depend on geography, skill mix, and bargaining institutions.

Your project:
State what should travel, what is local, and which institutional complements matter.

## 8. Welfare or distributional object

Primary welfare object:
Long-run distributional losses through lower public goods, mobility, market access, and worker opportunity.

Your project:
State whose welfare, protection, bargaining power, mobility, risk, or income distribution the project speaks to.

## 9. Main threats

Primary threat:
The historical boundary may correlate with geography, later policy, or other persistent fundamentals.

Challenge threat:
Legal enforcement may move with industrial change, worker organization, or local political economy.

Your project:
Add the two threats that a skeptical seminar participant would ask first.

## Transfer exercise

Primary transfer prompt:
Use a historical institutional boundary to study a modern labor outcome while naming the channel that persists.

Challenge transfer prompt:
Study a contract-enforcement change that alters worker exit, not just a law-on-the-books change.

Optional extension transfer prompt:
Adapt the exposure logic to a migration, commuting, credential-recognition, or work-permit reform.

Your project:
Write a five-sentence abstract using the sequence: outcome, mechanism, setting, design, portability, welfare.
