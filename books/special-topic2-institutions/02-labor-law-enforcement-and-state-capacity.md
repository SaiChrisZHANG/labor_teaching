# Labor Law, Enforcement, and State Capacity

## Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 2, students should be able to:

1. explain why labor law is a labor-market institution rather than only a legal text;
2. distinguish law on the books, effective law in practice, enforcement intensity, worker knowledge, realized compliance, and labor-market incidence;
3. connect labor-law domains to hiring, dismissal, wages, hours, benefits, complaints, sanctions, and contract form;
4. analyze enforcement and state capacity as institutions that shape wages, employment, job quality, formalization, and sorting;
5. evaluate empirical designs by naming whether the identifying variation isolates legal rules, enforcement, knowledge, or effective compliance.

Week 2 asks when labor laws alter wages, employment, job quality, and worker protection, and when they remain only rules on paper. The answer is not found in doctrine alone. Labor law matters for labor economics when it changes feasible contracts, dismissal costs, wage and hour compliance, mandated benefits, bargaining positions, complaint behavior, firm observability, and the formal-informal boundary.

The central object is therefore a chain: legal protection on paper, expected enforcement, worker and firm knowledge, realized compliance, firm adaptation, and labor-market incidence. MacLeod's contract framework is the conceptual anchor because employment law changes what promises can be made credible inside the employment relationship [@macleod2011]. Autor, Donohue, and Schwab show how court-made dismissal protections can be used as empirical variation in labor outcomes [@autorDonohueSchwab2004]. Almeida and Carneiro show why enforcement intensity is a treatment in its own right when informality is an equilibrium margin [@almeidaCarneiro2012]. Bertrand and Crepon show that knowledge of labor law can be a separate implementation margin even when the statute already exists [@bertrandCrepon2021].

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important

- Labor law affects labor markets through contracts, dismissal, wage and hour compliance, mandated benefits, anti-retaliation, dispute resolution, inspection, and sanctions.
- The relevant treatment is not only **law on the books**. It is the effective rule workers and firms expect to govern the employment relationship.
- Enforcement is selective, costly, and uneven. Those features are central to labor-market incidence, not implementation noise.
- State capacity shapes whether legal protection reaches firms, sectors, territories, and workers that are hard to observe.
- Worker knowledge of rights can matter independently from inspection because rights are partly exercised through complaints, claims, bargaining, and take-up.
- Stronger enforcement can raise compliance and protection while also changing wages, employment, formalization, and firm-worker sorting.

:::

## Bridge

Week 1 separated formal institutions from informal institutions and insisted that institutional analysis begin from a labor-market outcome. Week 2 narrows that discipline to formal labor law. The question is not whether a labor code is generous or restrictive in the abstract. The question is whether a rule becomes an operative constraint on the employment relationship and which labor-market margins move when it does.

Three distinctions organize the week.

First, **law on the books** is the statutory or court-made rule: dismissal doctrine, overtime law, benefit mandate, written-contract requirement, or complaint right. Second, **effective law in practice** is the expected constraint workers and firms perceive after enforcement, courts, worker knowledge, and implementation are taken into account. Third, **labor-market incidence** is the realized adjustment in wages, employment, compliance, formality, benefits, turnover, claims, or sorting.

A compact pedagogical device is:

```{math}
:label: eq:effective-law-week2
L^{eff}_{jt} = L^{book}_{jt} \times E_{jt} \times K_{jt},
```

where {math}`L^{book}_{jt}` is the formal legal rule facing job or jurisdiction {math}`j` in period {math}`t`, {math}`E_{jt}` is enforcement or implementation intensity, and {math}`K_{jt}` is worker and firm knowledge or salience of the rule. Equation {eq}`eq:effective-law-week2` is not a structural identity. It is a teaching device that forces students to keep legal text, enforcement, and knowledge separate. If any component is close to zero, the effective rule can be weak even when the formal right is strong.

This distinction also explains why the formal-informal margin is central rather than peripheral. In many labor markets, stronger enforcement changes the value and cost of formal employment, which can increase compliance for covered workers while shifting some production, workers, or tasks toward uncovered contracts. A credible lecture on labor law must therefore study protection and avoidance in the same frame.

## Field Core

### Why Labor Law Matters In Labor Economics

Labor law changes the employment relationship by changing the feasible set and the threat points. It defines which contracts can be written, what promises are enforceable, how separations can occur, which benefits must be paid, how workers can claim rights, and what sanctions firms expect if they violate standards. These are labor-market objects because they alter hiring, wage-setting, retention, bargaining, mobility, compliance, and job quality.

MacLeod's framework is useful because it treats law as part of the contract environment rather than as an external add-on [@macleod2011]. An employment contract is incomplete: effort, training, discretion, discipline, and separation are hard to specify fully. Legal rules and enforcement institutions help determine which promises are credible and which deviations are costly. That means labor law can change both sides of the market. Workers may accept jobs with lower cash wages if benefits and protection are credible. Firms may change hiring, contract form, outsourcing, or technology when compliance costs rise. The observed effect depends on which margin adjusts.

Mandated benefits affect the wage-benefit bundle and formalization incentives. Anti-retaliation and dispute-resolution rules affect whether workers report violations or bargain over rights. Inspection and sanction regimes affect compliance, evasion, firm observability, and formal-informal sorting.

### Labor Law On Paper Versus In Practice

The same written rule can mean different things across firms, regions, and workers. A dismissal rule may be salient to large registered employers but irrelevant to household enterprises. An overtime rule may bind where payroll records are reliable but fail where hours are unobserved. A benefit mandate may raise worker compensation when claims are enforceable but become a tax on formal labor when firms and workers expect evasion. A complaint right may exist in law while retaliation risk prevents workers from using it.

The course therefore separates six objects.

1. **Law on the books:** the formal statutory or court-made rule.
2. **Effective law in practice:** the rule as mediated by enforcement, knowledge, courts, and administrative reach.
3. **Enforcement intensity:** the probability and credibility of inspection, litigation, sanction, or administrative action.
4. **Worker knowledge of rights:** whether workers and firms know the rule, understand remedies, and believe claims are feasible.
5. **Realized compliance:** whether the employer actually pays, records, benefits, hours, and procedures match the legal standard.
6. **Labor-market incidence:** the wage, employment, formality, benefits, turnover, sorting, or welfare adjustment that follows.

Autor, Donohue, and Schwab study variation in wrongful-discharge doctrines across U.S. states [@autorDonohueSchwab2004]. The identifying variation is court-made legal change. The observed margin is employment and related labor-market outcomes. The object is not inspection intensity; it is a change in dismissal protection and employment-contract doctrine. That distinction matters because a dismissal doctrine can affect hiring and separations even when no labor inspector is involved.

Almeida and Carneiro study enforcement of labor regulation in Brazil [@almeidaCarneiro2012]. The identifying variation is exposure to enforcement intensity. The observed margins include informality, wages, and compliance-related outcomes. The object is not a new statute on the books; it is variation in how likely the existing rule is to bind. Comparing the two papers is a useful way to keep the treatment definition sharp.

### Enforcement As A Labor-Market Institution

Enforcement is not a mechanical afterthought. It is an institution with personnel, geography, discretion, information constraints, sanctions, legal procedures, and political pressures. Inspectors decide where to go, what to observe, how to interpret violations, when to warn, when to sanction, and when to rely on complaints. Courts determine delay, damages, precedent, and the expected value of litigation. Administrative agencies determine whether payroll records, firm registries, and benefit systems make violations visible.

Enforcement is selective because inspection budgets are scarce, firms and workers are unevenly observable, and violations differ in evidentiary cost. A ministry cannot inspect every worksite every week. It may target large firms because they are visible, high-risk sectors because violations are severe, complaint-heavy localities because workers report, or politically salient industries because enforcement has public value. Selectivity changes labor-market incidence. If only registered firms face credible enforcement, enforcement can raise compliance in the formal sector while strengthening incentives to remain informal.

Almeida and Carneiro are central because their enforcement variation changes the expected probability that firms must comply, and the observed margins include formality and wages [@almeidaCarneiro2012]. That makes enforcement a labor-market treatment, not merely a control. Amengual adds an institutional mechanism: inspectors may leverage linkages with unions, workers, and civil society when formal bureaucratic autonomy is limited [@amengual2014]. In that setting, enforcement capacity is partly produced through social organization. The labor question is whether those linkages make claims, inspections, and sanctions credible enough to change compliance and job quality.

### State Capacity And Heterogeneity

State capacity enters this week only through labor-market consequences. The relevant question is not whether a state is abstractly strong. It is whether public institutions can identify employment relationships, observe violations, process claims, sanction noncompliance, and sustain credibility at the margins where workers and firms make decisions.

Four capacity margins matter most.

First, **administrative reach** determines whether the state can find firms, workers, payrolls, contracts, hours, and benefits. Second, **territorial heterogeneity** determines whether the same rule binds in capital cities, secondary towns, rural labor markets, export zones, and remote production sites. Third, **observability** determines whether firms and workers can be monitored through registration, tax records, payroll systems, workplace inspections, complaints, or platform data. Fourth, **complementarity with worker organization and social linkages** determines whether unions, NGOs, community monitors, or worker networks help convert legal rights into claims and evidence.

Berliner, Greenleaf, Lake, and Noveck connect state capacity to labor rights across developing countries [@berlinerGreenleafLakeNoveck2015]. The design is comparative and the observed object is broad labor-rights performance rather than a single firm-level compliance margin. That breadth is useful for asking why similar legal commitments travel differently across administrative settings, but it also makes mechanism discipline essential. Students should ask whether the measure captures law on the books, bureaucratic capacity, realized compliance, or worker outcomes.

Ronconi is a useful frontier measurement anchor because the title question is exactly this week's problem: how to measure effective labor regulation rather than statutory regulation alone [@ronconi2024]. A good measure would combine legal texts, inspection capacity, complaints, sanctions, compliance, and worker knowledge. A weak measure would treat a statute as a treatment even when implementation is unobserved.

### Worker Knowledge And Implementation

Legal protection can fail even when inspectors exist if workers do not know the rule, do not know how to claim it, fear retaliation, mistrust the institution, or misunderstand the cost of exercising rights. Knowledge is therefore a separate implementation margin from inspection. It changes beliefs, complaints, bargaining, take-up, and perceived constraints.

Bertrand and Crepon study a randomized labor-law information intervention in South Africa [@bertrandCrepon2021]. The identifying variation is information, not statutory reform and not inspector allocation. The observed margins are firm beliefs and employment behavior. The paper is valuable for Week 2 because it shows that the perceived legal environment can differ from the written legal environment. If firms overestimate restrictions or workers underestimate rights, information can change behavior without changing the law on the books.

Worker knowledge has an asymmetric logic. Workers must know rights well enough to recognize violations, decide whether a claim is worth the risk, and navigate procedures. Firms must know rules well enough to price compliance and avoid litigation. Misinformation on either side can weaken the effective law in equation {eq}`eq:effective-law-week2`. That is why legal information, posting requirements, advice centers, and complaint systems belong in a labor-economics chapter on implementation.

### Equilibrium Margins And Incidence

The outcome of labor law is not one-dimensional. A legal rule can raise one measure of protection while lowering another measure of access. The empirical question is always: which margin is observed, and which margin is still moving offstage?

The core margins are:

- **Employment:** hiring, separations, vacancies, job-finding, and job destruction.
- **Wages:** cash wages, wage compression, compensating differentials, and incidence of mandated costs.
- **Compliance and benefits:** actual receipt of wages, hours protections, leave, severance, payroll benefits, or procedures.
- **Formality versus informality:** registration, payroll status, contract status, and uncovered work.
- **Firm and worker sorting:** movement across firm size, sector, contract type, region, and observability.

The formal-informal margin deserves special attention. Stronger enforcement can increase the value of formal employment if benefits and protections become real. It can also raise the expected cost of formal labor for firms. When wages are flexible, some costs may shift into cash wages. When wages are rigid or benefits are valued differently by workers and firms, adjustment can occur through employment, informality, outsourcing, or firm exit. Almeida and Carneiro are therefore not only an enforcement paper; they are also an incidence paper because the observed margins include formality and wages [@almeidaCarneiro2012].

### Empirical Designs And Evidence

Empirical designs in this week differ because they identify different institutional objects. Students should not summarize results without naming the variation, the observed labor margin, and the object identified.

```{include} assets/tables/02-identification-and-outcomes-map.md
```

**Legal adoption, repeal, and court-made variation** identify changes in law on the books or in legal doctrine. Autor, Donohue, and Schwab use court-made wrongful-discharge variation; the observed margins are employment-related outcomes, and the key issue is the counterfactual path of state labor markets absent the doctrine [@autorDonohueSchwab2004]. These designs are powerful for studying legal rules, but they usually do not isolate enforcement intensity.

**Inspection-access or inspector-allocation designs** identify enforcement intensity. Almeida and Carneiro use variation in enforcement exposure to study informality, wages, and compliance-related labor outcomes [@almeidaCarneiro2012]. The central threat is that inspector access may be correlated with local state capacity, firm composition, or labor demand. The design identifies enforcement more directly than legal adoption does, but it does not by itself identify worker knowledge.

**Worker-information experiments** identify knowledge or salience. Bertrand and Crepon use randomized information about labor law; the observed margins are beliefs and employment behavior [@bertrandCrepon2021]. Such designs are clean about information, but they do not identify the effect of stronger sanctions unless enforcement changes too.

**Comparative institutional indices** identify broad legal environments, labor-rights regimes, or state-capacity bundles. Berliner and coauthors connect state capacity to labor rights across countries [@berlinerGreenleafLakeNoveck2015]. Botero and coauthors, introduced in Week 1, show how formal labor regulation can be coded comparatively [@botero2004]. These designs are useful for large-scale comparison but coarse about mechanism and implementation.

**Matched employer-employee or firm-administrative evidence** identifies incidence and sorting when data link workers, firms, wages, contracts, inspections, and benefits. The strength is that compliance and adjustment can be observed inside labor markets. The limitation is that such data are setting-specific and often unavailable for the most informal workers.

**Cross-country state-capacity and labor-rights designs** identify associations between administrative capacity and labor protection. They are valuable for portability questions, but they require discipline about what is measured: statutory law, enforcement capacity, realized compliance, or worker welfare.

### Forward Bridge To Week 3

Week 2 studies formal labor law and the institutions that make it effective or ineffective. Week 3 asks what happens when formal contracts, payroll systems, and legal protections govern only part of work. The bridge is the formal-informal margin. If enforcement is uneven, informality is not simply missing data or residual noncompliance. It is an institutional regime that shapes wages, benefits, mobility, risk, and public protection.

## Research Lab

The Week 2 lab is built around **Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer**. It is a bounded teaching path, not an official replication package. It runs locally with synthetic data so students can practice the design logic without confidential microdata.

**Reproduce.** The primary anchor is Almeida and Carneiro [@almeidaCarneiro2012]. Students generate a synthetic locality-year panel in which inspection intensity changes compliance, benefits, formalization, and wage incidence. The goal is to reproduce the conceptual object: enforcement as the treatment and informality as an equilibrium margin.

**Diagnose.** The secondary challenge anchor is Autor, Donohue, and Schwab [@autorDonohueSchwab2004]. Students compare an enforcement design to a court-made legal-variation design. The required diagnosis is whether a paper identifies law on the books, enforcement, knowledge, or effective compliance.

**Transfer.** The optional extension anchor is Bertrand and Crepon [@bertrandCrepon2021]. Students transfer the framework to one of three bounded designs: apply an inspection-intensity design to a different labor standard, compare formalization versus wage incidence, or compare information and enforcement as separate implementation treatments.

The lab lives at `labs/02-labor-law-enforcement-and-state-capacity/`. The smoke test runs the synthetic data builder, the Almeida-Carneiro style reproduction, and the transfer classifier. The outputs are small CSV and text files designed for discussion, not publication.

## Methods Box

The methods lesson of Week 2 is simple: the source of variation determines the institutional object identified.

Legal adoption, repeal, and court-made variation are best suited to the effect of legal rules. They identify what changes when the formal doctrine changes, as in wrongful-discharge law variation [@autorDonohueSchwab2004]. They do not automatically tell us whether enforcement or knowledge changed.

Inspection-access and inspector-allocation designs are best suited to enforcement intensity. They identify what changes when firms expect a higher probability of inspection, sanction, or administrative follow-up [@almeidaCarneiro2012]. They require careful attention to local state capacity, firm observability, and endogenous targeting.

Worker-information experiments are best suited to knowledge and salience. They identify what changes when workers or firms learn the rule or remedies [@bertrandCrepon2021]. They do not necessarily identify the effect of stronger penalties.

Comparative institutional indices and cross-country labor-rights designs are best suited to broad portability and state-capacity questions [@berlinerGreenleafLakeNoveck2015]. They must be interpreted carefully because legal text, implementation, and outcomes are often bundled.

Matched employer-employee and firm-administrative evidence is best suited to incidence and sorting. It can show whether compliance gains arrive as benefits, wage offsets, formalization, employment changes, or movement across firms. Its limitation is coverage: the most important uncovered workers may be absent from formal records.

## Reading Ladder And References

**Contract foundation.** Start with MacLeod for the view that labor law and enforcement shape the credibility of employment contracts [@macleod2011].

**Legal protection and employment.** Read Autor, Donohue, and Schwab as the legal-variation anchor. Track the identifying variation, the observed employment margins, and the difference between doctrine and enforcement [@autorDonohueSchwab2004].

**Enforcement and informality.** Read Almeida and Carneiro next. Ask how enforcement intensity changes compliance, wages, and the formal-informal margin [@almeidaCarneiro2012].

**Knowledge and implementation.** Read Bertrand and Crepon to see why information about labor law can change behavior without changing the law on the books [@bertrandCrepon2021].

**State capacity and social linkages.** Pair Amengual with Berliner, Greenleaf, Lake, and Noveck to connect inspectors, social organizations, bureaucratic capacity, and labor-rights performance [@amengual2014; @berlinerGreenleafLakeNoveck2015].

**Frontier measurement.** Use Ronconi as an optional measurement anchor for the problem of effective labor regulation in low-capacity settings [@ronconi2024].

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. Choose one labor-law domain from the dimensions table. State the law-on-the-books object, the likely enforcement margin, the worker-knowledge margin, and two possible labor-market incidence margins.
2. Compare [@autorDonohueSchwab2004] and [@almeidaCarneiro2012]. Which paper identifies legal change, which identifies enforcement intensity, and which labor outcomes are observed in each?
3. Suppose inspections increase in formal firms but not informal firms. Predict the possible effects on cash wages, benefits, formal employment, and worker sorting.
4. Design a worker-information experiment about wage theft, overtime, dismissal rights, or benefits. State why the design identifies knowledge rather than enforcement.
5. Pick one comparative labor-rights index. What would you need to add to distinguish statutory law from effective compliance?
