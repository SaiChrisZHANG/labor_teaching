# Labor Markets and Political/Cultural Institutions

## Course role

This flagship 10-week course studies labor markets as institutionally embedded systems shaped by law, state capacity, culture, identity, and political conflict.

## Weekly outline

### Week 1. Institutions, culture, and labor-market mechanisms

**Central question:** What counts as a political or cultural institution in labor economics, and through which mechanisms do these institutions affect labor outcomes?

The opening week defines the course vocabulary and clarifies the difference between formal rules, informal norms, and deep social structures. It also establishes the main empirical challenge: institutions are often slow-moving, bundled, and hard to isolate cleanly.

### Week 2. State capacity, labor law, and enforcement

**Central question:** When do labor laws matter in practice, and how much depends on enforcement capacity rather than statutory design?

Students study labor regulation through inspections, administrative reach, legal access, and implementation frictions. The week emphasizes that enforcement can be a labor-market mechanism in its own right rather than a footnote to policy design.

### Week 3. Family systems, norms, and labor supply

**Central question:** How do family structure and inherited social norms shape labor supply and household allocation?

This week situates labor decisions inside kinship systems, marriage patterns, and intergenerational expectations. The aim is to explain why similar policies or economic incentives can generate different labor responses across cultural contexts.

### Week 4. Gender norms, identity, and work

**Central question:** How do gendered norms and identities shape work, bargaining, and occupational choice?

The focus is on participation, care, social sanctions, identity-congruent work, and the interaction between norms and market opportunities. This week overlaps with Gender Study but remains institution-first rather than inequality-first in its framing.

### Week 5. Religion, ethnicity, and social stratification

**Central question:** How do social boundaries and stratification systems organize labor-market opportunities?

Students study labor-market sorting through caste-like hierarchies, ethnic segmentation, discrimination, and group-specific institutions. The key issue is whether persistent labor gaps are driven mainly by exclusion, norms, networks, or political structure.

### Week 6. Political economy of labor regulation and redistribution

**Central question:** Who gets labor-market protection, and why do labor institutions differ across political coalitions?

This week connects labor policy to voting, elite bargaining, redistribution, and party competition. It asks students to think about labor institutions as endogenous political outcomes rather than as fixed constraints.

### Week 7. Conflict, migration, and institutional shocks

**Central question:** How do conflict, displacement, and institutional rupture reorganize labor markets?

Wars, regime shifts, forced migration, and administrative redesigns provide unusually sharp windows into institutional change. The week uses those shocks to study labor allocation, social trust, and the persistence of institutional effects.

### Week 8. Informality, clientelism, and labor markets

**Central question:** What governs labor exchange when formal labor institutions are partial, weak, or selectively applied?

Students analyze informal employment, patronage, brokerage, and clientelist intermediation in labor allocation. The week is especially important for connecting labor economics to settings where legal formality is not the main organizing margin.

### Week 9. Local governance, place, and labor adjustment

**Central question:** How do subnational institutions and local political structures shape labor-market adjustment?

The focus shifts to municipalities, regions, and local public goods. Students study how local governance affects labor demand, mobility, informality, public employment, and the ability of places to absorb shocks.

### Week 10. Synthesis and research design

**Central question:** How should a labor economist design credible research on institutions, culture, and work?

The final week organizes the course into a research toolkit spanning political shocks, historical persistence, norm measurement, cross-place comparison, and administrative enforcement data. The goal is to turn a broad institutional topic into a disciplined labor field.
