# Labor Markets and Political/Cultural Institutions

## Course role

This flagship 10-week course studies how law, enforcement, informal exchange, worker organization, norms, identity, institutional persistence, reform, and comparative state capacity shape labor-market allocation and inequality. It is a labor-economics course first: every week begins from wages, employment, informality, mobility, occupational sorting, bargaining power, job quality, or worker welfare.

## Cumulative logic

The course sequence is deliberately cumulative:

1. define institutions as labor-market mechanisms rather than background context;
2. build the legal and organizational foundations of labor exchange;
3. add norms, identity, and social hierarchy as allocation mechanisms;
4. study persistence, reform, and comparative development;
5. end with frontier research design.

The course should stay distinct from the core sequence. Labor II covers minimum wages, unions, regulation, and enforcement as core labor-policy topics; this course asks why institutional regimes differ, how rules become effective or ineffective, and how formal and informal institutions jointly organize labor markets. Labor I covers discrimination and households; this course studies identity and norms as institutional systems that allocate work and protection. Behavioral Labor studies decision-making wedges; this course studies durable social, legal, and organizational structures.

## Internal roadmap

- **Foundational:** Week 1
- **Legal and institutional foundations:** Weeks 2 to 4
- **Culture and identity focused:** Weeks 5 to 6
- **Persistence, reform, and global comparison:** Weeks 7 to 9
- **Frontier and research-design oriented:** Week 10

## Week-by-week outline

### Week 1. Why Institutions Matter for Labor Markets: Framing, Concepts, and Scope

**Central question:** What counts as an institution in labor economics, and how can institutions be studied without turning the course into a generic political-economy survey?

The opening week defines formal rules, enforcement capacity, informal norms, identity systems, collective organizations, and historical institutions as distinct mechanisms that can affect labor-market outcomes. It introduces the course discipline: start from a labor object, identify the institutional channel, separate theory from evidence, and ask what empirical design could isolate the mechanism.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Institutions matter here because they change the allocation of workers to jobs, the terms of employment, the distribution of rents, and the meaning of observed labor-market inequality.

### Week 2. Labor Law, Enforcement, and State Capacity

**Central question:** When do labor laws alter wages, employment, job quality, and worker protection, and when do they remain only rules on paper?

This week studies labor law through enforcement capacity, inspections, courts, administrative data, legal access, compliance costs, and firm response. The emphasis is not on any one policy instrument, but on the institutional chain that turns statutory rights into lived labor-market constraints for workers and firms.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Enforcement determines the effective wage floor, contract boundary, safety standard, dismissal cost, and legal protection workers actually face.

### Week 3. Informality, Dualism, and Contract Enforcement

**Central question:** How are labor markets organized when formal contracts, payroll systems, and legal protections cover only part of work?

The third week moves from weak or uneven enforcement to informality and dualism. Students study formal-informal sorting, registration, payroll taxation, social insurance, employer evasion, worker preferences, and the role of relational contracts when legal enforcement is limited or selective.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Informality is a labor allocation system that shapes wages, benefits, mobility, productivity, risk, and access to public protection.

### Week 4. Worker Voice, Collective Action, and Bargaining Institutions

**Central question:** How do collective institutions change wages, rents, governance, inequality, and workplace power?

This week studies unions, collective bargaining, works councils, worker representation, strike threats, and collective action constraints as institutional responses to fragmented individual bargaining. The focus is comparative and organizational: how bargaining regimes are built, what they cover, and how they interact with firms, law, product markets, and inequality.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Worker voice institutions alter surplus division, wage-setting, retention, workplace governance, and the distribution of labor-market rents.

### Week 5. Culture, Norms, and Labor-Market Allocation

**Central question:** How do norms and cultural expectations shape labor supply, search, care work, migration, and occupational sorting?

The fifth week introduces culture as a labor-market allocation mechanism rather than as a residual explanation. Students study norms about work, family obligations, status, mobility, entrepreneurship, public employment, and acceptable jobs, with attention to measurement and to separating norms from prices, constraints, and selection.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Norms change who works, where they search, which jobs are acceptable, how households allocate time, and how workers respond to incentives.

### Week 6. Identity, Social Hierarchy, and Labor-Market Institutions

**Central question:** How do institutionalized social categories structure access to jobs, networks, authority, and protection?

This week studies caste, race, ethnicity, gender, religion, migrant status, and other social categories as durable systems of labor allocation. The emphasis is on institutions that reproduce hierarchy through networks, credentialing, occupation boundaries, residential sorting, employer beliefs, public rules, and local governance.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Social hierarchy becomes a labor-market institution when it changes hiring, promotion, bargaining, occupational access, mobility, and exposure to protection or exploitation.

### Week 7. Institutional Persistence, Path Dependence, and Historical Labor-Market Inequality

**Central question:** Why do past labor institutions continue to shape modern labor-market outcomes after the original rules disappear?

The seventh week studies persistence through coercive labor systems, migration regimes, land and property institutions, schooling access, local public goods, and employer-worker power. Students learn how historical shocks can be used carefully, and where persistence claims become too broad without a concrete labor mechanism.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Historical institutions persist in labor markets through skills, mobility costs, networks, occupational structure, employer power, local demand, and unequal access to jobs.

### Week 8. Institutional Reform, Policy Implementation, and Labor-Market Adjustment

**Central question:** Why do similar institutional reforms produce different labor-market effects across places, firms, and workers?

This week turns from persistence to change. Students study labor-law reforms, enforcement expansions, formalization policies, anti-discrimination and equal-opportunity rules, decentralization, and administrative reforms, with emphasis on implementation, compliance, firm adaptation, worker beliefs, and equilibrium adjustment.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Institutional reform changes labor outcomes through take-up, compliance, hiring, firing, wage-setting, contract form, and worker mobility, not through legal text alone.

### Week 9. Comparative and Global Labor-Market Institutions

**Central question:** How do labor-market institutions differ across development settings, welfare states, and global production systems?

The ninth week places the course in comparative perspective. Students compare employment protection, bargaining coverage, public employment, informality, migration regimes, social insurance, industrial relations, and global supply-chain governance while asking which lessons travel across institutional environments and which are setting-specific.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Comparative institutions shape labor demand, labor supply, wage-setting, formality, worker protection, and the development path of labor markets.

### Week 10. Synthesis, Frontier Questions, and Student Research Designs

**Central question:** What does a credible research design on institutions and labor markets look like?

The closing week integrates the course into a research architecture. Students choose a labor outcome, specify the institutional mechanism, define the comparison group or counterfactual, assess threats to identification, consider portability, and state the welfare or distributional object. The week also identifies frontier questions at the boundary of labor, development, history, political economy, and culture.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** The course succeeds only if institutional detail is converted into research designs about work, wages, allocation, bargaining power, inequality, and worker welfare.
