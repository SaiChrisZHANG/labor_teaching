# Labor I: Workers, Human Capital, and Inequality

Labor I is the first semester of a PhD labor-economics field sequence. The course is organized around the worker side of labor markets: labor supply, human capital, wage determination, households, amenities, inequality, discrimination, mobility, worker-targeted public policy, and behavioral frictions. The goal is not only to teach the canonical models and empirical designs, but also to show how worker-side questions connect to contemporary labor research.

The course is written for three overlapping audiences:

- **Bridge**: advanced undergraduate and master's students who need intuition, institutional background, and empirical motivation.
- **Field Core**: PhD students who need models, identification logic, and command of the literature.
- **Research Lab**: students developing research questions, replication skills, and extensions.

By the end of the course, students should be able to move from a labor-market fact or policy question to a researchable economic question, an empirical design, and a clear statement of mechanism. The final weeks also position the worker side within modern labor economics more broadly and set up the transition to **Labor II: Firms, Frictions, and Institutions**.
