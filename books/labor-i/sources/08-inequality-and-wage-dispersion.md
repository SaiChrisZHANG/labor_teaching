# Week 8 source pack — Inequality and Wage Dispersion

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Week 8. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How should labor economists measure labor-market inequality, what margins generate observed wage and earnings dispersion, and how do technology, institutions, firms, sorting, and nonwage job quality each shape the distribution of labor-market outcomes?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor I
- Week: 8
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60--90 minute extension block on linked employer--employee data, firm wage premia, and welfare-oriented interpretations of inequality
- Position in sequence: this is the heavy architecture week after Week 7 job amenities and before Week 9 discrimination, identity, and segmentation
- Goal: teach students to separate measurement from mechanism, wages from broader labor-market value, and descriptive decompositions from causal explanations

## Why this week is intentionally heavier

Week 8 is one of the conceptual anchor weeks of Labor I. It should feel broader and more synthetic than earlier weeks because it consolidates material from Weeks 1--7 and sets up Weeks 9--11.

It should establish:
- why "inequality" is not a single object;
- why hourly wages, annual earnings, employment, hours, amenities, and household resources can tell different distributional stories;
- why inequality can rise through prices, quantities, institutions, firms, sorting, or composition;
- why lower-tail, middle, and upper-tail inequality need not move together;
- why worker-firm sorting, firm rents, and geography changed the modern labor-inequality conversation;
- and why the interpretation of group gaps in Week 9 depends on having this distributional architecture in place.

This week should therefore have a clear core/extension structure:
- **Core material** should comfortably fill a full 3-hour field-course meeting;
- **Extension material** can be used for a second session, appendix slides, or a more advanced lab.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. distinguish wage inequality, earnings inequality, employment inequality, hours inequality, total-compensation inequality, and household inequality;
2. interpret common inequality statistics such as percentile gaps, log-variance measures, and top shares;
3. explain between-group versus within-group inequality and write down a compact variance decomposition;
4. explain the roles of skill supply and demand, tasks and polarization, labor-market institutions, firms, rents, and sorting in modern labor inequality;
5. distinguish composition changes from price changes and understand why both matter;
6. explain why worker-firm heterogeneity changes how we interpret inequality and mobility;
7. connect Week 7 amenities/job quality to a broader notion of labor-market value and welfare inequality;
8. understand why Week 9 discrimination and Week 10 mobility/intergenerational persistence are not substitutes for a general inequality architecture.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should read like a serious labor field-course chapter, not a generic inequality survey and not a political polemic.
- The opening should explicitly say that Week 5 explained wage determination, Week 6 showed how households transform labor-market opportunities, and Week 7 showed why wages do not exhaust job value; Week 8 now asks how those objects aggregate into inequality.
- The chapter should be explicit that inequality is multi-object, multi-mechanism, and multi-level.
- It should separate clearly:
  - measurement objects,
  - descriptive facts,
  - decomposition tools,
  - causal mechanisms,
  - and welfare interpretation.
- The chapter should not imply that one master mechanism explains the entire post-1980 wage distribution.
- It should be empirically literate and method-aware: students should leave knowing why different papers answer different inequality questions.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening section: inequality is not one object

This opening must explicitly connect back to Week 7.

Minimum content:
- Week 7 showed that wages do not equal total job value because amenities matter;
- Week 8 now asks how different labor-market objects map into inequality;
- hourly wages, annual earnings, hours, employment, and total compensation can move differently;
- the distributional question is therefore partly about *what* we are measuring before it is about *why* it changed.

The opening should make clear that many disagreements in the inequality literature come from studying different objects or different parts of the distribution.

### 2. Measurement architecture: objects, statistics, and interpretation

This section should teach the language of inequality.

Minimum content:
- define the distribution function of wages or earnings and the percentile operator;
- explain percentile gaps such as {math}`p90-p10`, {math}`p90-p50`, and {math}`p50-p10`;
- explain why the log variance and percentile ratios emphasize different features of the distribution;
- explain top shares conceptually, even if the chapter does not center them;
- distinguish wage inequality from earnings inequality through the role of hours and employment.

This section should also explain why unequal job quality or amenities from Week 7 complicate any welfare interpretation based on wages alone.

### 3. Between-group, within-group, and residual inequality

Minimum content:
- define group-based decomposition logic using education, occupation, industry, firm, or place groups;
- explain why within-group inequality can rise even when observed composition changes are modest;
- explain the idea of residual inequality after controlling for observed characteristics;
- relate Juhn--Murphy--Pierce style residual thinking to the broader question of skill prices and unobservables.

This section should teach students that decomposition is descriptive but still extremely informative.

### 4. Canonical facts: what changed, where, and in which tails

This is the descriptive core.

Minimum content:
- rising upper-tail inequality;
- different lower-tail dynamics across subperiods;
- changes in the college premium and broader returns to skill;
- polarization or hollowing-out patterns in tasks/occupations;
- the need to distinguish composition-adjusted from raw changes.

This section should not become a long chronology of papers. Organize it around empirical facts the field treats as durable reference points.

### 5. Mechanism block I: supply, demand, tasks, and technology

Minimum content:
- canonical supply-demand logic for skill premia;
- task-based and routine-biased technological change interpretations;
- why upper-tail growth and middle compression need not have the same cause;
- what this mechanism block can and cannot explain.

This section should explicitly connect back to Week 4 human capital and Week 5 returns to skill.

### 6. Mechanism block II: institutions and the lower tail

Minimum content:
- unions, wage-setting institutions, and minimum wages as lower-tail and middle-distribution forces;
- why institutional compression can affect some percentiles more than others;
- how DiNardo--Fortin--Lemieux style counterfactual logic fits into this question;
- why institutional explanations are often distribution-segment specific rather than global.

This section should keep policy discussion analytical rather than normative.

### 7. Mechanism block III: firms, rents, sorting, and geography

This is the modern inequality frontier block.

Minimum content:
- worker heterogeneity versus firm wage premia;
- positive sorting between high-wage workers and high-premium firms;
- between-firm versus within-firm inequality;
- geography/place-based sorting where useful;
- why linked employer--employee data changed the field.

This section should make clear that "firm inequality" is not separate from worker inequality; it is a way of understanding how labor markets allocate workers across wage-setting environments.

### 8. Welfare, total labor-market value, and the bridge from Week 7

Minimum content:
- wages alone may overstate or understate welfare inequality if job quality differs systematically across workers or firms;
- total labor-market value should conceptually combine wages, amenities, stability, hours, and risk;
- inequality in job quality can offset or amplify wage inequality;
- students should understand why Week 8 still focuses on wage/earnings dispersion empirically while acknowledging the broader welfare object.

This section is the conceptual bridge from Week 7 to Week 8.

### 9. Optional extension block

This chapter should contain an explicit optional extension block that can be used in one or more of the following ways:
- a second session on linked employer--employee data and AKM-style decompositions;
- an extension on structural models of the wage distribution and imperfect competition;
- an applied discussion of inequality measurement when job quality and place-based costs of living differ.

## Required formal objects

The retrofitted chapter should contain at least five formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: distributional object and percentile gaps

Use a compact object such as:

```tex
q_t(\tau) = F_t^{-1}(\tau), \qquad D_t^{90-10} = q_t(0.9) - q_t(0.1)
```

Interpretation:
- inequality comparisons are comparisons of quantiles or functions of the distribution;
- upper-tail and lower-tail inequality need not move together.

### Equation 2: between-group / within-group variance decomposition

Use a compact decomposition such as:

```tex
\operatorname{Var}(\log w) = \operatorname{Var}(\mathbb{E}[\log w \mid g]) + \mathbb{E}[\operatorname{Var}(\log w \mid g)]
```

Interpretation:
- inequality can rise because group means diverge or because dispersion within groups rises.

### Equation 3: descriptive wage equation and residual inequality object

Use a compact wage equation such as:

```tex
\log w_{it} = X_{it}'\beta_t + u_{it}
```

Interpretation:
- observed covariates explain part of inequality;
- the residual term captures unobserved or unmodeled dispersion.

### Equation 4: AKM-style worker-firm decomposition

Use a compact linked employer--employee wage equation such as:

```tex
\log w_{it} = \alpha_i + \psi_{J(i,t)} + X_{it}'\beta + \varepsilon_{it}
```

Interpretation:
- inequality may reflect worker heterogeneity, firm wage premia, and sorting between them.

### Equation 5: total labor-market value / total compensation bridge

Use a compact welfare-oriented object such as:

```tex
v_i = w_i + a_i
```

or a slightly richer equivalent where {math}`a_i` captures the value of amenities and other nonwage job attributes.

Interpretation:
- wage inequality is not the same thing as inequality in total labor-market value;
- this is the bridge from Week 7's amenities framework.

### Transfer exercise requirements

Students should not just rerun descriptive code. The bounded extension should ask them to do one of the following:
- construct an inequality factbook for another subgroup, geography, or subperiod;
- compare wage inequality to earnings inequality;
- compare wages to a simple total-compensation proxy;
- or implement a simple between/within or synthetic worker-firm decomposition on a new dataset.
