# Week 10 source pack — Mobility, Migration, and Intergenerational Transmission

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Week 10. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How do workers move across employers, occupations, and places; how are those movements shaped by frictions and labor-market opportunities; and how are labor-market outcomes transmitted across generations?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor I
- Week: 10
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60--90 minute extension block on immigrant upward mobility, place effects, and advanced mobility measurement
- Position in sequence: this is the mobility and transmission week after Week 9 discrimination and before Week 11 worker-side public policies
- Goal: teach students to view mobility as a labor-market allocation problem that spans employer-to-employer moves, occupational change, migration across space, and intergenerational transmission of labor-market outcomes

## Why this week matters

Week 10 is where Labor I moves from static differences in outcomes to the dynamic processes through which workers reallocate and families transmit labor-market advantage or disadvantage. The chapter should make clear that:
- mobility is not one thing: workers move across employers, occupations, industries, locations, and generations;
- migration is not just demography or public economics: it is a labor-market reallocation margin shaped by wages, amenities, information, networks, and moving costs;
- intergenerational persistence is not just about inequality at large: it is about how labor-market opportunities, careers, firms, and places connect parents to children;
- recent progress in empirical labor economics has come not only from new datasets but from new identification strategies and measurement tools.

This week should feel explicitly more empirical-methods-aware than many other weeks in Labor I.

## Content emphasis for this week

The lecture should remain labor-focused. It should not drift into a generic public-finance or political-economy lecture.

The organizing logic should be:
1. define the relevant mobility objects;
2. explain the labor-market mechanisms behind movement and persistence;
3. show how modern empirical designs identify mobility frictions, migration returns, and intergenerational transmission;
4. connect mobility to earlier weeks on wages, inequality, discrimination, and sorting.

Applications involving housing, neighborhoods, immigration law, or place-based policy can appear when they sharpen labor-market concepts, but the chapter should keep labor-market outcomes at the center:
- wages,
- employment,
- occupational upgrading,
- firm assignment,
- job ladders,
- geographic labor mobility,
- and intergenerational transmission of labor-market rank and careers.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. distinguish employer mobility, occupational mobility, geographic migration, and intergenerational mobility;
2. define the main empirical objects used to study mobility, including transition rates, hazards, transition matrices, rank-rank slopes, intergenerational elasticities, and occupational following;
3. explain a Roy-style selection logic for migration and mobility choices;
4. explain why migration and job mobility depend on information, moving costs, credit constraints, and labor-market frictions;
5. explain how employer-to-employer mobility and occupational mobility relate to worker sorting and wage growth;
6. interpret major causal designs used in this literature, including lotteries, reforms, border or exposure designs, linked administrative data, and rank-rank methods;
7. explain how intergenerational persistence can operate through skills, careers, firms, and places rather than treating it as a black-box correlation;
8. connect mobility to Week 8 inequality and Week 9 discrimination without collapsing the topics together.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should read like a serious labor field-course chapter with a strong empirical backbone.
- The opening should explicitly tell students that labor-market inequality and discrimination are dynamic because workers are sorted across firms, occupations, locations, and families over time.
- The prose should be disciplined about the distinction between:
  - contemporaneous inequality,
  - mobility across labor-market states,
  - migration across local labor markets,
  - and persistence across generations.
- The chapter should use migration and intergenerational mobility as labor-market concepts, not as excuses to leave labor economics.
- The methods discussion should be explicit and pedagogical rather than tacked on.

## Why this week is intentionally method-heavy

This week should foreground empirical progress in the field. A major part of what students need to learn is not just the concepts, but how labor economists have become more credible in measuring them:
- linked employer-employee data changed what we can learn about worker reallocation and sorting;
- linked parent-child administrative data changed what we can learn about mobility across generations;
- policy reforms, lotteries, quasi-random placement rules, and new exposure designs changed what we can learn about migration and mobility frictions;
- new measurement work shows that even the baseline mobility objects can be mismeasured.

The chapter should therefore include a dedicated empirical-design block and treat causal inference as central rather than optional.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening section: mobility as labor-market reallocation and transmission

Minimum content:
- Week 8 studied inequality levels and Week 9 studied discrimination objects; Week 10 asks how workers and families move through the labor market.
- Define the relevant margins:
  - employer-to-employer mobility,
  - occupational mobility,
  - geographic migration,
  - and intergenerational mobility.
- Emphasize that these are related but not identical objects.
- Make clear that labor economists care about mobility because it shapes wage growth, sorting, matching, spatial adjustment, and the persistence of inequality.

### 2. Measurement architecture: what is a mobility object?

Minimum content:
- transition probabilities and transition matrices;
- hazards and duration dependence;
- employer-to-employer flows;
- occupational upgrading or downgrading;
- migration rates and net migration;
- rank-rank slopes, intergenerational elasticities, and transition probabilities across income or occupational bins.

This section should teach students that the mobility object itself is a choice.

### 3. Worker mobility across employers and occupations

Minimum content:
- employer-to-employer mobility as a central labor-market margin;
- wage growth along job ladders and movement across firms;
- occupational mobility as skill reallocation rather than a residual category;
- links to worker-firm sorting from Week 5 and discrimination/sorting from Week 9;
- why measuring employer-to-employer reallocation is harder than it first appears.

This section should use job mobility to connect micro labor-supply choices to equilibrium sorting.

### 4. Migration as labor-market reallocation across places

Minimum content:
- migration as a comparison of expected gains across locations net of moving costs and amenities;
- Roy-style selection and expected income logic;
- information frictions, search frictions, housing constraints, and credit constraints;
- migration as a labor-market adjustment margin rather than only a population outcome;
- the role of networks and initial assignment rules in generating quasi-experimental variation.

This section should remain labor-market focused, even when place-based language appears.

### 5. Intergenerational transmission of labor-market outcomes

Minimum content:
- define intergenerational persistence in rank, level, and career terms;
- distinguish persistence in labor-market outcomes from broader wealth or consumption persistence;
- show that occupation, firm type, place, and family investments can all transmit labor-market advantage;
- explain the difference between intergenerational income rank, occupational following, and career persistence;
- connect this section back to Week 4 human capital and Week 6 households/family.

### 6. Empirical progress block: how the field got better at measuring mobility

This is the empirical core of the lecture.

Minimum content:
- linked administrative data for workers, firms, parents, and children;
- job-mobility-based identification of firm heterogeneity and reallocation;
- measurement correction for employer-to-employer mobility;
- policy-reform and border/exposure designs in migration;
- lottery or credit-access designs for mobility frictions;
- rank-rank and transition-matrix approaches in intergenerational mobility;
- occupation-based decomposition of intergenerational persistence.

This block should explicitly teach the progression in empirical design.

### 7. Causal inference block: what do key designs identify?

Minimum content:
- what a lottery design identifies in a mobility context;
- what a reform-induced migration design identifies;
- what refugee-placement or dispersal rules identify;
- what rank-rank regressions do and do not identify;
- how measurement error and selected samples distort mobility estimates;
- why descriptive mobility patterns and causal mobility effects are different objects.

This section should be unusually explicit about identifying assumptions.

### 8. Mobility, sorting, and inequality

Minimum content:
- mobility can reduce, preserve, or amplify inequality depending on which transitions are feasible;
- sorting across firms, occupations, and places is part of the inequality process, not a separate afterthought;
- intergenerational transmission can operate through labor-market institutions rather than only within the household;
- migration and mobility frictions can turn local labor-market shocks into persistent inequality.

This section should be the conceptual payoff from the week.

### 9. Optional extension block

This chapter should contain an explicit optional extension block that can be used for:
- immigrant upward mobility across generations;
- place-based variation in mobility and local labor-market opportunity;
- measurement error in historical or linked-record mobility studies;
- structural migration models and dynamic moving decisions.

## Required formal objects

The retrofitted chapter should contain at least six formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: transition matrix or hazard object

Use a simple mobility object such as:

```tex
p_{ab} = \Pr(S_{t+1}=b \mid S_t=a)
```

or a hazard:

```tex
h_t = \Pr(T=t \mid T \ge t)
```

Interpretation:
- mobility depends on the state space and time horizon being studied.

### Equation 2: migration choice index

Use a compact migration-choice object such as:

```tex
U_{il} = \mathbb{E}[w_{il}] + A_{il} - C_{il}
```

where wages, amenities, and moving costs jointly determine location choice.

Interpretation:
- migration is a labor-market choice net of frictions, not just a demographic event.

### Equation 3: employer or occupation mobility wage-growth object

Use a wage-growth decomposition or event-study style object linking wage changes to employer or occupation moves.

Interpretation:
- mobility changes wages partly because workers move across different opportunity sets.

### Equation 4: reform or exposure design

Use a compact reduced-form specification such as a difference-in-differences or exposure design for migration or mobility.

Interpretation:
- policy reforms and quasi-random placement rules can isolate movement-induced labor-market effects.

### Equation 5: rank-rank mobility object

Use a canonical intergenerational mobility equation such as:

```tex
\text{rank}^{child}_i = \alpha + \beta \,\text{rank}^{parent}_i + \varepsilon_i
```

Interpretation:
- rank persistence is a descriptive mobility object, not yet a causal mechanism.

### Equation 6: occupation-based transmission or decomposition object

Use a decomposition that separates direct family transmission from occupational or place channels.

Interpretation:
- intergenerational persistence can be mediated by labor-market allocation, not only by human-capital transmission in the narrow sense.

## Code-lab transfer ideas

Possible bounded transfer exercises:
1. build a small transition-matrix exercise for occupation or employer moves using public or synthetic panel data;
2. estimate a simplified rank-rank relationship on a synthetic or public linked parent-child dataset;
3. replicate a stylized migration-return calculation using small public commuting or earnings data;
4. compare descriptive mobility measures before and after correcting a simple missing-data or selection problem.

The transfer exercise should remain small and local. It should not require restricted linked microdata.

## Things the draft should avoid

- Do not turn the lecture into a generic immigration policy debate.
- Do not let intergenerational mobility become a generic public-finance lecture.
- Do not treat mobility as a single number without explaining the underlying object.
- Do not present causal designs without stating the identifying variation.
- Do not imply that intergenerational rank-rank slopes are themselves causal mechanisms.
- Do not neglect the labor-market channels through occupations, firms, places, and search frictions.
