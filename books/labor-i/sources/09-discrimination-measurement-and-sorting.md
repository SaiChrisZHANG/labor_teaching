# Week 9 source pack — Discrimination: Measurement, Mechanisms, and Sorting

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Week 9. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

What should labor economists mean by discrimination, how can discrimination be distinguished from broader group gaps, which mechanisms can generate discriminatory outcomes, and what empirical designs let us identify discrimination and its interaction with sorting and segmentation?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor I
- Week: 9
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60--90 minute extension block on employer report cards, task-based discrimination, and equilibrium sorting
- Position in sequence: this is the conceptual discrimination week after Week 8 inequality and before Week 10 mobility, migration, and intergenerational transmission
- Goal: teach students to separate discrimination from broader inequality, measurement from mechanism, and reduced-form gaps from equilibrium discrimination objects

## Content emphasis for this week

This week should **not** be organized around one protected group. Race, gender, ethnicity, and other identities should appear as applications and illustrations, but the lecture's organizing logic should be more foundational:
- what discrimination is;
- why outcome gaps are not automatically discrimination;
- how taste-based, statistical, identity-based, and task/sorting mechanisms differ;
- how discrimination can operate in hiring, wages, promotions, separations, and task assignment;
- why equilibrium sorting and segmentation matter;
- and how causal inference designs can isolate discriminatory treatment.

The chapter should therefore feel more like a field-course architecture lecture than a case-study lecture.

## Why this week is intentionally heavier

Week 9 is one of the conceptual pivot weeks of Labor I. It needs more structure than a single reduced-form topic because students often conflate:
- raw group gaps,
- conditional gaps,
- causal discriminatory treatment,
- equilibrium sorting,
- and broader social or pre-market inequality.

This week should disentangle those objects carefully. It should also build the conceptual bridge from Week 8 inequality to later topics in mobility, institutions, and behavioral labor.

The chapter should be built as:
- **Core material** for a full 3-hour field-course meeting;
- **Optional extension** for a second discussion, appendix slides, or a research-lab session on firm-level report cards and structural/task-based discrimination.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. explain why a raw gap is not yet a measure of discrimination;
2. distinguish taste-based discrimination, statistical discrimination, identity/norm-based mechanisms, and sorting/segmentation mechanisms;
3. explain why discrimination can operate at multiple margins: hiring, wages, promotions, tasks, retention, and firm assignment;
4. write down a simple decomposition that separates observed characteristics from residual group differences;
5. explain why omitted variables, post-treatment controls, and endogenous selection complicate empirical measurement;
6. explain the logic of correspondence/audit studies, paired audits, regression decompositions, and structural/equilibrium approaches;
7. explain how discrimination and sorting interact in labor markets rather than treating them as separate topics;
8. connect discrimination to Week 8 inequality without collapsing the two concepts.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should read like a serious labor field-course chapter, not a generic social-science essay.
- The opening should explicitly say that Week 8 studied the architecture of inequality, while Week 9 asks which part of observed group differences should be interpreted as discrimination.
- The prose should be precise about the difference between:
  - outcome gaps,
  - treatment differences,
  - belief differences,
  - and equilibrium allocation outcomes.
- The chapter should avoid implying that discrimination is only about hiring or only about one identity dimension.
- It should also avoid implying that any conditional residual is automatically discrimination.
- Use examples from race, gender, and other identities, but keep the lecture organized around concepts and empirical logic.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening section: from inequality to discrimination

Minimum content:
- Week 8 established that inequality is a multi-object, multi-mechanism concept.
- Week 9 asks a narrower and harder question: when should a gap in labor outcomes be interpreted as discrimination?
- The chapter should state immediately that observed group gaps can reflect:
  - pre-market skill differences,
  - endogenous schooling or occupation choices,
  - differential treatment by employers or coworkers,
  - differences in information or beliefs,
  - and sorting across firms, occupations, places, or tasks.

The opening should make clear that discrimination is both a conceptual and an empirical identification problem.

### 2. What is discrimination? Competing economic definitions

Minimum content:
- introduce the difference between group gaps and discriminatory treatment;
- define a taste-based wedge in employer utility or profits;
- define statistical discrimination as differential treatment driven by group-contingent beliefs or information;
- discuss identity/norm-based or social-interaction mechanisms where preferences and workplace norms shape outcomes;
- emphasize that multiple mechanisms can generate similar reduced-form gaps.

This section should not become a history-of-thought lecture, but it should make students fluent in the classic mechanisms.

### 3. Measurement architecture: why gaps are not enough

Minimum content:
- define raw group gaps and conditional gaps;
- explain why conditioning choices matter;
- explain bad controls and post-treatment controls, especially education, occupation, or firm assignment when these are themselves potentially affected by discrimination;
- introduce decomposition logic while warning that decompositions are descriptive rather than fully causal;
- explain why selection into employment or application pools can distort inference.

This section should train students to resist naive interpretations of residual gaps.

### 4. Mechanism block I: taste-based discrimination

Minimum content:
- employer, coworker, or customer prejudice as wedges in effective labor cost or utility;
- why competition may or may not eliminate discriminatory behavior depending on margins and market structure;
- why taste-based discrimination can affect hiring, wages, task assignment, and segregation.

This section should make clear that taste-based models predict both treatment differences and sorting patterns.

### 5. Mechanism block II: statistical discrimination and belief-based mechanisms

Minimum content:
- imperfect information and group-conditioned beliefs;
- employer use of group averages when signals are noisy;
- self-confirming or equilibrium feedback possibilities;
- dynamic responses in schooling or training when workers anticipate biased treatment.

This section should connect naturally to Week 4 human capital and Week 5 returns to skill.

### 6. Mechanism block III: discrimination, segmentation, and sorting

Minimum content:
- occupational, firm, and task segregation as equilibrium outcomes;
- group differences in search, referral, or queueing environments;
- sorting into different wage-setting firms or tasks;
- the difference between within-firm discriminatory treatment and between-firm segregation;
- why the same market can feature both discriminatory treatment and discriminatory allocation.

This section should explicitly connect to Week 8 firm inequality and to future mobility topics.

### 7. Empirical identification block: designs and what they identify

This is the empirical core of the lecture.

Minimum content:
- correspondence and audit studies;
- paired audits and within-firm comparisons;
- regression and decomposition approaches;
- quasi-experimental designs where policy or information changes shift discriminatory treatment;
- structural and equilibrium models that infer discrimination from sorting, wages, and task allocation.

This section should be explicit about target parameters:
- a callback/contact gap;
- a wage gap conditional on selected controls;
- a firm-specific discrimination measure;
- an equilibrium discrimination wedge inferred from occupational sorting.

### 8. How to think about causality in discrimination research

Minimum content:
- what an experimental hiring gap identifies;
- what a decomposition does and does not identify;
- why selected samples complicate wage-gap inference;
- why discrimination at one margin can distort observed data at another margin;
- why equilibrium responses mean that the object of interest may differ from a direct treatment effect.

This should be the place where the chapter most clearly teaches causal-inference discipline.

### 9. Optional extension block

This chapter should contain an explicit optional extension block that can be used for:
- employer-level report cards and ranking uncertainty;
- task-based discrimination and structural occupational sorting;
- algorithmic screening and new measurement designs;
- links between discrimination, monopsony, and firm power.

## Required formal objects

The retrofitted chapter should contain at least six formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: raw and conditional group gaps

Use a compact object such as:

```tex
\Delta = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid G=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y \mid G=0]
```

and a conditional analogue such as:

```tex
\Delta(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid G=1, X=x] - \mathbb{E}[Y \mid G=0, X=x]
```

Interpretation:
- observed gaps depend on what outcome and conditioning set are used;
- raw gaps and conditional gaps answer different questions.

### Equation 2: Oaxaca--Blinder style decomposition

Use a compact decomposition such as:

```tex
\mathbb{E}[Y \mid G=1] - \mathbb{E}[Y \mid G=0]
= \big(\mathbb{E}[X \mid G=1]-\mathbb{E}[X \mid G=0]\big)\beta_0
+ \mathbb{E}[X \mid G=1](\beta_1-\beta_0)
```

Interpretation:
- decompositions separate observed-composition differences from coefficient differences;
- coefficient differences are not automatically a causal measure of discrimination.

### Equation 3: taste-based employer problem

Use a compact employer profit object such as:

```tex
\pi_j = p \cdot q_{ij} - w_i - d_j \mathbf{1}\{G_i=1\}
```

Interpretation:
- discriminatory tastes act like a group-specific cost wedge;
- taste-based discrimination can alter both hiring and compensation.

### Equation 4: statistical discrimination hiring rule

Use a compact decision rule such as:

```tex
\text{Hire if } \mathbb{E}[a_i \mid s_i, G_i] \geq c_j
```

Interpretation:
- group-conditioned beliefs matter when productivity is imperfectly observed;
- the mechanism operates through beliefs, not necessarily tastes.

### Equation 5: audit-study callback effect

Use a compact object such as:

```tex
\tau^{cb} = \mathbb{E}[C_i(1) - C_i(0)]
```

where treatment changes the signaled group identity while holding qualifications fixed.

Interpretation:
- correspondence designs identify discriminatory treatment at a specific margin;
- they do not by themselves identify every equilibrium consequence of discrimination.

### Equation 6: sorting / segmentation object

Use a compact allocation object such as:

```tex
s_{gf} = \Pr(F_i = f \mid G_i = g)
```

or an expected firm-premium object such as:

```tex
\mathbb{E}[\psi_{F_i} \mid G_i=g]
```

Interpretation:
- discrimination can show up through group-specific assignment to firms, occupations, or tasks;
- sorting is part of the discrimination architecture, not merely an omitted background fact.

### Transfer idea

The bounded student transfer exercise should not require students to run their own discrimination field experiment. Instead, it should ask them to transfer one estimator or measurement idea to a small public or synthetic dataset, for example:
- estimating callback/contact gaps across subgroups or firm categories in a reduced public dataset;
- implementing a simple report-card/ranking routine on synthetic employer-gap data;
- exploring how sorting can alter observed group differences even when direct treatment effects are small.
