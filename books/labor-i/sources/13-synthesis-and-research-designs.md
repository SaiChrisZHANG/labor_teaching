# Week 13 source pack — Synthesis, Student Research Designs, and the Bridge to Labor II

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Week 13. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and research-design lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How do the worker-side topics from Labor I fit together inside modern labor economics, and how can students turn those building blocks into a clean research idea that naturally opens into Labor II?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor I
- Week: 13
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60--90 minute research-workshop extension block
- Position in sequence: final week of Labor I after Week 12's introduction to behavioral labor
- Goal: synthesize the semester, convert topic mastery into research capability, and show how worker-side questions plug into firms, frictions, institutions, and equilibrium adjustment in Labor II

## Why this week matters

This week should not feel like a loose review session. It should function as a **research-design studio**.

By Week 13, students have seen worker choice, dynamic responses, human capital, wage determination, households, amenities, inequality, discrimination, mobility, policy, and behavioral frictions. The final week should make those pieces legible as parts of a larger research program.

The main message is:
- Labor I gives the primitives and margins on the worker side;
- modern labor economics studies how those worker-side primitives interact with firms, search frictions, wage-setting institutions, organizations, and aggregate shocks;
- a good labor paper usually combines a mechanism, a clean object of interest, credible design, and a clear statement of where equilibrium or institutional responses matter.

## Content emphasis for this week

This is a capstone and application lecture. It should do three things at once:

1. **Synthesize** the conceptual architecture of Labor I.
2. **Teach students how to formulate a research idea** using the semester's topics.
3. **Foreshadow Labor II** by showing why worker-side questions are incomplete without firms, labor demand, search, monopsony, institutions, and aggregate adjustment.

This week must remain labor-focused. It should not drift into generic methodology or broad social-science advice. Every research-design discussion should stay tied to labor-market objects such as:
- participation, hours, effort, search, mobility, and job choice;
- human-capital investment and returns;
- household constraints and joint decision-making;
- wage formation, amenities, and job quality;
- inequality, discrimination, and sorting;
- policy design and behavioral responses.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. explain how the core worker-side topics of Labor I fit together as one field rather than a list of lectures;
2. distinguish a descriptive gap, a behavioral margin, a mechanism, and a research question;
3. map a research idea into objects, mechanism, data, design, and contribution;
4. explain why labor papers must often connect worker-side behavior to firms, markets, and institutions;
5. identify where a project remains partial-equilibrium and where Labor II questions begin;
6. distinguish strong from weak labor research questions;
7. formulate a short research memo related to at least one Labor I topic;
8. explain how the same worker-side question can look different once firms, frictions, and institutions are added;
9. identify several concrete pathways from Labor I into Labor II and into Behavioral Labor.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should feel like a serious PhD field-course capstone.
- It should be more synthetic and reflective than the previous weeks, but still concrete and methodologically disciplined.
- The prose should repeatedly ask: what is the object? what is the mechanism? what design isolates it? what equilibrium or institutional forces are left in the background?
- The tone should be encouraging but demanding. Students should leave believing they can formulate a paper, but also understanding that good ideas need discipline.

## Why this week is intentionally heavier

Week 13 needs more room than a typical wrap-up lecture because it has to do real intellectual work:
- integrate all previous lectures;
- classify research ideas;
- teach how to move from a topic to a question;
- teach how to move from a question to a design;
- teach how to judge whether a project belongs in Labor I alone or whether it really needs Labor II tools.

Treat this as a full 3-hour core lecture plus an optional workshop block in which students begin building their own proposal.

## Synthetic map of Weeks 1--12

The chapter should explicitly connect the semester as follows:

- Week 1 measurement -> what labor economists try to explain;
- Weeks 2--3 labor supply -> core worker-choice margins over static and dynamic horizons;
- Week 4 human capital -> skill accumulation and investment;
- Week 5 wages and returns -> how worker attributes map into pay;
- Week 6 households -> how family structure and care constraints reshape worker behavior;
- Week 7 amenities -> why wages are not the only object in job choice;
- Week 8 inequality -> how worker outcomes aggregate into dispersion;
- Week 9 discrimination -> how differential treatment and sorting distort allocation;
- Week 10 mobility and migration -> how workers reallocate across jobs, places, and generations;
- Week 11 policy -> how the state changes incentives, insurance, and constraints;
- Week 12 behavioral frictions -> why observed choices may not reveal frictionless preferences or beliefs.

Then the chapter should add the key bridge:

**Labor II asks what happens when those worker-side objects are embedded in firms, search frictions, labor demand, wage-setting, workplace organization, regulation, and aggregate shocks.**

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening section: what Labor I has accomplished

Minimum content:
- clearly state that Labor I has built the worker side of labor economics;
- summarize the main objects, not just the topics;
- explain why the worker side is indispensable but incomplete;
- tell students that the purpose of Week 13 is to convert a field sequence into a research agenda.

### 2. The unified worker-side framework

Minimum content:
- build a compact framework that nests labor supply, human capital, household constraints, job amenities, mobility, policy, and behavioral frictions;
- explain how each week changes one margin or one wedge in the worker problem;
- show that many apparently different literatures are asking related questions about choices, constraints, and allocation.

This section should make the semester feel cumulative rather than episodic.

### 3. What makes a good labor research question?

Minimum content:
- distinguish a topic from a question;
- distinguish a descriptive pattern from a mechanism;
- distinguish a mechanism from an identification strategy;
- explain the difference between a labor question that is worker-side and one that already requires Labor II tools.

This section should offer explicit criteria for a good paper idea.

### 4. The mechanism--data--design--contribution pipeline

Minimum content:
- teach students to map a proposal into:
  1. object of interest,
  2. mechanism,
  3. data,
  4. identifying variation or model discipline,
  5. contribution to the literature;
- explain common failure modes: too broad, no estimand, weak design, unclear mechanism, or no labor contribution.

This is one of the week's central teaching blocks.

### 5. Research-idea templates based on Labor I topics

Minimum content:
- at least six research templates drawn from the semester, for example:
  - labor supply under new constraints or information structures;
  - skill investment and training design;
  - wage determination with selection or sorting;
  - household/time-allocation responses to policy or shocks;
  - job quality and amenities;
  - inequality or discrimination with richer sorting or measurement;
  - mobility and reallocation;
  - behavioral frictions in work, search, or take-up.

Each template should state:
- question,
- mechanism,
- candidate data,
- candidate design,
- likely pitfalls,
- and whether Labor II tools become necessary.

### 6. Bridge to Labor II: where worker-side questions become market/equilibrium questions

Minimum content:
- explain that many Labor I questions become richer once one adds:
  - labor demand,
  - search and matching,
  - wage-setting,
  - monopsony / labor market power,
  - personnel economics,
  - institutions and regulation,
  - aggregate shocks and adjustment;
- illustrate how a worker-side question changes when embedded in firm behavior or equilibrium.

This section should explicitly foreshadow Labor II rather than just mentioning it in passing.

### 7. Methods block: research design in modern labor economics

Minimum content:
- explain how the semester's empirical designs fit together:
  - descriptive measurement and decomposition,
  - quasi-experimental policy variation,
  - event studies and dynamic designs,
  - experiments and audits,
  - matched worker--firm designs,
  - structural or equilibrium models where reduced form is insufficient;
- teach students that design choice must match the object and mechanism;
- explain that moving from Labor I into Labor II often changes the required design or model.

### 8. Student research memo studio

Minimum content:
- provide a structured proposal template;
- explain what a 2--3 page research memo should contain;
- describe how students can start from any week in Labor I and turn it into a proposal;
- provide guidance on how to pitch one clean idea rather than a literature survey.

This section should be concrete enough that students could draft a memo immediately after class.

### 9. Conclusion: where Labor I sits in modern labor economics

Minimum content:
- summarize Labor I as the worker-side core of the field;
- explain that modern labor economics studies worker choices jointly with firms, institutions, and shocks;
- end with a clear roadmap into Labor II and into specialized fields like Behavioral Labor, Gender, and Institutions.

The conclusion should leave students with both closure and forward motion.

## Required formal objects / equations

The chapter should contain at least six formal objects.

### Equation 1: compact worker-side choice problem

Use a compact nested worker problem that can accommodate many semester topics, for example:

```tex
V_i = \max_{h, s, j, m} U(c_i, h_i, a_j, \ell_i; \theta_i) + \beta E[V_{i,t+1}] \quad \text{subject to worker-side constraints}
```

Interpretation:
- {math}`h` for labor supply or effort;
- {math}`s` for skill investment;
- {math}`j` for job choice / amenity bundle;
- {math}`m` for mobility or migration;
- worker-side constraints summarize policy, household, discrimination, and behavioral wedges.

### Equation 2: human-capital accumulation object

Use a dynamic skill object such as:

```tex
k_{t+1} = g(k_t, s_t, x_t, \varepsilon_t)
```

Interpretation:
- reminds students that current worker choices shape future opportunities.

### Equation 3: job-value object

Use a job-choice object such as:

```tex
J_{ij} = w_{ij} + A_{ij} - C_{ij}
```

Interpretation:
- wages, amenities, and worker-specific costs jointly determine job choice.

### Equation 4: causal estimand object

Use a clean potential-outcomes object such as:

```tex
\tau = E[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)]
```

Interpretation:
- emphasizes that every labor project needs a well-defined object of interest.

### Equation 5: assignment / sorting object

Use a simple allocation object such as:

```tex
P(j \mid i) = f(k_i, z_i, a_j, w_j, d_{ij})
```

Interpretation:
- worker outcomes depend on sorting across jobs, firms, and places.

### Equation 6: bridge-to-Labor-II object

Use a compact equilibrium or market-side bridge, such as a matching or wage-setting object:

```tex
M(U, V) \quad \text{or} \quad w_{ij} = \phi_i + \psi_j + \varepsilon_{ij}
```

Interpretation:
- shows the point at which Labor I questions become Labor II questions.

## Reading ladder guidance

This week should not overload students with new mandatory readings. Instead, it should assign a light synthesis menu and then point students back to chosen weeks.

A good structure is:
- one broad worker-side synthesis reading;
- one firm/equilibrium bridge reading;
- one methods or research-design bridge reading;
- a short menu of exemplar papers students can use as proposal anchors.

## Research memo deliverable

This week should end with a concrete student deliverable.

Recommended assignment:
- a 2--3 page research memo;
- one central question;
- one mechanism;
- one proposed data source;
- one identification strategy or model discipline;
- one paragraph on why the idea belongs in labor economics;
- one paragraph on whether the idea already needs Labor II tools.

The week should encourage students to write a **feasible first project**, not a gigantic agenda.

## Lab design for Week 13

The Week 13 lab should be a **research-design studio**, not a heavy standalone replication.

Preferred lab structure:
- **Reproduce**: choose one bounded anchor from earlier weeks or one small illustrative result from a provided synthesis packet;
- **Diagnose**: explain the mechanism, data, estimand, and identification logic;
- **Propose**: write a structured extension memo that either deepens the worker-side question or motivates a Labor II extension.

The bounded pedagogical path should avoid large restricted data and should run locally.

## Suggested anchor-paper menu for the Week 13 lab

Use a small menu rather than a single required paper. Good options include:
- `@card1999` as the returns-to-education anchor;
- `@autorKatzKearney2008` as the inequality anchor;
- `@cardCardosoHeiningKline2018` or `@songEtAl2019` as the worker--firm bridge anchor;
- `@neumark2018` as the discrimination-identification anchor;
- `@dellavigna2009` as the behavioral bridge anchor.

The point is not to re-teach the whole paper, but to help students see how to extend an established design.

## Labor II foreshadowing requirements

The chapter, slides, and lab should all foreshadow Labor II explicitly.

At minimum, Week 13 should preview:
- labor demand;
- search and matching;
- wage-setting and bargaining;
- labor market power / monopsony;
- personnel economics;
- labor-market institutions and regulation;
- technology, trade, and aggregate adjustment.

The message should be that Labor I is the worker-side half of the field, and Labor II embeds those worker-side decisions into firms and markets.

## What success looks like

A strong Week 13 chapter should make students feel:
- they understand the first semester as one coherent field;
- they can formulate a researchable labor question of their own;
- they know where worker-side analysis ends and Labor II begins;
- and they are leaving the semester with a research agenda, not just lecture notes.
