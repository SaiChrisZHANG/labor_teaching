# Week 11 source pack — Evaluating Public Policies Targeting Workers

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Week 11. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How should labor economists evaluate policies that primarily act on workers' constraints, incentives, information sets, and insurance environments rather than directly regulating firms?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor I
- Week: 11
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60--90 minute extension block on heterogeneous policy effects, welfare measurement, and external validity
- Position in sequence: this is the worker-side policy capstone after Week 10 mobility and before Week 12 labor supply under frictions
- Goal: synthesize the first ten weeks of Labor I by showing how worker-targeted policies operate through participation, hours, search, skill formation, household allocation, mobility, and take-up

## Why this week matters

Week 11 should feel like the semester's worker-side application lecture rather than a disconnected policy sampler. By this point students have already studied:
- labor supply and lifecycle responses;
- human capital and wage determination;
- households and fertility;
- amenities and compensating differentials;
- inequality, discrimination, mobility, and intergenerational transmission.

This lecture asks how policies intervene in those margins.

The chapter should make clear that worker-targeted policies can work through several distinct channels:
1. **incentives**: taxes, credits, benefit schedules, earnings tests, phase-ins, phase-outs, and dynamic reward structures;
2. **constraints**: liquidity, childcare, transport, disability, retirement rules, and eligibility conditions;
3. **frictions**: incomplete information, complexity, administrative burden, take-up barriers, search frictions, and delayed adjustment;
4. **insurance**: income smoothing, downside protection, consumption stabilization, and risk sharing;
5. **investment**: training, job-readiness, and skill-building policies that shift future earnings opportunities.

The lecture should therefore help students see policy evaluation as the place where the semester's theory and empirical design meet.

## Content emphasis for this week

This week must remain labor-focused. It should not become a generic public-finance lecture about optimal taxes or a general social-policy survey.

The organizing logic should be:
1. define what counts as a worker-targeted policy in labor economics;
2. map policies to the worker-side margins developed in Weeks 2--10;
3. show how modern empirical designs identify policy effects on labor-market behavior;
4. distinguish statutory policy from actual policy exposure once take-up, salience, and frictions are considered;
5. emphasize the research questions that matter when moving from reduced-form policy effects to interpretation and design.

Applications can include:
- in-work tax credits and earnings subsidies;
- unemployment insurance and search assistance;
- training and job-readiness programs;
- childcare and family-support policies;
- disability and retirement incentives;
- take-up design, information, and administrative simplification.

But all of them should be framed in terms of labor-market outcomes such as:
- employment and participation;
- hours and earnings;
- nonemployment duration and reemployment wages;
- search effort and job finding;
- skill acquisition and future earnings;
- household labor allocation;
- mobility and persistence.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. distinguish worker-targeted policies from firm-targeted or market-level labor policies;
2. map a policy into the worker-side margins it changes: participation, hours, search, training, household time allocation, mobility, or take-up;
3. explain why the same policy can have different positive and normative implications when it operates through incentives, insurance, or frictions;
4. interpret how EITC-style schedules affect labor supply at the extensive and intensive margins;
5. explain the core labor-economics questions in unemployment insurance and search-assistance evaluation;
6. explain why training and skill policies should be evaluated dynamically rather than only using short-run employment effects;
7. understand how disability and retirement policies identify labor-supply responsiveness through institutional rules, judge assignment, or bunching designs;
8. explain why policy take-up is itself a labor-economics object rather than a nuisance detail;
9. describe the major empirical designs used in this literature, including kink and bunching designs, judge IV, randomized encouragement, field experiments, reform/event studies, and difference-in-knowledge designs;
10. articulate what researchers must still learn beyond average treatment effects: heterogeneity, equilibrium incidence, dynamic responses, welfare, and external validity.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should read like a serious PhD labor field-course capstone.
- It should repeatedly remind students that policies are evaluated not only by whether they move a single labor-supply margin, but by **which** workers respond, **how** they respond, and **whether** those responses reflect incentives, constraints, or frictions.
- The chapter should be explicit about the distinction between:
  - statutory generosity versus effective generosity,
  - eligibility versus take-up,
  - short-run versus dynamic effects,
  - partial-equilibrium versus broader-incidence or equilibrium effects,
  - reduced-form effects versus design or welfare interpretation.
- The prose should be more synthetic than earlier weeks: this is a wrap-up/application lecture, so it should tie backward to Weeks 2--10 and forward to Week 12 frictions.

## Why this week is intentionally heavy

This week should be longer and more thorough than a standard single-program lecture. It is where students should start to think like researchers who must decide:
- what labor-market margin a policy is supposed to affect;
- whether the identifying design matches the policy question;
- whether measured effects reflect incentives, information, or constraints;
- how to interpret null results when frictions or low take-up are present;
- when welfare claims are credible and when they are not.

Treat this chapter as a full 3-hour core with a clearly marked optional extension block on frontier research questions.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening section: what counts as a worker-targeted policy?

Minimum content:
- define worker-targeted policies as policies that primarily alter worker-side incentives, constraints, insurance, information, or investments;
- distinguish them from firm-targeted regulation, bargaining institutions, or labor-market-wide price setting that will dominate Labor II;
- emphasize that the lecture synthesizes Weeks 2--10 rather than introducing a brand-new theoretical core.

The opening should explicitly map prior weeks onto policy-relevant margins:
- Week 2 static labor supply -> taxes and transfer schedules;
- Week 3 dynamic labor supply -> timing and duration of benefits;
- Week 4 human capital -> training and skill policy;
- Week 6 households -> childcare, leave, and care constraints;
- Week 10 mobility -> search assistance and mobility support.

### 2. Unified framework: constraints, incentives, frictions, insurance, and investment

Minimum content:
- a worker-choice framework showing how taxes, benefits, childcare prices, or search assistance alter feasible sets or returns;
- a discussion of why seemingly similar policies can operate through different channels;
- a decomposition between margins of behavior and margins of welfare.

This section should be the conceptual backbone of the lecture.

### 3. In-work subsidies, taxes, and earnings support

Minimum content:
- EITC-style phase-in, plateau, and phase-out logic;
- extensive-margin versus intensive-margin predictions;
- why labor-market knowledge, filing behavior, and take-up matter for interpretation;
- connection to Weeks 2 and 3 through static and dynamic labor supply.

This section should use EITC-style programs as the cleanest canonical worker-side policy object.

### 4. Unemployment insurance, benefit timing, and job-search support

Minimum content:
- UI as both insurance and incentive policy;
- nonemployment duration, job-finding, and reemployment wage margins;
- benefit timing and dynamic incentive design;
- randomized or quasi-experimental job-search interventions as complements rather than substitutes to UI.

This section should teach students to think beyond the single question "does UI reduce work?"

### 5. Training, job readiness, and skill-building policy

Minimum content:
- training policies as human-capital interventions rather than just employment nudges;
- why short-run placement effects are incomplete metrics;
- dynamic earnings, match quality, and information acquisition;
- connection to Week 4 human capital and Week 10 mobility.

### 6. Family-support, care, and time-allocation policy

Minimum content:
- childcare and care-price policies as worker-side time-allocation interventions;
- how household structure changes incidence and behavioral margins;
- connection to Week 6 households/fertility;
- enough coverage to show the logic, without turning the lecture into a duplicate of Week 6.

### 7. Disability and retirement incentives

Minimum content:
- disability insurance receipt as a labor-supply and household-insurance object;
- retirement rules and earnings tests as dynamic labor-supply incentives;
- judge-assignment IV and bunching/threshold designs as major research tools;
- connection to dynamic labor supply from Week 3.

### 8. Take-up, salience, and administrative design

This is one of the core sections of the chapter.

Minimum content:
- eligible versus enrolled populations;
- information frictions, hassle costs, and administrative burden;
- why take-up is central for both positive analysis and welfare analysis;
- why null results may reflect low salience or weak transmission rather than no structural responsiveness.

This section should also bridge directly to Week 12 labor supply under frictions.

### 9. Empirical design block: what do the main designs identify?

This section should be unusually explicit and methods-heavy.

Minimum content:
- kink and bunching logic for nonlinear schedules and earnings tests;
- randomized field experiments and encouragement designs for take-up or training;
- reform-based event studies and difference-in-differences;
- judge assignment or examiner assignment IV;
- difference-in-knowledge or local exposure designs;
- why design choice must match the policy margin and institutional setting.

### 10. Welfare and policy-design block

Minimum content:
- why labor economists care about more than employment effects;
- trade-offs between insurance and incentives;
- why heterogeneity in constraints and frictions changes policy ranking;
- the role of dynamic responses and external validity;
- when partial-equilibrium evidence is enough for a first-pass policy claim and when it is not.

### 11. Capstone synthesis: the researcher's checklist

The lecture should end with a practical research checklist.

Minimum content:
- what is the policy object?
- what worker-side margin is supposed to move?
- what frictions or take-up problems intervene?
- what design identifies the relevant effect?
- how should researchers think about heterogeneity, dynamics, and welfare?
- which earlier Labor I weeks supply the needed conceptual tools?

### 12. Optional extension block

This chapter should contain an explicit optional extension block that can be used for:
- frontier work on administrative burden and take-up;
- broader transfer reforms such as basic income or redesign of unemployment assistance;
- external validity across policy environments;
- structural sufficient-statistics approaches to worker-side policy design.

## Required formal objects

The retrofitted chapter should contain at least six formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: policy-adjusted budget or participation object

Use a simple participation or static budget equation such as:

```math
c = y + w h - T(w h, z) + B(w h, z)
```

where {math}`T(\cdot)` is taxes or phase-outs and {math}`B(\cdot)` is benefits or credits.

### Equation 2: extensive/intensive margin decomposition

Include an elasticity or decomposition object showing why policy effects can differ across the participation and hours margins.

### Equation 3: dynamic value of nonemployment or UI timing

Include a compact dynamic object showing how benefit timing affects search or continuation values.

### Equation 4: take-up condition

Include a simple take-up inequality with benefit value, information, hassle cost, and stigma or administrative burden.

### Equation 5: judge-IV or encouragement-design estimand

Include a Wald-style or experimental design object to connect policy evaluation to causal inference.

### Equation 6: welfare or sufficient-statistics expression

Include a compact marginal welfare object that highlights insurance benefits, incentive costs, or heterogeneity.

The Week 11 lab should follow the standard Reproduce -> Diagnose -> Transfer template.

### Transfer ideas

Good bounded transfer exercises include:
- replicate a reduced policy-response figure using synthetic or public data and re-estimate it for a different subgroup;
- map a nonlinear schedule into predicted worker incentives and compare those predictions to observed reduced-form behavior;
- use a public or synthetic job-search dataset to distinguish participation, search, and reemployment margins;
- construct a take-up funnel for a worker-targeted program using public administrative summaries or a synthetic analog.

The bounded student path must run without restricted administrative microdata.
