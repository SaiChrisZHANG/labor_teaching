# Week 4 source pack — Human Capital and Skill Formation

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Week 4. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How is productive capacity accumulated over the life cycle, and how do schooling, training, parental investments, and credit or information frictions shape earnings profiles and labor-market inequality?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor I
- Week: 4
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60--90 minute extension block if you want to teach the full early-childhood or credit-constraints material in class rather than leave it as reading/lab material
- Position in sequence: the accumulation counterpart to Week 3's dynamic labor-supply timing problem
- Goal: move from dynamic labor-supply timing to human-capital accumulation, connect Ben-Porath-style investment to schooling/training decisions and lifecycle wages, and then show how early-childhood skill formation and frictions reshape the standard benchmark

## Why this week is intentionally heavier

Week 4 is allowed to be denser than earlier weeks because it does a great deal of conceptual work for the rest of the course.

It should establish:
- why wage profiles are endogenous outcomes of investment,
- why schooling and training are foregone-earnings decisions rather than static traits,
- why early-childhood conditions and parental investments matter for later labor outcomes,
- why credit and information frictions distort human-capital choices,
- and why later weeks on wages, inequality, households, policy, and labor supply under frictions all rely on this architecture.

The chapter should therefore have a clear core/extension structure:
- **Core material** should comfortably fit in a 3-hour class;
- **Extension material** can be taught in an additional session, assigned as reading, or moved into the lab/research section.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. explain the human-capital investment problem as a dynamic tradeoff between current earnings and future productivity;
2. write down and interpret a compact Ben-Porath-style law of motion for skills or human capital;
3. distinguish schooling, post-school training, and early-childhood investment as different accumulation margins with different constraints and timing;
4. explain the difference between general and specific training and why imperfect labor markets can make firm-sponsored general training viable;
5. understand the ideas of self-productivity and dynamic complementarity in skill formation;
6. connect reduced-form policy evidence, randomized interventions, and structural production-function estimation to the same human-capital framework;
7. explain why credit constraints, information frictions, or behavioral frictions can distort otherwise efficient human-capital investment.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should sound like an advanced labor field-course chapter, not a generic education-economics review and not a development lecture detached from labor economics.
- The opening should explicitly say that Week 3 treated labor supply as timing given productive capacity, while Week 4 asks where productive capacity itself comes from.
- Ben-Porath should be taught as a useful organizing benchmark, not as the only true model.
- The chapter should avoid the false dichotomy between "schooling" and "early childhood." It should instead present human-capital accumulation as a life-cycle process with different margins and technologies at different ages.
- The chapter should make clear that modern human-capital research is as much about investments, constraints, and production technologies as it is about wages.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening section: from labor-supply timing to productive-capacity accumulation

This section should make four points immediately:
- labor supply is partly about choosing when to work, but wages themselves are endogenous to past investment;
- observed age-earnings profiles reflect both prices and accumulated productive capacity;
- schooling, training, and early-childhood investment are different manifestations of the same broader accumulation problem;
- policy can change human capital through subsidies, constraints, information, or direct inputs.

The opening must explicitly connect back to Week 3:
- Week 3 asked how workers shift hours over time;
- Week 4 asks how they shape future wages and opportunities over time.

### 2. Ben-Porath benchmark and lifecycle earnings profiles

This is the conceptual backbone of the week.

Minimum content:
- define a compact human-capital state variable;
- specify a law of motion in which current investment raises future human capital;
- explain why investment is higher when remaining work life is longer;
- connect the model to rising-then-flattening age-earnings profiles;
- show how schooling and early-career training fit into the same framework.

This section should emphasize:
- foregone earnings,
- investment intensity over the life cycle,
- and the distinction between observed wages and latent productive capacity.

### 3. Schooling, college, training, and the composition of human-capital investment

This section should explain the main accumulation margins:
- formal schooling,
- college and postsecondary investment,
- firm-sponsored training,
- learning-by-doing or career ladders.

Minimum content:
- difference between time in school and time in production;
- general versus specific human capital;
- why imperfect competition, wage compression, or turnover frictions can support employer investment in transferable skills;
- why observed training incidence is informative but incomplete as a measure of human-capital investment.

This section can preview Week 5 by noting that observed wage returns depend on both accumulated skill and the wage-setting process.

### 4. Skill formation in childhood: self-productivity and dynamic complementarity

This section should broaden the benchmark to early life.

Minimum content:
- define self-productivity and dynamic complementarity;
- explain why the timing of investment matters;
- explain why disadvantaged early environments can have persistent labor-market consequences;
- distinguish cognitive, socio-emotional, and health-related channels where helpful.

This section should not become a generic child-development survey. It should remain anchored to labor-economics questions:
- future wages,
- employment,
- occupation choice,
- and inequality.

### 5. Frictions, finance, and policy distortions

This section should explain why investment may deviate from the frictionless optimum.

Minimum content:
- borrowing constraints or limited commitment;
- family background and parental resources;
- imperfect information about returns or program eligibility;
- behavioral frictions where appropriate;
- policy instruments such as schooling subsidies, credit provision, early-childhood programs, or training support.

This section should connect to both micro policy evaluation and distributional consequences.

### 6. What empirical designs identify in human-capital research

Organize this section by design, not by paper chronology.

Minimum design buckets:
- schooling subsidy or draft-lottery designs;
- randomized early-childhood interventions;
- within-program heterogeneity or input-composition evidence;
- structural production-function estimation;
- reduced-form evidence on credit constraints.

This section should emphasize:
- what each design learns about the investment technology,
- what it learns about returns,
- and what it cannot learn without stronger structure.

### 7. Optional extension block

This chapter should have an explicit optional extension block that can be used in one of three ways:
- taught as an extra 60--90 minute session,
- assigned as reading plus the lab,
- or turned into appendix slides.

The extension block should cover one or both of:
- deeper treatment of early-childhood production-function estimation;
- deeper treatment of credit constraints and schooling finance.

## Required formal objects

The retrofitted chapter should contain at least four formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: Ben-Porath-style human-capital law of motion

Use a compact law of motion such as:

```tex
h_{t+1} = (1-\delta_h)h_t + A_t i_t^{\alpha}
```

or an equivalent formulation with time and goods inputs.

Interpretation:
- human capital is a state variable;
- current investment affects future productivity;
- the timing of investment matters because the payoff horizon matters.

### Equation 2: earnings or wage equation linked to human capital

Use a compact earnings relation such as:

```tex
w_t = r_t h_t
```

or a log formulation that makes clear observed wages depend on both accumulated human capital and prices.

Interpretation:
- earnings profiles are endogenous lifecycle objects;
- wage growth is partly investment-driven rather than purely exogenous.

### Equation 3: investment tradeoff / shadow value condition

Write a compact first-order condition or Euler-style investment condition linking current cost to discounted future returns.

Interpretation:
- human-capital accumulation is costly because it displaces work or other inputs today;
- the remaining horizon governs optimal investment intensity.

### Equation 4: early-childhood skill-production function

Use a compact production function such as:

```tex
\theta_{t+1} = f_t(\theta_t, I_t, P_t)
```

where `\theta_t` is current skill, `I_t` is parental or material investment, and `P_t` can capture parental quality, time, or environment.

Interpretation:
- this object introduces self-productivity and dynamic complementarity;
- it also clarifies why policy may operate through changing investments rather than directly changing outcomes.

## Lab anchor

The Week 4 lab should be built around one official replication package and one bounded transfer exercise.

### Primary lab candidate

Use `@attanasioEtAl2020HumanCapital` because the American Economic Review article page includes an official replication package and the paper lets students see how parental investments and skill-production estimation fit together.

#### Reproduce

Students should reproduce one reduced-form treatment effect or one production-function/inputs result from the official package or a reduced pedagogical subset.

#### Transfer

Students should adapt the workflow to one bounded alternative:
- alternative child subgroup,
- alternative skill outcome,
- alternative treatment/input margin,
- or a synthetic simplified skill-formation dataset used to show how changes in baseline skill alter the apparent return to later investment.

### Secondary lab candidate

Use `@walters2015` if you want a cleaner reduced-form lab focused on variation in early-childhood program inputs rather than structural estimation.

#### Reproduce

Students reproduce one figure or table showing cross-center heterogeneity or the relationship between inputs and Head Start effectiveness using the official replication package or a reduced pedagogical subset.

#### Transfer

Students adapt the same workflow to one bounded alternative:
- an alternative outcome,
- an alternative subgroup,
- or a synthetic center-level dataset where they re-estimate how input composition maps into treatment heterogeneity.

### Optional theory/quantitative extension

Use `@lochnerMongeNaranjo2011` as an optional instructor-led extension if you want a policy-finance bridge.

#### Reproduce

Students or the instructor reproduce one constrained-versus-unconstrained comparative-static result or one calibration table from the official package.

#### Transfer

Students alter one bounded policy or credit parameter in a simplified script to see how schooling investment changes.
