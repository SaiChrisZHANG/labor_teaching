# Week 2 source pack — Static Labor Supply

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Week 2. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How do workers choose whether and how much to work in a static setting once wages, taxes, transfers, work costs, and nonlinear budget constraints are taken seriously?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor I
- Week: 2
- Length: 3 hours
- Position in sequence: first formal worker-choice module after the Week 1 measurement map
- Goal: establish the static labor-supply benchmark, the distinction between intensive and extensive margins, and the way taxes/transfers/nonconvexities reshape labor-supply predictions

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. write down and interpret the basic static labor-supply problem;
2. distinguish income and substitution effects in a way that is useful for empirical work rather than merely diagrammatic;
3. explain the difference between intensive and extensive margins and why policy often loads more strongly on one than the other;
4. analyze piecewise-linear budget sets generated by taxes and transfers;
5. explain why fixed costs, work costs, and nonconvexities matter for participation responses;
6. connect canonical empirical evidence on the EITC, bunching, and optimization frictions to the static benchmark.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should sound like an advanced graduate labor field-course chapter, not an undergraduate principles lecture.
- The opening should move from the benchmark problem to empirical relevance quickly.
- The narrative should emphasize that the static model is both indispensable and incomplete.
- The chapter should explain why elasticities differ across margins, populations, and policy environments.
- Avoid generic prose such as "workers choose leisure and consumption." Always tie the object to a research or policy question.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening section: why static labor supply is still the benchmark

This section should make three points immediately:
- the static model is the cleanest way to map wages and tax wedges into labor-supply predictions;
- it disciplines policy interpretation even when later weeks add dynamics and frictions;
- its main empirical objects are intensive hours responses and extensive participation responses.

The opening should explicitly connect Week 2 back to Week 1 objects:
- hours,
- participation,
- earnings,
- tax-adjusted wage incentives.

### 2. Benchmark model and margin decomposition

This section should define the static choice problem over consumption and hours or leisure.

Minimum content:
- preferences and budget constraint;
- interior first-order condition;
- interpretation of the after-tax wage as the price of leisure;
- intensive versus extensive margins;
- why nonparticipation cannot be treated as a minor corner case.

This section should also state clearly that many empirical policy debates are really about which margin is moving.

### 3. Taxes, transfers, and nonlinear budget sets

This section should introduce:
- piecewise-linear budget sets;
- tax/transfer kinks and notches;
- work costs or fixed costs that create nonconvexities;
- the idea that participation responses may dominate hours responses for some groups.

This section should connect the textbook geometry to empirical settings such as:
- the Earned Income Tax Credit;
- welfare reforms and participation incentives;
- bunching at kink points.

### 4. What empirical labor-supply elasticities actually mean

This section should organize the evidence using an elasticity taxonomy rather than a list of numbers.

Minimum distinctions:
- uncompensated versus compensated;
- intensive versus extensive;
- micro versus macro;
- taxable income versus real labor-supply responses.

The section should explain why Chetty's synthesis is useful: the literature disagrees partly because adjustment and optimization frictions make different empirical designs recover different objects `@chetty2012`.

### 5. Policy applications: EITC, participation, and bunching

This section should use the EITC as the main applied bridge.

Minimum content:
- participation responses for single mothers `@eissaLiebman1996`;
- married-couple participation effects `@eissaHoynes2004`;
- broad lessons from the EITC literature `@eissaHoynes2006`;
- bunching and schedule salience `@saez2010`;
- local knowledge and information frictions `@chettyFriedmanSaez2013`.

The chapter should end by making clear that the static benchmark will be revisited in Weeks 3, 11, and 12 once dynamics, policy evaluation, and informational/behavioral frictions are added.

## Required formal objects

The retrofitted chapter should contain at least three formal objects and cross-reference them.

### Equation 1: static labor-supply problem

Use a compact benchmark problem. A suitable version is:

```tex
\max_{h \in [0,\bar h]} u(c, \bar h-h)
\quad \text{s.t.} \quad c = y + wh - T(wh)
```

Interpretation: workers trade off consumption against leisure, but the relevant return to work is shaped by the tax/transfer schedule.

### Equation 2: interior first-order condition

Use an interior condition such as:

```tex
\frac{u_{\ell}(c,\ell)}{u_c(c,\ell)} = w\bigl(1-T'(wh)\bigr)
```

Interpretation: the marginal rate of substitution between leisure and consumption equals the net-of-tax wage.

### Equation 3: participation condition with a work cost

Use a participation inequality or value comparison such as:

```tex
V^{\text{work}}(w,y,F) \ge V^{\text{nonwork}}(y)
```

where `F` is a fixed or work-related cost.

Interpretation: extensive-margin responses depend on whether work clears a threshold after accounting for fixed costs, care costs, commute time, or benefit loss.

Figure purpose:

Create a stylized EITC-like or transfer-like budget set with:

Figure purpose:

## Lab anchor

The Week 2 lab should be built around one official replication package and one bounded transfer exercise.

### Primary lab candidate

Use `@saez2010` because the article has an official AEA replication package and gives students a direct encounter with nonlinear budget sets, kinks, and bunching behavior.

#### Reproduce

Students should reproduce one reduced bunching figure or one core binned-density object using the official package or a reduced pedagogical subset.

#### Transfer

Students should apply the same binning/bunching workflow to one bounded alternative:
- alternative bin width,
- alternative subgroup,
- alternative kink region,
- or a stylized synthetic schedule used to test intuition.

### Secondary lab candidate

Use `@chettyFriedmanSaez2013` if the goal is to emphasize information and knowledge frictions in static labor-supply responses to the EITC.

#### Reproduce

Students reproduce one reduced-form figure comparing high-knowledge and low-knowledge areas.

#### Transfer

Students run the same design on a bounded alternative subgroup or alternative knowledge cutoff.
