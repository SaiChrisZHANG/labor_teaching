# Week 12 source pack — Behavioral Frictions and an Introduction to Behavioral Labor

## Purpose of this file

This is the intellectual control file for Week 12. Edit this file first when changing the chapter's scholarly spine. Then ask Codex to sync the chapter, slides, and code lab from this source pack.

## Central question

How should labor economists adapt standard labor models when workers or employers deviate systematically from the fully rational benchmark through nonstandard preferences, nonstandard beliefs, or nonstandard decision-making?

## Week identity

- Course: Labor I
- Week: 12
- Length: 3 hours core, with an optional 60--90 minute extension block on structural behavioral estimation, welfare analysis, and links to a full Behavioral Labor field course
- Position in sequence: this is the final substantive week of Labor I after the worker-side policy capstone in Week 11 and before the Week 13 synthesis
- Goal: introduce behavioral labor as a coherent field rather than a loose collection of biases

## Why this week matters

This lecture should not try to squeeze every behavioral bias into a single week. Instead, it should teach students a **portable organizing framework** for behavioral labor research.

Use DellaVigna's survey structure as the backbone:
1. **nonstandard preferences**;
2. **nonstandard beliefs**;
3. **nonstandard decision-making**;
4. **responses of rational firms, employers, and policymakers**;
5. **conditions under which markets, incentives, and experience attenuate or amplify behavioral frictions**.

The lecture should therefore feel like an introduction to Behavioral Labor:
- it should connect behavioral mechanisms to canonical labor objects;
- it should show how empirical labor economists identify behavioral frictions;
- it should distinguish reduced-form evidence from structural and welfare interpretation;
- it should give students a map for the stand-alone Behavioral Labor course that could come later.

## Content emphasis for this week

This week must remain labor-focused. It should not drift into generic consumer finance, charitable giving, or political persuasion examples unless used very briefly for intuition.

The chapter should repeatedly anchor behavioral ideas to labor-market objects such as:
- labor supply and hours choices;
- dynamic labor supply and self-control;
- effort provision and productivity;
- job search and reservation behavior;
- training and human-capital investments;
- household labor allocation and take-up;
- job choice, amenities, and identity;
- employer contract design and workplace incentives.

The lecture should also explain why behavioral labor is not just "adding psychology" to standard labor economics. The real contribution is:
- new empirical predictions;
- new identification challenges;
- new design questions for employers and policymakers;
- and new welfare problems when observed choices may not equal normative preferences.

## Non-negotiable learning goals

By the end of the week, students should be able to:
1. explain DellaVigna's tripartite taxonomy of nonstandard preferences, beliefs, and decision-making in a labor context;
2. distinguish a standard labor-market wedge from a behavioral wedge;
3. map major behavioral mechanisms into labor objects such as hours, search, effort, job choice, training, and take-up;
4. explain why present bias, reference dependence, social preferences, identity, limited attention, and incentive complexity matter for labor economics;
5. distinguish behavioral responses by workers from rational responses by firms, managers, and policymakers;
6. explain how field experiments, quasi-experiments, surveys of expectations, and structural estimation each contribute to behavioral labor;
7. explain why behavioral evidence raises special issues for welfare analysis;
8. identify when a behavioral interpretation is credible and when standard heterogeneity or information frictions may be enough;
9. understand how Week 12 serves as a bridge to a full Behavioral Labor course.

## Tone and authorial voice

- This chapter should read like a serious PhD labor field-course introduction to a neighboring field.
- It should be conceptually disciplined and methodologically explicit.
- It should keep asking: relative to which benchmark? which labor margin? what identifying variation? what welfare object?
- The prose should emphasize that behavioral labor is strongest when it produces **distinctive labor-market predictions** and not just post hoc stories.

## Why this week is intentionally synthetic

This lecture is meant to consolidate and reinterpret the whole first semester:
- Week 2 static labor supply -> standard benchmark for choice and distortion;
- Week 3 dynamic labor supply -> self-control, present bias, and state dependence;
- Week 4 human capital -> investment under imperfect self-control, expectations, or salience;
- Week 5 wages and returns -> beliefs and perceived returns;
- Week 6 households -> norms, identity, and within-household frictions;
- Week 7 amenities -> choice under limited attention and reference points;
- Week 8 inequality -> behavioral amplification or mitigation of dispersion;
- Week 9 discrimination -> stereotypes, beliefs, identity, and sorting;
- Week 10 mobility -> search, expectations, and movement frictions;
- Week 11 policy -> take-up, salience, simplification, and behavioral design.

The chapter should therefore be longer and more interpretive than a standard single-bias lecture.

## Section-level content requirements

### 1. Opening section: why behavioral labor needs a framework

Minimum content:
- explain why this lecture is an introduction to Behavioral Labor rather than an encyclopedic review of biases;
- state the fully rational benchmark clearly;
- explain that labor economists care about behavioral frictions because they change behavior, empirical interpretation, contract design, and welfare;
- signal the organizing role of DellaVigna's taxonomy.

The opening should also tell students that the right question is not "is there a bias?" but:
- what is the benchmark?
- what labor margin is affected?
- what evidence distinguishes the behavioral mechanism from standard alternatives?

### 2. Benchmark object: standard labor choice and the behavioral wedge

Minimum content:
- a compact worker-choice object from static or dynamic labor supply;
- a general wedge showing where preferences, beliefs, or decision-making can enter;
- language distinguishing:
  - preference misspecification,
  - expectation errors,
  - limited attention / complexity,
  - and institutional constraints.

This section should define the week's analytic baseline.

### 3. Nonstandard preferences in labor

Minimum content:
- present bias / self-control problems in effort, savings, training, job search, or work-related habits;
- reference dependence and fairness in effort or wage-setting;
- social preferences, reciprocity, and gift exchange at work;
- identity-based labor supply or job choice.

This section should not become a huge literature survey. It should give students a disciplined map of how preference-based deviations enter labor models.

### 4. Nonstandard beliefs in labor

Minimum content:
- biased beliefs about returns, productivity, job-finding, or future self-control;
- overconfidence and expectation errors where relevant;
- why subjective beliefs matter for job search, training, and policy take-up;
- the distinction between true belief distortions and researcher measurement error.

This section should stay tightly labor-focused. Use job search, returns to work, or policy-knowledge applications rather than broad consumer examples.

### 5. Nonstandard decision-making in labor

Minimum content:
- limited attention, salience, framing, and complexity;
- why workers may fail to respond to incentives they do not fully perceive;
- administrative frictions and bounded rationality in take-up or work incentives;
- incentive opacity and contract complexity.

This section is where students should see a direct bridge from Week 11 worker policy into Behavioral Labor.

### 6. Rational responses by firms, managers, and policymakers

Minimum content:
- how employers design incentives when workers are behaviorally bounded;
- gifts, motivational framing, monitoring, simplicity, and contract design;
- when firms exploit biases, accommodate them, or partially offset them;
- when policymakers should simplify, commit, inform, or nudge.

This section should make clear that behavioral labor is not only about worker mistakes; it is also about equilibrium or design responses by organizations.

### 7. Methods block: how behavioral labor is identified

This section should be explicit and central.

Minimum content:
- field experiments on effort, incentives, gifts, or complexity;
- survey and expectation-based designs;
- quasi-experimental designs that isolate behavioral channels;
- structural behavioral estimation and its role in welfare and policy counterfactuals;
- why design must distinguish behavioral mechanisms from standard heterogeneity or friction stories.

This section should rely on DellaVigna (2018) for the role of structural estimation and how-to guidance.

### 8. Welfare and policy interpretation

Minimum content:
- why observed labor choices are not automatically welfare-revealing;
- the need to distinguish positive response from normative evaluation;
- internalities versus externalities in labor settings;
- when commitment devices, simplification, reminders, or contract redesign can improve welfare;
- caution about overreaching welfare claims from reduced-form evidence.

### 9. Capstone bridge: from Labor I to a Behavioral Labor course

Minimum content:
- summarize the main behavioral-labor research programs:
  1. self-control and labor supply / training / search,
  2. fairness and reciprocity at work,
  3. identity and social norms,
  4. attention, salience, and complexity,
  5. beliefs and expectations,
  6. employer design and behavioral personnel economics,
  7. welfare and behavioral policy design.
- explain that a full Behavioral Labor course would go deeper mechanism by mechanism.

The chapter should end by making students feel that they now have a map for the field.

## Required formal objects / equations

The chapter should contain at least six formal objects.

### Equation 1: benchmark labor-choice object

Use a compact benchmark such as:

```tex
U(c, h) \quad \text{subject to} \quad c = y + w h - T(w h + y)
```

Interpretation:
- this is the standard worker-side benchmark against which behavioral wedges are introduced.

### Equation 2: present-bias or dynamic inconsistency object

Use a quasi-hyperbolic object such as:

```tex
U_t = u(c_t, h_t) + \beta \sum_{s=t+1}^{T} \delta^{\,s-t} u(c_s, h_s)
```

Interpretation:
- labor, search, or investment choices may differ when immediate costs loom larger than future benefits.

### Equation 3: reference-dependent or fairness-augmented effort object

Use a compact effort/utility object with a reference point or fairness term.

Interpretation:
- effort depends not only on pay but also on whether outcomes feel fair relative to a benchmark or gift.

### Equation 4: distorted-belief or subjective-return object

Use a simple subjective expectation or misperceived return object.

Interpretation:
- workers may optimize relative to subjective beliefs, not objective returns.

### Equation 5: attention / complexity / opacity object

Use a reduced-form incentive-perception object such as an effective net reward:

```tex
\tilde{w} = \lambda w + (1-\lambda)\bar{w}
```

or another compact opacity mapping.

Interpretation:
- workers may respond to perceived incentives rather than true incentives.

### Equation 6: welfare object

Include a compact object distinguishing observed choice utility from normative or long-run utility.

Interpretation:
- behavioral labor raises a welfare problem, not only a prediction problem.

## Code-lab transfer ideas

Possible bounded transfer exercises:
1. translate a complex incentive schedule into an "effective perceived incentive" object on a small synthetic worker-task dataset;
2. compare effort responses under simple versus complex piece-rate designs using toy or public data;
3. estimate a small reduced-form response to reminders, gifts, or commitment features on synthetic data;
4. build a mini diagnostic exercise that separates preference-based and attention-based explanations for the same observed response pattern.

The transfer exercise should remain small and local. It should not require proprietary employer data.
