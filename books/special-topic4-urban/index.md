# Urban and Labor: Space, Work, and Local Opportunity

Urban and Labor is a compact 6-week advanced special topic on how space shapes labor-market access, wages, job search, amenities, housing constraints, commuting, safety, environment, migration, and local opportunity. The course treats cities and regions as labor-market systems: workers choose where to live and work, firms choose where to locate and hire, and housing, transportation, neighborhood structure, and local risk determine which jobs are actually feasible.

This course exists as a labor special topic because geography is not only a control variable, fixed effect, or backdrop for wage regressions. Space changes outside options, search sets, commuting costs, exposure to jobs, rents, amenities, local shocks, segregation, and the ability of workers to move toward opportunity. The course is intentionally narrower than a full urban economics field course. Its organizing question is always: how does place change labor allocation, worker welfare, and the distribution of economic opportunity?

## Prerequisites

- Labor I or a strong equivalent in worker-side labor economics
- Labor II or readiness to work with search, frictions, local shocks, and equilibrium adjustment
- Graduate microeconomic theory
- Applied econometrics and comfort reading place-based empirical work
- Prior urban or public economics exposure is useful but not required

## What Students Will Be Able To Do

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

1. explain how commuting, amenities, rents, and neighborhood structure enter labor-market choices;
2. distinguish urban productivity effects from selection, learning, compensating differentials, and housing-cost incidence;
3. evaluate evidence on spatial mismatch, segregation, neighborhood effects, crime, safety, and environmental exposure as labor-market mechanisms;
4. analyze migration and local labor-market adjustment after demand, policy, housing, transportation, or amenity shocks;
5. design urban-labor research questions with clear labor outcomes, spatial mechanisms, counterfactuals, and identification strategies.

## Course Format

Urban and Labor is designed as a 6-week compact advanced special topic for PhD students and strong advanced MA students. It is reading-heavy, research-oriented, and suitable for a half-semester elective, intensive module, or graduate reading group. Each week centers on one cumulative urban-labor mechanism and one research-design problem rather than trying to survey the entire urban field.

Future weekly chapters will follow the portfolio's special-topics structure: Bridge, Field Core, Research Lab, Methods Box, reading ladder, exercises or discussion prompts, reproducibility note, and slide companion note. Special-topics chapters should include a visible **Core points** box near the top; extension boxes are optional rather than a default requirement.

## Connection To Labor I And Labor II

Labor I supplies the worker-side objects: labor supply, job amenities, household constraints, wage determination, inequality, discrimination, mobility, migration, and worker-targeted policy. Labor II supplies the market architecture: labor demand, search, wage-setting, monopsony, firm location, institutions, shocks, and adjustment. Urban and Labor uses those foundations to ask when spatial frictions and local environments change the feasible set of work, the value of wages, and the distribution of opportunity.

## Weekly Sequence

1. **[Space and labor markets: urban access, commuting, amenities, and local opportunity](01-space-and-labor-markets-urban-access-commuting-amenities-and-local-opportunity.md).** The course opens by defining local labor markets as systems of jobs, workers, commute costs, amenities, rents, and outside options.
2. **[Housing, rents, mobility constraints, and labor-market adjustment](02-housing-rents-moving-costs-and-labor-market-adjustment.md).** Students study how housing supply, rent incidence, residential sorting, and moving costs shape who can access high-opportunity labor markets.
3. **[Spatial mismatch, segregation, neighborhood effects, and job access](03-spatial-mismatch-segregation-neighborhood-effects-and-job-access.md).** The course turns to unequal exposure to jobs, networks, schools, neighborhoods, and local institutions.
4. **[Crime, safety, environment, and the urban feasible set for work](04-crime-safety-environment-and-the-urban-feasible-set-for-work.md).** Students analyze safety and environmental quality as labor-market constraints that affect work timing, search, productivity, mobility, and welfare.
5. **[Migration, local labor demand, and urban labor reallocation](05-migration-local-labor-demand-and-urban-labor-reallocation.md).** Local shocks are studied through wages, employment, population adjustment, commuting flows, and the uneven ability of workers to move.
6. **[Synthesis, frontier questions, and student research designs](06-synthesis-frontier-questions-and-student-research-designs.md).** The final week converts the course into research proposals on spatial labor mechanisms, credible counterfactuals, and policy-relevant welfare objects.

## Internal Roadmap

- **Foundational:** Week 1 defines the course's labor-market objects and spatial vocabulary.
- **Space and housing focused:** Weeks 1 and 2 build the access, commuting, amenities, rents, and mobility-constraint architecture.
- **Segregation, crime, and environment focused:** Weeks 3 and 4 study unequal job access, neighborhood exposure, safety, and environmental constraints.
- **Migration and reallocation focused:** Week 5 studies local labor demand shocks, migration, commuting, and adjustment.
- **Synthesis and research design:** Week 6 turns the sequence into frontier questions and student research designs.

## Research Orientation

The course is designed to help students build labor research that takes space seriously without drifting into generic urban economics. A successful project in this course starts from a labor-market outcome, names the spatial mechanism, explains the worker or firm margin, and states the counterfactual needed to evaluate wages, employment, mobility, job access, amenities, or welfare.
