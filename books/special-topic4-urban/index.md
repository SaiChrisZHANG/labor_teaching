# Urban and Labor

Urban and Labor is a compact 6-week advanced elective on cities as labor-market systems. It studies how place, agglomeration, housing, commuting, local shocks, and neighborhood structure reshape employment, wages, mobility, and inequality.

The course sits downstream from both Labor I and Labor II. Labor I contributes mobility, worker sorting, amenities, and inequality; Labor II contributes shocks, firm behavior, and spatial adjustment. This elective makes cities and local labor markets the unit of analysis rather than treating geography as background.

## Course type

6-week compact advanced elective

## Prerequisites

- Labor I or a solid equivalent in labor economics
- Basic urban or public economics exposure is helpful but not required
- Applied econometrics and comfort with place-based empirical work

## Course goals

- understand cities as equilibrium labor markets shaped by firms, workers, and housing constraints;
- analyze how agglomeration, mobility frictions, and local amenities interact with wages and productivity;
- study how local shocks and neighborhood structures create unequal labor-market opportunity;
- prepare students to read and design place-based labor research with strong identification logic.

## Course format

The elective is intentionally compact. Each week introduces one major urban-labor mechanism, one empirical design family, and one research extension that shows how spatial labor questions connect to broader field concerns in inequality, mobility, and policy.

## Week-by-week path

- Week 1. Cities as labor markets. The course opens by defining the city as a matching, production, and amenity system.
- Week 2. Agglomeration, skills, and productivity. Students study why dense places raise wages and why the gains are unequally distributed.
- Week 3. Housing, commuting, and spatial sorting. Labor-market opportunity is linked to affordability, access, and local amenities.
- Week 4. Local shocks and spatial adjustment. The course examines how trade, technology, and sectoral change move through local labor markets.
- Week 5. Segregation, neighborhoods, and opportunity. Students analyze how neighborhood structure shapes labor supply, networks, and long-run mobility.
- Week 6. Place-based policy and frontier research. The course ends with a policy-and-research synthesis on regional development, transit, zoning, and local labor resilience.

## Research orientation

Urban and Labor is built as a sharp, bounded elective rather than a full urban field survey. Its research orientation is explicitly labor-facing: the question is always how place changes labor allocation, labor-market access, and the distribution of opportunity.
