# Housing incidence framework

| Question | Labor interpretation | Main objects |
|---|---|---|
| Does a productive place actually improve worker welfare? | Compare nominal wages to rents, commuting costs, and amenities | Nominal wage, real wage, rent, commute, amenity |
| Who can access high-opportunity places? | Access depends on housing supply, wealth, tenure, and moving costs | Housing supply elasticity, deposits, down payments, homeownership, credit |
| How do places adjust to shocks? | Local shocks can be absorbed by wages, rents, population, commuting, migration, or reduced access | Local demand shock, rent capitalization, employment, population, commuting flows |
| Who captures the gains? | Incidence can fall on workers, landlords, incumbent homeowners, firms, or commuters | Worker incidence, landlord incidence, homeowner capitalization, firm labor costs |
| What is the main friction? | Research should identify the binding margin rather than assume one | Supply constraints, moving costs, wealth constraints, commuting technology, regulation, rent capitalization |
