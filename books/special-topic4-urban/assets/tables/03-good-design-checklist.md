# Good design checklist

| Design question | What a good paper does | Common failure |
|---|---|---|
| What is the mechanism? | States one main channel: access, stigma, networks, peers, schools, or safety | Treats “neighborhood effects” as a single residual explanation |
| What is the relevant geography? | Justifies labor-market scale using commuting, flows, or institutions | Uses arbitrary geographic units with no labor-market interpretation |
| What is measured? | Measures actual job access, commute cost, or exposure duration | Uses tract poverty or distance alone as a catch-all proxy |
| What is the comparison? | Uses a comparison that isolates the mechanism of interest | Bundles neighborhood, school, and family changes together |
| How is sorting handled? | Explicitly addresses residential selection and timing | Treats residence as quasi-random without evidence |
| Are equilibrium responses relevant? | Discusses displacement, resorting, or re-capitalization when needed | Interprets local treatment effects as partial-equilibrium by default |
| Which labor margin moves? | Identifies employment, wages, commute, search, mobility, or long-run earnings separately | Reports a broad “better outcomes” effect without margin discipline |
| Does the result travel? | Explains why the mechanism is portable beyond the setting | Sells the result as universal without a mechanism |
