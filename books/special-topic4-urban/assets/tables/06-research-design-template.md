:::{table} Urban-labor research design template
:label: tbl:urban-research-design-template

| Design element | Guiding question | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Labor outcome | What is the labor object: employment, earnings, commuting, retention, job access, welfare, mobility? | Keeps the paper labor-focused rather than purely urban-descriptive. |
| Urban mechanism | What spatial force is doing the work: rents, distance, transit, neighborhood exposure, safety, local demand, environmental risk? | Prevents mechanism slippage. |
| Unit of geography | What is the relevant geography: tract, ZIP, county, metro, commuting zone, school zone, transit catchment? | Geography must match the mechanism. |
| Comparison / counterfactual | What does treatment change relative to? | Urban effects are rarely interpretable without a clear counterfactual. |
| Adjustment margins | Who can respond: workers, firms, commuters, landlords, migrants, schools, local governments? | Urban labor outcomes are often multi-margin incidence problems. |
| Spillovers / equilibrium | How might migration, rents, commuting, or firm entry attenuate or amplify the effect? | Spatial equilibrium is usually first-order, not an afterthought. |
| Welfare object | What improves: nominal wages, real wages, access, safety, opportunity, child outcomes, total surplus? | Nominal wages alone are often insufficient. |
| External relevance | Why does this setting reveal a broader mechanism? | Turns a place-specific paper into a broadly interesting labor paper. |

:::
