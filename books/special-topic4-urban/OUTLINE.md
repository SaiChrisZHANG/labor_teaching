# Urban and Labor

## Course role

Urban and Labor is a 6-week advanced elective on local labor markets, spatial sorting, and place-based labor adjustment.

## Weekly outline

### Week 1. Cities as labor markets

**Central question:** What does it mean to treat a city as a labor-market equilibrium rather than as a background location?

The opening week defines the local labor market as a joint system of jobs, workers, commuting, housing, and amenities. It sets up the core spatial objects the rest of the course will use.

### Week 2. Agglomeration, skills, and productivity

**Central question:** Why do dense places generate productivity and wage advantages, and who captures those gains?

Students study agglomeration economies, human-capital spillovers, and urban skill sorting. The key issue is whether urban wage premia reflect productivity, selection, learning, or amenity compensation.

### Week 3. Housing, commuting, and spatial sorting

**Central question:** How do housing markets and commuting frictions reshape labor-market access?

This week connects labor outcomes to rents, residential sorting, transit, and amenity access. The central theme is that local labor opportunities are mediated by where workers can afford to live and how easily they can reach work.

### Week 4. Local shocks and spatial adjustment

**Central question:** How do trade, technology, and sectoral shocks propagate through local labor markets?

The course turns to regional exposure designs and local adjustment. Students ask why some places absorb shocks through wages, others through employment or mobility, and still others through long-run decline.

### Week 5. Segregation, neighborhoods, and opportunity

**Central question:** How do neighborhoods and spatial inequality shape labor-market opportunity?

The focus is on segregation, networks, neighborhood exposure, and intergenerational mobility. This week links urban structure to labor-market inequality in a way that cannot be captured by worker characteristics alone.

### Week 6. Place-based policy and frontier research

**Central question:** Which place-based interventions plausibly improve labor-market opportunity, and how should they be evaluated?

The closing week studies regional development policy, transit, zoning, housing reform, and local public investment. Students leave with a research map connecting urban policy to labor allocation and local resilience.
