# Urban and Labor: Space, Work, and Local Opportunity

## Course role

Urban and Labor is a 6-week compact advanced elective on how space shapes labor-market access, job search, wages, amenities, housing constraints, commuting, safety, environment, segregation, migration, and local opportunity. It is a labor-economics course first: every week begins from work, wages, employment, job access, mobility, search, worker welfare, or local adjustment.

## Cumulative logic

The course sequence is deliberately cumulative:

1. define cities and local labor markets as systems of access, wages, amenities, and commuting costs;
2. add housing, rents, and mobility constraints as limits on labor-market adjustment;
3. study segregation, spatial mismatch, neighborhoods, and unequal job access;
4. bring in crime, safety, and environmental exposure as constraints on the feasible set for work;
5. analyze migration and local labor reallocation after demand and policy shocks;
6. end with frontier research design.

The course should not become a generic urban economics survey. Agglomeration, land use, transit, amenities, crime, pollution, and housing enter only when they change labor-market access, wages, employment, mobility, or welfare.

## Internal roadmap

- **Foundational:** Week 1
- **Space and housing focused:** Weeks 1 and 2
- **Segregation, crime, and environment focused:** Weeks 3 and 4
- **Migration and reallocation focused:** Week 5
- **Synthesis and research-design oriented:** Week 6

## Week-by-week outline

### Week 1. Space and Labor Markets: Urban Access, Commuting, Amenities, and Local Opportunity

**Central question:** How does space enter labor-market choice, wage determination, and worker welfare?

The opening week defines local labor markets as linked systems of jobs, workers, commuting costs, amenities, rents, and outside options. Students learn to treat the city not as a fixed location label, but as a set of constraints and opportunities that changes search behavior, job choice, wage interpretation, and welfare. Agglomeration and urban wage premia appear here only as labor-market objects: do dense places raise productivity, accelerate learning, attract selected workers, or compensate for higher costs?

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Space matters because workers do not choose jobs from a placeless menu; commuting, amenities, wages, and outside options determine the feasible labor-market set.

### Week 2. Housing, Rents, Mobility Constraints, and Labor-Market Adjustment

**Central question:** When do housing markets and moving costs prevent workers from reaching better labor-market opportunities?

This week adds housing supply, rents, residential sorting, moving costs, and local price adjustment to the Week 1 access framework. Students study how a high-wage city can be inaccessible once rents capitalize productivity or amenities, how housing constraints generate spatial misallocation, and why low-skill or low-wealth workers may be less able to move after local shocks. The focus is on incidence: a local wage gain may accrue to workers, landlords, incumbent residents, firms, or migrants depending on housing and mobility margins.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Housing and moving costs shape labor supply to places, the real value of wages, the adjustment to shocks, and who can access high-productivity jobs.

### Week 3. Spatial Mismatch, Segregation, Neighborhood Effects, and Job Access

**Central question:** How do residential segregation and neighborhood exposure shape access to jobs, networks, and long-run labor-market opportunity?

The third week turns from aggregate access to unequal access. Students study spatial mismatch, residential segregation, neighborhood networks, public goods, schools, local institutions, and exposure effects. The key discipline is to separate mechanisms: distance to jobs, transit access, employer discrimination, referral networks, neighborhood peers, school quality, and local safety can all produce unequal labor outcomes, but they imply different counterfactuals and policy levers.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Segregation and neighborhoods matter when they change job search, hiring networks, human-capital accumulation, commuting feasibility, earnings, employment, and intergenerational mobility.

### Week 4. Crime, Safety, Environment, and the Urban Feasible Set for Work

**Central question:** How do safety and environmental conditions change which jobs workers can take and how much work is worth?

This week treats crime, harassment, public safety, commuting risk, pollution, heat, and environmental amenities as labor-market constraints rather than as background urban conditions. Students ask how local risk changes work timing, job search radius, occupation choice, productivity, absenteeism, mobility, and compensating differentials. The week also clarifies why a wage can be a poor welfare statistic when workers face unequal exposure to risk or pollution.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Safety and environmental quality change the feasible set for work, the disutility of commuting or job tasks, worker productivity, and the welfare value of wages.

### Week 5. Migration, Local Labor Demand, and Urban Labor Reallocation

**Central question:** How do workers, firms, wages, rents, and commuting flows adjust when local labor demand changes?

The fifth week studies local labor demand shocks, trade and technology exposure, sectoral decline, local booms, migration, population adjustment, commuting flows, and persistence. Students compare adjustment through wages, employment, labor-force participation, rents, out-migration, in-commuting, and firm entry or exit. The central lesson is that local shocks are labor-market shocks with spatial incidence: the same demand shift can have very different effects depending on worker mobility, housing supply, commuting openness, and local institutions.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Migration and local reallocation determine whether workers escape shocks, capture booms, remain attached to declining places, or bear persistent employment and earnings losses.

### Week 6. Synthesis, Frontier Questions, and Student Research Designs

**Central question:** What does a credible urban-labor research design look like?

The closing week integrates the sequence into research design. Students choose a labor outcome, specify the spatial mechanism, define the relevant geography, name the worker or firm margin, and state the counterfactual. Possible applications include transit expansions, housing supply reforms, place-based policies, environmental improvements, safety interventions, local demand shocks, neighborhood exposure, and migration policies. The goal is not to draft full papers yet, but to leave with disciplined research memos that connect urban structure to labor-market mechanisms and welfare.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** The course succeeds when spatial questions become research designs about work, wages, access, mobility, allocation, inequality, and worker welfare.
