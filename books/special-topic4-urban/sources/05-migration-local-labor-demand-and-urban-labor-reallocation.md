# Week 5. Migration, Local Labor Demand, and Urban Labor Reallocation

## Central question

How do workers, firms, wages, rents, and commuting flows adjust when local labor demand changes?

## Why this week matters

Local labor demand shocks are never just “place effects” or just “worker effects.” A boom or bust changes migration incentives, commuting flows, wages, rents, housing demand, firm entry and exit, and the welfare of incumbent residents relative to movers. This week asks students to treat migration and urban reallocation as a labor-adjustment system rather than a stand-alone migration topic.

## Core points

- Local labor-demand shocks reallocate workers through several margins: wages, employment, labor-force participation, commuting, migration, rents, and firm dynamics.
- Adjustment depends on housing supply, commuting openness, worker heterogeneity, liquidity, local institutions, and the durability of urban capital.
- The same shock can have different incidence on incumbent workers, migrants, firms, landlords, and local taxpayers.
- “Places versus people” is not a slogan but an empirical distinction: local labor markets can recover differently than individual workers.
- Gentrification and diversification are part of urban labor reallocation when they change job composition, occupational access, resilience, and who can remain in high-opportunity places.

## Bridge

Weeks 1–4 established that labor-market opportunity in cities is shaped by commuting, rents, segregation, crime, environment, and the urban feasible set for work. Week 5 adds the dynamic question: when local labor demand changes, who moves, who stays, who benefits, and who is priced out? The answer requires integrating migration, commuting, housing, and firm adjustment into one framework.

## Field Core

### 1. A spatial labor-adjustment framework

Start from a spatial labor-market equilibrium in which workers choose among job–residence bundles. A local labor-demand shock affects not only wages and employment but also rents, commuting, neighborhood composition, and firm dynamics. The key objects are therefore:

- nominal wage responses
- employment and labor-force responses
- in-migration and out-migration
- commuting adjustment
- housing price and rent capitalization
- firm entry, exit, and local sectoral composition

This framework should make clear why local demand shocks are neither purely “macro” nor purely “urban” objects: they are labor-market shocks with spatial incidence [@moretti2011; @notowidigdo2020LocalDemandShocks].

### 2. Migration, commuting, and incidence after local demand shocks

A core lesson of the literature is that adjustment margins differ sharply across places. Some shocks induce migration, others induce commuting, others show up mainly in rents or unemployment persistence. A key benchmark is that commuting openness can absorb a large share of local labor-demand shocks, so employment elasticities are heterogeneous across local labor markets [@monteReddingRossiHansberg2018].

Workers also differ in mobility and attachment. Low-skill and low-wealth workers may remain in declining places longer, which changes both the distributional incidence and the measured persistence of shocks [@notowidigdo2020LocalDemandShocks]. Conversely, in receiving places, migration can lower unemployment partly because migrants increase housing demand, local construction, and derived labor demand [@howard2020migration].

### 3. Persistence, hysteresis, and local labor-market recovery

The next question is whether local places recover after shocks, and whether people do. Recent work emphasizes the difference between place-level rebound and worker-level scarring. A location can recover in employment levels while still leaving incumbent workers with persistent earnings or employment losses [@autor2025places; @hershbein2024evolution].

This distinction should be taught explicitly:
- **place recovery**: employment, establishment counts, rents, population, and commuting may normalize
- **worker recovery**: incumbent workers may still bear long-term earnings losses, occupational downgrades, or relocation costs

That distinction is one of the most important conceptual payoffs of the local-labor-demand literature.

### 4. Gentrification as labor-market restructuring

This week should not treat gentrification as a housing-only topic. Through a labor lens, gentrification is a local reallocation process that changes occupational composition, local demand for services, commuting patterns, land use, and who can access specific neighborhoods.

The relevant questions are:
- does gentrification create or displace jobs, and in which sectors?
- does it restructure neighborhood labor demand toward services or professional work?
- who gains from new opportunity and who is priced out or displaced?
- how do job location and neighborhood change interact?

Useful anchors are the job-location and employment-composition literatures on gentrification and neighborhood change [@kolko2009joblocation; @hartley2013longterm; @couture2023neighborhood].

### 5. Diversification, resilience, and worker insurance

A second frontier extension is diversification. The labor question is not merely whether cities specialize or diversify, but whether diversified local economies provide insurance against labor-demand shocks, smoother worker reallocation, and better welfare protection in downturns.

Students should learn to distinguish:
- **specialization** as exposure to high upside and high downside
- **diversification** as resilience, reallocation capacity, and insurance for workers

This gives an urban-labor interpretation to city resilience and local industrial policy [@desoyres2025diversity; @bouvard2024should].

### 6. Methods and data box

This week should include a practical methods/data box that explicitly links setting to design.

#### Common empirical designs

- **Shift-share / Bartik exposure designs**: local industry shares interacted with national sectoral growth to measure demand exposure [@goldsmithpinkham2020bartik; @borusyak2025practical].
- **Predicted-destination IVs for migration**: historical destination shares used to instrument migrant allocation [@howard2020migration].
- **Commuting-linked designs**: commuting openness or travel-time changes used to understand within-city and cross-city adjustment [@monteReddingRossiHansberg2018; @severenCommutingLaborHousing2023].
- **Spatial-equilibrium calibration / structural models**: used when wages, rents, commuting, and migration jointly adjust [@notowidigdo2020LocalDemandShocks; @desoyres2025diversity].
- **Dynamic local-shock event studies**: useful for persistence, hysteresis, and medium-run recovery [@hershbein2024evolution].
- **Neighborhood/job-location designs**: tract-level or neighborhood-level labor demand combined with urban change and gentrification [@kolko2009joblocation; @hartley2013longterm].

#### What a good design should do

A good empirical design for this week’s questions should:
- identify the shock separately from endogenous worker sorting
- specify which margin is expected to adjust first
- account for rents and housing supply when interpreting wages
- distinguish incumbent-worker effects from migrant-composition effects
- clarify whether the outcome is nominal earnings, real opportunity, or welfare
- show whether commuting is an omitted adjustment margin
- justify the local labor-market definition

#### Frontier data sources

- LEHD/LODES/QWI for worker–firm–place relationships
- ACS / Decennial Census / PUMS for migration, commuting, and demographic heterogeneity
- IRS county-to-county migration flows
- Zillow / CoreLogic / FHFA / ACS rents for housing capitalization
- longitudinal matched employer–employee data where available
- neighborhood or tract-level employment geodata for gentrification/job location
- transit/travel-time matrices for commuting access
- cell-phone or device data when mobility measurement is central
- local administrative data on construction, permits, and firm births for housing and firm adjustment

## Research Lab

### Replication anchor

A natural replication anchor is [@howard2020migration], because it forces students to think about migration, housing, and labor demand jointly rather than treating migration as a purely demographic object.

### Challenge / extension anchors

- [@monteReddingRossiHansberg2018] for commuting vs migration adjustment
- [@notowidigdo2020LocalDemandShocks] for incidence under immobility and housing capitalization
- [@hershbein2024evolution] for persistence and local recovery
- [@couture2023neighborhood] or [@hartley2013longterm] for gentrification as labor-market restructuring
- [@desoyres2025diversity] for diversification/resilience as frontier urban labor research

### Research opportunities

Promising research directions include:
- heterogeneity in mobility by wealth, skill, and family structure
- remote work and the redefinition of local labor markets
- migration responses to climate and weather shocks in urban labor markets
- neighborhood change and occupational access
- diversification as worker insurance rather than city-planning doctrine
- comparing commuting-based vs migration-based adjustment across countries

## Forward bridge

The final synthesis week should return to a unifying question: when cities generate labor-market opportunity, who can actually access it, who captures the gains, and what determines whether local shocks translate into worker mobility, neighborhood change, or persistent inequality?
