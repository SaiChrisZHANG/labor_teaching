# Week 1. Space and Labor Markets: Urban Access, Commuting, Amenities, and Local Opportunity

## Week identity

**Central question:** How does space enter labor-market choice, wage determination, and worker welfare?

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Space matters because workers do not choose jobs from a placeless menu; commuting, amenities, wages, rents, and outside options determine the feasible labor-market set.

## Core design goal

This opening lecture should give students a compact **theoretical framework** for how space enters labor-market choice before turning to empirical work. The city should not appear as a background label or a descriptive category. It should appear as a labor-market technology that changes:
- which jobs are reachable,
- which residences are affordable,
- which outside options are relevant,
- how wages should be interpreted,
- and how worker welfare should be measured.

The lecture is **not** a generic introduction to urban economics. It is a labor-market framing lecture. The chapter should show students that once commuting costs, rents, job access, and amenities are present, standard labor objects—search, wages, sorting, local opportunity, and welfare—look different.

## What the chapter should do

### Opening orientation
The opening should explain why labor economists should care about space:
- workers search over **job–location pairs**, not just jobs;
- employment opportunities depend on where jobs are relative to residences;
- high nominal wages may be offset by rents and commuting burdens;
- dense places may raise productivity or learning, but may also attract selected workers or compensate for higher costs;
- local labor markets are partially open through commuting and migration, so shocks propagate spatially.

### Core points box
The required **Core points** box should summarize at least the following:
- local labor markets are linked systems of jobs, workers, residences, commuting costs, rents, and amenities;
- space changes the feasible labor-market set, not just the location label;
- wages alone are not enough to compare opportunity across places;
- workplace location and residential location should be analytically separated;
- urban wage premia can reflect productivity, learning, sorting, or compensating differentials.

## Required section structure

### 1. Bridge
Use this section to move from the labor sequence into an explicitly spatial framework.

Must distinguish:
- placeless labor choice vs spatially constrained labor choice,
- nominal wages vs welfare-relevant opportunity,
- administrative places vs economically relevant local labor markets.

### 2. Field Core

The Field Core should be organized into **five blocks**.

#### Block A. A worker job–residence choice framework
This section should introduce a compact formal framework for how space enters labor choice. Workers choose among combinations of:
- job location,
- residential location,
- commuting cost,
- rents,
- amenities,
- and outside options.

The goal is not technical generality. The goal is to discipline the rest of the course. Students should come away knowing that space enters labor choices through both **costs** and **opportunity**.

#### Block B. Local labor markets and spatial job search
This section should cover:
- local labor markets as search spaces,
- why labor markets can be “quite local” even in large cities,
- how commuting frictions shrink the effective opportunity set,
- why congestion and competition from other job seekers matter,
- and why local labor markets are better understood through flows and access than administrative labels alone.

#### Block C. Spatial equilibrium: wages, rents, commuting, amenities
This section should explain:
- the Roback logic that wages, rents, and amenities jointly allocate workers across space;
- why workplace wages should be separated from residential costs;
- why observed wage gradients cannot be interpreted without rents and commuting;
- how spatial equilibrium changes welfare interpretation.

This is where the lecture should make clear that “good jobs” and “good places” are not the same object.

#### Block D. Urban wage premia as labor-market objects
This section should frame the urban wage premium as an empirical and conceptual object that can reflect:
- productivity/agglomeration,
- learning and faster human-capital accumulation,
- selection/sorting,
- compensating differentials for costs or disamenities.

The chapter should keep this firmly labor-focused: what does the premium mean for worker choice, job access, and welfare?

#### Block E. Preview of later urban-labor themes
This should be a short closing block that previews:
- Week 2: housing, rents, and mobility constraints,
- Week 3: spatial mismatch and segregation,
- Week 4: crime, safety, and environment,
- Week 5: migration and local labor-demand adjustment,
- Week 6: synthesis and research designs.

The point is to show that each later topic is a way in which space enters labor-market opportunity.

### 3. Research Lab
The Research Lab should train students to think like researchers, not just readers.

It should:
- separate search/access objects from equilibrium objects,
- distinguish commuting from migration,
- distinguish job access from realized employment,
- distinguish urban wage premia generated by productivity vs selection vs compensation,
- ask what data are needed to measure worker opportunity in space.

It should also ask how students can turn a place-specific setting into a broadly interesting labor contribution:
- by isolating the labor-market mechanism,
- by making the spatial constraint measurable,
- by separating local features from portable mechanisms.

## Methods and measurement requirements

The chapter should explicitly distinguish:
- workplace location vs residential location,
- nominal wages vs real/welfare-relevant opportunity,
- commuting as a margin distinct from migration,
- access to jobs vs realized job matches,
- geographic market definitions vs flow-based market definitions,
- urban wage premia generated by productivity vs selection vs compensation.

It should also mention key empirical objects:
- commuting flows,
- travel-time / generalized commuting cost,
- workplace-based wage measures,
- local rent gradients,
- job suburbanization / access shifts,
- commuting-zone / ward / travel-time labor-market definitions,
- spatial-equilibrium counterfactuals.

- `[@monteReddingRossiHansberg2018]`

Optional extension anchor:

The bounded student path must run locally without confidential microdata.

The lab should train students to distinguish:
- what the local labor market is,
- what counts as access,
- what the equilibrium object is,
- whether a result is about sorting, productivity, or commuting frictions,
- and how a place-specific result can still matter broadly.
