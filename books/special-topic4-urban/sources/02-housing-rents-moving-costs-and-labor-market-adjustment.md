# Week 2. Housing, Rents, Moving Costs, and Labor-Market Adjustment

## Opening orientation

Week 1 established that local labor markets are bundles of jobs, wages, amenities, commuting costs, rents, and outside options. Week 2 adds the housing system explicitly. A productive place can fail to improve worker welfare if rents capitalize productivity or amenities, if housing supply is too inelastic, or if moving costs prevent workers from relocating in response to shocks. This lecture therefore treats housing as part of labor-market adjustment rather than as a background market.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Housing matters for labor because workers care about **real access** to jobs, not nominal wages alone.
- Local shocks can be absorbed through wages, rents, commuting, migration, or reduced access, depending on the housing and mobility margins.
- A central research task is to identify which friction is binding: housing supply, moving costs, wealth/liquidity and credit constraints, commuting technology, regulation, or rent capitalization.
- The incidence of local opportunity can fall on workers, firms, landlords/homeowners, or incumbent residents rather than on migrants alone.
- The best research in this area connects a clean labor question to a disciplined spatial framework, credible local variation, and housing data that measure access and adjustment directly.
:::

## Bridge

This lecture takes the spatial labor framework from Week 1 and asks when local opportunity is blocked or redistributed by the housing market. The right way to think about this topic is not “housing as another sector,” but “housing as a gatekeeper to labor-market opportunity.” The key labor question is therefore: when do rents and moving frictions stop workers from turning high-productivity places into higher welfare?

## Field Core

### 1. A labor-space-housing framework

The starting point is a spatial equilibrium lens in which a worker compares bundles of:
- local wages,
- rents and housing costs,
- commuting costs,
- amenities,
- and idiosyncratic attachment or moving costs.

A high-wage city is not necessarily attractive if higher wages are offset by rents, worse commuting, or limited housing access. This is the core intuition of Rosen-Roback style spatial equilibrium work and its labor-market extensions [@roback1982; @moretti2011].

For labor economists, the crucial distinction is:

- **nominal wage gain**: the worker earns more in a high-productivity place;
- **real access gain**: the worker is actually better off after housing and mobility costs;
- **adjustment margin**: whether the place accommodates more labor through migration, commuting, or neither.

### 2. Housing supply constraints and spatial misallocation

The most visible housing-labor mechanism is spatial misallocation. If high-productivity places restrict housing supply, then productivity growth is not translated into large employment reallocation; instead it shows up in higher nominal wages and especially higher rents. This is the core mechanism in [@hsiehMoretti2019HousingConstraints] and also underlies the convergence argument in [@ganongShoag2017RegionalConvergence].

This block should teach students to distinguish:
- *worker-side access*: can workers move to the productive place?
- *aggregate efficiency*: how much output is lost because labor cannot reach productive places?
- *distributional incidence*: which workers are kept out, and who benefits from capitalization?

A useful conceptual split is:
1. **quantity constraint**: too little new housing in productive places;
2. **price constraint**: access exists but only at very high rents;
3. **sorting effect**: high housing costs select in higher-skill or higher-wealth workers.

### 3. Moving costs, migration frictions, and unequal adjustment

A second research block asks why workers do not move even when local opportunities diverge. Housing is central because moving requires deposits, search, relocation costs, and often homeownership liquidation. This is why a negative local labor-demand shock can generate persistent joblessness if workers do not migrate out and if housing markets insulate some residents more than others [@notowidigdo2020LocalDemandShocks; @zabelMigrationHousingMarket2012].

The lecture should separate:
- **moving-cost frictions** from **housing-supply frictions**;
- **voluntary immobility** from **constrained immobility**;
- **migration elasticity** from **welfare**.

Recent work such as [@amiorEducationGeographicalMobility2024] is useful because it reframes mobility not as a residual but as a friction that differs by worker type and job surplus. That is especially valuable for students trying to turn “low mobility” into a labor question rather than a demographic fact.

### 4. Commuting as a substitute for migration

Migration is not the only way workers access productive labor markets. Commuting can partially substitute for relocation, especially when workers can live in one place and work in another. This makes commuting a labor adjustment margin in its own right rather than just a transportation variable [@monteReddingRossiHansberg2018].

This perspective is important because:
- commuting can attenuate the employment effects of local shocks,
- commuting can increase access to productive jobs without changing residence,
- but commuting costs can also reproduce inequality if only some workers can absorb the time and money costs.

The transport/housing connection is particularly visible in [@severenCommutingLaborHousing2023] and in policy experiments like [@broughEliminatingFaresExpand2025], which show how transport access interacts with spatial labor-market reach.

### 5. Incidence: who captures local opportunity?

One of the most important contributions of this literature is incidence analysis. A local productivity or labor-demand shock does not automatically benefit workers. Depending on the local housing system, the gains may instead accrue to:
- landlords through rent capitalization,
- homeowners through higher house prices,
- firms through labor-supply responses,
- incumbent residents rather than new entrants,
- or commuters rather than migrants.

This is the unifying logic behind [@notowidigdo2020LocalDemandShocks], [@suarezserratoWhoBenefitsState2016], and related local-shock papers. Students should leave this lecture knowing that “a place got more productive” is not yet a welfare statement about workers.

### 6. Data survey: what do researchers actually use?

This field is unusually data-intensive, and students often need a roadmap more than another theory paragraph. A practical data survey should therefore cover at least:

- **ACS / ACS PUMS**: migration, commuting, rents, housing tenure, worker characteristics;
- **Decennial Census / Census long form**: long-run migration, housing and neighborhood structure;
- **LEHD / LODES / QWI**: worker-job flows, workplace/residence linkages, employment by location;
- **IRS migration data**: inter-county/state mobility;
- **Zillow / FHFA / CoreLogic / transaction-level housing data**: prices, rents, capitalization, turnover;
- **HUD administrative data / voucher data**: housing assistance, mobility, neighborhood access where program access is relevant;
- **HMDA**: mortgage access and financing conditions where homeownership or wealth is relevant;
- **commuting-zone / MSA / tract / ZIP-level definitions**: the geography choice is itself a design decision;
- **housing-supply and regulation measures**: Saiz-style elasticities, zoning restrictions, permit data, land-use regulations.

The lecture should not just name these datasets; it should tell students what labor question each one can help answer.

## Research Lab

### Research architecture for this field

A useful way to organize the empirical literature is into five buckets:

1. **Foundational spatial labor framework**  
   [@roback1982; @moretti2011; @diamond2016LocationChoices]

2. **Housing constraints and misallocation**  
   [@hsiehMoretti2019HousingConstraints; @ganongShoag2017RegionalConvergence; @hoxieMovingDensityHalf2023; @saizGeographicDeterminantsHousing2010]

3. **Moving costs and migration adjustment**  
   [@notowidigdo2020LocalDemandShocks; @zabelMigrationHousingMarket2012; @amiorEducationGeographicalMobility2024]

4. **Commuting and within-metro access**  
   [@monteReddingRossiHansberg2018; @severenCommutingLaborHousing2023; @broughEliminatingFaresExpand2025]

5. **Local shocks, incidence, and complementary evidence**  
   [@suarezserratoWhoBenefitsState2016; @morettiLocalMultipliers2010]

### Dominant empirical designs

Students should recognize several recurring research designs:

- **spatial equilibrium / calibrated quantitative counterfactuals**  
  Used when the question is aggregate access, misallocation, or incidence.

- **Bartik-style or shift-share local labor-demand shocks**  
  Useful for asking how housing changes the incidence of place-level shocks.

- **housing-supply heterogeneity**  
  A common way to test whether constrained places adjust through prices instead of population.

- **commuting or transport shocks**  
  Useful for separating access from relocation.

- **migration-event or difference-in-differences designs**  
  Often useful for adjustment timing and heterogeneous mobility.

### Empirical shortfalls and contribution margins

This field is rich, but students can still contribute a lot. Common shortfalls include:
- treating migration as the only adjustment margin and ignoring commuting;
- measuring nominal wages without housing costs;
- using a geography that does not match the actual labor market;
- failing to separate housing-supply constraints from moving costs;
- assuming equal mobility across skill, wealth, race, or tenure groups;
- undermeasuring landlord/homeowner incidence.

Strong research opportunities include:
- real-wage and welfare incidence rather than nominal effects,
- heterogeneity in moving frictions by worker resources,
- the interaction of housing assistance and labor adjustment,
- housing and labor-market access for low-wealth workers,
- cross-country or comparative work on urban access with harmonized labor outcomes.
