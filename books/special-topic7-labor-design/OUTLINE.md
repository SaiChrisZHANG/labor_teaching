# Labor Market Design, Contracting, and Mechanism Design

## Course role

Labor Market Design, Contracting, and Mechanism Design is a 6-week compact advanced elective on the design of labor-market institutions. It studies matching rules, recruiting systems, contracts, screening, incentives, assignment algorithms, wage rules, platforms, public-sector staffing, and empirical evaluation as labor-economics objects.

The course should not become a generic mechanism-design or contract-theory survey. Every week begins from work, hiring, assignment, wages, effort, worker-firm matching, public staffing, or worker welfare, then asks how institutional design changes behavior and allocation.

## Cumulative logic

The course sequence is deliberately cumulative:

1. define matching rules as labor-market allocation institutions;
2. study how decentralized recruiting can fail through congestion, timing, and unraveling;
3. move inside employment relationships through contracts, screening, incentives, and moral hazard;
4. extend assignment and pay design to platforms, algorithms, and wage rules;
5. study professional and public-sector labor markets where fairness, transparency, and staffing constraints are first order;
6. end with empirical design and frontier research questions.

Labor II is the closest core-sequence neighbor. Labor II teaches search, matching, wage-setting, personnel economics, monopsony, regulation, and institutions; this compact elective asks how those environments are deliberately designed and how design changes can be evaluated.

## Internal roadmap

- **Matching-market focused:** Weeks 1 and 2
- **Contract and incentive focused:** Week 3

## Weekly outline

### Week 1. Matching, Market Design, and Labor Allocation

**Central question:** When do designed matching rules improve labor allocation relative to decentralized search and bargaining?

The opening week introduces matching stability, market thickness, priority rules, strategy-proofness, and the institutional logic of centralized clearing. Students study why some labor markets require explicit matching rules, why preferences and priorities are hard to elicit, and how design choices alter match quality, fairness, and welfare. Professional entry markets provide the labor anchor rather than serving as generic examples.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Matching rules determine which workers enter which jobs, training slots, schools, hospitals, agencies, or firms, so they directly shape labor allocation, career starts, wages, and worker welfare.

### Week 2. Recruiting, Congestion, Timing, and Unraveling

**Central question:** Why do decentralized hiring markets often move too early, too fast, or too chaotically?

This week studies application congestion, interview bottlenecks, exploding offers, early contracting, signaling devices, and unraveling. The aim is to show how labor-market inefficiency can arise from the design of the hiring process itself rather than from worker or firm fundamentals. Students compare markets that centralize, markets that coordinate timing, and markets that rely on decentralized screening under capacity constraints.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Recruiting design determines the cost of search, the timing of job offers, the information workers and employers observe, and the allocation of early-career opportunities.

### Week 3. Contracts, Incentives, Screening, and Moral Hazard

**Central question:** How should labor contracts be designed when effort, type, commitment, and multitasking are imperfectly observed?

The course turns from market-level matching rules to employment relationships. Students connect screening, signaling, performance pay, probation, promotions, tournaments, monitoring, multitask moral hazard, and relational contracting to real workplace design. The discipline is to keep the employment margin visible: contracts allocate risk, elicit effort, reveal information, and shape retention inside firms.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Labor contracts govern effort, pay, supervision, promotion, retention, and worker-firm surplus, making screening and incentive design central to personnel economics and workplace organization.

### Week 4. Assignment, Wages, Platforms, and Pricing Rules

**Central question:** How do assignment algorithms, platform rules, and wage-setting systems shape labor allocation and worker welfare?

This week studies environments where matching, visibility, scheduling, pay, and evaluation are jointly designed by intermediaries or organizations. Applications include online labor markets, gig platforms, algorithmic recommendations, wage posting, pay transparency, and structured pay scales. The key issue is whether design improves information and allocation, changes bargaining power, or shifts risk onto workers.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Platform and wage rules determine who sees which jobs, which workers receive offers, how pay is set, how effort is monitored, and how the value of flexibility or job quality is distributed.

### Week 5. Professional Labor Markets and Public-Sector Design

**Central question:** What can professional and public-sector labor markets teach us about mechanism design under fairness, transparency, and staffing constraints?

Residency matches, teacher assignment, public hiring, civil-service rules, internship markets, and other structured professional systems provide concrete design laboratories. Students study the tradeoff between efficiency, priority, transparency, diversity, local staffing needs, and participant strategy. The week emphasizes institutional detail: the same mechanism can perform differently when preferences, outside options, legal constraints, and public objectives differ.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Professional and public-sector design allocates workers to jobs and service institutions where staffing quality, career formation, public value, and worker welfare are jointly at stake.

### Week 6. Empirical Design, Experiments, and Frontier Questions

**Central question:** How can labor-market design interventions be evaluated credibly?

The final week brings together reduced-form evaluation, field experiments, audits, structural matching models, platform experiments, administrative data, and institutional case studies. Students learn to define the mechanism, state the counterfactual, measure match quality or effort, diagnose equilibrium response, and separate welfare claims from descriptive improvements. The week ends by turning the compact course into student research memos.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** The course succeeds when design changes become evidence-based claims about hiring, assignment, wages, effort, worker-firm sorting, public staffing, and welfare.
