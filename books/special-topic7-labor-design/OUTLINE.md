# Labor Market Design, Contracting, and Mechanism Design

## Course role

Labor Market Design, Contracting, and Mechanism Design is a 6-week advanced elective on the design of matching institutions, recruiting systems, and labor contracts.

## Weekly outline

### Week 1. Matching, market design, and labor allocation

**Central question:** When do designed matching rules improve labor-market allocation relative to decentralized search?

The opening week introduces matching stability, market thickness, and the logic of centralized design. Students study why some labor markets require explicit clearing rules while others function tolerably through decentralized search.

### Week 2. Recruiting, congestion, timing, and unraveling

**Central question:** Why do decentralized hiring markets often move too early, too fast, or too chaotically?

This week studies congestion, strategic timing, exploding offers, and unraveling. The aim is to show how labor-market inefficiency can arise from the design of the hiring process itself rather than from worker or firm fundamentals.

### Week 3. Contracts, incentives, screening, and moral hazard

**Central question:** How should labor contracts be designed when effort, type, and commitment are imperfectly observed?

The course turns from matching rules to workplace contracts. Students connect screening, incentive pay, probation, and multidimensional effort to real labor-market contract design.

### Week 4. Assignment, wages, platforms, and pricing rules

**Central question:** How do algorithmic assignment and pricing rules shape labor allocation in modern platforms and matching environments?

This week studies environments where matching, pay, and visibility are jointly designed, often by digital intermediaries. The key issue is whether algorithmic allocation improves efficiency, worsens power imbalances, or both.

### Week 5. Professional labor markets and public-sector design

**Central question:** What can high-stakes professional and public-sector labor markets teach us about mechanism design?

Residency matches, teacher assignment, public hiring, and other structured allocation systems provide concrete design laboratories. The week emphasizes institutional detail and the tradeoff between fairness, transparency, and efficiency.

### Week 6. Empirical design, experiments, and frontier questions

**Central question:** How can labor-market design interventions be evaluated credibly?

The final week brings together structural reasoning, natural experiments, field experiments, and institutional case studies. Students leave with a clear sense of how mechanism design becomes empirical labor economics rather than remaining purely theoretical.
