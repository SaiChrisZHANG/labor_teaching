# Week 4. Assignment, Wages, Platforms, and Pricing Rules

## Opening orientation

This week studies labor markets where assignment, pricing, and evaluation are not passive outcomes of decentralized search, but are actively designed by an intermediary. Platform labor markets and structured pay systems are useful because they make labor-market rules unusually visible: who sees which jobs, which workers receive attention, how wages are quoted, how risk is allocated, and how worker quality is inferred are all partly products of design.

The main discipline of the week is to keep the labor object visible. Platforms are not just software firms or marketplaces. They are labor-market institutions that choose visibility, ranking, pricing, monitoring, and governance rules. Those choices can improve matching and reduce frictions, but they can also change bargaining power, shift risk onto workers, and create new allocative distortions.

```{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Assignment, ranking, wage posting, commissions, and platform governance rules are labor-market institutions.
- The same design rule can improve information and matching while simultaneously redistributing risk, surplus, or bargaining power.
- A good applied labor-design paper identifies the **specific rule**, the **margin it changes**, and the **welfare object** that matters.
- Frontier work in this area often uses experiments, platform policy changes, and high-frequency administrative data because platform rules are manipulable and directly encoded.
```

## Bridge

Week 3 focused on contracts inside employment relationships. Week 4 moves to labor markets where contracts and wages are partly mediated by platforms or other intermediaries. The key bridge is that hidden information, incentives, and risk-sharing are still present, but they now operate through visibility algorithms, price rules, ratings, endorsements, and rule-based governance.

## Field Core

### 1. Platforms and intermediaries as labor-market designers

A labor intermediary can influence outcomes at several stages:
1. whether workers and jobs find each other at all;
2. which opportunities are shown to whom;
3. what wage or bid range is visible;
4. how quality is summarized or guaranteed;
5. how disputes, cancellation, and monitoring are handled.

That means the intermediary’s objective function matters. If the platform prioritizes fill rates, revenue, trust, growth, or repeat usage, it may choose rules that differ from the rules that maximize worker surplus or long-run matching efficiency. This is the core reason labor economists should care about platform design: the platform is effectively designing the search, matching, and wage-setting process.

### 2. A compact framework for assignment and pricing

A simple labor-design framework can be written around a rule vector:
- `A`: assignment / visibility / ranking rule;
- `P`: pricing rule (posted wage, bids, commissions, wage floors, surge multipliers);
- `I`: information rule (ratings, histories, employer messages, guarantees, salary disclosure);
- `M`: monitoring and governance rule.

Workers care about expected earnings, flexibility, risk, search costs, and the nonwage attributes of jobs. Firms or clients care about quality, speed, reliability, and cost. The intermediary may care about market thickness, trust, revenue, or long-run retention.

This framework helps students organize empirical questions:
- when does a rule improve allocation?
- when does it simply steer surplus?
- when does it create dependence or opacity?
- when does it reveal information that was previously missing?

### 3. Assignment, ranking, and visibility

The first design object is visibility. Workers typically do not observe a neutral market menu. They see jobs through search rankings, recommendation systems, invitation rules, and employer-side filters. Similarly, employers see workers through rankings, endorsements, ratings, and application pools that are already shaped by platform design.

This is why information and assignment often move together. In online labor markets, richer public evaluations can raise future employment and wages for low-experience workers [@pallais2014]. Platform guarantees or endorsements can steer buyers toward selected workers because the platform’s intervention is itself informative about quality [@barachHorton2019steering]. Employers’ cheap-talk statements about willingness to pay can reshape both the types of applicants who apply and the wage bids they submit [@hortonJohariKircher2024sorting].

The labor-economics lesson is that assignment is often partly algorithmic and partly informational. A strong paper therefore has to separate:
- the **mechanical exposure effect** of being shown,
- the **belief effect** induced by platform signals,
- and the **equilibrium response** of both sides of the market.

### 4. Wage-setting, price rules, and commissions

The second design object is how compensation is structured and displayed. This includes:
- posted wages vs bargaining,
- bid-based pricing,
- wage floors,
- platform commissions,
- dynamic pricing,
- pay transparency,
- and structured pay scales.

These are not just nominal details. They affect:
- who applies,
- how workers target search,
- whether employers screen more aggressively,
- the value of flexibility,
- and who bears demand and scheduling risk.

Several recent literatures make these design objects concrete. Job posts often contain very little wage information, which itself is a meaningful search friction [@batraTaska2023wageInfo]. Pay-transparency rules in postings affect wages and the information environment workers face [@arnoldQuachTaska2025payTransparency]. Employers on online labor markets use compensation history in ways that matter for future offers and sorting [@barachHorton2021compensationHistory]. In ride-hailing and related platform work, algorithmic pay rules can trade off flexibility against earnings predictability and may discipline workers through schedule-specific price differentiation [@chenLuoYuan2023drivingDrivers; @hallKrueger2018analysis].

### 5. Worker welfare, flexibility, and risk

A central question in platform labor markets is how design changes the **quality** of labor-market participation, not just earnings. Flexibility can be valuable, but so are predictability, insurance, autonomy, and the ability to reject work without penalty. Platform rules can reallocate these dimensions in ways that are not visible in wage data alone.

Ride-hailing evidence is especially useful here. Drivers appear to value schedule flexibility, but the value of flexibility is intertwined with nonstandard hours, uncertainty, and platform-mediated earnings rules [@chenChevalierRossiOehlsen2019; @hallKrueger2018analysis]. In knowledge-work platforms, workers may retain surplus despite apparent labor abundance, in part because matching, timing, and wage-setting are structured in ways that create informational and positional rents [@stantonThomas2021whoBenefits].

This means the welfare object in a platform-design paper should be chosen carefully. Depending on the setting, the relevant welfare margin could be:
- earnings,
- volatility,
- flexibility,
- match quality,
- continuation opportunities,
- or access to better future jobs.

### 6. Platform governance, market power, and rule incidence

Assignment and pricing rules are also power rules. If a platform intermediates search, ranking, wage posting, ratings, and dispute resolution, then it can influence both allocation and the division of rents. This is why platform design naturally connects to labor-market power, risk shifting, and worker voice.

The main applied question is not just whether platforms reduce frictions. It is also:
- whether they reallocate bargaining power,
- whether they amplify dependence on reputation systems,
- whether they shift market risk to workers,
- and whether they make labor-market access more or less equitable.

### 7. From theory to applied labor research

A useful theory-to-applied bridge is to organize papers around one of four design questions:

#### (i) Assignment question
How should workers or jobs be ranked, recommended, or routed?

#### (ii) Information question
What should be disclosed, certified, or signaled?

#### (iii) Pricing question
How should wages, bids, commissions, or pay floors be structured?

#### (iv) Governance question
Who bears risk, and who has discretion to revise the rules?

To become an applied labor paper, the design question must be linked to:
- a measurable labor margin,
- a counterfactual rule,
- and a clear welfare object.

### 8. Frontier methods box

This literature is unusually well suited to experiments because many rules are literally coded into the platform.

#### Common experimental and quasi-experimental methods

**(i) Platform A/B tests**  
Randomize ranking, visibility, or recommendation rules and trace effects on applications, hires, and worker earnings.

**(ii) Randomized information disclosure**  
Reveal ratings, histories, or wage information to subsets of users [@pallais2014].

**(iii) Randomized steering / guarantee interventions**  
Use platform endorsement or guarantees to identify the role of trust and steering [@barachHorton2019steering].

**(iv) Wage-rule experiments**  
Randomize posted minimum wages, price floors, or other pay rules [@horton2025minimumWage].

**(v) Platform policy changes**  
Exploit staggered rollouts of transparency, pricing, or governance rules [@arnoldQuachTaska2025payTransparency].

**(vi) Conjoint, lab-in-the-field, and survey experiments**  
Elicit worker and employer responses to algorithmic assignment, fairness, or flexibility rules.

#### What a strong design should do

A strong empirical design should identify:
- the exact rule that changed,
- the labor margin affected,
- the counterfactual design,
- and the welfare incidence across workers, firms, and the platform.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper
**Pallais (2014)** is the primary anchor because it gives a clean experimental intervention in an online labor market, changing information and effective visibility for inexperienced workers [@pallais2014].

### Reproduce
Recreate one reduced fact about how public information affects:
- worker hiring probability,
- future employment,
- or wage outcomes for low-experience workers.

### Diagnose
Ask:
- what exactly changed: assignment, information, or both?
- does the paper identify the marginal worker helped by better information?
- is the main effect employer learning, platform-mediated visibility, or both?
- what is the welfare object: worker employment, match quality, or market surplus?

### Transfer
Apply the same logic to another platform-rule setting:
- recommendation ranking,
- guarantees,
- salary disclosure,
- or minimum posted wages.

### Challenge / extension anchor
Use **Barach and Horton (2019)** for platform steering and guarantees, or **Horton, Johari, and Kircher (2024)** for employer messages, targeted search, and wage bids [@barachHorton2019steering; @hortonJohariKircher2024sorting].

5. How would you distinguish the platform’s objective from workers’ welfare in an empirical design?
