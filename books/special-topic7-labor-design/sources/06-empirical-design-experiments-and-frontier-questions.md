## Opening orientation

This final week turns the course into a research architecture. By now, students have seen that labor-market design affects allocation through matching rules, recruiting processes, contracts, platform policies, and public-sector staffing systems. The capstone task is to convert those institutional details into empirical designs that isolate mechanisms, state credible counterfactuals, and speak to welfare.

```{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Labor-market design research should begin with a mechanism: what margin does the rule or intervention actually move?
- Credible evaluation requires a clear counterfactual, an observable labor-market object, and a design that separates direct effects from equilibrium response.
- Experiments, audits, institutional pilots, administrative data, platform data, and structural models are complementary rather than competing approaches.
- A strong applied paper in this field translates matching or contract theory into an empirically measurable labor object such as assignment quality, hiring timing, effort, retention, wages, or worker welfare.
```

## Bridge

The earlier weeks of this course moved from matching design to recruiting frictions, contract design, platform rules, and structured public or professional systems. This final lecture asks how to evaluate those institutions credibly. The key discipline is to keep labor outcomes visible: the object is not “does the mechanism work?” in the abstract, but rather what it does to hiring, assignment, effort, retention, wages, training, worker-firm sorting, staffing, and welfare.

## Field Core

### Mechanism-first empirical design

A labor-market design project should begin by naming the mechanism under study. Examples include:
- a deferred-acceptance algorithm that changes match stability,
- a timing rule that alters offer pressure and congestion,
- a contract that changes effort incentives or screening,
- a platform rule that changes visibility or wage posting,
- a public assignment rule that changes staffing and career formation.

The empirical question should then be tied to a labor object:
- match quality,
- assignment efficiency,
- worker welfare,
- employer fill rates,
- effort/productivity,
- promotion,
- turnover,
- wages,
- public-service staffing.

A useful discipline is to write the object as:
```{math}
\text{Observed labor outcome}
=
\text{direct design effect}
+
\text{behavioral response}
+
\text{equilibrium adjustment}.
```

### Counterfactuals and welfare

The central counterfactual in design research is often subtle. In a matching market, the relevant comparison may be a different mechanism, a different timing rule, or a decentralized process. In a labor-contract setting, the counterfactual may be a different information regime, a different monitoring technology, or a different wage/bonus rule. In platform markets, the comparison may be a change in ranking, pricing, or disclosure.

The welfare object is also crucial. A change that improves stability may lower wages; a change that speeds recruiting may worsen worker fit; a transparency rule may improve perceived fairness while changing who captures rents. Design research therefore needs to specify:
- whose welfare is being measured,
- whether the design changes surplus or merely redistributes it,
- whether any measured improvement is robust once strategic or equilibrium responses are allowed.

### Experiments, audits, and institutional pilots

Labor-market design is unusually well suited to experimental and quasi-experimental work because institutions often change through explicit rule changes. Key empirical approaches include:
- field experiments on offers, wage rules, information, or visibility,
- randomized platform A/B tests,
- audit-style interventions that vary information or rules,
- natural experiments generated by policy or institutional pilots,
- staggered or rule-based reforms in centralized assignment systems.

These designs are particularly valuable when they move a well-defined mechanism directly. The challenge is that even apparently local changes can create spillovers through congestion, applicant reallocation, employer response, and general-equilibrium sorting.

### Administrative data, platform data, and structural models

This field increasingly uses administrative or platform data that allow researchers to observe the full recruiting or assignment process rather than only realized matches. Such data can reveal:
- application sets,
- interview or shortlist margins,
- offer timing,
- waitlist dynamics,
- reassignment,
- contract terms,
- post-match performance and attrition.

Structural matching models become particularly useful when:
- preferences are only partially observed,
- welfare depends on unobserved counterfactual matches,
- policy changes alter strategic behavior,
- or market-clearing constraints matter.

The main practical lesson is not that every question requires a structural model. It is that the empirical design must match the institutional object. Reduced-form designs are often best for local mechanisms; structural designs become more valuable when the object of interest is matching quality, counterfactual assignment, or market-wide welfare.

### A research architecture for this field

A strong labor-market design paper usually has five layers:

1. **Institutional mechanism**  
   Which rule, contract, or intermediary design is changing?

2. **Labor-market object**  
   What margin should move if the theory is right?

3. **Empirical leverage**  
   Where does exogenous or quasi-exogenous variation come from?

4. **Equilibrium diagnosis**  
   What spillovers or reallocations could contaminate a naive estimate?

5. **Welfare interpretation**  
   Does the intervention improve allocation, change bargaining power, or simply move rents?

### Where the literature is and where it is going

The literature is strongest where institutions create clear variation and rich data:
- centralized professional matches,
- online labor platforms,
- experiments on wage posting and information,
- internal assignment markets,
- public-sector staffing systems.

The frontier is now moving toward:
- within-vacancy and within-firm margins,
- ranking and visibility algorithms,
- AI-mediated screening and recommendation,
- richer welfare analysis under heterogeneity,
- portability of results across institutions and countries,
- and interaction between design rules and labor-market power.

The broad lesson is that labor-market design is increasingly an applied empirical field with a strong theory backbone, rather than a purely theoretical branch of market design.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper

Use **Agarwal, “An Empirical Model of the Medical Match”** [@agarwal2015] as the primary anchor. It is a good capstone paper because it shows how a formal matching model can be estimated using observed matches and then used for policy counterfactuals.

### Challenge / extension anchor

Use **Davis, Greenberg, and Jones on deferred acceptance in Army officer labor markets** [@davisGreenbergJones2026] as the challenge paper. It extends the design conversation to internal labor markets and experimental evaluation of assignment mechanisms in a structured labor setting.

### Reproduce

Reproduce one central object from the primary paper:
- the structure of observed matches,
- a preference or assignment statistic,
- or a welfare-relevant comparison implied by the model.

The goal is not to rebuild the entire structural system, but to understand what data object is doing the identification work.

### Diagnose

Ask:
- what variation identifies the result?
- what assumptions make the welfare claim possible?
- which preferences, priorities, or strategic responses are left unobserved?
- what happens if equilibrium spillovers extend beyond the observed market?

### Transfer

Translate the design logic to a new setting:
- a teacher assignment reform,
- a platform ranking change,
- a public internship allocation system,
- a recruiting timing intervention,
- or a structured internal assignment market.

The transfer exercise should state:
1. the institutional rule,
2. the labor-market object,
3. the comparison group or counterfactual,
4. the likely spillovers,
5. the welfare object.

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. Pick one design intervention from the course and write down:
   - the mechanism,
   - the labor object,
   - the counterfactual,
   - the likely spillovers,
   - and the welfare object.

2. Compare a reduced-form design and a structural design for the same institutional question. What does each identify, and what does each miss?

3. Suppose a platform changes ranking, visibility, and pricing simultaneously. Which labor outcomes could be measured directly, and which would require stronger assumptions?

4. Design a research project that studies one of:
   - exploding offers,
   - pay transparency,
   - internal assignment,
   - or public-sector matching.
   What data would you need?

## Bridge Forward

This compact course should leave students able to translate labor-market design theory into an applied labor paper. The main frontier question is no longer whether institutions matter, but how to measure what they do, for whom, and under what design or market environment.
