# Week 2. Recruiting, Congestion, Timing, and Unraveling

## Opening orientation

This week studies labor-market inefficiency that arises **inside the recruiting process itself**. The key idea is that decentralized hiring markets can fail even when workers and firms are fundamentally well matched, because applications, interviews, signals, and offers are organized poorly. In many markets the main bottleneck is not vacancy posting alone, but the chain from posted vacancy to screened applicant pool to interview slot to offer to accepted hire.

```{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Recruiting frictions often operate through **within-vacancy margins**: applicant pools, screening standards, interview bottlenecks, wage information, offer timing, and vacancy yields.
- Congestion and unraveling are labor-market design problems, not just equilibrium curiosities.
- Centralization, timing rules, wage disclosure, and application-cost design can change match quality, fairness, and welfare.
- A strong empirical paper in this area maps a specific design failure to an observed recruiting margin, a measurable counterfactual, and a labor-market welfare object.
```

## Bridge

Week 1 introduced centralized matching as a response to decentralized frictions. This week explains why some markets become chaotic or prematurely fast in the first place. The key bridge is that instability in labor allocation can arise not only from preferences and priorities, but also from the **timing and organization of recruitment**.

## Field Core

### 1. Recruiting as a labor-market design problem

A decentralized labor market can fail at several distinct stages:
1. workers choose where and when to apply;
2. firms screen large or noisy applicant pools;
3. interviews and callbacks are capacity-constrained;
4. offers can be simultaneous, sequential, or exploding;
5. firms and workers act strategically in anticipation of others doing the same.

This sequence implies that “matching frictions” are often not one object. They may reflect:
- too many applications per vacancy,
- too little directed search,
- too many low-value applications,
- interview congestion,
- offer timing failures,
- wage opacity,
- or strategic acceleration of hiring.

### 2. Congestion and within-vacancy margins

Traditional vacancy-based models often treat the vacancy as a black box, but many modern empirical papers open that box. A vacancy can differ along at least six margins:
- number of applicants,
- applicant composition,
- interview slots,
- hiring standards,
- wage generosity / disclosed wage information,
- conversion from applicants to interviews to hires.

This is why vacancy yields and recruiting intensity matter. Establishment-level evidence shows that hiring speed is not pinned down by vacancy counts alone: firms also vary search effort, standards, and recruiting policies [@davisFabermanHaltiwanger2013; @carrilloTudelaMenzioVisschers2023]. Online job-board evidence shows that applicant behavior responds to wage information and other posting characteristics, so within-vacancy allocation is itself a meaningful design object [@banfiVillenaRoldan2019; @balgovaTekleselassieHenselWitte2025].

### 3. Timing, exploding offers, and unraveling

Markets unravel when transactions happen inefficiently early. In labor markets, this can arise when firms fear missing top candidates, when workers accept early offers to insure against risk, and when later information about fit or quality cannot be waited for without losing opportunities. Classic labor examples include medical fellowship markets, clerkships, psychologists, and elite law hiring [@rothXing1994; @niederleRoth2003; @roth2012timing].

Unraveling matters for labor economics because it changes:
- who gets to wait for better information,
- how much uncertainty workers absorb,
- whether lower-information matches are formed,
- whether market thickness is preserved or destroyed,
- and whether access to early information becomes a source of inequality.

### 4. Centralized versus decentralized responses

Different labor markets respond differently:
- some centralize matching;
- some impose timing rules;
- some rely on norms;
- some remain decentralized but adjust information or recruiting rules;
- some use quasi-centralized hybrids.

The welfare comparison is not only “centralized good, decentralized bad.” Centralization can improve timing and reduce congestion, but it can also restrict learning, compress flexibility, or require preference reporting that is itself noisy. The key applied question is therefore: **which frictive margin is actually being fixed?**

### 5. Descriptive and empirical evidence on timing failures

The classic professional-market literature is still essential because it shows that recruiting chaos can be observed institutionally, even before modern administrative data. Gastroenterology fellowships provide a particularly useful case because the market operated both with and without a match, allowing evidence on timing, offer structure, and placement outcomes [@niederleRoth2007]. Roth’s later discussion of lawyers and other professional markets is especially useful as a descriptive labor-market design reference [@roth2012timing].

Modern recruiting data complement that literature by making the within-vacancy margins visible:
- applicant flows,
- vacancy yields,
- wage information,
- offer speed,
- and application congestion [@davisFabermanHaltiwanger2013; @carrilloTudelaMenzioVisschers2023; @banfiVillenaRoldan2019; @balgovaTekleselassieHenselWitte2025].

### 6. From theory to empirical design

A good applied labor-design paper in this area should make five choices explicit:

#### (i) What is the frictive object?
Examples:
- congestion,
- interview bottlenecks,
- exploding offers,
- wage opacity,
- early contracting,
- queueing at attractive jobs.

#### (ii) Which observable margin captures it?
Examples:
- applications per vacancy,
- vacancy yield,
- interview-to-offer ratio,
- vacancy duration,
- timing of offers,
- acceptance timing,
- mismatch between applicant quality and eventual hires.

#### (iii) What is the counterfactual?
Examples:
- centralized clearing,
- delayed offer regime,
- wage-disclosure regime,
- application cap,
- token/queue system,
- recruiting-policy change.

#### (iv) What data reveal the mechanism?
Examples:
- professional-market historical records,
- vacancy/posting data,
- employer administrative recruiting data,
- applications and interview logs,
- offer-timing data,
- platform experiments.

#### (v) What welfare object matters?
Examples:
- match quality,
- speed of hiring,
- worker risk,
- fairness of access,
- congestion externalities,
- allocative efficiency.

### 7. Research architecture

This week naturally supports at least four applied project types:

1. **Process-mapping papers**  
   Open the black box between vacancy and hire.

2. **Timing-regime papers**  
   Study exploding offers, deadlines, and early contracting.

3. **Information-design papers**  
   Wage disclosure, application guidance, queue visibility, or ranking disclosure.

4. **Institutional redesign papers**  
   Compare centralized, partially centralized, and decentralized designs.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper
**Carrillo-Tudela, Menzio, and Visschers (2023)** on recruitment policies, job-filling rates, and matching efficiency is the primary anchor because it directly opens the vacancy black box and makes recruiting intensity observable [@carrilloTudelaMenzioVisschers2023].

### Reproduce
Recreate one reduced fact about within-vacancy margins:
- vacancy yield,
- recruiting intensity,
- or the relation between hiring speed and recruiting policy.

### Diagnose
Ask:
- Which part of the hiring process is actually changing?
- Is the main action wage setting, standards, or effort?
- What is observed, and what remains latent inside the recruiting funnel?
- Does the design identify the effect of a recruiting policy, or only correlate it with hiring outcomes?

### Transfer
Apply the same logic to a bounded decentralized-hiring setting:
- application congestion on a platform,
- wage disclosure in postings,
- interview-slot scarcity,
- or timing rules in an early-career labor market.

### Challenge / extension anchor
Use **Niederle and Roth (2007)** or **Roth and Xing (1994)** as the timing / unraveling contrast:
- move from vacancy-funnel inefficiency to offer-timing inefficiency,
- compare centralized vs decentralized resolution of labor-market frictions [@niederleRoth2007; @rothXing1994].
