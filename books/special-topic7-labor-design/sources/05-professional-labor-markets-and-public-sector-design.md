# Professional Labor Markets and Public-Sector Design

## Opening orientation

This week studies professional and public-sector labor markets as design laboratories. These markets are especially useful because wages are often compressed, service obligations are explicit, priorities are formalized, and staffing quality is jointly valued by workers, organizations, and the public. That makes them unusually transparent settings for seeing how assignment rules, timing rules, career ladders, and institutional constraints shape labor allocation.

```{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Professional and public-sector labor markets reveal design problems that are often hidden in decentralized private markets: fairness, transparency, local staffing needs, and career formation.
- The same design can perform very differently depending on outside options, wage rigidity, service obligations, and training pipelines.
- Internal job ladders, promotions, and training are part of labor-market design, not merely downstream personnel details.
- Public-sector entry and exit decisions change the effective design environment by altering participation, selection, and retention.
```

## Field Core

### Professional and public-sector markets as labor-design environments

Residency matches, teacher placement, civil-service recruitment, and military branching are labor markets in which decentralized bargaining is constrained by training pipelines, public missions, and fairness requirements. They are often thick enough to benefit from explicit clearing rules, yet they also face political and organizational constraints that make simple efficiency benchmarks incomplete.

The natural benchmark is a matching or priority system that trades off:
- efficiency in worker–job fit,
- fairness and transparency,
- staffing in underserved places or units,
- career development and retention,
- legal or public-service obligations.

### Centralized assignment, priorities, and institutional objectives

Centralized matching can improve coordination, reduce justified envy, and make preferences more truthful, but its performance depends on what preferences and priorities actually encode. In labor markets with wage rigidity or public-service obligations, assignment rules also substitute for price signals. This makes priority design especially important.

Medical residency and military assignment systems are useful contrasts. The residency match shows how centralized design can stabilize an unraveling market. The Army/West Point setting shows how assignment with contracts, service commitments, and organizational objectives changes both the design problem and the outcome metrics [@rothPeranson1999; @sonmezSwitzer2013; @greenbergPathakSonmez2021; @davisGreenbergJones2026].

### Teacher and public-service allocation

Teacher labor markets and other public-service markets add location-specific staffing needs, wage compression, and policy goals such as equal access. Here, design choices influence not just match quality but geographic inequality, retention, and public-service delivery. Assignment rules interact with worker preferences over place, career ladders, and work conditions.

Public-sector recruiting can also be designed through contracts. Performance-pay recruitment evidence from Rwanda is especially useful because it separates selection from effort, showing how contract design changes who enters, how they perform, and whether they stay [@leaverOzierSerneelsSabarwal2021].

### Internal ladders, training, and on-the-job search

Professional labor markets do not end at initial assignment. Workers move inside organizations, climb internal ladders, acquire training, and sometimes search on the job. These margins are part of market design because initial placements determine later opportunities, and because organizations can choose rules that facilitate or restrict internal mobility.

The internal-labor-market evidence from the U.S. Army and related work on internal talent markets shows how assignment algorithms, managerial discretion, and organizational rules influence later promotions, retention, and career progression [@davisGreenbergJones2026; @cowgillDavisMontagnesPerkowski2025]. More generally, job-ladder logic and on-the-job search matter because training and match quality unfold dynamically rather than at one hiring instant.

### In and out of the public sector

Public-sector design cannot be studied in isolation from outside options. Public jobs compete with private jobs, and public rules affect selection into application, entry, retention, and exit. Wage compression, pensions, security, mission value, and promotion rules all affect who enters and who leaves. This makes public/private transitions a mechanism-design issue: the design of the public-sector career affects both participation and sorting.

The comparative evidence on public-sector wage-setting and recruitment suggests that public-sector design changes labor supply not just at the hiring margin but across the life cycle of employment [@fontaineLobelRoux2020; @chassamboulliPavlopoulou2023].

### Research architecture

A good applied paper in this area starts from a design friction and then asks which labor margin it changes.

Typical design frictions:
- timing and congestion,
- weak preference revelation,
- poorly aligned priorities,
- opaque internal assignment,
- compressed or rigid pay,
- service obligations,
- insufficient incentives for underserved locations,
- weak internal mobility or training rules.

Typical labor outcomes:
- match quality,
- retention,
- promotions,
- staffing quality,
- geographic allocation,
- worker welfare,
- public-service output.

This is why professional/public-sector design belongs in labor economics: the core objects are labor allocation, career formation, and worker–organization surplus, not design for its own sake.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor

**Primary anchor:** Davis, Greenberg, and Jones, *An Experimental Evaluation of Deferred Acceptance: Evidence from Over 100 Army Officer Labor Markets* [@davisGreenbergJones2026].

### Reproduce

Reproduce a reduced version of the market-level comparison between deferred-acceptance assignment and manager-directed assignment. The target is a small, bounded teaching exercise around match quality, preference reporting, or administrative burden.

### Diagnose

Diagnose what the design actually identifies:
- the mechanism is a market-level institutional change,
- internal communication and strategic coordination can attenuate design benefits,
- assignment design is interacting with internal career structures, not just one-shot matching.

### Transfer

Transfer the logic to a different structured labor market such as teacher placement, public-service recruitment, or another professional assignment market. Ask which ingredients are essential for a centralized design to improve outcomes:
- thick enough market?
- measurable preferences?
- meaningful priorities?
- rigid wages?
- service obligations?
- staffing goals?

### Extension anchor

**Challenge / extension anchor:** Davis, *Labor Market Design Can Improve Match Outcomes: Evidence from Matching Teach For America Teachers to Schools* [@davis2024tfa].

Use it to compare external assignment into a public-service role with internal reassignment inside a large organization.

5. What evidence would convince you that a design reform improved welfare rather than only administrative order?
