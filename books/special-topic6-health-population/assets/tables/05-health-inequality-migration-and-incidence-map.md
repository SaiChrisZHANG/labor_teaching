## Health inequality, migration, and labor-market incidence

| Question | Why it matters for labor economists | Representative objects |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Who is more exposed to disease or environmental risk? | Exposure is rarely random across occupations, neighborhoods, race, wealth, or migrant status | frontline jobs, crowded housing, poor transit access, unsafe worksites |
| Who can adjust through migration or commuting? | Adjustment capacity determines whether health shocks translate into job loss, wage loss, or immobility | out-migration, in-commuting, remote work, retirement, job switching |
| Who bears the welfare loss when nominal wages do not move? | Health shocks can reduce the real value of work through fatigue, risk, and unsafe amenities | absenteeism, presenteeism, job quality, stress, housing costs |
| How do firm and local responses amplify inequality? | Firms and places adapt unevenly, creating persistent differences in job access and recovery | automation, shift redesign, closures, rent changes, local demand shifts |
| How do Global South settings differ? | Informality, weak insurance, crowding, and public-health capacity change both exposure and adjustment | HIV/AIDS, pollution, epidemic response, migrant vulnerability, labor informality |

Research implication: health inequality is not just a health outcome. It is a labor-market incidence and mobility problem.
