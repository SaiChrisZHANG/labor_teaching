## Historical methods and data sources

| Design family | What it identifies well | Common settings | Main threats | Common data |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cohort-by-place epidemic exposure | medium- and long-run labor effects of historical health shocks | plague, TB, influenza, HIV/AIDS, early public-health campaigns | omitted local trends, survival selection, migration | historical census, mortality records, archival public-health data |
| Event studies around onset/intensity shocks | short-run labor-market disruption and recovery | COVID, plant/region outbreaks, pollution spikes, heat waves | concurrent policy shocks, measurement breaks, dynamic composition | admin earnings, UI, payroll, mobility, private-sector real-time data |
| Exposure linked to environment | productivity and labor-supply effects | pollution, wildfire smoke, heat, toxic release | avoidance behavior, endogenous residence, correlated local conditions | pollution monitors, satellite/weather data, payroll/attendance data |
| Demographic-structure quasi-experiments | effects of aging or fertility shifts on labor markets and automation | regional aging, cohort fertility decline | migration endogeneity, endogenous technology adoption | census/ACS, admin retirement records, regional labor panels |
| Migration / sorting analyses | who can escape or absorb health shocks | COVID migration, pollution exposure, aging regions | selection on wealth, amenities, remote-work options | IRS migration, ACS/Census, cellphone mobility, commuting flows |

Good design principle: define whether the shock is health, environment, demography, or policy; then define whether the main response margin is work capacity, firm behavior, migration, or welfare.
