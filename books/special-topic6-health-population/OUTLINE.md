# Labor Market, Health, and Population

## Course role

Labor Market, Health, and Population is a 6-week advanced elective on health, demography, and labor-market outcomes over the life cycle.

## Weekly outline

### Week 1. Health capital, work capacity, and labor supply

**Central question:** How should labor economists model health as both a productive asset and a constraint on work?

The opening week introduces health capital, work capacity, and dynamic labor supply. It sets up the main measurement challenge of the course: health is both economically consequential and difficult to observe cleanly.

### Week 2. Early-life conditions and adult labor outcomes

**Central question:** How do childhood health and developmental conditions shape labor-market outcomes decades later?

This week focuses on fetal origins, early-life shocks, and human-capital development. Students study how early health conditions enter adult labor outcomes through education, cognition, physical capacity, and family resources.

### Week 3. Disability, mental health, and labor-market attachment

**Central question:** How do disability and mental health affect work capacity, labor-force attachment, and policy dependence?

The course turns to health limitations during working age, with attention to stigma, diagnosis, program interaction, and dynamic exit from employment. The key issue is how to separate health effects from labor-market conditions and institutional responses.

### Week 4. Fertility, mortality, aging, and population structure

**Central question:** How do demographic transitions reshape labor supply, care burdens, and labor-market composition?

Students study fertility decline, aging, longevity, mortality shocks, and dependency structure. This week emphasizes how population change alters labor markets through both composition and behavioral responses.

### Week 5. Insurance, healthcare policy, and labor-market incidence

**Central question:** How do health insurance systems and healthcare policy affect labor-market choices?

The focus is on employer-provided insurance, public coverage, job lock, take-up, and the interaction between protection and incentives. Students are asked to think about labor-market incidence rather than treating health policy as separate from work.

### Week 6. Research design and frontier questions

**Central question:** What are the strongest empirical strategies for studying health, population, and labor together?

The final week synthesizes the course around timing, dynamic selection, program interaction, and long-run incidence. Students leave with a clear map of the frontier questions at the intersection of labor, health, and demography.
