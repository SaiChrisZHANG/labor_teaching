# Labor Market, Health, and Population

## Course role

Labor Market, Health, and Population is a 6-week compact advanced elective on how health risks and demographic structure shape labor supply, job choice, productivity, mobility, wages, worker welfare, firm behavior, and labor-market adjustment. It is a labor-economics course first: every week begins from work, wages, employment, job quality, productivity, household labor allocation, firm response, or worker welfare.

## Cumulative logic

The course sequence is deliberately cumulative:

1. define health and population as labor-market objects rather than background characteristics;
2. study working-age health limitations and their effects on labor supply, job choice, and program interaction;
3. add fertility, mortality, caregiving, aging, and lifecycle allocation;
4. move inside workplaces through mental health, stress, productivity, and worker welfare;
5. scale up to disease exposure, environmental health, demographic change, and adjustment;
6. end with frontier research design.

The course should not become a generic health-economics, epidemiology, or demography survey. Health, disease, fertility, mortality, caregiving, and aging enter only when they change labor supply, labor demand, productivity, mobility, job amenities, wages, allocation, inequality, or worker welfare.

## Internal roadmap

- **Foundational:** Week 1
- **Worker-health and labor-supply focused:** Week 2
- **Lifecycle and population focused:** Week 3
- **Workplace and productivity focused:** Week 4
- **Aggregate and demographic-adjustment focused:** Week 5
- **Synthesis and research-design oriented:** Week 6

## Weekly outline

### Week 1. Health, Risk, and Labor Markets: Core Frameworks and Measurement

**Central question:** How should labor economists model and measure health when it affects both productivity and the feasible set for work?

The opening week defines health as human capital, work capacity, risk, and a source of labor-market heterogeneity. Students study the measurement problems that make this area difficult: self-reported health, diagnosis, disability status, mortality risk, insurance coverage, omitted socioeconomic status, reverse causality, and dynamic selection. The week sets up the core map for the rest of the course: health changes labor supply, job choice, productivity, mobility, wages, and welfare through both worker-side and firm-side channels.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Health matters here because it changes whether people can work, how much they can work, which jobs are feasible, how productive they are, and how wages and job amenities should be interpreted.

### Week 2. Disability, Chronic Conditions, Labor Supply, and Job Choice

**Central question:** How do disability and chronic conditions reshape labor-force attachment, hours, occupation, accommodations, and exit?

This week studies working-age health limitations as labor-market constraints. The scope includes disability insurance, chronic illness, program interaction, workplace accommodations, job amenities, job lock, occupational sorting, employer response, and dynamic exit from employment. Students learn to separate the direct effect of health on work capacity from measurement error, program incentives, local labor-demand conditions, and selection into benefit receipt.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Disability and chronic conditions alter labor supply, reservation wages, job matching, workplace accommodation, program take-up, and the boundary between employment and nonemployment.

### Week 3. Fertility, Mortality, Caregiving, and Lifecycle Labor Allocation

**Central question:** How do fertility timing, mortality risk, aging, and caregiving burdens reshape labor allocation over the life cycle?

The third week moves from individual health limitations to household and lifecycle allocation. Students study fertility timing, child penalties, family labor-supply responses to severe health shocks, informal caregiving, spousal mortality, aging, retirement timing, and the interaction between care burdens and career dynamics. The emphasis is on timing and persistence: health and demographic events can alter current hours, future wages, occupation, savings, mobility, and household specialization.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Fertility, mortality, caregiving, and aging determine who works, when they work, which jobs remain feasible, how earnings trajectories evolve, and how families insure labor-market risk.

### Week 4. Mental Health, Stress, Workplace Productivity, and Worker Welfare

**Central question:** How do mental health and stress affect productivity, job quality, retention, and welfare inside labor markets?

This week treats mental health as a labor-market force rather than as an individual residual. Students study depression, anxiety, stress, pain, substance use, absenteeism, presenteeism, supervision, workplace conditions, job control, safety, stigma, and the measurement of productivity losses. The week connects Labor I's worker welfare and job amenities to Labor II's personnel economics by asking how firms observe, accommodate, or amplify mental-health constraints.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Mental health and stress change effort, attendance, job search, productivity, retention, management, workplace amenities, and the welfare value of employment.

### Week 5. Disease Exposure, Environmental Health, Demographic Change, and Labor-Market Adjustment

**Central question:** How do disease, environmental exposure, and demographic structure reshape workers, firms, local labor markets, and aggregate adjustment?

The fifth week scales the course up. Students study infectious disease, pollution, heat, workplace exposure, mortality shocks, population aging, fertility decline, dependency structure, and demographic composition as labor-market shocks. The focus is adjustment: workers may change hours, jobs, mobility, or retirement; firms may alter scheduling, technology, hiring, or accommodations; local and aggregate labor markets may adjust through wages, productivity, sectoral composition, migration, and automation.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Disease, environmental exposure, and demographic change matter because they shift labor supply, labor demand, productivity, firm organization, local adjustment, wages, and inequality.

### Week 6. Synthesis, Frontier Questions, and Student Research Designs

**Central question:** What does a credible research design on health, population, and labor markets look like?

The closing week integrates the course into research design. Students choose a labor outcome, identify the health or population mechanism, specify the worker, household, firm, or aggregate margin, define timing and exposure, select a comparison group or counterfactual, and state the welfare object. Possible applications include disability policy, workplace accommodations, mental-health interventions, caregiving shocks, disease exposure, environmental risk, population aging, fertility timing, or firm responses to health constraints.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** The course succeeds when health and population questions become research designs about work, wages, productivity, mobility, allocation, inequality, firm response, and worker welfare.
