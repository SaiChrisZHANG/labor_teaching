# Week 2. Disability, Chronic Conditions, Labor Supply, and Job Choice

**Central question:** How do disability and chronic conditions reshape labor-force attachment, hours, occupation, accommodations, and exit?

## Opening orientation

This week studies disability and chronic conditions as labor-market constraints that unfold over time rather than as static worker characteristics. A disability onset or chronic diagnosis can change work capacity immediately, but it can also generate slower adjustments through workplace accommodation, occupational downgrading, benefit applications, insurance coverage, caregiving needs, and expectations about future decline. The same condition may therefore have small short-run employment effects and large long-run earnings or job-quality consequences, or vice versa.

## Core points

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Disability and chronic conditions reshape labor outcomes through work capacity, reservation wages, job feasibility, accommodations, and program incentives [@meyerMok2019; @collischonHiesingerPohlan2023].
- Short-run effects and long-run effects can look very different: acute shocks may cause immediate disruption, while chronic conditions can accumulate through persistent job loss, occupational downgrading, and welfare losses [@dobkinFinkelsteinKluenderNotowidigdo2018; @garciaGomezJonesRice2013].
- Treatment, accommodation, and return-to-work investments can change labor-market decisions, so reduced-form disability effects do not necessarily reveal immutable health constraints [@hillMaestasMullen2016; @zaresani2018].
- Disability and chronic conditions may generate monopsony power through job lock, accommodation dependence, local scarcity of feasible jobs, and higher switching/search costs [@madrian1994; @ameriSchurAdyaBentleyEtAl2018].
- Credible causal work in this area often relies on event studies, program thresholds, administrative onset designs, treatment-access changes, twin/sibling designs, or genetic approaches, each with distinct assumptions and limits [@bound1991; @bockermanCawley2018; @vonHinkeKesslerScholder2016].
:::

## Bridge

Week 1 established health as work capacity, risk, and a measurement problem. Week 2 narrows the focus to disability and chronic conditions, where the same health state can interact with labor supply, workplace accommodation, benefit rules, employer behavior, and local labor-demand conditions. The main empirical discipline is to keep these channels separate.

## Field Core

### Disability and chronic conditions as dynamic labor-market constraints

Disability and chronic conditions alter labor-market behavior through several margins at once: they can reduce hours or productivity directly, change the set of tolerable tasks and schedules, raise the value of insurance or workplace flexibility, and alter the expected value of remaining employed versus applying for benefits. Chronic conditions in particular are dynamic: workers may remain attached initially but gradually reallocate into different jobs, reduce hours, or exit employment over time [@meyerMok2019; @currieMadrian1999].

### Short-term versus long-term effects

One of the key distinctions in this literature is between short-run disruption and long-run incidence. Acute health shocks such as hospital admissions often produce immediate reductions in earnings, work, and credit access, but longer-run paths depend on insurance, employer quality, household responses, and the severity/persistence of the condition [@dobkinFinkelsteinKluenderNotowidigdo2018; @garciaGomezJonesRice2013]. Chronic and severe work-limiting conditions can produce much larger persistent losses in earnings, income, and consumption than average disability measures suggest [@meyerMok2019].

A central research question is therefore not just “what is the effect?” but: **on what horizon, for which condition, and through which labor margin?**

### Treatment, accommodation, and return-to-work investments

Labor-market consequences are not fixed; they depend on how workers, employers, and programs respond. Employer accommodation can delay labor-force exit and reduce disability claiming, but accommodation itself is unequally supplied and often endogenous to worker and firm characteristics [@hillMaestasMullen2016]. Treatment access and return-to-work policies can likewise change both reported disability and actual work outcomes, which means that treatment investment is part of the labor response, not outside it [@zaresani2018; @frenchSong2014].

This makes treatment and accommodation especially useful for research design: they help distinguish work-capacity effects from institutional or organizational frictions.

### Disability, chronic conditions, and monopsony power

A labor-market perspective also implies that disability and chronic conditions may increase employer power. Several mechanisms can make labor supply to the current employer or local labor market more inelastic:

- dependence on employer-provided insurance or accommodations,
- reduced ability to search or commute,
- a thinner set of feasible jobs,
- uncertainty about employer acceptance of accommodations,
- stigma or employer beliefs about future productivity,
- and local scarcity of jobs compatible with chronic conditions or disability.

This is a natural monopsony setting even without explicit concentration. Workers with disability-related constraints may face higher switching costs and lower outside options, allowing employers to extract rents or shape contracts in ways that would not arise for otherwise similar workers without those constraints [@madrian1994; @ameriSchurAdyaBentleyEtAl2018]. A useful way to teach this is to treat disability as changing the elasticity of labor supply to firms and feasible occupations, not only the level of labor supply.

### Frontier methods and identification

Causal identification is especially difficult here because health, work, benefits, and accommodation are jointly determined. A frontier methods view helps students see the main design families:

- **Administrative onset/event-study designs:** compare workers before and after newly recognized disability status or acute health shocks [@collischonHiesingerPohlan2023; @dobkinFinkelsteinKluenderNotowidigdo2018].
- **Program thresholds and rule-based designs:** exploit disability-insurance decisions, reassessments, or return-to-work reforms [@frenchSong2014; @zaresani2018].
- **Treatment-access or care reforms:** identify how accommodations, treatment, or insurance alter work outcomes.
- **Twin/sibling comparisons:** help net out family background and latent health endowments in related health–labor settings.
- **Genetic / Mendelian-randomization designs:** increasingly used to study chronic-condition channels such as obesity and health-related labor outcomes, though they require strong assumptions and careful interpretation [@bockermanCawley2018; @vonHinkeKesslerScholder2016].
- **Matched employer–employee or firm-heterogeneity designs:** identify whether some firms retain or accommodate health-impaired workers better than others [@hillMaestasMullen2016; @biroGerardGyetvaiPapp2024].

A good design in this area should say clearly: (i) what health object is being measured, (ii) what labor margin is the outcome, (iii) what generates quasi-random variation, and (iv) why the design identifies health effects rather than program, firm, or family responses.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper

Use **Meyer and Mok (2019)** as the primary anchor for reproduction and diagnosis. It is especially useful because it makes long-run incidence visible and separates chronic/severe disability from average disability outcomes [@meyerMok2019].

### Challenge / extension anchor

Use **Hill, Maestas, and Mullen (2016)** as the challenge anchor. It is useful because it shows that accommodation changes labor-force exit and SSDI claiming, which immediately raises questions about treatment, employer heterogeneity, and dynamic incidence [@hillMaestasMullen2016].

### Reproduce → Diagnose → Transfer

**Reproduce**
- Recover one bounded disability-onset or chronic-condition event-study pattern for employment, earnings, or hours.
- Show how outcomes evolve over several horizons rather than at a single point in time.

**Diagnose**
- Ask whether the design identifies health effects, program effects, or employer responses.
- Diagnose what would be different if the key margin were accommodations or benefit receipt.
- Explain whether the observed path is short-run disruption or long-run scarring.

**Transfer**
- Apply the same logic to one bounded alternative setting:
  - hospital admission,
  - disability-status onset,
  - employer accommodation,
  - or treatment/return-to-work reform.
- Keep the labor object fixed while changing the identification strategy.

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. Why can the same chronic condition have small short-run employment effects but large long-run earnings losses?
2. In what sense are accommodation and treatment part of the labor-market response rather than separate from it?
3. How can disability or chronic illness generate monopsony power even in the absence of obvious employer concentration?
4. Compare an acute health-shock event study with a disability-program threshold design. What does each identify, and what does each miss?
5. Why should a labor economist care whether a health-related wage loss comes from productivity, job sorting, accommodations, or employer power?

## Reproducibility And Code Lab Note

The Week 2 lab should follow the standard **Reproduce → Diagnose → Transfer** structure and prioritize a bounded teaching path. A strong pedagogical design would compare a long-run disability-onset profile with a shorter-horizon acute-shock design or accommodation design, so that students learn to distinguish timing, measurement, and institutional channels.
