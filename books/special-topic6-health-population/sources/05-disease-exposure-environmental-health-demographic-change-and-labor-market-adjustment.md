# Disease Exposure, Environmental Health, Demographic Change, and Labor-Market Adjustment

## Opening orientation

This week scales the course up from individual and household health constraints to shocks that move entire labor markets. Infectious disease, pollution, heat, mortality shocks, fertility decline, and population aging all reshape the feasible set for work, firm labor demand, migration, and local adjustment. The key labor-economics question is not only whether health shocks reduce work, but who bears the incidence, how quickly labor markets reallocate, and which losses persist in wages, mobility, productivity, and welfare.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Disease, environmental exposure, and demographic change affect labor markets through both worker capacity and firm/local equilibrium adjustment.
- Historical health episodes are useful labor-economics cases because they reveal short-run disruption, medium-run reallocation, and long-run distributional or institutional persistence.
- Health inequality matters because unequal exposure and unequal ability to move, insure, or adapt generate unequal labor-market incidence.
- Aging, fertility decline, and mortality structure are labor-market forces because they change labor supply, retirement, migration, automation incentives, and wage pressure.
- A good empirical design in this area separates direct health effects, policy effects, environmental exposure, and equilibrium adjustment.
:::

## Bridge

Weeks 1 to 4 treated health as work capacity, disability, lifecycle allocation, and mental-health constraints. Week 5 asks what happens when health and population forces operate at larger scales. The lecture links environmental and infectious shocks to labor demand, local adjustment, migration, and welfare, while also bringing demographic structure into the same framework.

## Field Core

### A labor-market framework for disease, environment, and demographic shocks

A useful starting point is to write labor-market adjustment as a system rather than a single reduced form. Let local employment, wages, participation, and migration depend on worker health capacity, disease and environmental exposure, demographic composition, firm labor demand, and policy response. The key empirical object is not simply whether exposure lowers employment, but whether the resulting incidence appears in wages, hours, productivity, rents, migration flows, firm exit, or technology adoption.

A useful decomposition is:
1. **worker-capacity channel**: exposure changes health, fatigue, mortality risk, or cognitive/physical performance;
2. **firm/production channel**: firms adapt through scheduling, task reallocation, capital substitution, hiring, or exit;
3. **migration/reallocation channel**: workers and households move, commute, or become trapped;
4. **distributional channel**: exposure and adjustment are unequal across skill, wealth, race, age, and geography;
5. **policy/institution channel**: insurance, public health, labor regulation, and local infrastructure mediate outcomes.

### Historical health episodes as labor-market shocks

Historical epidemics are not just historical curiosities; they are structured labor-market shocks. The Black Death is the classic case where labor scarcity, bargaining power, and institutional adaptation all move together. Jedwab, Johnson, and Koyama show that the economic effects of the plague depended on market structure, land-labor ratios, and institutions rather than operating mechanically through population loss alone [@jedwabJohnsonKoyama2022].

Early public-health campaigns against tuberculosis are also relevant because they illustrate how disease reporting, treatment, and public-health capacity can alter labor-market productivity and inequality over time. Anderson and coauthors show how reporting laws changed the spread of tuberculosis and generated downstream effects on public health capacity and human capital [@andersoncharlesreeswang2017]. In this setting, the central labor question is not only whether disease declines, but whether healthier cohorts work more, learn more, and move differently.

The HIV/AIDS literature is especially useful because it links mortality and morbidity to wages, employment, household labor allocation, and skill premia in development settings. Chicoine shows that AIDS mortality altered wages and employment in South African labor markets, illustrating both local labor-supply effects and distributional consequences [@chicoine2012]. This literature is helpful because it sits between individual health shock work and aggregate disease shocks, and it forces students to think about morbidity, mortality, caregiving, and inequality together.

### COVID in historical perspective

COVID belongs in the same labor-economics family as earlier health shocks, but it differs in the speed, policy intensity, and sectoral concentration of the shock. Albanesi and Kim show that the pandemic recession had highly uneven employment effects across occupation, gender, and family status, and they emphasize the possibility of durable labor-market change rather than a simple temporary decline [@albanesiKim2021]. Chetty and coauthors document how private-sector and administrative data can reveal fast-moving labor-market incidence and heterogeneous recovery patterns, which makes COVID a methodological turning point as well as a substantive one [@chettyfriedmanhendrenstepner2020].

A useful comparative lesson is that plague, TB, HIV/AIDS, and COVID differ in epidemiology and policy response, but they all force the same labor questions:
- does labor scarcity raise wages or does disruption dominate?
- who leaves employment and who returns?
- do firms substitute toward automation or different work organization?
- do place-based effects persist after the shock fades?
- which inequalities widen because some workers are more exposed or less mobile?

### Environmental health, productivity, and hidden labor-market incidence

Environmental exposures such as pollution and heat are labor-market shocks because they reduce worker productivity, attendance, and feasible hours even if nominal wages do not move one-for-one. Hanna and Oliva show that air pollution affects labor supply and productivity, while work by Graff Zivin and Neidell highlights how heat changes time allocation and labor effort [@hannaOliva2015pollutionLaborSupply; @graffzivinNeidell2014]. Chang et al. show that pollution reduces office-worker productivity, which is important because it demonstrates that environmental health is not only a manual-labor issue [@changGraffZivinGrossNeidell2019callCenters].

These papers are useful because they teach students to think beyond employment counts. Health and environmental shocks can generate hidden losses through presenteeism, absenteeism, reduced quality-adjusted output, or sorting into safer but lower-paying jobs. In labor-economics terms, environmental exposure changes the welfare value of wages and can generate compensating differentials, but only under strong assumptions about worker information and mobility.

### Health inequality, migration, and labor-market choice

Health inequality matters because workers are not symmetrically exposed and they are not equally able to adjust. Households with low wealth, weak insurance, poorer housing, or constrained mobility may absorb more exposure and move less. This makes migration, commuting, and local labor supply part of the health-incidence problem, not just the recovery margin. Deaton’s classic discussion of health and inequality is useful here because it pushes students to specify mechanisms rather than simply noting correlations [@deaton2003].

A labor-focused way to think about health inequality and migration is to ask:
- who can leave polluted or disease-exposed places?
- who can afford preventive housing or safer commuting?
- who is locked into risky work because of credential, language, or contract constraints?
- when do health shocks change internal or international migration decisions?

Recent evidence also shows that exposure and adjustment can differ sharply for migrants. Bossavie and coauthors document that migrant workers often face higher disease exposure and worse labor-market risks during COVID, which helps connect migration, occupational sorting, and health risk in a single framework [@bossaviedaviesgerardporter2021]. This is especially relevant for development and Global South settings where informality, crowding, and limited insurance amplify labor-market incidence.

### Demographic change, aging, and aggregate adjustment

Population aging, fertility decline, and changing dependency ratios are labor-market shocks because they alter the size and composition of labor supply, retirement, caregiving burdens, and employer incentives to automate or reorganize production. Acemoglu and Restrepo show that aging can induce automation, which means demographic change affects labor markets not only through labor supply but through endogenous technology adoption [@acemogluRestrepo2022demographicsAutomation]. Regional and cross-country work on aging further shows that adjustment can appear in wage structures, retirement behavior, migration, or job redesign rather than in simple employment losses [@bohm2021; @little2002].

Aging also brings late-life labor choices to the forefront:
- delayed retirement,
- bridge jobs,
- rehire arrangements,
- informal post-retirement work,
- and care burdens for older family members.

This is why demographic change belongs in a labor course: it changes work at both the beginning and the end of the life cycle, and it can alter firm choices over technology, scheduling, and workforce composition.

### Methods, data, and research architecture

A strong Week 5 project needs to be explicit about:
1. the **shock**: disease, pollution, heat, mortality, aging, fertility decline, or demographic composition;
2. the **margin**: hours, participation, wages, productivity, migration, retirement, automation, or welfare;
3. the **timing**: short-run disruption versus medium-run reallocation versus long-run persistence;
4. the **unit of analysis**: worker, household, firm, region, or cohort;
5. the **equilibrium adjustment**: wages, rents, migration, entry/exit, technology adoption, or public policy response.

The methods and data map for this week is especially important because the strongest papers in this literature often combine:
- linked administrative labor and health records,
- environmental exposure data,
- cohort or regional variation,
- mortality and vital statistics,
- administrative earnings and disability records,
- private-sector or real-time data in pandemic settings,
- and spatial data on migration, commuting, or local labor demand.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper

**Albanesi and Kim (2021)** is the primary anchor for reproduction because it is a clean labor-market paper on a major health shock and it highlights heterogeneity across workers, occupations, and family types [@albanesiKim2021].

### Reproduce

Reproduce a bounded labor-market fact from the pandemic adjustment literature:
- employment or participation changes by occupation or worker type,
- or a compact version of the unequal incidence pattern.

### Diagnose

Diagnose what the design captures and what it misses:
- direct health risk versus policy shutdowns,
- labor demand collapse versus worker-side constraints,
- temporary closures versus persistent reallocation,
- nominal employment versus welfare losses.

### Transfer

Transfer the same logic to a different setting:
- a historical epidemic,
- a pollution or heat shock,
- or a demographic-shift setting.

The key transfer question should be: does the shock change worker capacity, firm behavior, migration, or all three at once?

### Challenge / extension paper

A useful contrast paper is **Chicoine (2012)** on HIV/AIDS and labor-market outcomes in South Africa, because it pushes students to think about mortality-driven labor-supply effects, development settings, and skill-premium adjustment [@chicoine2012].

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. Why can the same health shock appear as an employment shock in one setting, a wage shock in another, and an automation shock in a third?
2. How would you distinguish a worker-capacity channel from a firm-reorganization channel in pandemic or pollution data?
3. Which workers are least able to adjust to disease or environmental exposure, and why?
4. When should aging be modeled as a labor-supply shock, and when should it be modeled as a technology-adoption shock?
5. Design a project that compares one historical health episode with one contemporary health shock using a common labor-market framework.

## Reproducibility And Code Lab Note

The Week 5 lab should use a bounded teaching path built around one modern health-shock labor paper and one historical or development comparison paper. The goal is not full replication of epidemic history, but a reproducible labor-incidence workflow with clear diagnosis and transfer.
