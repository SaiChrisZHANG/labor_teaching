# Week 3 Source Pack — Fertility, Mortality, Caregiving, and Lifecycle Labor Allocation

## Positioning

This week moves from individual work-capacity limitations to **dynamic household and lifecycle allocation**. The core object is not a one-period labor-supply response, but a sequence of labor, care, savings, mobility, and retirement decisions that unfold after fertility, mortality, caregiving, and aging shocks. The lecture should feel like a labor-economics week organized by a lifecycle model, not a generic family-economics or aging-economics overview.

## Why this week matters

The same demographic or health event can alter:
- current hours and participation,
- future wages and occupational paths,
- savings and retirement timing,
- household specialization and informal care,
- the value of job flexibility and amenities,
- and the incidence of pensions, caregiving burdens, and labor-market risk.

Aging populations make these margins more central, not less. A labor market can look stable in aggregate while particular workers and households bear large dynamic costs.

## Core points

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Lifecycle labor allocation is a **dynamic** choice problem involving work, care, savings, health, and timing.
- Fertility timing, caregiving, and mortality shocks can move both **current labor supply** and **future earnings trajectories**.
- Aging and retirement are labor topics because pension rules, work flexibility, health, and care needs reshape the feasible set for late-life work.
- Global aging matters for labor economists because demographic change alters labor-force attachment, retirement, care demand, and the design of older-worker jobs.
- A good empirical design must separate **health/demographic shocks**, **policy incentives**, **selection**, and **dynamic adjustment**.
:::

## Bridge

Week 2 treated disability and chronic conditions as direct work-capacity constraints and labor-market frictions. Week 3 broadens the frame to **households over the lifecycle**: children, parents, spouses, mortality, aging, and retirement all alter labor allocation. This week also foreshadows later course themes about workplace flexibility, care infrastructure, and demographic adjustment.

## Field Core

### A. Brief theoretical framework: lifecycle labor allocation

Start with a compact lifecycle labor-allocation framework. The lecture does **not** need a full technical model, but it should make explicit that the relevant problem is dynamic:

- workers/households choose labor supply, occupation, savings, and care over time;
- wages and job amenities are state-dependent;
- health, fertility, mortality, and care needs are shocks or state transitions;
- pension rules and retirement incentives alter the late-life budget set;
- current choices affect future earnings, skills, and retirement resources.

A useful generic object is:
- state variables: age, children, health, spouse/parent health, assets, pension eligibility, job type;
- choice margins: hours, participation, occupation, mobility, informal care, savings, retirement;
- outcomes: earnings, experience accumulation, job quality, well-being, and late-life consumption.

The point of the framework is to discipline the rest of the lecture:
- fertility timing is about **when** shocks arrive;
- caregiving is about the interaction of time constraints and insurance;
- aging is about changing work capacity, pension incentives, and the value of flexibility;
- retirement is not purely a pension problem; it is a labor-allocation problem.

### B. Fertility timing, children, and dynamic career effects

This section should cover:
- childbirth/children as a lifecycle shock to labor allocation;
- the difference between immediate labor-supply responses and persistent career effects;
- child penalties as a dynamic labor object;
- fertility timing and the interaction with human-capital accumulation, promotion timing, and job choice.

Keep the key distinction clear:
1. **timing effects** — when fertility occurs relative to education, tenure, and promotion windows;
2. **persistent specialization / career-path effects** — how shocks create long-run earnings and occupation consequences.

Suggested anchors:
- Adda, Dustmann, and Stevens on the career costs of children [@addaDustmannStevens2017];
- Kleven, Landais, and Søgaard on child penalties [@klevenLandaisSogaard2019];
- Lundborg, Plug, and Rasmussen on causal fertility effects [@lundborgPlugRasmussen2017].

### C. Severe health shocks, mortality risk, and family labor supply

This section should emphasize that households adjust labor **jointly** when major shocks hit:
- severe illness,
- disability onset,
- mortality risk,
- spousal death,
- and other household health crises.

Important distinctions:
- short-run vs medium-run family labor-supply responses;
- income-loss channel vs time-demand channel;
- anticipation vs surprise;
- whether the response is hours, participation, job change, or mobility.

Suggested anchors:
- Fadlon and Nielsen on family labor-supply responses to severe health shocks [@fadlonNielsen2021];
- García-Gómez et al. on health shocks and labor outcomes [@garciaGomezJonesRice2013].

### D. Caregiving and household reallocation

This section should focus on **informal care** as labor-market reallocation:
- parent care,
- spouse care,
- elder care,
- longer-run caregiving trajectories,
- and the interaction with labor supply and retirement.

Do not treat caregiving as just another household chore. It is a labor-market force because it changes:
- hours,
- participation,
- retirement timing,
- occupational feasibility,
- geographic mobility,
- and demand for flexibility.

Suggested anchors:
- Maestas, Mullen, and Powell on caregiving using administrative data [@maestasMullenPowell2023];
- Coile, Rossin-Slater, and Su on paid family leave and family health shocks [@coileRossinSlaterSu2022];
- Mommaerts on long-term care and family insurance [@mommaerts2018].

### E. Aging, pensions, retirement timing, and late-life work

This should be a major section, not a short coda.

Key objects:
- pension incentives and retirement timing;
- social security / pension eligibility as labor-supply shifters;
- the role of flexible jobs in extending work lives;
- post-retirement work, bridge jobs, rehire, and partial retirement;
- employer-side late-career job design;
- why aging societies make these margins increasingly important.

Students should see that:
- older-worker labor supply is shaped by both **health** and **institutional incentives**;
- retirement is not just “leaving work,” but often a transition into a new labor arrangement;
- demand for flexibility is a first-order labor object.

Suggested anchors:
- Ameriks et al. on flexibility and older workers [@ameriksBriggsCaplin2020];
- Bi and Zubairy on public pension reforms [@biZubairy2023];
- Coile et al. / ISS project on retirement around the world [@coileMilliganWise2018];
- Saez et al. on mandatory retirement / employment protection for older workers [@saezSeimSchoefer2024].

### F. Global aging and comparative evidence

This section should not become a macro detour. Keep it labor-focused.

The key question:
**How do aging societies change labor-market adjustment, retirement institutions, and care burdens across settings?**

Topics:
- differences in retirement ages and pension rules;
- older-worker participation across countries;
- demographic drag and labor-force composition;
- care-demand growth and labor-market effects;
- whether firms and institutions adapt sufficiently to aging workforces.

Suggested anchors:
- Coile, Milligan, and Wise on international retirement institutions [@coileMilliganWise2018];
- Kotschy and Bloom on population aging and growth, used cautiously as comparative background [@kotschyBloom2023].

### G. Research architecture

The conceptual and empirical architecture should be made explicit at the end of Field Core:

1. A lifecycle labor paper should identify:
   - the **event/shock** (birth, death, caregiving onset, pension reform, aging threshold),
   - the **margin** (hours, participation, occupation, savings, retirement),
   - the **dynamic horizon** (short run, medium run, long run),
   - the **unit of adjustment** (worker, spouse, household, firm),
   - the **institutional object** (leave, pension, flexibility, care system).

2. Common identification problems:
   - timing endogeneity,
   - differential anticipation,
   - selective survival and selection into treatment,
   - endogenous benefit take-up,
   - confounding labor-demand changes,
   - mismeasurement of care intensity and post-retirement work.

3. A good paper in this area should make clear whether it is identifying:
   - work-capacity effects,
   - incentive effects,
   - household insurance responses,
   - or dynamic earnings-path consequences.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper
Use **Fadlon and Nielsen (2021)** as the primary Reproduce → Diagnose → Transfer anchor [@fadlonNielsen2021].

Why it works:
- strongly labor-focused;
- clear family-response design;
- dynamic response object;
- strong administrative-data setting;
- conceptually transferable to other lifecycle shocks.

### Reproduce
Students should reproduce one core dynamic family-response object:
- how household labor supply changes around a severe health shock,
- distinguishing short-run and medium-run responses where feasible.

### Diagnose
Students should diagnose:
- what variation identifies the effect;
- whether the design isolates income loss, care demand, or both;
- what household margin is observed and what margins remain hidden;
- how dynamic selection or anticipation could bias interpretation.

### Transfer
Students should transfer the design logic to a bounded alternative lifecycle setting, inspired by one of:
- child penalty / fertility timing,
- caregiving onset,
- retirement-age reform,
- flexible late-life work / bridge-job transitions.

A conservative transfer anchor is **Ameriks et al. (2020)** or **Bi and Zubairy (2023)**, depending on the student’s interests [@ameriksBriggsCaplin2020; @biZubairy2023].

### Optional challenge paper
An optional challenge contrast is **Kleven, Landais, and Søgaard (2019)** or **Lundborg, Plug, and Rasmussen (2017)**:
- same broad lifecycle logic,
- but different timing, identification, and mechanism structure.

## Exercises and Discussion Prompts

1. In a lifecycle labor-allocation problem, how do fertility timing and severe health shocks differ as labor-market events?
2. Why can a household labor-supply response to illness be larger in the medium run than in the immediate short run?
3. What distinguishes a work-capacity explanation from an institutional-incentive explanation in retirement behavior?
4. When does post-retirement work indicate weak retirement incentives, and when does it indicate demand for flexible jobs?
5. What makes a country-specific aging paper broadly interesting to labor economists outside that setting?
