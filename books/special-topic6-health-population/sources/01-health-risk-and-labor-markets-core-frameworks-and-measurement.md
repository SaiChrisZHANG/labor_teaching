# Week 1. Health, Risk, and Labor Markets: Core Frameworks and Measurement

**Central question:** How should labor economists model and measure health when it affects both productivity and the feasible set for work?

## Opening orientation

This opening week frames health as a labor-market object, not as a background demographic characteristic. In labor economics, health matters because it changes whether work is feasible, how productive workers are on the job, which occupations and schedules are tolerable, and how wages and amenities should be interpreted. The same observed employment loss can reflect diminished work capacity, endogenous exits from demanding jobs, insurance and benefit rules, employer screening, or dynamic selection into healthier workers.

## Core points

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Health enters labor economics as human capital, work capacity, risk, and labor-market heterogeneity.
- Measurement is central: self-reports, diagnoses, disability status, mortality risk, biomarkers, treatment access, and mental health do not capture the same object.
- Causal interpretation is hard because health is jointly determined with work through income, stress, insurance, job conditions, and selection [@currieMadrian1999; @bound1991].
- Health affects labor outcomes through worker-side channels and firm-side channels, so reduced-form effects can mix supply, productivity, sorting, and employer responses.
- One of the field’s enduring research opportunities is to connect better measurement to more credible identification and better welfare interpretation.
:::

## Bridge

This course uses health and population as a labor lens: who can work, under what risk, in which jobs, and with what welfare consequences. The opening week therefore has two jobs. First, it gives students a conceptual map of how health enters labor-market choices. Second, it shows why this is a difficult empirical field: measurement error, reverse causality, omitted socioeconomic status, and dynamic selection can all make “health effects” ambiguous unless the labor margin is specified carefully [@currieMadrian1999; @blundellBrittonCostaDiasFrench2023].

## Field Core

### Health as work capacity, human capital, and risk

The benchmark labor perspective treats health as simultaneously:
- a productivity input,
- a determinant of work capacity and feasible hours,
- a risk process affecting future employment and wages,
- and a source of heterogeneity in tolerance for job tasks, schedules, and amenities.

In this sense, health is not just another control variable. It changes the budget set, the feasible set, and the expected value of future labor-market states. A worsening health shock may reduce hours, force occupational downgrading, alter search intensity, or raise demand for safer or more flexible jobs, even when observed employment does not move immediately [@currieMadrian1999; @french2000].

### Measurement: what exactly is “health” in labor data?

A central open question in the field is what labor economists are actually measuring when they claim to measure health. Week 1 should make clear that common measures capture different objects:

- **Self-reported health** can summarize private information about functioning, pain, and latent conditions, but it is vulnerable to reporting heterogeneity and justification bias.
- **Diagnosed conditions / biomarkers / hospitalization records** can be more objective, but they are incomplete proxies for work capacity and may miss functional limitations relevant for labor.
- **Disability program status** is not pure health; it is a joint outcome of health, program rules, administrative criteria, and incentives.
- **Mortality risk / acute shocks** can identify severe disruptions, but they may not map cleanly into everyday work capacity.
- **Mental health** often matters through cognition, stress, persistence, and social functioning, yet is especially difficult to measure cleanly in labor data.
- **Insurance and treatment access** can change observed health, labor supply, and reported health simultaneously.

The classic measurement lesson is that neither subjective nor objective measures are “obviously correct.” Bound’s classic paper shows that self-reported and objective measures each have bias problems, and the newer Blundell et al. work shows how richer objective measures and careful dynamic modeling can reconcile some of those differences [@bound1991; @blundellBrittonCostaDiasFrench2023]. Baker, Stabile, and Deri also remain useful for showing that even allegedly objective self-reported measures contain substantial measurement error [@bakerStabileDeri2001].

### Reverse causality, dynamic selection, and omitted socioeconomic status

A good Week 1 lecture in this field has to make clear why naive regressions are often uninformative.

- **Reverse causality:** work changes health through stress, injuries, insurance access, income, and job conditions.
- **Omitted socioeconomic status:** early-life resources, education, neighborhood context, and insurance status shape both health and labor outcomes.
- **Dynamic selection:** healthier people remain in the labor market longer and sort into different jobs, firms, and schedules.
- **Program rules:** disability and insurance institutions alter labor supply independently of underlying health.

This is why acute-shock designs, hospitalization studies, disability threshold designs, and dynamic retirement models remain so important. García-Gómez and coauthors show how acute shocks can have persistent labor-market effects, while Meyer and Mok illustrate how disability onset affects earnings, income, and consumption over time [@garciaGomez2013; @meyerMok2013].

### Worker-side and firm-side channels

Although much of the literature emphasizes labor supply, Week 1 should set up the course’s broader map by separating worker-side and firm-side channels.

**Worker-side channels**
- work capacity
- labor supply and hours
- job choice and occupational feasibility
- mobility and commuting tolerance
- search intensity
- retirement / exit timing
- welfare and amenities valuation

**Firm-side channels**
- hiring and screening on perceived health/work capacity
- task assignment and accommodation
- absenteeism expectations
- insurance provision and job lock
- workplace safety investments
- scheduling and contract design
- productivity and retention responses to health risk

This distinction helps students see why the same health shock can generate different reduced-form labor outcomes depending on the institutional environment and firm technology.

### A field map for the rest of the course

The rest of the course builds on this opening map:

- disability and chronic conditions
- fertility, mortality, caregiving, and lifecycle labor allocation
- mental health, stress, and workplace productivity
- disease exposure, environmental health, and demographic adjustment
- synthesis, frontier questions, and research design

Throughout the course, the key empirical discipline is to state:
1. the labor margin of interest,
2. the health object being measured,
3. the likely sources of bias,
4. and the welfare object being interpreted.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper

Use **Blundell, Britton, Costa Dias, and French (2023)** as the primary reproduction/diagnostic anchor. It is a strong opening-lab paper because it is explicitly about how different health measures and methods change estimates of the effect of health on employment [@blundellBrittonCostaDiasFrench2023].

### Challenge / extension anchor

Use **Bound (1991)** as the conceptual measurement challenge anchor. The purpose is not necessarily to reproduce the full original analysis, but to diagnose why subjective and objective measures can diverge and why measurement is the core design problem in this field [@bound1991].

### Reproduce → Diagnose → Transfer

**Reproduce**
- Recover one simple reduced-form relationship between a health index or health status measure and employment/hours.
- Show how estimates change when the health measure changes.

**Diagnose**
- Ask which part of the estimate is likely measurement-driven.
- Identify whether the design is vulnerable to reverse causality, omitted SES, or dynamic selection.
- Explain why “objective” does not automatically mean “better.”

**Transfer**
- Apply the same logic to a bounded alternative setting:
  - acute hospitalization,
  - disability onset,
  - or mental-health treatment timing.
- The transfer exercise should keep the question fixed—how health affects labor supply or employment—but vary the health measure or identification design.

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. In what sense is health simultaneously a productivity input and a constraint on the feasible set for work?
2. Why is it misleading to speak of “the effect of health on employment” without specifying a health measure and a labor margin?
3. Compare self-reported health and administrative disability status as labor-economics variables. What does each capture, and what does each miss?
4. Give one example of reverse causality and one example of dynamic selection in the health–labor literature.
5. Suppose two papers study the same condition but reach different employment effects. What are the first three design questions you would ask before interpreting the difference substantively?

## Reproducibility And Code Lab Note

The Week 1 lab should follow the standard **Reproduce → Diagnose → Transfer** workflow and prioritize a bounded pedagogical path that can run on reduced or synthetic data. Because this week is measurement-heavy, the most useful teaching code will likely compare alternative health measures or alternative identification assumptions rather than aim at a visually complex empirical replication.
