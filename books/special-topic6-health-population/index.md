# Labor Market, Health, and Population

Labor Market, Health, and Population is a compact 6-week advanced elective on how health, demography, mortality, fertility, and insurance systems shape labor-market outcomes over the life cycle. It is designed to bridge labor economics with health economics and population questions without becoming a general health field course.

Relative to the core labor sequence, the course draws from Labor I material on human capital, family, fertility, and policy, while also connecting to Labor II questions about regulation, insurance, and adjustment. Its focus is labor-market incidence: how health and population dynamics change work capacity, opportunity, and inequality.

## Course type

6-week compact advanced elective

## Prerequisites

- Labor I or equivalent preparation in labor and human-capital economics
- Graduate microeconomics and applied econometrics
- Prior exposure to health economics is useful but not required

## Course goals

- study health as both a human-capital stock and a labor-market constraint;
- connect early-life conditions, disability, fertility, aging, and mortality to later labor outcomes;
- analyze how insurance and healthcare policy change work incentives and protection;
- prepare students to evaluate health-and-population labor research with attention to timing, selection, and policy incidence.

## Course format

This compact elective is organized around lifecycle causality. Each week centers one health or population margin, one identification challenge, and one policy or research extension so the course stays tightly labor-focused.

## Week-by-week path

- Week 1. Health capital, work capacity, and labor supply. The course opens by asking how health enters labor models as both productivity and constraint.
- Week 2. Early-life conditions and adult labor outcomes. Students study childhood health, development, and long-run labor-market effects.
- Week 3. Disability, mental health, and labor-market attachment. The focus shifts to work capacity, stigma, insurance, and dynamic exit from employment.
- Week 4. Fertility, mortality, aging, and population structure. This week links demographic change to labor supply, care burdens, and labor-market composition.
- Week 5. Insurance, healthcare policy, and labor-market incidence. Public and private health systems are analyzed through employment, take-up, and protection tradeoffs.
- Week 6. Research design and frontier questions. The course closes by organizing the main empirical strategies and open questions at the health-population-labor boundary.

## Research orientation

The elective is meant for students who want a disciplined labor view on health and population rather than a full health-economics survey. Its research orientation emphasizes dynamic effects, selection problems, and policy designs that jointly affect health risk and labor-market behavior.
