# Technology, Innovation, and Labor

## Course role

Technology, Innovation, and Labor is a 6-week compact advanced special topic on how technological change reshapes labor demand, worker adjustment, firm organization, market equilibrium, inequality, and worker welfare. It is a labor-economics course first: every week begins from work, tasks, wages, employment, skills, hiring, mobility, institutions, or welfare.

## Cumulative logic

The course sequence is deliberately cumulative:

1. define the task-based and equilibrium vocabulary for studying technology in labor markets;
2. study automation and AI as labor-demand shocks;
3. add worker-side adjustment through skills, training, mobility, and career transitions;
4. move inside firms to adoption, hiring, management, and job redesign;
5. study inequality, rents, market structure, and labor-market institutions;
6. end with frontier research design.

The course should not become a generic technology, innovation, or industrial-organization survey. Innovation, diffusion, platforms, AI, and market structure enter only when they change labor demand, labor supply adjustment, wages, employment, mobility, job quality, bargaining, institutions, or worker welfare.

## Internal roadmap

- **Foundational:** Week 1
- **Demand-side:** Week 2
- **Worker-adjustment focused:** Week 3
- **Firm and organization focused:** Week 4
- **Inequality and institution focused:** Week 5
- **Synthesis and research-design oriented:** Week 6

## Weekly outline

### Week 1. Technology, Tasks, and Labor-Market Adjustment: Core Frameworks

**Central question:** How should labor economists map technologies into tasks, skills, labor demand, labor supply adjustment, and equilibrium outcomes?

The opening week builds the course around task-based and skill-based frameworks. Students distinguish technologies that substitute for labor, technologies that augment labor, and technologies that create new tasks or occupations. The week also sets the course's equilibrium map: technology first changes the task content of production, then workers and firms adjust through wages, training, mobility, hiring, reorganization, and entry or exit.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Technology matters here because it changes what work is demanded, which skills are valuable, how workers adjust, and how wages and employment are determined.

### Week 2. Automation, AI, and Labor Demand

**Central question:** Which tasks and workers are displaced or augmented when automation and AI shift labor demand?

This week studies robots, software, AI, and generative AI as labor-demand shocks. Students compare displacement, augmentation, output-demand effects, and new-task creation while learning why exposure measures differ across occupations, industries, firms, establishments, and places. The emphasis is on causal incidence: employment and wages can move differently depending on substitution, productivity, product demand, and adjustment frictions.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Automation and AI are labor-demand topics because they change the marginal value of tasks, the composition of vacancies, the demand for skills, and the distribution of employment and wages.

### Week 3. Worker Adjustment, Skills, Training, and Career Transitions

**Central question:** How do workers adjust when technology changes task prices, skill demand, and career risk?

The third week turns explicitly to the supply side. Students study skill portability, task-specific human capital, occupational mobility, retraining, sectoral programs, learning on the job, and career transitions after technological shocks. The week separates aggregate adaptation from individual incidence: a labor market can reallocate successfully while particular workers face earnings losses, mobility barriers, or welfare costs.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Worker adjustment is central because technology changes returns to skills, the cost of moving across jobs, the value of training, and the welfare consequences of displacement.

### Week 4. Firms, Hiring, Management, and Organizational Response to New Technology

**Central question:** How do firms adopt technologies and reorganize work, hiring, management, and internal labor markets around them?

This week moves inside firms. Students study technology adoption as an organizational choice involving complementary investments, management practices, job redesign, worker screening, training, supervision, and internal reallocation. Digital platforms and algorithmic tools enter as labor-market institutions that change matching, monitoring, scheduling, flexibility, and the boundary between employment and contracting.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Firm adoption matters because technology is implemented through jobs, vacancies, managers, teams, contracts, promotion systems, and worker-firm matching.

### Week 5. Technology, Inequality, Market Structure, and Labor-Market Institutions

**Central question:** How does technological change reshape inequality, rents, labor-market power, and institutions?

The fifth week studies distributional and institutional consequences. Students connect technology to wage polarization, firm wage premia, superstar firms, patent rents, labor share, employer concentration, bargaining power, unions, platform rules, and regulation. The focus is equilibrium incidence: technology can raise productivity while changing who captures rents, which jobs are protected, and which workers bear risk.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** Inequality and institutions are labor topics because technology changes wages, rent sharing, bargaining power, job quality, worker voice, and the policy environment governing work.

### Week 6. Synthesis, Frontier Questions, and Student Research Designs

**Central question:** What does a credible research design on technology and labor markets look like?

The closing week integrates the sequence into research design. Students choose a labor outcome, specify the technology margin, define the relevant worker or firm adjustment channel, select an exposure or adoption measure, and state the counterfactual. Possible designs use task measures, vacancy data, patents, firm adoption, worker panels, local labor-market exposure, experiments, or platform data.

**Why this is specifically labor economics:** The course succeeds when technology questions become research designs about labor demand, labor supply adjustment, firms, wages, mobility, inequality, institutions, and worker welfare.
