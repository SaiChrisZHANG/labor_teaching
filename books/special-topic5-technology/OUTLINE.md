# Technology, Innovation, and Labor

## Course role

Technology, Innovation, and Labor is a 6-week advanced elective on task change, technological adoption, and labor-market adjustment.

## Weekly outline

### Week 1. Tasks, skills, and technological change

**Central question:** How should labor economists map technologies into tasks, skills, and labor demand?

The opening week builds the course around task-based and skill-based frameworks. Students study why some technologies substitute for labor, others complement it, and many reshape job content without cleanly fitting either label.

### Week 2. Automation, AI, and worker adjustment

**Central question:** When technology displaces workers, what adjustment margins actually matter?

This week studies occupational transition, within-job redesign, retraining, and mobility. The central issue is how to separate headline job-loss claims from the more complex margins through which workers and firms adapt.

### Week 3. Innovation, firm organization, and adoption

**Central question:** How do firms decide whether and how to adopt new technologies?

The focus shifts from exposure to adoption and implementation. Students study complementary investments, organizational redesign, managerial quality, and heterogeneous diffusion across firms and sectors.

### Week 4. Wage inequality, polarization, and rents

**Central question:** How does technological change feed into inequality across workers, occupations, firms, and places?

This week connects technology to job polarization, superstar effects, productivity dispersion, and rent capture. It is the main distributional week of the course and ties technology back to core labor inequality questions.

### Week 5. Education, training, and policy response

**Central question:** Which training and policy responses plausibly improve worker adaptation to technological change?

Students revisit human capital and active labor-market policy in a technology context. The week studies reskilling, training design, wage insurance logic, and the difficulty of matching fast-moving technological change with slower-moving institutions.

### Week 6. Measurement frontiers and research design

**Central question:** How can the labor effects of technology be measured credibly?

The closing week studies exposure metrics, task measures, patent-linked designs, text-based measurement, and causal interpretation. The aim is to give students a durable empirical toolkit for a rapidly changing topic.
