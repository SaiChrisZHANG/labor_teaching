# Technology, Innovation, and Labor

Technology, Innovation, and Labor is a compact 6-week advanced elective on how new technologies change tasks, firms, wages, training, and worker adjustment. It is designed to be narrower and more analytically focused than the broad technology week in Labor II.

The course sits most naturally after Labor II, but it also draws heavily on Labor I material on human capital, inequality, and worker adaptation. Its purpose is to turn a large and often diffuse literature into a disciplined labor-market course centered on tasks, reorganization, adjustment, and incidence.

## Course type

6-week compact advanced elective

## Prerequisites

- Labor II is strongly recommended; advanced Labor I preparation is the minimum fallback
- Graduate microeconomics and applied econometrics
- Comfort reading work that mixes labor, industrial organization, and innovation questions

## Course goals

- understand the distinction between technological change, automation, innovation, and diffusion;
- connect task change and firm reorganization to wages, employment, and mobility;
- study worker adjustment through education, retraining, and occupational transition;
- evaluate how the empirical literature identifies technology exposure and causal incidence.

## Course format

The elective runs as a six-part arc from measurement to adjustment. Each week pairs one organizing framework with one design problem and one research extension so the course stays compact without feeling like a literature list.

## Week-by-week path

- Week 1. Tasks, skills, and technological change. The course opens by defining the task-based logic that links technology to labor demand.
- Week 2. Automation, AI, and worker adjustment. Students study displacement, augmentation, retraining, and occupational transition.
- Week 3. Innovation, firm organization, and adoption. The focus shifts to how firms implement technology and redesign work.
- Week 4. Wage inequality, polarization, and rents. Labor-market distributional consequences are traced through tasks, firms, and market structure.
- Week 5. Education, training, and policy response. The course asks which human-capital and labor-market policies plausibly improve adaptation.
- Week 6. Measurement frontiers and research design. The final week turns the technology-and-labor literature into a usable empirical toolkit.

## Research orientation

This course is aimed at students who want to move beyond slogans about automation or AI and ask disciplined labor questions about exposure, mechanism, heterogeneity, and welfare. It is especially useful as a compact elective because the topic changes quickly but the core identification problems are durable.
