# Week 4. Firms, Hiring, Management, and Organizational Response to New Technology

## Opening orientation

Week 4 moves inside the firm. The earlier weeks asked how technology changes tasks, labor demand, and worker adjustment. This week asks how those changes are actually implemented: which firms adopt, how they redesign jobs, whom they hire, which workers they train, how they supervise, and why the same technology can generate very different labor outcomes across organizations.

The core labor-economics question is not simply whether firms buy a technology. It is how technology adoption reorganizes vacancies, internal labor markets, delegation, monitoring, and bargaining inside the firm. AI makes that question especially sharp because it can operate both as a substitute for labor and as a productivity-enhancing tool for workers and managers [@autorLevyMurnane2003; @acemogluRestrepo2019].

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: note
- Technology adoption is a firm choice that typically requires complementary investments in management, reorganization, hiring, training, and supervision.
- AI can both substitute for labor and augment labor, so its incidence depends on which tasks are automated, which are redesigned, and which workers/firms can adapt.
- Firm implementation creates a principal-agent problem: managers may value control, monitoring, or speed differently from workers who face effort, privacy, or job-security concerns.
- Worker and manager attitudes toward AI can affect adoption, delegation, productivity, and inequality, so “technology exposure” is not the same thing as successful implementation.
- A good empirical design separates adoption, usage, attitudes, and realized labor-market outcomes.
:::

## Bridge

Weeks 1 and 2 introduced task-based frameworks and labor-demand channels; Week 3 turned to worker-side adjustment and frictions. Week 4 adds the organizational layer: technologies are implemented through firms, and firms differ in who adopts, how they adopt, and how they reorganize labor around new tools. This week therefore connects technology demand shocks to concrete labor-market institutions inside firms.

## Field Core

### A. Technology adoption as an organizational choice

Technology adoption is rarely a plug-and-play substitution. Firms must decide whether a technology complements existing workflows, requires new managerial practices, or forces redesign of tasks, teams, and internal reporting structures. That is why management practices and complementary investments matter so much in the diffusion of new technologies [@bloomSadunVanReenen2012; @babinaFedykHeHodson2022].

A useful decomposition is:
1. **acquisition**: whether the firm invests in the technology;
2. **implementation**: whether the firm reorganizes tasks and incentives to use it effectively;
3. **integration**: whether the technology becomes part of hiring, monitoring, scheduling, training, promotion, or delegation.

The same measured technology can therefore imply very different labor outcomes depending on whether firms merely purchase it, integrate it into production, or reorganize around it.

### B. Job redesign, hiring, and internal labor markets

Once firms adopt new technologies, they must decide whether to replace tasks, redesign jobs, or expand output with a new skill mix. This shows up in vacancy composition, job-posting language, training requirements, and internal reassignment. The empirical literature increasingly measures these responses using job-posting text, worker resumes, firm adoption surveys, and linked employer–employee panels [@babinaFedykHeHodson2022; @aghionBunelJaravelMikaelsenRouletSogaard2025].

A labor-economics way to frame this is:
- which tasks disappear,
- which tasks become more valuable,
- which jobs are re-bundled,
- which skills become hiring margins rather than training margins.

This distinction matters for inequality: some firms adopt technologies by upgrading incumbent workers, while others respond by hiring different workers altogether.

### C. AI as substitution, augmentation, and delegation

AI is unusually important for labor economics because it can affect not only production tasks, but also decision-making, forecasting, hiring, screening, and supervision. That means firms can use AI both to reallocate work across workers and to reallocate authority between workers and managers.

Three margins are especially useful:
- **substitution**: AI replaces tasks previously performed by workers;
- **augmentation**: AI raises output or quality conditional on worker effort/skill;
- **delegation/recontrol**: AI shifts which decisions are made by workers, managers, or algorithms.

This is why firm-level adoption can raise employment even when some tasks are displaced: output effects and reorganization may dominate narrow substitution effects [@aghionBunelJaravelMikaelsenRouletSogaard2025].

### D. Supervision, monitoring, and algorithmic management

New technologies also change supervision. Digital systems can lower monitoring costs, generate more performance data, and change contracts or incentive schemes. In some settings, better monitoring improves productivity and profit; in others, it increases worker strain, gaming, or conflict. The firm’s problem is therefore not just whether more information exists, but how it gets used in contracts and workplace governance [@kelleyLaneSchonholzer2024].

This organizational layer is essential for labor economists because monitoring technologies alter effort, risk-taking, job quality, and bargaining power. Algorithmic management matters here not only in gig platforms, but also in more conventional firms where scheduling, evaluation, and delegation become partially automated.

### E. Worker and manager attitudes toward AI adoption

A central frontier question is why seemingly similar technologies produce very different adoption and productivity outcomes across firms. One answer is **attitudes**.

Workers may distrust AI because they fear:
- job loss or de-skilling,
- surveillance or unfair evaluation,
- opacity and loss of control,
- unequal access to augmentation benefits.

Managers may differ in:
- trust in algorithmic recommendations,
- willingness to delegate decisions,
- beliefs about fairness and liability,
- beliefs about how workers will respond.

These attitudes matter because they affect implementation itself. A technology may be available and even productive in principle, but if workers resist it, managers do not trust it, or both sides anticipate conflict, adoption can stall or remain superficial. Conversely, firms with aligned expectations and clear training may realize larger gains.

This is also a distributional issue. If better-managed or higher-wage firms are more successful at integrating AI, then worker attitudes, manager beliefs, and organizational trust can become channels through which technology adoption widens inequality across firms and workers [@bickBlandinDeming2024; @mcelheranYangKroffBrynjolfsson2025].

### F. Methodological box: measuring attitudes toward AI adoption

:::{admonition} Methods box: measuring adoption attitudes and implementation frictions
:class: important
Common frontier designs in this literature include:

1. **Management surveys linked to firm outcomes**  
   Measure managers’ beliefs about AI, expected productivity gains, hiring needs, or barriers, then link those responses to firm-level employment, sales, productivity, or vacancy data.  
   *Strength*: captures managerial expectations directly.  
   *Limitation*: beliefs can proxy for unobserved firm quality rather than causal effects.

2. **Worker surveys linked to workplace outcomes**  
   Measure worker trust, usage, fear of replacement, perceived fairness, or willingness to use AI, then connect those attitudes to adoption, retention, or productivity.  
   *Strength*: directly captures worker-side frictions.  
   *Limitation*: attitudes may themselves be outcomes of firm implementation.

3. **Randomized pilots / staggered rollouts within firms**  
   Compare workers or teams that receive AI tools earlier versus later.  
   *Strength*: best way to separate the tool from selection into adoption.  
   *Limitation*: still may not isolate long-run equilibrium reorganization.

4. **Lab / online experiments on algorithm aversion or delegation**  
   Elicit whether workers or managers prefer algorithmic versus human decision-making in hiring, evaluation, or assignment.  
   *Strength*: clean identification of attitudes and beliefs.  
   *Limitation*: external validity depends on task realism and institutional context.

5. **Conjoint / vignette experiments**  
   Vary transparency, profiling, delegation, and performance information to identify what drives acceptance of AI-mediated decisions.  
   *Strength*: useful for unpacking mechanisms like fairness or transparency.  
   *Limitation*: measures preferences over scenarios, not realized workplace behavior.

6. **Matched employer–employee panels around adoption timing**  
   Study how worker outcomes evolve before and after firm adoption, possibly interacting with survey-based measures of trust or usage.  
   *Strength*: ties adoption timing to worker incidence.  
   *Limitation*: identifying the adoption shock itself remains difficult.

A strong design should distinguish:
- *technology availability* from *actual usage*,
- *usage* from *trust/acceptance*,
- *attitudes* from *productivity effects*,
- *short-run implementation* from *long-run organizational redesign*.
:::

### G. Research architecture and frontier questions

The research frontier is moving toward three linked questions:

1. **Who adopts?**  
   Which firms adopt AI or related technologies, and how does adoption correlate with preexisting management quality, workforce composition, or market power?

2. **How is adoption implemented?**  
   Do firms hire differently, retrain incumbents, alter supervision, or reassign authority? How much do attitudes and trust constrain this?

3. **Who benefits?**  
   Do gains accrue to firms, high-skill workers, managers, or consumers? Are low-trust or lower-skill workers left behind? Do firm-side adoption differences magnify inequality?

These are the questions that turn a technology lecture into a labor-economics lecture.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper

Use **Babina, Fedyk, He, and Hodson (2022)** as the primary anchor for the week. It is a strong example of using firm-level AI investment measures combined with worker-resume and job-posting data to study organizational change.

### Challenge / extension anchor

Use **Dargnies et al. (2024/2026)** as the challenge paper on algorithm aversion in hiring. It is useful because it shifts the focus from “what firms do after adoption” to “whether workers and managers actually want algorithmic delegation in the first place.”

### Reproduce

Reproduce one bounded result from the primary anchor:
- a descriptive comparison of adopting vs non-adopting firms,
- or a reduced-form relationship between adoption and workforce composition / job-posting shifts.

### Diagnose

Diagnose:
- what “AI adoption” is actually measuring,
- whether the design identifies adoption itself or adoption by high-quality firms,
- whether observed labor effects reflect substitution, augmentation, or selection,
- and whether implementation frictions or attitudes are visible in the empirical design.

### Transfer

Transfer the logic to a new bounded question:
- how would you adapt the design to study manager trust, worker resistance, or perceived fairness in AI-mediated screening or evaluation?
- what additional data would be needed?
- which attitudes could be measured directly, and which would have to be inferred from behavior?
