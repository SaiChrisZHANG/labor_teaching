# Week 5. Technology, Inequality, Market Structure, and Labor-Market Institutions

## Opening orientation

The earlier weeks tracked technology from tasks to labor demand to worker adjustment to firm adoption. Week 5 asks what happens when those adjustments scale up to markets. Who captures the gains from new technology? Do productivity improvements translate into higher wages, lower prices, or larger rents? Does technology increase competition, or does it reinforce concentration and labor-market power? And when workers reallocate across sectors, do they move into better jobs, more precarious jobs, or simply different low-productivity activities?

This lecture treats technology as a market-level labor force. The same innovation can raise productivity while also lowering labor share, increasing concentration, changing bargaining institutions, and reallocating workers across sectors with very different job quality. The point is not to decide whether technology is “good” or “bad,” but to understand the channels through which its gains are distributed.

## Core points

:::{admonition} Core points
- Technology changes inequality through several linked channels: task substitution, skill demand, firm rents, labor-market power, and sectoral reallocation.
- Market-level outcomes depend not only on the technology itself, but on who adopts it, which firms expand, how institutions distribute rents, and whether workers can move toward better opportunities.
- Current AI research should be situated in a longer history of mechanization, electrification, computerization, and platform-mediated work rather than treated as a completely isolated episode.
- Structural change is central: technology may move workers across agriculture, manufacturing, and services, and job quality can rise or fall depending on the destination sector, formality, bargaining conditions, and local institutions.
- Global evidence matters because diffusion, adoption capacity, and labor-absorbing adjustment look very different in the Global South, where informality, services, energy constraints, and outsourcing often shape incidence.
:::

## Bridge

Weeks 2–4 studied demand shocks, worker adjustment, and firm adoption. Week 5 aggregates these pieces. Technology no longer appears only as a firm or worker shock; it becomes a market-level force that changes concentration, labor share, structural change, and the institutional environment governing work.

## Field Core

### 1. From technology adoption to market-level incidence

A useful way to discipline the lecture is to treat market outcomes as the composition of three margins:
1. **within-firm productivity and wage effects**;
2. **between-firm reallocation**, where more productive or more technology-intensive firms gain market share;
3. **between-sector reallocation**, where labor moves across agriculture, manufacturing, and services.

This decomposition immediately explains why micro evidence and macro evidence can differ. A technology may raise productivity within adopting firms but reduce aggregate labor share if adoption shifts demand toward firms with lower labor shares or greater markups [@autorDornKatzPattersonVanReenen2020; @karabarbounis2023].

### 2. Technology, labor share, and superstar firms

One central market-level question is whether technology raises the share of value added captured by capital, intangibles, or dominant firms rather than labor. The superstar-firm literature argues that technology and globalization can shift sales toward more productive, more capital- or intangible-intensive firms, thereby lowering aggregate labor share even if nothing within a representative firm looks especially anti-labor [@autorDornKatzPattersonVanReenen2020].

This literature is useful for labor economists because it links technology to:
- labor share,
- firm wage premia,
- rent-sharing,
- concentration,
- and the distribution of good jobs.

It also creates a bridge to labor-market power: if technology intensifies concentration on the product-market side, labor-market power may strengthen on the hiring side as well [@azarMarinescuSteinbaum2019; @benmelechBergmanKim2022].

### 3. Market structure, labor-market power, and institutions

Technology does not only change production. It can change the institutional environment in which wages are set. Platform technologies can reshape contracting boundaries, scheduling, and algorithmic control. Intangible-heavy production may strengthen firms with scale advantages. Automation may weaken traditional worker voice in some sectors while creating new organizing challenges in others.

A labor-economics way to state the problem is:
- who captures the rents created by technology,
- which institutions mediate that capture,
- and which workers are protected by bargaining, transparency, or regulation?

This is why the week must treat unions, labor-market concentration, and policy institutions as endogenous mediators of technological incidence rather than afterthoughts.

### 4. Historical technology waves: persistence, reversal, and interaction

Current AI and automation research becomes more legible once it is compared to earlier technology waves. Mechanization, electrification, computerization, and robotics all changed task content, firm boundaries, and the sectoral composition of employment. But they differed in speed, complementarity, diffusion, and the persistence of labor-market consequences.

A useful historical set of questions is:
- how long do technology-induced market changes last?
- when do they reverse?
- when does a second wave undo the effects of an earlier one?
- when do multiple waves reinforce each other?

Historical and long-run work on mechanized cotton spinning, industrialization, and task-biased change is useful here because it reminds students that implementation lags, complementary investments, and organizational change can be as important as the invention itself [@juhaszTechnologyAdoptionProductivity2020; @autorLevyMurnane2003; @autor2015].

One lesson from this comparison is that AI should not be framed only as “job destruction” or “productivity growth.” The deeper question is whether AI is primarily another automation wave or whether it creates enough new tasks and complementary reorganization to alter the longer-run path of labor demand [@acemogluRestrepo2019; @acemoglu2019wrong].

### 5. Structural change and employment reallocation

Technology is also a structural-change force. In developing economies, technical change in agriculture can release labor into manufacturing or services. In advanced economies, automation and trade may accelerate reallocation out of manufacturing and toward services. But these transitions are not mechanically welfare-improving. The destination sector matters: labor can move into productive formal services, or into fragmented low-productivity services with weaker wage-setting institutions and lower job quality [@mcmillanRodrik2011; @herrendorfRogersonValentinyi2013; @rodrikSandhuSuri2024].

This means labor economists should distinguish:
- **employment reallocation** from **quality reallocation**;
- formal from informal services;
- high-wage from low-wage service growth;
- and productivity growth from worker welfare.

Recent structural-change work also emphasizes that reallocation happens both **across firms** and **within firms**, and that service-sector growth increasingly involves technology, outsourcing, and internal production of knowledge services [@dingFortMichaelsMorrowSchott2022; @helmKuglerSchonberg2023].

### 6. Global and Global South evidence

The global picture differs sharply from the standard U.S. automation narrative. In many lower- and middle-income economies, the main issues are not just robot exposure, but technology sophistication, diffusion constraints, management quality, informality, and whether services can absorb labor productively. Cross-establishment evidence shows large dispersion in technology sophistication, especially in developing economies, with implications for productivity and labor demand [@cireraCominCruzLeeMartins2024].

This also affects how we think about AI. In some settings, AI may complement scarce skilled labor; in others, its immediate labor-market effect may run through outsourcing, digital services, data work, or changes in the geography of infrastructure and energy demand. Recent policy and measurement work suggests that AI may shift electricity demand, data-center location, and digital service organization in ways that are labor-relevant even before traditional occupation-level displacement is visible [@worldBank2025GenAILaborDemand; @iea2026EnergyAI; @stiglitzRodrik2021AIglobalization].

This is not yet a settled empirical literature, but it is an important frontier. Labor economists should ask whether AI changes:
- where complementary infrastructure is built,
- which regions attract compute-intensive investment,
- which countries specialize in data work or outsourced service tasks,
- and how those changes interact with local labor institutions.

### 7. Methods and data for market-level technology incidence

:::{admonition} Methods box: how this literature is identified
:class: important
Common design families in this area include:

1. **Market-share decomposition and concentration accounting**  
Used to separate within-firm from between-firm contributions to labor share, productivity, and wage premia.  
Representative paper: [@autorDornKatzPattersonVanReenen2020].

2. **Shift-share / exposure designs**  
Used to measure local exposure to automation, trade, or technology shocks. They are especially useful for studying spatial and sectoral labor-market incidence, but require careful attention to identifying assumptions and shock structure.

3. **Long-run historical comparison**  
Used to compare mechanization, electrification, computerization, and current AI/automation episodes.  
Representative paper: [@juhaszTechnologyAdoptionProductivity2020].

4. **Sectoral and structural-change decompositions**  
Used to study reallocations across agriculture, manufacturing, and services, and to distinguish employment growth from productivity-enhancing growth.  
Representative papers: [@mcmillanRodrik2011; @dingFortMichaelsMorrowSchott2022].

5. **Cross-country establishment and technology-adoption data**  
Used to study how technology sophistication, management, and diffusion differ across development settings.  
Representative paper: [@cireraCominCruzLeeMartins2024].

6. **Frontier infrastructure and spatial data**  
An emerging design family links technology shocks to electricity, data-center, and digital-infrastructure geographies. These designs are still evolving and often rely on administrative infrastructure data, energy reports, or spatially matched firm data.

A strong design in this literature should make clear:
- what the technology measure actually captures,
- whether the object is within-firm, between-firm, or between-sector adjustment,
- whether job quantity and job quality move together,
- and whether the incidence falls on workers, firms, landlords, consumers, or local governments.
:::

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper

Use **Autor, Dorn, Katz, Patterson, and Van Reenen (2020)** as the primary anchor paper. It is the clearest example of how to connect technological change, market concentration, and labor share in a unified empirical design [@autorDornKatzPattersonVanReenen2020].

### Challenge / extension anchor

Use **Ding, Fort, Michaels, Morrow, and Schott (2022)** as the challenge/extension anchor. It is useful because it shifts the focus from concentration and labor share to structural change within versus across firms and the rise of knowledge services [@dingFortMichaelsMorrowSchott2022].

### Reproduce

Reproduce one bounded decomposition or reduced-form relationship from the primary anchor:
- a concentration/labor-share relationship,
- a between-firm reallocation object,
- or a simple industry-level market-share decomposition.

### Diagnose

Diagnose:
- what exactly the primary design identifies,
- whether technology is being measured directly or inferred through firm outcomes,
- whether the labor-share result is really about technology, market power, or both,
- and which labor margins are visible versus hidden.

### Transfer

Transfer the logic to one of three adjacent settings:
- structural change and service reallocation [@dingFortMichaelsMorrowSchott2022; @helmKuglerSchonberg2023],
- global technology sophistication and development [@cireraCominCruzLeeMartins2024],
- or a frontier AI-infrastructure question where energy/data-center expansion may shift regional labor demand [@iea2026EnergyAI].
