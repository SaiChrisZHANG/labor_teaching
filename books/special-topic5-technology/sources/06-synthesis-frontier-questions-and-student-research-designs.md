# Week 6. Synthesis, Frontier Questions, and Student Research Designs

## Opening orientation

This final week turns the course into a research architecture. The question is no longer only whether a technology displaces or augments labor, or whether workers and firms adjust. The deeper question is how to design a technology-and-labor project that is recognizably a **labor economics** paper: one that has a disciplined labor-market object, a coherent technology margin, a persuasive comparison, and a clear welfare or distributional stake.

The course has moved from **frameworks** (Week 1), to **labor demand** (Week 2), to **worker adjustment** (Week 3), to **firm organization and adoption** (Week 4), to **inequality, market structure, and institutions** (Week 5). This week integrates those pieces into a research workflow that students can actually use to start projects.

:::{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- A good technology-and-labor paper starts with a **labor object**—wages, employment, vacancies, task content, mobility, promotion, training, bargaining power, or worker welfare—not with technology alone.
- Technology is not one thing: **innovation, adoption, use, exposure, and organizational implementation** are distinct empirical objects.
- Persuasive research designs require clarity about the **unit of analysis**, the **comparison/counterfactual**, and the **timing of adjustment**.
- Frontier work increasingly studies not only direct labor-demand effects, but also **worker adaptation, firm reorganization, market power, institutions, and welfare incidence**.
- The best projects in this area make clear why the technology margin they study matters for **labor economics** rather than generic business or engineering questions.
:::

## Bridge

A compact way to synthesize the course is to think of technology-and-labor research as following a sequence:

1. **Technology margin**: what changed?
2. **Economic object**: what labor outcome moved?
3. **Adjustment channel**: through workers, firms, or both?
4. **Incidence**: who captured the gains and who bore the risk?
5. **Welfare**: what changed beyond observed wages or employment?

The course has repeatedly emphasized that measurement, timing, and equilibrium interpretation are not side details. They are central design choices.

## Field Core

### A research architecture for technology and labor

A useful project template is:

1. Define the technology margin:
   - invention
   - adoption
   - worker use
   - vacancy demand
   - task exposure
   - organizational implementation

2. Define the labor outcome:
   - employment / nonemployment
   - wages / earnings / hours
   - job quality
   - promotion / authority
   - training / reskilling
   - mobility / occupational switching
   - labor share / rents / bargaining power
   - worker welfare

3. Define the adjustment channel:
   - displacement
   - augmentation
   - retraining
   - occupational reallocation
   - within-firm task redesign
   - firm entry / exit / reorganization
   - market-structure or institutional response

4. Define the empirical comparison:
   - before vs after adoption
   - exposed vs less-exposed workers/firms/places
   - local labor-market exposure
   - firm-level adoption event
   - quasi-experimental policy/institutional variation
   - experimental or lab-style evidence

5. Define the equilibrium and welfare object:
   - partial vs equilibrium incidence
   - rents, pass-through, and labor share
   - adjustment speed and persistence
   - distribution across workers and places
   - whether wages alone are a sufficient welfare statistic

### Where the literature is

A simple map of the field today looks like this:

**Foundational frameworks**
- task-based substitution and complementarity
- automation vs new-task creation
- skill-biased and routine-biased change
- compensating forces through output demand and reorganization

**Strongly developed empirical literatures**
- local labor-market incidence of automation and robots [@acemogluRestrepo2019]
- computerization and task content [@autorLevyMurnane2003]
- worker adjustment, training, and career effects [@demingNoray2020]
- firm adoption, organization, and workforce composition [@babinaHowMuchShould2024; @aghionDifferentUsesAI2025]
- superstar firms, labor share, and rent capture [@autorConcentrationFallingLabor2017]

**Rapidly expanding frontier literatures**
- generative AI and worker productivity [@brynjolfssonGenerativeAIWork2025]
- AI adoption measurement at the firm and worker level [@aghionDifferentUsesAI2025]
- workplace attitudes toward algorithmic decision-making [@dargniesAlgorithmAversionHiring2024]
- technology and energy / compute infrastructure
- global and Global South technology adoption
- outsourcing and remote-service reorganization in the AI era

### Where the literature is going

Several directions look especially promising.

**1. Better measurement of technology**
Researchers are still using very different proxies for technology: patents, robots, IT capital, job-posting text, firm surveys, worker-use measures, and direct platform logs. A major frontier question is how these measures relate to one another and what each really identifies.

**2. Adoption versus use**
A firm may purchase a technology, but the economically relevant margin may be who uses it, how often, under what managerial rules, and with what complementary training or reorganization.

**3. Organizational implementation**
New technology often changes internal labor markets, performance evaluation, delegation, supervision, and bargaining. This is where labor and organization meet.

**4. Distributional incidence**
Productivity gains do not mechanically imply worker gains. A persistent frontier is who captures rents when technology diffuses.

**5. Global and infrastructural spillovers**
Technology is increasingly linked to electricity demand, data-center geography, service outsourcing, cloud dependence, and new spatial labor-market arrangements. These are labor questions because they shape where jobs are created, which workers benefit, and how firms reorganize production.

### Common design mistakes

Some recurring weak designs in this field are:

- treating a technology proxy as if it were the technology itself
- confusing exposure with adoption
- ignoring the timing of worker and firm adjustment
- reading equilibrium results from partial-equilibrium evidence
- using wage effects alone as the welfare object
- studying a high-profile technology without articulating the labor-market mechanism

### Research Architecture

A strong research design usually answers five questions crisply:

1. What exactly is the technology margin?
2. What is the labor object of interest?
3. Why is the variation informative?
4. Which adjustment margins are likely to operate?
5. Why should labor economists care about the answer?

This is where the course should leave students: not only with papers to read, but with a disciplined way to formulate their own.

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper

A natural primary anchor for a bounded reproduction is [@acemogluRestrepo2019], which gives students a clean example of how local exposure designs are constructed and how technology incidence can be interpreted through labor-demand margins.

### Challenge / transfer anchor

A useful challenge or transfer anchor is [@aghionDifferentUsesAI2025] or [@brynjolfssonGenerativeAIWork2025], because these papers move the field toward firm-level AI adoption and worker-use margins that do not fit neatly into older local-exposure frameworks.

### Reproduce

Reproduce one bounded empirical object from a canonical technology-and-labor design:
- a technology exposure measure,
- a simple reduced-form labor outcome,
- or a headline figure/table from a foundational paper.

### Diagnose

Diagnose:
- what the technology measure actually captures,
- what unit of analysis it privileges,
- which margins it misses,
- whether the result is partial-equilibrium or closer to equilibrium incidence.

### Transfer

Transfer the design logic to a different setting:
- from robots to AI,
- from local exposure to firm adoption,
- from labor-demand incidence to worker adjustment,
- or from U.S. evidence to a new global or sectoral context.

The purpose is not only to rerun code, but to learn how design choices map into labor questions.

## Exercises And Discussion Prompts

1. Choose one technology margin and one labor outcome. Why is this a labor-economics project rather than a generic technology project?
2. Compare a local-exposure design with a firm-adoption design. What do they identify differently?
3. Give one example where a wage effect would be an incomplete welfare statistic.
4. Which frontier direction from this course looks most promising for a student project, and why?
5. What would make a country- or sector-specific technology paper broadly interesting to labor economists?

## Bridge Forward

This course ends by turning technology into a disciplined labor research agenda. The broader lesson is that the most durable projects are not the ones with the newest buzzword, but the ones that make technology empirically and conceptually legible as a question about labor demand, worker adjustment, firms, institutions, inequality, and welfare.
