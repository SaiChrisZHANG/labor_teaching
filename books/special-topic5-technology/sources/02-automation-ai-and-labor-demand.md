# Week 2. Automation, AI, and Labor Demand

## Opening orientation

This lecture asks a narrowly labor-economic question: when automation and AI enter production, how do they change the demand for tasks, occupations, workers, and establishments? The right object is not “technology” in the abstract, but a sequence of labor-demand adjustments: displacement, augmentation, reorganization, product-demand expansion, and new-task creation. The same technology can reduce labor demand in one set of tasks, raise it in another, and change equilibrium outcomes through wages, vacancies, firm growth, and worker reallocation [@autorLevyMurnane2003SkillContent; @acemogluRestrepo2019AutomationNewTasks].

This week also situates current AI research in a broader history of labor-demand studies. Robotics and generative AI matter because they are current and empirically exciting, but they are not the first technologies to reorganize labor demand. Mechanization, electrification, and computerization all changed the task content of production and induced worker and firm adjustment. Understanding what is genuinely new about AI requires comparing it explicitly to earlier technology episodes [@autor2015Jobs; @goldinKatz1998origins; @fiszbeinLafortuneVamplew2020electrification; @deming2025technologicalDisruption].

```{admonition} Core points
:class: important
- Automation and AI affect labor demand through displacement, augmentation, output-demand expansion, and new-task creation.
- Employment and wage effects can diverge because technologies change task composition, bargaining positions, vacancy structure, and firm growth at the same time.
- Historical technology episodes matter because they show which modern AI patterns are recurrent and which are genuinely new.
- Measurement is not an afterthought: robots, task exposure, patents, firm adoption, job postings, and worker-use data identify different economic objects.
- A good empirical design in this literature must state clearly which technology object, which exposure margin, and which equilibrium margin it is identifying.
```

## Bridge

Week 1 defined the course’s basic map: technologies change task content, workers adjust through skills and mobility, firms reorganize production, and equilibrium outcomes depend on how labor demand and labor supply interact. This week moves to the first major substantive question in that map: how automation and AI shift labor demand across tasks, workers, firms, industries, and local labor markets [@autorLevyMurnane2003SkillContent; @acemogluRestrepo2019AutomationNewTasks].

## Field Core

### 1. A labor-demand taxonomy: displacement, augmentation, output effects, and new tasks

The task-based literature gives the cleanest starting point. If a technology substitutes for labor in tasks workers currently perform, it generates a displacement effect. If it raises the productivity of workers in complementary tasks, it generates an augmentation effect. If productivity gains expand output and downstream demand, labor demand may recover or even increase. If new tasks or occupations are created, labor demand can be redirected into new activities rather than simply reduced [@autorLevyMurnane2003SkillContent; @autor2015Jobs; @acemogluRestrepo2019AutomationNewTasks].

This taxonomy is useful because it prevents the lecture from collapsing into the simplistic question “does AI destroy jobs?” Labor economists instead ask: which tasks move, for whom, at which margins, and through which equilibrium channels? [@autor2015Jobs; @acemogluRestrepo2019AutomationNewTasks].

### 2. Causal incidence: why employment and wages need not move together

A technology shock can affect:
- employment in exposed tasks,
- wages within surviving jobs,
- vacancy composition,
- firm growth or contraction,
- labor’s bargaining position,
- local labor-market demand,
- and worker mobility/adjustment.

That is why wage effects and employment effects often diverge. Local robot exposure in the United States shows sizable displacement and wage declines in exposed commuting zones [@acemogluRestrepo2020Robots]. But firm-level robot evidence in France highlights that technology can also shift composition within firms and interact with productivity and scale [@acemogluLelargeRestrepo2020competingRobots]. Recent firm-level AI evidence from France points to employment growth after adoption in some settings, suggesting productivity effects can outweigh direct displacement, at least in the short run and for some uses of AI [@aghionBergeaudBoppartKlenowLi2025differentUsesAI].

The empirical lesson is that the relevant treatment effect depends on the level of analysis:
- worker/occupation,
- firm/establishment,
- industry,
- or local labor market.

### 3. Measurement: what is “technology” and what is “innovation” here?

A central open question in this field is measurement. Researchers now use several very different proxies:
- industrial robots per worker or per task,
- occupation/task exposure measures,
- patent-based or text-based technology exposure,
- firm adoption indicators,
- AI-use measures from worker or firm surveys,
- vacancy/job-posting measures of changing task demand,
- worker-level productivity/use data in specific settings.

These are not interchangeable. Robot exposure captures embodied automation capital. Task-exposure measures capture susceptibility of tasks to substitution or augmentation. Patent/text measures often proxy innovation direction rather than actual adoption. Firm-adoption measures identify a treatment closer to organizational implementation. Job-posting measures capture intended demand rather than realized employment [@acemogluRestrepo2020Robots; @koganPapanikolaouSchmidtSeegmiller2023; @aghionBergeaudBoppartKlenowLi2025differentUsesAI].

A useful rule for students is: every empirical paper in this literature should answer three measurement questions explicitly:
1. What exactly is the technology variable?
2. At what unit does it vary?
3. Does it measure invention, adoption, exposure, or usage?

### 4. Historical comparison: AI and automation inside the longer technology literature

AI and robotics belong in a much older labor-demand conversation. Historical technology episodes help students see both continuity and novelty.

#### 4.1 Mechanization and steam power

Historical mechanization shows the core displacement/productivity tension clearly. Mechanization raised labor productivity, but its labor-demand effects depended on how production reorganized and which tasks were routinized. Historical manufacturing evidence on steam-powered mechanization is useful because it reminds students that “automation anxiety” is not new, and because many modern debates about substitution versus scale effects already existed in earlier industrial transitions [@atackBatemanMargo2020inanimatePower; @mokyrVickersZiebarth2015historyAnxiety].

#### 4.2 Electrification and organizational change

Electrification is especially useful as a comparison for AI because it was a general-purpose technology whose labor effects depended on complementary organizational redesign. U.S. electrification evidence emphasizes that productivity gains often required capital deepening and reorganization rather than immediate one-for-one labor replacement [@fiszbeinLafortuneVamplew2020electrification]. This is one of the strongest historical analogies to AI: new technologies often matter most when firms redesign workflows, not merely when they install a new tool.

#### 4.3 Computerization and ICT

Computerization and ICT gave rise to the task-based framework that still structures much of today’s research. Autor, Levy, and Murnane’s routine-task replacement logic and the historical skill-complementarity work of Goldin and Katz are the clearest bridge from past technology episodes to modern AI/automation research [@autorLevyMurnane2003SkillContent; @goldinKatz1998origins]. These literatures show that technological change can both complement skilled labor and hollow out middle tasks.

#### 4.4 What may be distinctive about AI?

Compared with earlier technologies, AI may differ in at least four ways:
- it reaches more cognitive/nonroutine tasks,
- it is often measured through digital traces and within-firm usage rather than capital stocks,
- diffusion can be rapid in some occupations and firms,
- and it often works through organizational redesign and learning rather than direct machine replacement alone [@brynjolfssonLiRaymond2025GenerativeAI; @aghionBergeaudBoppartKlenowLi2025differentUsesAI; @huiReshefZhou2025shortTermGenAI].

But the historical comparison also disciplines the hype: earlier technologies likewise generated anxiety, lags, reorganization, heterogeneous incidence, and a mix of displacement and new work [@autor2015Jobs; @deming2025technologicalDisruption].

### 5. Recent frontier evidence on AI and labor demand

The frontier literature is valuable because it shows how new data change the questions we can ask.

- **Within-firm AI use and productivity:** customer-support and worker-use settings show substantial productivity effects and heterogeneity across worker skill levels [@brynjolfssonLiRaymond2025GenerativeAI].
- **Firm adoption and labor demand:** French firm-level evidence suggests some forms of AI adoption raise employment and sales, consistent with growth/productivity channels [@aghionBergeaudBoppartKlenowLi2025differentUsesAI].
- **Short-run labor-market outcomes after generative AI release:** recent work on the introduction of ChatGPT and image tools emphasizes occupation-level heterogeneity, changing contracting structures, and short-run reallocation margins [@huiReshefZhou2025shortTermGenAI].
- **Technology exposure from patent text:** newer measures link innovation content more directly to occupations and worker earnings, helping separate different types of innovation exposure [@koganPapanikolaouSchmidtSeegmiller2023].

The broader point is that AI is not replacing the older labor-demand literature; it is expanding the set of measurable technology shocks and the speed at which we can observe firm and worker response.

### 6. Research architecture for this week

A strong paper in this area usually identifies five objects clearly:
1. the technology measure,
2. the exposure mapping,
3. the outcome margin,
4. the adjustment horizon,
5. and the equilibrium channel.

Typical contribution styles include:
- local labor-market incidence,
- firm-level adoption event studies,
- worker-level productivity or wage effects,
- occupation/task reallocation,
- and historical comparison or long-run occupational churn [@acemogluRestrepo2020Robots; @deming2025technologicalDisruption].

## Research Lab

### Primary anchor paper

Use **Acemoglu and Restrepo (2020), “Robots and Jobs: Evidence from US Labor Markets”** as the primary anchor [@acemogluRestrepo2020Robots]. It is a canonical design for thinking about local exposure, displacement, incidence, and adjustment.

### Challenge / extension paper

Use **Aghion et al. (2025), “How Different Uses of AI Shape Labor Demand: Evidence from France”** as the challenge paper [@aghionBergeaudBoppartKlenowLi2025differentUsesAI]. It helps students see how firm-level AI adoption can yield different estimates than local robot exposure.

### Reproduce

Reproduce one headline object from the primary paper:
- a local-exposure employment relationship,
- a wage effect,
- or a simple event-study style labor-demand pattern if full replication is not feasible.

### Diagnose

Ask what the design is actually identifying:
- robot exposure or local industrial composition?
- displacement within occupations or local equilibrium incidence?
- short-run adjustment or long-run reallocation?
- direct treatment or a bundled technological environment?

Then compare that diagnostic to the challenge paper’s firm-level adoption design.

### Transfer

Transfer the logic to a bounded alternative technology setting:
- AI adoption,
- patent-text-based exposure,
- job-posting demand shifts,
- or a historical technology shock such as electrification or computerization.

The transfer question should force students to specify:
- the technology object,
- the unit of exposure,
- the margin of labor demand,
- and the likely equilibrium channel.

1. What is the economic difference between a robot-exposure design and an AI-adoption design?
2. When should employment fall but wages rise after a technology shock?
3. What is the best historical comparison for modern AI: mechanization, electrification, or computerization? Defend your answer.
4. Design a paper on AI and labor demand that uses job postings rather than observed employment. What does that gain and what does it lose?
5. Which technology measures identify invention, adoption, exposure, and usage? Why does that distinction matter?
